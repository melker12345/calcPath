# Foundations of Probability

Probability quantifies uncertainty. It assigns a number between $0$ and $1$ to each possible outcome, where $0$ means impossible and $1$ means certain.

Probability theory gives us the language to reason about randomness, and it is the foundation that all of statistical inference is built upon.

We start with basic rules, then build up to conditional probability, independence, and Bayes' theorem — tools used in everything from medical testing to spam filtering.

## Basic Probability Rules

The sample space $S$ is the set of all possible outcomes. An event $A$ is any subset of $S$. The probability of $A$ must satisfy $0 \leq P(A) \leq 1$, $P(S) = 1$, and for mutually exclusive events $P(A_1 \cup A_2 \cup \cdots) = P(A_1)+P(A_2)+\cdots$.

For equally likely outcomes: $P(A) = |A|/|S|$ — the number of outcomes in $A$ divided by the total number of outcomes.

**The complement rule**: $P(A^c) = 1 - P(A)$. Often easier to compute the complement.

**The addition rule**: $P(A \cup B) = P(A) + P(B) - P(A \cap B)$. Subtract the intersection to correct double-counting. For mutually exclusive events, $P(A \cup B) = P(A) + P(B)$.

**ELI5**: Probability is like assigning percentages to outcomes that must all add up to 100%. If 30% chance of rain, 70% chance of no rain. The addition rule corrects for double-counting: if 40% like cats, 30% like dogs, 10% like both, then cats or dogs = 40+30-10=60%.

**Worked Example**
- Card drawn from standard deck. $P(\text{heart or face card})$.
- $P(\text{heart})=13/52$, $P(\text{face})=12/52$, overlap $3/52$.
- $P = 13/52 + 12/52 - 3/52 = 22/52 = 11/26 \approx 0.423$.

## Conditional Probability and Independence

The conditional probability $P(A|B) = P(A \cap B)/P(B)$. It rescales to the world where $B$ is known to have happened.

Events $A$ and $B$ are **independent** if $P(A|B) = P(A)$ (knowing $B$ gives no info about $A$). Equiv: $P(A \cap B) = P(A) \cdot P(B)$.

For dependent events (e.g. cards without replacement), use $P(A \cap B) = P(A) \cdot P(B|A)$.

**ELI5**: Conditional is "narrowing the world". $P(\text{rain}|\text{cloudy})$ = among cloudy days, what fraction rain? Independence: coin flips have no memory — past flips don't affect next.

## Bayes' Theorem

Bayes' theorem reverses conditioning: $P(B|A) = \frac{P(A|B) \cdot P(B)}{P(A)}$.

- $P(B)$ = prior (belief before data)
- $P(A|B)$ = likelihood (prob of data if B true)
- $P(B|A)$ = posterior (updated belief)

The denominator uses law of total probability.

**Important medical insight**: Even accurate tests have low positive predictive value for rare diseases because false positives dominate.

**ELI5**: "Given this evidence, how do I update my beliefs?" A positive test for rare disease — Bayes accounts for how rare it is, often making most positives false.

**Worked Example: Medical test PPV**
- Prevalence $P(D)=0.01$, sens $P(+|D)=0.99$, FPR $P(+|D^c)=0.05$.
- $P(+) = 0.99\times0.01 + 0.05\times0.99 \approx 0.0594$.
- $P(D|+) \approx 0.0099/0.0594 \approx 0.167$ (only ~17% of + tests are true positives!).

## Counting Principles

Multiplication principle: total outcomes = product of choices per step.

- Permutations (order matters): $P(n,k) = n! / (n-k)!$
- Combinations (order doesn't): $\binom{n}{k} = n! / (k!(n-k)!)$

**ELI5**: Permutations for race podium (1st/2nd/3rd distinct). Combinations for choosing committee members (group same regardless of order listed).

The binomial coefficient appears in binomial probability too.

## Law of Total Probability

If $B_i$ partition the sample space (exhaustive + mutually exclusive), then $P(A) = \sum_i P(A|B_i) P(B_i)$.

This is the key denominator for Bayes. Break the world into cases, weight the conditional probs.

**ELI5**: To find overall prob of A, split into exhaustive cases, compute A in each, weight by case likelihood (like weighted average of section grades).

**Common Mistakes**
- Forgetting to subtract the overlap in $P(A \cup B)$ when events overlap.
- Confusing mutually exclusive with independent (mutually exclusive events with P>0 cannot be independent).
- Swapping $P(A|B)$ and $P(B|A)$ — Bayes is exactly for converting between them.
- Applying independence multiplication rule without verifying independence.
- Using permutations when combinations needed (ask: does order matter?).
