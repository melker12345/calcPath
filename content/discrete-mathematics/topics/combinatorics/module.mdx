# Combinatorics

Combinatorics is the art and science of counting. While it may sound elementary, clever counting arguments solve hard problems in probability, computer science (algorithm analysis, complexity), cryptography, and optimization. The fundamental question is "how many ways can this happen?" under various constraints.

We begin with the basic rules (product and sum), move to permutations (ordered selections) and combinations (unordered), introduce binomial coefficients that appear everywhere from Pascal's triangle to probability, then the pigeonhole principle (existence proofs via counting), and finish with inclusion-exclusion for counting unions with overlaps.

Mastering these tools lets you compute the size of huge sets without enumerating them and proves statements that would otherwise require exhaustive casework.

## Basic Counting Principles

<!-- section: counting -->

Two fundamental rules:

- **Product rule** (multiplication principle): If a process consists of k independent stages with n_i choices at stage i, then total outcomes = n_1 × n_2 × ⋯ × n_k.
- **Sum rule** (addition principle): If choices are mutually exclusive categories with n_i options in category i, total = n_1 + n_2 + ⋯ + n_k.

These extend to more complex situations via trees of decisions or partitioning the set of outcomes.

**ELI5**

- Product: "to make an outfit you choose a shirt (5 options) AND pants (4 options) AND shoes (3) → 5×4×3=60 possible outfits."
- Sum: "number of desserts = number of cakes + number of pies + number of ice creams" (categories don't overlap).

### Worked Examples

**Worked Example:**

**License plates**

A license plate has 3 letters (A-Z, 26 possibilities) followed by 4 digits (0-9).

Total plates: 26 × 26 × 26 × 10 × 10 × 10 × 10 = 26^3 × 10^4 = 175760000.

If repetition not allowed for letters: 26 × 25 × 24 × 10^4.

## Permutations

<!-- section: permutations -->

A **permutation** of n distinct objects is an ordering of them. There are n! = n × (n-1) × ⋯ × 1 such orderings.

The number of ways to choose and arrange k distinct items from n (k ≤ n), where order matters, is the **permutation** P(n,k) = n! / (n-k)! = n × (n-1) × ⋯ × (n-k+1).

When some objects are identical, divide by the factorials of the counts of each repeated item: for multiset with counts n_i, number of distinct permutations = n! / (n_1! n_2! ⋯ ).

**ELI5**

- Permutations answer "in how many different sequences can these people stand in line?" or "how many different ways can these tasks be scheduled?"
- If two people are twins (indistinguishable), you overcounted by swapping the twins, so divide by 2!.

### Worked Examples

**Arranging letters with repeats**

"MISSISSIPPI" has 11 letters: M:1, I:4, S:4, P:2.

Distinct permutations: 11! / (1! 4! 4! 2!) = 39916800 / (1 × 24 × 24 × 2) = 34650.

**Circular permutations**

For n distinct people around a round table (rotations considered the same): (n-1)! ways. Fix one person's position to break rotational symmetry.

## Combinations

<!-- section: combinations -->

A **combination** is an unordered selection. The number of ways to choose k items from n distinct items, where order does not matter, is the **binomial coefficient** (or combination) 

$$\binom{n}{k} = \frac{n!}{k!(n-k)!} = \frac{n (n-1) \cdots (n-k+1)}{k!}.$$

Key identity: $\binom{n}{k} = \binom{n}{n-k}$ (choosing who is in is same as choosing who is out).

Combinations appear when we care only about the group, not roles inside it.

**ELI5**

- Combination: "how many different teams of 5 can I pick from 12 players?" — the team is the same set regardless of who is captain on Tuesday.
- Contrast with permutations: if the 5 then get assigned distinct jerseys 1-5, multiply by 5! to get P(12,5).

### Worked Examples

**Committee selection**

From 7 men and 5 women, form a committee of 4.

Total committees: $\binom{12}{4} = 495$.

All-male: $\binom{7}{4} = 35$.

Exactly 2 women: $\binom{5}{2} \times \binom{7}{2} = 10 \times 21 = 210$.

## Binomial Coefficients and the Binomial Theorem

<!-- section: binomial -->

The **binomial theorem**:

$(x + y)^n = \sum_{k=0}^n \binom{n}{k} x^{n-k} y^k $.

Each term's coefficient tells how many ways to pick the y's (or x's) in the expansion.

Pascal's identity: $\binom{n}{k} = \binom{n-1}{k} + \binom{n-1}{k-1}$.

This generates Pascal's triangle, where each entry is sum of the two above it.

Other useful identities: $\sum_{k=0}^n \binom{n}{k} = 2^n$ (total subsets), $\binom{n}{k} = \frac{n}{k} \binom{n-1}{k-1}$.

**ELI5**

- Binomial coefficients count paths through a grid (right and down moves), subsets, and the ways terms multiply in (x+y) multiplied n times.
- Pascal's triangle is a beautiful number pyramid where every number is the sum of the two parents — and it secretly counts everything from coin flips to committee choices.

### Worked Examples

**Coefficient extraction**

Coefficient of x^4 y in (x + y)^5 is \binom{5}{4} = 5 (or \binom{5}{1}=5).

Expand (2 + 1)^4 = 81 = \binom{4}{0}2^4 + \binom{4}{1}2^3 + \binom{4}{2}2^2 + \binom{4}{3}2 + \binom{4}{4} = 16+32+24+8+1=81.

## The Pigeonhole Principle

<!-- section: pigeonhole -->

The **pigeonhole principle** (Dirichlet principle): If you have more pigeons than pigeonholes, then at least one pigeonhole contains more than one pigeon.

Generalized: If n pigeons into m holes, then at least one hole has at least ceil(n/m) pigeons.

To guarantee at least k in one hole: you need (k-1)×m + 1 pigeons.

It is an "existence proof" tool: you prove something must exist without constructing it.

**ELI5**

- With only 12 months, you need 13 people to guarantee at least two share a birth month — you don't need to look at names, pure counting forces it.
- "You can't put 3 socks into 2 drawers without one drawer getting at least 2" in worst case thinking.

### Worked Examples

**Guarantee duplicate birthday (simplified)**

Ignoring leap years, 365 days. To guarantee two people share birthday: 366 people (worst case 365 distinct, 366th collides).

For at least 3 sharing one day with d=365: worst case 2 per day = 730, +1 = 731 people.

**In a set of 6 people, either 3 mutual friends or 3 mutual strangers** (Ramsey R(3,3)=6 is small case of pigeonhole on graph edges).

## Inclusion-Exclusion Principle

<!-- section: inclusion -->

The inclusion-exclusion principle gives the exact count for the union of sets when there are overlaps:

For two sets: |A ∪ B| = |A| + |B| - |A ∩ B|

For three: |A ∪ B ∪ C| = |A| + |B| + |C| - |A∩B| - |A∩C| - |B∩C| + |A∩B∩C|

In general, alternate signs: add singles, subtract pairwise intersections, add triple, subtract quadruples, etc.

It is derived by counting how many times each element is overcounted in the naive sum.

**ELI5**

- When you add "people who like cats" + "people who like dogs", the cat-dog lovers were counted twice, so subtract them once to correct.
- For three clubs, the triple-overlap people were added 3 times then subtracted 3 times → net 0, but they belong in the union once, so add them back at the end.

### Worked Examples

**Survey problem**

30 students. 18 read novel A, 12 read B, 5 read both.

Students who read at least one: 18+12-5=25.

Read neither: 30-25=5.

**Three sets**

|A|=20, |B|=15, |C|=10; |A∩B|=5, |A∩C|=4, |B∩C|=3; |A∩B∩C|=2.

|A∪B∪C| = 20+15+10 -5-4-3 +2 = 45 -12 +2 = 35.

## Common Mistakes

- Using n! for permutations when items are not distinct — always divide by the repeats.
- Confusing P(n,k) (order matters) with C(n,k) (order irrelevant). Ask: "does the arrangement/assignment matter?"
- Forgetting that 0! = 1 (empty product) and \binom{n}{0}=1.
- Applying pigeonhole without the "+1" for the guarantee (the worst-case +1).
- In inclusion-exclusion, messing up the sign pattern or forgetting the triple intersections get added back.
- Computing \binom{n}{k} with k > n or negative (undefined or zero by convention).
- Assuming all outcomes equally likely when using counting for probability — only true in uniform spaces.
- For circular arrangements, forgetting to divide by n (or fix one position) and overcounting rotations.
