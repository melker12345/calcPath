# Logic and Proofs

Logic is the language of rigorous reasoning. In discrete mathematics and computer science, we use formal logic to express statements precisely, determine when they are true, and construct airtight proofs. Without clear logic, algorithms, program correctness, and mathematical theorems cannot be trusted.

This topic introduces propositions (the building blocks), the connectives that combine them, quantifiers for talking about "all" or "some", and the main techniques for proving statements: direct proof, contradiction, and mathematical induction. These tools appear everywhere from verifying software to proving the correctness of sorting algorithms.

## Propositions and Truth Values

<!-- section: propositions -->

A **proposition** (or statement) is a declarative sentence with a definite truth value: it is either true or false (but not both or neither). Examples: "2 + 2 = 4" (true), "The moon is made of cheese" (false). Commands, questions, and open expressions without assigned values are not propositions.

Every proposition has exactly one of two possible truth values: true (often denoted T or 1) or false (F or 0). This binary nature is what makes logic computable and foundational to digital circuits and Boolean algebra.

Two propositions are **logically equivalent** if they always have the same truth value. We write $p \equiv q$.

**ELI5**

- A proposition is like a light switch that is definitely on or off — no "maybe" or "it depends without context." "The light is on right now" can be checked; "Turn the light on" cannot.
- In code, propositions become boolean expressions: `isRaining && temperature < 0` evaluates to a single true/false.

### Worked Examples

**Worked Example:**

**Identifying propositions**

1. "Every even integer greater than 2 is the sum of two primes." — This is a proposition (Goldbach conjecture; its truth value is unknown but definite).
2. "What is the capital of France?" — Not a proposition (question).
3. "x + 1 > 5" — Not a proposition by itself (open; becomes one only when x is given a value).
4. "This sentence is false." — Paradoxical; not a well-formed proposition in classical logic.

## Logical Connectives and Truth Tables

<!-- section: connectives -->

We combine simple propositions using **logical connectives** (operators) to form compound propositions.

- **Conjunction** ($\land$, "and"): $p \land q$ holds precisely when $p$ is true and $q$ is true.
- **Disjunction** ($\lor$, "or"): $p \lor q$ holds when at least one of $p$ or $q$ is true (inclusive or).
- **Negation** ($\neg$, "not"): $\neg p$ flips the truth value of $p$.
- **Implication** ($\to$, "if ... then"): $p \to q$ is false only when $p$ is true and $q$ is false. The antecedent $p$ is the "if" part; $q$ the consequent.
- **Biconditional** ($\leftrightarrow$, "if and only if"): $p \leftrightarrow q$ is true when $p$ and $q$ have the same truth value.

A **truth table** lists every possible combination of truth values for the component propositions and shows the resulting value of the compound statement. For two propositions there are $2^2 = 4$ rows; for three, 8 rows.

The implication is subtle: "If it rains, then the ground is wet" is true whenever it does not rain (vacuous truth). In mathematics we routinely use implications this way.

**ELI5**

- $\land$ is both conditions must hold (like getting dessert only if you finish vegetables AND clean your plate).
- $\lor$ is "at least one" (you may have cake or ice cream or both).
- Implication is a one-way promise: when the premise happens, the conclusion must follow. If the premise never happens, the promise is kept vacuously.

### Worked Examples

**Building and evaluating a truth table for $p \to (q \land \neg r)$**

1. We have three atomic propositions, so 8 rows.
2. Compute inner: $\neg r$ is the flip of r.
3. Then $q \land \neg r$: true only if q true and r false.
4. Finally $p \to$ that result: false only in the row where p=true and (q and not r)=false.

Full table (T=1, F=0):

| p | q | r | ¬r | q∧¬r | p→(q∧¬r) |
|---|---|---|---|------|-----------|
| T | T | T | F | F | F |
| T | T | F | T | T | T |
| T | F | T | F | F | F |
| T | F | F | T | F | F |
| F | T | T | F | F | T |
| F | T | F | T | T | T |
| F | F | T | F | F | T |
| F | F | F | T | F | T |

## Logical Equivalences and Laws

<!-- section: equivalences -->

Two compound propositions are **logically equivalent** if their truth tables are identical in the final column. We can prove equivalence by truth table or by using a small set of basic laws (identities) that hold for all propositions.

Important laws (all can be verified by truth tables):

- Double negation: $\neg(\neg p) \equiv p$
- De Morgan's laws: the negation of a conjunction is the disjunction of negations, and vice versa.
- Distributive: $p \land (q \lor r) \equiv (p \land q) \lor (p \land r)$ and $p \lor (q \land r) \equiv (p \lor q) \land (p \lor r)$
- Absorption, idempotent, etc.
- Implication as disjunction: $p \to q$ is equivalent to $\neg p \lor q$ .
- Contrapositive: $p \to q \equiv \neg q \to \neg p$ (extremely useful in proofs)

A **tautology** is always true (e.g., $p \lor \neg p$). A **contradiction** is always false (e.g., $p \land \neg p$). These are the logical analogues of "1=1" and "1=0".

**ELI5**

- De Morgan's laws are like "not (both A and B)" means "not A or not B" — the same rule that turns "I will not eat both cake and pie" into "I will skip cake or skip pie (or both)".
- Equivalences let us rewrite statements into forms easier to prove or implement in code.

### Worked Examples

**Using laws to simplify $\neg(\neg p \lor q) \land p$**

1. Apply De Morgan to the negation: $\neg(\neg p \lor q) \equiv p \land \neg q$.
2. Now the expression is $(p \land \neg q) \land p$.
3. Idempotent law (p ∧ p ≡ p): $p \land \neg q \land p \equiv p \land \neg q$.
4. Result: $p \land \neg q$, which is "p and not q".

**Proving equivalence via contrapositive**

To prove "if n is divisible by 4 then n is even", the contrapositive is "if n is not even (i.e. odd) then n is not divisible by 4". Both are easier or equally easy in different contexts.

## Quantifiers

<!-- section: quantifiers -->

**Quantifiers** let us make statements about collections of objects.

- Universal quantifier $\forall$ ("for all"): $\forall x \in S, P(x)$ means "for every x in set S, P(x) holds".
- Existential quantifier $\exists$ ("there exists"): $\exists x \in S, P(x)$ means "there is at least one x in S such that P(x) holds".

The **negation** of quantified statements flips the quantifier and negates the predicate:

- $\neg(\forall x, P(x)) \equiv \exists x, \neg P(x)$
- $\neg(\exists x, P(x)) \equiv \forall x, \neg P(x)$

Order matters: $\forall x \exists y$ ("for every x there is a y") is different from $\exists y \forall x$ ("there is a single y that works for every x").

**ELI5**

- $\forall$ is "no exceptions allowed" — like "every student in the class passed" (check all of them).
- $\exists$ is "at least one" — "someone in the class got 100%" (you only need to find one).
- Negating "all students passed" gives "there exists a student who did not pass".

### Worked Examples

**Negating a quantified statement**

1. Original: "Every integer greater than 1 has a prime factor." $\forall n>1, \exists p$ prime such that p divides n.
2. Negation: "There exists an integer n > 1 that has no prime factor." (i.e., 1 or a composite with none — but actually the negation is false, proving the original).
3. Simpler example: $\neg(\forall x \in \mathbb{R}, x^2 \geq 0) \equiv \exists x \in \mathbb{R}, x^2 < 0$. (The right side is false.)

## Direct Proofs and Proof Strategies

<!-- section: direct-proofs -->

A **direct proof** of an implication "if P then Q" proceeds by assuming P is true and deriving Q using definitions, previously proved theorems, and logical rules.

Common strategies:

- Use definitions (e.g., "even" means "divisible by 2").
- Work forward from assumptions or backward from the goal.
- Use the contrapositive when the original implication is awkward.
- Cases: split into exhaustive mutually exclusive cases (e.g., n even or n odd).

A proof is a chain of justified statements ending at the desired conclusion. Every step must be reversible or clearly follow from prior steps and axioms.

**ELI5**

- Direct proof is like following a recipe: start with the ingredients (assumptions), apply allowed operations step by step, and arrive at the finished dish (the conclusion). You must justify why each step is legal.

### Worked Examples

**Direct proof that the sum of two even integers is even**

1. Let m and n be even integers. By definition, m = 2a and n = 2b for some integers a, b.
2. Then m + n = 2a + 2b = 2(a + b).
3. Since a + b is an integer, m + n is even by definition. QED.

**Proving "if n is odd then n² is odd" (contrapositive version)**

Contrapositive: if n² is even then n is even.

1. Assume n² even: n² = 2k for integer k.
2. If n were odd, n = 2m+1, then n² = 4m² + 4m + 1 = 2(2m²+2m) + 1, which is odd — contradiction.
3. (Better: factor or note that odd squared is odd.) Thus n must be even.

## Proof by Contradiction

<!-- section: contradiction -->

**Proof by contradiction** (reductio ad absurdum): to prove P, assume both P is false (¬P) and any other given premises, then derive a logical contradiction (something known to be false, like 0=1 or "n is both even and odd").

Because the assumption ¬P led to impossibility, ¬P must itself be false, so P is true.

This is powerful for statements that are hard to prove directly, such as "√2 is irrational", "there are infinitely many primes", or "no rational number squared equals 2".

**ELI5**

- Assume the opposite of what you want to prove, then show that the world explodes into nonsense (a contradiction). Therefore your opposite assumption was wrong, and the original claim must be correct. Like detective work: "if the butler didn't do it, then the clock must have been lying and the door locked from the inside — impossible, so the butler did it."

### Worked Examples

**Classic: √2 is irrational (proof by contradiction)**

1. Assume for contradiction that √2 is rational: √2 = a/b where a, b are integers with no common factors (b > 0) and gcd(a,b)=1.
2. Square both sides: 2 = a²/b² ⇒ a² = 2 b².
3. Thus a² is even, so a must be even (if a odd, a² odd). Let a = 2c.
4. Then (2c)² = 2 b² ⇒ 4c² = 2 b² ⇒ 2 c² = b². So b² even ⇒ b even.
5. But then a and b share common factor 2, contradicting gcd=1.
6. Therefore the assumption is false: √2 is irrational.

## Mathematical Induction

<!-- section: induction -->

**Mathematical induction** proves statements of the form "P(n) holds for all integers n ≥ n₀".

It consists of two parts:

1. **Base case**: Prove P(n₀) directly.
2. **Inductive step**: Assume P(k) is true for some arbitrary k ≥ n₀ (the inductive hypothesis, IH). Using the IH, prove that P(k+1) is true.

If both succeed, then by the "domino effect" P holds for all n ≥ n₀: base falls, knocks over the next, and so on forever.

Variants: strong induction (assume all previous cases up to k), or complete induction.

Induction is the discrete analogue of "proof by continuity" or recursive reasoning.

**ELI5**

- Imagine an infinite line of dominoes. You prove the first one falls (base). You prove that if any domino falls, the next one must fall (inductive step). Therefore they all fall.

### Worked Examples

**Prove by induction: $1 + 2 + \dots + n = \frac{n(n+1)}{2}$ for n ≥ 1**

1. Base case (n=1): 1 = 1(2)/2 = 1. Holds.
2. Inductive hypothesis: Assume true for n = k: sum = k(k+1)/2.
3. Inductive step (for k+1): sum up to k+1 = sum to k + (k+1) . Using the IH this simplifies algebraically to the formula with (k+1) plugged in.
4. This is exactly the formula with n replaced by k+1. By induction, true for all positive integers n.

## Common Mistakes

- **Converse is not automatic**: Treating "p implies q" as "q implies p" is invalid. The converse is not automatically true (counterexamples exist).
- **Vacuous truth is real**: The implication $p \to q$ is true whenever $p$ is false, regardless of $q$. Do not forget this case.
- **De Morgan duality**: "not (A and B)" is "not A or not B" (and vice versa). It is not "not A and not B".
- **Induction requires both parts**: Forgetting the base case, or assuming the conclusion inside the inductive step (circular reasoning), breaks the proof.
- **Quantifier negation rules**: $\neg \forall x \, P(x) \equiv \exists x \, \neg P(x)$ (and dually for $\exists$). Order and scope matter; swapping without care is invalid.
- **Choose the right technique**: Use contradiction only when a direct proof is hard or unnatural; direct proofs are often clearer when available.
- **Quantifier order is not commutative**: $\forall \exists$ is not the same as $\exists \forall$ in general; they are not equivalent.
