# Number Theory

Number theory is the study of the integers and their properties — especially divisibility, primes, and modular arithmetic. Though ancient, it powers modern cryptography (RSA, Diffie-Hellman), hashing, random number generation, and error-correcting codes.

We focus on the computational side: recognizing primes, computing gcd efficiently, solving linear congruences, and using the Chinese Remainder Theorem to combine modular information. These algorithms are efficient and form the backbone of public-key cryptography.

## Prime Numbers

<!-- section: primes -->

Prime numbers are the atoms out of which every integer is built by multiplication. Their distribution and arithmetic properties have fascinated mathematicians for millennia and today underwrite the security of internet commerce through RSA and related cryptosystems.

A prime is an integer greater than 1 whose only positive divisors are 1 and itself. The fundamental theorem of arithmetic asserts that every integer greater than 1 factors uniquely into primes (up to order). Euclid's ancient proof that there are infinitely many primes is still the simplest and most elegant; modern proofs use analysis, topology, and probability as well.

In this section you will learn the definition, Euclid's proof of infinitude, the fundamental theorem, basic properties of divisibility and gcd, and the computational techniques (trial division, sieves, Euclidean algorithm) that make number theory practical.

A positive integer p > 1 is **prime** if it has no positive divisors other than 1 and itself. Otherwise it is **composite**.

1 is neither prime nor composite.

There are infinitely many primes (Euclid's proof by contradiction: assume finitely many p1...pk, then p1...pk +1 is a new prime or has a new prime factor).

The **Fundamental Theorem of Arithmetic**: every integer n > 1 factors uniquely (up to order) as a product of primes: n = p1^{e1} ... pk^{ek}.

**Prime factorization** is the key to many divisibility questions.

**ELI5**

- Primes are the "atoms" of the integers — you can't break them into smaller integer factors >1.
- Every number is built by multiplying these atoms (with repeats allowed). The factorization is like the unique "ingredient list" with quantities.

### Worked Examples

**Worked Example:**

**Is 91 prime?**

sqrt(91)≈9.5. Check divis by 2,3,5,7. 91=7*13. Not prime.

**Prime factorization of 180**

180=2*90=2*2*45=2*2*3*15=2*2*3*3*5 = 2² × 3² × 5.

## Divisors and the Greatest Common Divisor

<!-- section: divisors -->

If d divides n (d | n), then n = d * k for integer k; d is a **divisor** (factor) of n.

The **greatest common divisor** gcd(a,b) is the largest positive integer that divides both a and b.

The **least common multiple** lcm(a,b) satisfies gcd(a,b) * lcm(a,b) = |a b| (for positive).

If a = p1^{a1}... , b=p1^{b1}... then gcd takes min exponents, lcm takes max.

Number of positive divisors of n = p1^{e1}... is ∏ (ei + 1).

**ELI5**

- Divisors of 12: 1,2,3,4,6,12. Common divisors of 12 and 18 are 1,2,3,6; greatest is 6.
- gcd is the largest "shared building block" pile; lcm is the smallest number that contains both as building blocks.

### Worked Examples

**Divisor count**

For 60=2²*3*5, number of divisors=(2+1)(1+1)(1+1)=3*2*2=12.

List: 1,2,3,4,5,6,10,12,15,20,30,60.

**gcd via primes**

gcd(48,180): 48=2^4*3, 180=2^2*3^2*5 → min 2^2 *3 =12.

## The Euclidean Algorithm

<!-- section: euclid -->

The **Euclidean algorithm** computes gcd(a,b) efficiently (a > b > 0):

gcd(a,b) = gcd(b, a mod b)

Repeat until remainder 0; last nonzero remainder is the gcd.

It is based on: any common divisor of a and b also divides a - q b, hence divides the remainder.

**Extended Euclidean algorithm** also finds integers x,y (Bézout coefficients) such that gcd(a,b) = a x + b y. These exist for any integers (Bézout's identity).

Running time is O(log min(a,b)) — very fast even for huge numbers.

**ELI5**

- Instead of listing all divisors of both numbers and finding the biggest shared one, you repeatedly replace the bigger number by the remainder when divided by the smaller — like the "difference game" but faster with mod.
- The extended version back-substitutes the remainders to express the gcd as a combination (like making change with two denominations).

### Worked Examples

**gcd(1071, 462)**

1071 = 2*462 + 147

462 = 3*147 + 21

147 = 7*21 + 0

gcd = 21.

**Extended for gcd(48,18)**

48 = 2*18 + 12

18 = 1*12 + 6

12 = 2*6 + 0

gcd=6.

Back-sub: 6 = 18 -1*12

12 = 48-2*18

6 = 18 -1*(48-2*18) = 3*18 -1*48

Thus 6 = -1*48 + 3*18. (x=-1, y=3)

## Modular Arithmetic and Congruences

<!-- section: modular -->

We say a ≡ b (mod m) if m divides (a - b), i.e., a and b leave the same remainder when divided by m.

Congruence is an equivalence relation; the classes are residue classes 0,1,...,m-1.

Arithmetic works on congruences:

- If a≡b (mod m) and c≡d (mod m) then a+c ≡ b+d, a-c ≡ b-d, a*c ≡ b*d (mod m)

- a^k ≡ b^k (mod m) for positive k.

Solving linear congruence ax ≡ b (mod m): solutions exist iff gcd(a,m) divides b. If d=gcd(a,m)|b, there are exactly d solutions mod m.

The **multiplicative inverse** of a mod m exists iff gcd(a,m)=1; then ax≡1 (mod m) has unique solution.

**ELI5**

- Modular arithmetic is "clock arithmetic": 10 o'clock + 5 hours = 3 o'clock (mod 12). You care only about the remainder.
- Two numbers are "the same mod m" if they differ by a multiple of m (a whole number of full cycles).

### Worked Examples

**Compute 7^3 mod 5**

7≡2 mod5, 2^3=8≡3 mod5. Or direct 343 /5 =68*5=340, rem 3.

**Solve 5x ≡ 3 (mod 7)**

gcd(5,7)=1 divides 3. Inverse of 5 mod7: find y,5y=1 mod7. Trial: 5*3=15≡1. y=3.

x ≡ 3*3 =9 ≡2 mod7.

Check: 5*2=10≡3 mod7 yes.

## The Chinese Remainder Theorem

<!-- section: chinese -->

When moduli are pairwise coprime, the Chinese Remainder Theorem (CRT) guarantees a unique solution modulo the product.

If m1, m2, ..., mk pairwise coprime (gcd(mi,mj)=1 for i≠j), and we have the system

x ≡ a1 (mod m1)

...

x ≡ ak (mod mk)

Then there is a unique solution modulo M = m1 m2 ... mk.

Explicit construction: x = sum ai * (M/mi) * yi  where yi is inverse of (M/mi) mod mi.

CRT lets us break a big modulus problem into smaller coprime pieces, solve, then combine — used heavily in RSA and fast arithmetic.

**ELI5**

- If you know a number mod 3, mod 5, and mod 7 (coprime), you can reconstruct the unique number mod 105 that satisfies all three simultaneously. Like having the time on three different clock cycles and figuring out the exact second.
- The coprimeness ensures the "cycles" mesh without common factors, giving full coverage.

### Worked Examples

**Solve x ≡ 2 mod 3, x ≡ 3 mod 5, x ≡ 2 mod 7. Modulus 105.**

One solution is x=23: 23/3 rem 2, 23/5=4*5+3, 23/7=3*7+2. Yes.

Using formula or successive substitution yields the class 23 + 105k.

**Simple two-modulus as in questions**

x≡2 mod3, x≡3 mod5 → x=8 works (8 mod3=2, 8 mod5=3), and 8+15=23 etc.

## Common Mistakes

- Thinking 1 is prime (it is not).
- Forgetting that gcd computations via primes require the prime factorization first (Euclidean is usually faster and doesn't need it).
- Confusing "divides" notation a|b (a divides b) with the fraction a/b.
- Assuming every linear congruence ax≡b mod m has a solution (needs d|gcd(a,m) divides b).
- Forgetting that inverses exist only when gcd(a,m)=1.
- In CRT, applying when moduli are not coprime (may have no solution or multiple solutions mod lcm).
- Computing a mod m as negative remainders without normalizing to [0,m-1].
- Believing unique factorization holds in every number system (false in some rings, but true for integers).
- Using Euclidean algorithm steps incorrectly (must replace larger by remainder each time).
- In extended Euclidean, sign errors when back-substituting the coefficients.
