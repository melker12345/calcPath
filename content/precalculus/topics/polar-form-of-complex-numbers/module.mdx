# Polar form of complex numbers

Polar form turns multiplication and powers into arithmetic on the modulus r and argument θ. De Moivre follows immediately.

## Polar Form of Complex Numbers

<!-- section: polar-form -->

Any nonzero complex z can be written z = r (cos θ + i sin θ) where r = |z| ≥ 0 and θ = arg(z).

This is also written r cis θ or, via Euler, r e^{iθ}.

Conversion:
- r = |z|, θ = atan2(Im, Re)
- a = r cos θ, b = r sin θ

Polar form makes multiplication, division, and powers trivial.

**ELI5**

- Instead of (x,y) coordinates, describe the number by "how far from origin" and "which direction." Multiplication then becomes "multiply the distances and add the angles."

**Worked Example:**

**Write -2 - 2i in polar form**

1. r = sqrt(4+4)=√8=2√2.
2. θ in quadrant III: θ = -3π/4 or 5π/4 (principal usually -3π/4).
3. -2 -2i = 2√2 (cos(-3π/4) + i sin(-3π/4)).

## Common Mistakes

- **Principal argument range**: arg usually taken in (-π, π]; using (0,2π] or other intervals can cause sign errors in polar form.
- **Polar to Cartesian conversion sign**: cos( quadrant II ) is negative; students often drop the sign when converting back.
- **r negative in polar plots**: Forgetting that negative r means go opposite direction produces points in the wrong half-plane.
- **Forgetting r=0 is allowed**: z=0 has r=0, θ arbitrary (but usually 0).
- **cis or e form not recognized**: Equivalent to the cos + i sin.
- **θ in degrees when using formulas**: The trig functions in the form expect radians for calculus, but identities hold either.
