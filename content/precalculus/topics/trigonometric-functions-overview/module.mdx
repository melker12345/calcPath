# Trigonometric functions

Trigonometric functions connect angles with ratios of lengths and are indispensable for describing periodic phenomena — waves, oscillations, circular motion, seasons, and alternating current.

## Trigonometric Functions Overview

<!-- section: trig-functions-overview -->

The six trigonometric functions are defined for an acute angle θ in a right triangle as ratios of sides:

- $\sin \theta = \frac{\text{opposite}}{\text{hypotenuse}}$
- $\cos \theta = \frac{\text{adjacent}}{\text{hypotenuse}}$
- $\tan \theta = \frac{\text{opposite}}{\text{adjacent}}$
- $\csc \theta = \frac{1}{\sin \theta}$, $\sec \theta = \frac{1}{\cos \theta}$, $\cot \theta = \frac{1}{\tan \theta}$

These extend to all angles via the unit circle or reference angles. All six are periodic; sine and cosine have period $2\pi$, tangent has period $\pi$.

**ELI5**

- Trigonometry turns angles into numbers you can calculate with. In a right triangle the "steepness angle" tells you exactly how the sides relate via these fixed ratios.

**Worked Example:**

**Finding all six trig values for a 3-4-5 right triangle with acute angle opposite the side of length 3**

1. opposite=3, adjacent=4, hypotenuse=5.
2. sin = 3/5, cos=4/5, tan=3/4.
3. csc=5/3, sec=5/4, cot=4/3.

## Common Mistakes

- **Radians vs degrees**: Calculators and most advanced math use radians by default. sin(30) in radians is not 0.5; always check mode.
- **Reference angle signs**: Forgetting to apply the correct sign for sine/cosine/tangent in each quadrant leads to wrong signs.
- **Identity direction**: sin² + cos² =1 can be used to find one from the other, but you must take ± square root and choose sign from quadrant info.
- **Undefined tangent**: tan and sec are undefined at odd multiples of π/2; students sometimes plot them or solve equations as if defined there.
- **Unit circle memorization gaps**: Not knowing the 30-45-60 exact values forces calculator use and loses exact answers on exams.
- **Forgetting the three reciprocal functions**: csc sec cot are 1 over the primary three; useful in identities.
