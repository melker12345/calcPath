# Complex Numbers & Polar Coordinates

Complex arithmetic, the complex plane, polar form, De Moivre's theorem, polar and parametric curves.

## Complex number

<!-- section: complex-numbers -->

Complex numbers extend the reals to solve equations like x²+1=0 and provide algebra for rotations and oscillations.

### Complex Numbers

A **complex number** is an expression z = a + bi where a, b are real numbers and i is the imaginary unit satisfying i² = -1.

- Real part Re(z) = a
- Imaginary part Im(z) = b
- Modulus |z| = √(a² + b²)
- Conjugate \bar z = a - bi (note \bar{(z w)} = \bar z \bar w, |z|² = z \bar z)

Arithmetic follows the distributive law, replacing i² with -1 whenever it appears.

The complex numbers form a field: every nonzero z has a multiplicative inverse 1/z = \bar z / |z|².

**ELI5**

- Complex numbers are "2D numbers": one real direction, one imaginary direction at right angles. Adding them is vector addition; multiplying scales and rotates.

**Worked Example:**

**Multiply (2 + 3i)(1 - i) and simplify**

1. Distribute: 2*1 + 2*(-i) + 3i*1 + 3i*(-i) = 2 -2i +3i -3 i².
2. -3 i² = -3(-1) = +3.
3. Combine: (2+3) + (-2i +3i) = 5 + i.

### Common Mistakes

- **i² = -1 arithmetic**: Forgetting to replace i² with -1 when multiplying complex numbers leads to wrong real/imaginary parts.
- **Inverse of complex**: 1/(a+bi) requires multiplying numerator and denominator by conjugate; forgetting yields non-real denominator.
- **Modulus of product**: |z w| = |z| |w| (magnitudes multiply); not |z + w|.
- **Conjugate of sum vs product**: \bar (z+w) = \bar z + \bar w always; same for product.
- **Writing j instead of i**: In some engineering contexts j is used, but in math i is standard.
- **Forgetting complex include the reals**: When b=0, z=a is real.

## Complex plane

<!-- section: complex-plane -->

Plotting complex numbers as points in the plane turns algebra into geometry and reveals multiplication as rotation+scaling.

### The Complex Plane

Plot z = a + bi as the point (a, b) in the Cartesian plane. The horizontal axis is the real axis; vertical is the imaginary axis.

- |z| is the distance from origin to the point.
- Argument arg(z) is the angle from positive real axis to the ray from origin through z (principal value in (-π, π]).

Addition is parallelogram law (vector sum). Multiplication by a fixed complex number is a similarity transformation (scale + rotate).

**ELI5**

- The complex plane turns algebra into geometry: every complex number is a point or an arrow from the origin. Pure imaginaries live on the vertical axis, reals on the horizontal.

**Worked Example:**

**Plot  -1 + 2i , compute its modulus and principal argument**

1. Point (-1, 2) in quadrant II.
2. |z| = sqrt(1 + 4) = √5.
3. arg = π - arctan(2/1) ≈ 3.1416 - 1.107 = 2.034 rad (or leave as π - arctan 2).

### Common Mistakes

- **Principal argument range**: arg usually taken in (-π, π]; using (0,2π] or other intervals can cause sign errors in polar form.
- **Plotting imag as x**: Real is horizontal x, imag vertical y. 3-2i is (3,-2) not (-2,3).
- **arg of negative real**: arg(-1) = π (or -π boundary), not 0.
- **Modulus vs argument mixup**: |z| is length (always ≥0), arg is angle.
- **Forgetting conjugate is reflection over real axis**: a-bi is (a,-b).
- **Vector addition vs complex**: Same, but remember i direction is perpendicular.

## Polar form of complex numbers

<!-- section: polar-form -->

Polar form turns multiplication and powers into arithmetic on the modulus r and argument θ. De Moivre follows immediately.

### Polar Form of Complex Numbers

Any nonzero complex z can be written z = r (cos θ + i sin θ) where r = |z| ≥ 0 and θ = arg(z).

This is also written r cis θ or, via Euler, r e^{iθ}.

Conversion:
- r = |z|, θ = atan2(Im, Re)
- a = r cos θ, b = r sin θ

Polar form makes multiplication, division, and powers trivial.

**ELI5**

- Instead of (x,y) coordinates, describe the number by "how far from origin" and "which direction." Multiplication then becomes "multiply the distances and add the angles."

**Worked Example:**

**Write -2 - 2i in polar form**

1. r = sqrt(4+4)=√8=2√2.
2. θ in quadrant III: θ = -3π/4 or 5π/4 (principal usually -3π/4).
3. -2 -2i = 2√2 (cos(-3π/4) + i sin(-3π/4)).

### Common Mistakes

- **Principal argument range**: arg usually taken in (-π, π]; using (0,2π] or other intervals can cause sign errors in polar form.
- **Polar to Cartesian conversion sign**: cos( quadrant II ) is negative; students often drop the sign when converting back.
- **r negative in polar plots**: Forgetting that negative r means go opposite direction produces points in the wrong half-plane.
- **Forgetting r=0 is allowed**: z=0 has r=0, θ arbitrary (but usually 0).
- **cis or e form not recognized**: Equivalent to the cos + i sin.
- **θ in degrees when using formulas**: The trig functions in the form expect radians for calculus, but identities hold either.

## De Moivre's theorem

<!-- section: de-moivre -->

De Moivre turns powers of complex in polar form into simple scaling of r and adding to the angle. It is the key to roots of unity and polynomial roots.

### De Moivre's Theorem

**De Moivre's theorem**: [r (cos θ + i sin θ)]^n = r^n (cos(nθ) + i sin(nθ)) for integer n.

Consequences:
- Powers are easy: just raise r and multiply angle.
- Roots: the n-th roots of re^{iθ} are √[n] r * exp( i (θ + 2π k)/n ) for k=0…n-1. They lie on a circle, equally spaced every 2π/n.

This is the key to finding roots of polynomials and understanding roots of unity.

**ELI5**

- Raising a complex number to a power is "stretch by r each time and turn by θ each time." After n times you have turned nθ and scaled r^n. The n-th roots are the n numbers that, when turned and stretched n times, land back on the starting number.

**Worked Example:**

**Find the three cube roots of 8 (i.e., solutions to z^3 = 8 = 8 + 0i)**

1. 8 = 8 cis 0.
2. Roots: 2 * cis( (0 + 2π k)/3 ) for k=0,1,2.
3. k=0: 2 cis 0 = 2
4. k=1: 2 cis(2π/3) = 2(-1/2 + i √3/2) = -1 + i√3
5. k=2: 2 cis(4π/3) = 2(-1/2 - i √3/2) = -1 - i√3

### Common Mistakes

- **De Moivre for fractional powers**: n must be integer for the basic statement; roots require the k=0 to n-1 branches.
- **Forgetting the n distinct roots**: There are always n roots counting multiplicity in complex; the formula gives all.
- **Arg not adding multiples of 2π**: The +2π k /n generates the different branches; k mod n.
- **r negative**: Take |r| and adjust arg by π.
- **Leaving roots in cis form when a+bi wanted**: Convert back using cos sin of the angles.
- **n=0 or negative**: Powers defined for negative integer via inverse; roots for positive integer n.

## Polar coordinates

<!-- section: polar-coordinates -->

Polar coordinates locate points by distance and direction from origin. They simplify many curves with rotational symmetry.

### Polar Coordinates

In the plane, a point can be located by (r, θ) instead of (x,y):

x = r cos θ

y = r sin θ

r² = x² + y²

θ = atan2(y, x)

Polar equations often simplify circles, cardioids, roses, and spirals that have ugly Cartesian forms.

Negative r and θ outside [0,2π) are allowed and converted via the equivalences (r,θ) ≡ (-r, θ+π) ≡ (r, θ+2π k).

**ELI5**

- Polar is "go distance r in direction θ from the origin." Perfect for anything with a center and rotational symmetry (wheels, flowers, radar).

**Worked Example:**

**Convert the Cartesian point (-3, 4) to polar (r>0, principal θ)**

1. r = 5.
2. θ = π - arctan(4/3) (quadrant II).
3. Exact: θ = π - arctan(4/3).

### Common Mistakes

- **r negative in polar plots**: Forgetting that negative r means go opposite direction produces points in the wrong half-plane.
- **Polar to Cartesian conversion sign**: cos( quadrant II ) is negative; students often drop the sign when converting back.
- **θ in degrees vs rad**: atan2 usually radians; convert if problem in degrees.
- **Forgetting r² = x² + y² for conversion**: Always.
- **atan(y/x) instead of atan2**: Loses quadrant info.
- **Assuming r≥0 always**: Negative r valid and useful.

## Parametric equation

<!-- section: parametric-equations -->

Parametric equations describe a curve by letting a parameter t vary, giving x and y as functions of t. They carry direction and timing information and can trace non-functions.

### Parametric Equations

A curve can be described by a pair of functions x = f(t), y = g(t) where t is a parameter (often time or angle).

Advantages:
- Easy to plot direction of travel as t increases.
- Can represent curves that fail the vertical line test (circles, loops).
- Derivatives dx/dt, dy/dt give velocity components.

Eliminating the parameter t sometimes recovers a Cartesian relation, but the parametric form keeps the timing information.

Classic: circle x= cos t, y= sin t; line x=t, y=mt+b; cycloid, helix in 3D, etc.

**ELI5**

- Parametric equations let the point "walk" according to a clock t. You describe where it is horizontally and vertically at each moment, without needing a single equation relating x and y directly.

**Worked Example:**

**Parametrize the line segment from (1,2) to (4,8)**

1. Let t go from 0 to 1.
2. x = 1 + 3t (starts at 1, ends at 4).
3. y = 2 + 6t (starts 2, ends 8).
4. When t=1: (4,8). The direction vector (3,6)=3(1,2) matches the segment.

### Common Mistakes

- **Parametric vs implicit**: A parametric curve may trace only part of the algebraic curve obtained by eliminating t, or may trace it multiple times.
- **Forgetting the range of t**: The interval for t determines which portion of the curve is drawn.
- **Eliminating t incorrectly**: Solve for t from one eq and sub; may introduce extraneous or lose parts.
- **Velocity vs speed**: (dx/dt, dy/dt) is velocity vector; speed is its magnitude.
- **Assuming t must be time**: Can be angle or any parameter.
- **Not checking orientation**: As t increases, may go clockwise or counter.
