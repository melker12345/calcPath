# Sequences, Series & Conics

Sequences, arithmetic and geometric progressions, the binomial theorem, and conic sections.

## Sequence

<!-- section: sequences -->

A sequence is an ordered list indexed by the natural numbers. It may converge to a limit or diverge.

### Sequences

A **sequence** is a function whose domain is the positive integers (or nonnegative integers), written as an ordered list a₁, a₂, a₃, … .

The nth term is denoted a_n. Sequences may be defined explicitly (closed form) or recursively (a_{n+1} in terms of previous terms).

A sequence converges to L if for every ε>0 there exists N such that for all n > N, |a_n - L| < ε. Otherwise it diverges.

**ELI5**

- A sequence is just a list of numbers indexed by 1,2,3,… like the daily temperature readings forever. We care about whether the numbers settle down to a single value as we go further out in the list.

**Worked Example:**

**The sequence defined by a_n = n / (n+1). Does it converge? To what?**

1. Write first terms: 1/2, 2/3, 3/4, 4/5, … 
2. a_n = 1 - 1/(n+1). As n→∞, 1/(n+1)→0.
3. Therefore lim a_n = 1. The sequence converges to 1.

### Common Mistakes

- **Sequence vs series**: A sequence is the list; a series is the sum of the list. Confusing a_n with S_n is common.
- **Convergence requires eventual closeness**: Early terms can be wild; only the tail matters.
- **Bounded does not imply convergent**: (-1)^n is bounded but diverges.
- **Monotonic + bounded implies convergent**: Important theorem; students forget one or the other.
- **Limit of sequence vs limit of function**: Similar but n integer; use same tools often (e.g. 1/n →0).
- **Recursive without base case**: Need initial term(s) to generate the list.

## Arithmetic progression

<!-- section: arithmetic-progressions -->

Constant difference between terms. Simple closed forms for nth term and sum of first n terms.

### Arithmetic Progressions

An **arithmetic progression** (AP) has constant difference d between consecutive terms: a_n = a_1 + (n-1)d .

The nth term formula is direct.

The sum of the first n terms of an AP:

S_n = n/2 * (2a_1 + (n-1)d)   or equivalently   S_n = n/2 * (first + last)

These formulas are derived by pairing first+last, second+second-last, etc. (each pair sums to 2a + (n-1)d or similar).

**ELI5**

- Arithmetic sequence is "add the same amount every step" — like saving a fixed $50 per month. The sum formulas let you compute the total after n steps without adding one by one.

**Worked Example:**

**Find the sum of the first 15 terms of 4, 9, 14, …**

1. a1=4, d=5.
2. a15 = 4 + 14*5 = 4 + 70 = 74.
3. S15 = 15/2 * (4 + 74) = (15/2)*78 = 15*39 = 585.

### Common Mistakes

- **Arithmetic vs geometric sum formulas**: Using n/2 (first+last) for a geometric series or a1(1-r^n)/(1-r) for arithmetic produces wrong answers.
- **n starts at 1**: a_n = a1 + (n-1)d ; off-by-one common.
- **Sum to n or to infinity**: AP infinite sum diverges unless d=0.
- **Common difference sign**: d can be negative (decreasing AP).
- **Forgetting the /2 in sum**: n/2 * (first+last).
- **Using last term without computing**: last = a1 + (n-1)d ; don't guess.

## Geometric progression

<!-- section: geometric-progressions -->

Constant ratio between terms. Finite and infinite sum formulas (when |r|<1).

### Geometric Progressions

A **geometric progression** (GP) has constant ratio r: a_n = a_1 * r^{n-1}.

Sum of first n terms (finite):

S_n = a_1 (r^n - 1)/(r - 1)   when r ≠ 1.   (or a1 (1 - r^n)/(1-r) )

For infinite series, when |r| < 1 the sum converges to S_∞ = a_1 / (1 - r).

When |r| ≥ 1 the infinite sum diverges (except the trivial r=1 case).

**ELI5**

- Geometric is "multiply by the same factor every step" — compound interest, half-life decay, or a bouncing ball losing height each bounce. The infinite sum formula is magic: even though you add forever, the total stays finite when the factor |r|<1.

**Worked Example:**

**Infinite sum of 1/2 + 1/4 + 1/8 + 1/16 + …**

1. a1=1/2, r=1/2.
2. |r|<1, S = (1/2) / (1 - 1/2) = (1/2)/(1/2) = 1.
3. (You can also see it as the binary fraction 0.1111…₂ = 1.)

### Common Mistakes

- **Arithmetic vs geometric sum formulas**: Using n/2 (first+last) for a geometric series or a1(1-r^n)/(1-r) for arithmetic produces wrong answers.
- **Infinite geometric only when |r|<1**: When |r|≥1 the partial sums grow without bound; the formula a1/(1-r) does not apply.
- **r=1 special case**: All terms equal a1; sum = n a1 (finite) or diverges (infinite).
- **Sign of r**: |r|<1 for convergence; negative r alternates but still converges if |r|<1.
- **First term a1 or a0**: Formula uses a1 the first term.
- **Confusing S_n with a_n**: S_n is the sum up to n; a_n the nth term.

## Binomial theorem

<!-- section: binomial-theorem -->

The binomial theorem gives a direct formula for expanding (x+y)^n without multiplying n times, using binomial coefficients.

### Binomial Theorem

The **binomial theorem** expands (x + y)^n for positive integer n:

(x + y)^n = Σ_{k=0}^n C(n,k) x^{n-k} y^k

where C(n,k) = n! / (k! (n-k)!) is the binomial coefficient ("n choose k").

Pascal's triangle gives a quick way to read the coefficients.

The theorem is proved by induction or by combinatorial counting (choosing k factors of y out of n).

**ELI5**

- Instead of multiplying (x+y) by itself n times, the theorem gives a direct formula for each term's coefficient. It's the "choose how many y's to pick" counting rule turned into algebra.

**Worked Example:**

**Expand (x + 2)^4 and identify the x^2 term**

1. C(4,0)x^4 * 2^0 = x^4
2. C(4,1)x^3 *2^1 = 4 x^3 *2 = 8 x^3
3. C(4,2)x^2 *2^2 = 6 x^2 *4 = 24 x^2
4. C(4,3)x *2^3 = 4 x *8 = 32 x
5. C(4,4)*16 = 16
6. Full: x^4 + 8x^3 + 24x^2 + 32x + 16. The x^2 coefficient is 24.

### Common Mistakes

- **Binomial coefficients**: C(n,k)=C(n,n-k); forgetting this or miscalculating factorials for larger n.
- **Off by one in k**: The term with y^k is C(n,k) x^{n-k} y^k ; power on x is n-k.
- **Forgetting the y^k power**: Even if y=1, the coeff is still C(n,k).
- **Negative or fractional n**: The basic theorem is for nonnegative integer n; generalized binomial for others.
- **Not using Pascal for small n**: Quick to build row by row instead of computing large factorials.
- **Sign errors with (x - y)**: Alternating signs from y^k with negative.

## Conic section

<!-- section: conic-sections -->

Conics unify circles, ellipses, parabolas, hyperbolas as quadratic curves or cone slices. Standard forms after completing square/rotation.

### Conic Sections

**Conic sections** are the curves obtained by slicing a double cone with a plane at various angles:

- Circle: plane perpendicular to cone axis.
- Ellipse: plane tilted but cuts only one nappe completely.
- Parabola: plane parallel to a generator line.
- Hyperbola: plane cuts both nappes.

Algebraically they are all degree-2 equations: Ax² + Bxy + Cy² + Dx + Ey + F =0.

Standard forms (after translation/rotation):

- Circle: (x-h)² + (y-k)² = r²
- Parabola: (x-h)² = 4p (y-k) or vertical swap
- Ellipse: (x-h)²/a² + (y-k)²/b² =1 (a>b or vice versa)
- Hyperbola: (x-h)²/a² - (y-k)²/b² =1 or swapped

Each has focus/directrix or foci definitions that are equivalent to the algebraic equations.

**ELI5**

- All these curves are "conic" because you can get them by cutting an ice-cream cone (double cone) with one flat cut at different tilts. The math unifies them under quadratic equations.

**Worked Example:**

**Identify and give key features of 9x² + 4y² - 36 = 0**

1. Divide by 36: x²/4 + y²/9 =1.
2. This is ellipse centered at (0,0), a=2 (under x, semi-major? Wait: 9>4 so b=3 under y, a=2 under x: major axis vertical.
3. Vertices at (0,±3), co-vertices (±2,0).
4. Foci at (0, ±c) where c=√(9-4)=√5.

### Common Mistakes

- **Conic identification from equation**: The sign of B²-4AC tells parabola/ellipse/hyperbola (degenerate cases aside); students often look only at signs of A and C.
- **Conic standard form completion**: For translated conics, students forget to complete the square or divide correctly after moving constants.
- **a>b or b>a in ellipse**: The larger denominator is the semi-major axis; vertices on that axis.
- **For hyperbola the transverse axis**: The one with the positive term (difference for the other).
- **Foci vs center**: c² = |a² - b²| for ellipse/hyperbola; foci offset from center by c along major/transverse.
- **Parabola p**: Distance from vertex to focus/directrix is p; 4p in the equation.
