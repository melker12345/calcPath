# Polar coordinates

Polar coordinates locate points by distance and direction from origin. They simplify many curves with rotational symmetry.

## Polar Coordinates

<!-- section: polar-coordinates -->

In the plane, a point can be located by (r, θ) instead of (x,y):

x = r cos θ

y = r sin θ

r² = x² + y²

θ = atan2(y, x)

Polar equations often simplify circles, cardioids, roses, and spirals that have ugly Cartesian forms.

Negative r and θ outside [0,2π) are allowed and converted via the equivalences (r,θ) ≡ (-r, θ+π) ≡ (r, θ+2π k).

**ELI5**

- Polar is "go distance r in direction θ from the origin." Perfect for anything with a center and rotational symmetry (wheels, flowers, radar).

**Worked Example:**

**Convert the Cartesian point (-3, 4) to polar (r>0, principal θ)**

1. r = 5.
2. θ = π - arctan(4/3) (quadrant II).
3. Exact: θ = π - arctan(4/3).

## Common Mistakes

- **r negative in polar plots**: Forgetting that negative r means go opposite direction produces points in the wrong half-plane.
- **Polar to Cartesian conversion sign**: cos( quadrant II ) is negative; students often drop the sign when converting back.
- **θ in degrees vs rad**: atan2 usually radians; convert if problem in degrees.
- **Forgetting r² = x² + y² for conversion**: Always.
- **atan(y/x) instead of atan2**: Loses quadrant info.
- **Assuming r≥0 always**: Negative r valid and useful.
