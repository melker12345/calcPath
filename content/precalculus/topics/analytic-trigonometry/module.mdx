# Analytic Trigonometry

Angle formulas, laws of sines and cosines, inverse trig, equations, and substitution preview.

## Double-angle formula

<!-- section: double-angle -->

Double angle lets you express trig of twice the angle using only the original angle. Extremely useful for integration and solving equations.

### Double-Angle Formulas

$\sin 2\theta = 2 \sin \theta \cos \theta$

$\cos 2\theta = \cos^2 \theta - \sin^2 \theta = 2\cos^2 \theta - 1 = 1 - 2\sin^2 \theta$

$\tan 2\theta = \frac{2 \tan \theta}{1 - \tan^2 \theta}$

These come from angle addition with α=β=θ. Extremely useful for integration and solving trig equations.

**ELI5**

- Double-angle lets you express the trig of "twice the angle" using only trig of the original angle. Handy when you know one and need the other.

**Worked Example:**

**Find sin(2θ) given cos θ = -3/5, θ in quadrant II**

1. sin θ = +√(1 - cos²) = √(1-9/25)=√(16/25)=4/5 (QII sign).
2. sin 2θ = 2 sin cos = 2*(4/5)*(-3/5) = -24/25.

### Common Mistakes

- **Double-angle misapplication**: Using sin(2θ)=2sinθ instead of 2sin cos; or confusing which double-angle form of cos 2θ to pick for simplification.
- **Sign when using 1-2sin²**: Choose the form that matches known value and quadrant.
- **For tan 2θ denominator 1-tan²**: Zero when tan θ = ±1, corresponding to 2θ = π/2 +kπ where tan undefined.
- **Forgetting these are identities not equations**: True for all θ (where defined).
- **Using for half angle backwards**: Half angle solves for half from double; different.
- **Calculator in degree when using π**: Formulas assume radian or consistent, but identities hold either.

## Half-angle formula

<!-- section: half-angle -->

Half-angle formulas let you "split" an angle and compute exact trig values when you know the full angle (useful for 15°, 22.5° etc).

### Half-Angle Formulas

$\sin(\theta/2) = \pm \sqrt{\frac{1 - \cos \theta}{2}}$

$\cos(\theta/2) = \pm \sqrt{\frac{1 + \cos \theta}{2}}$

$\tan(\theta/2) = \frac{\sin \theta}{1 + \cos \theta} = \frac{1 - \cos \theta}{\sin \theta}$

The ± sign is chosen according to the quadrant of θ/2.

These are derived from double-angle by solving for the half.

**ELI5**

- Half-angle formulas let you "split" an angle in half and still compute exact trig values when you know the full angle (very useful for exact values of 15°, 22.5°, etc.).

**Worked Example:**

**Exact sin(22.5°)**

1. 22.5 = 45/2, so θ=45°, cos45=√2/2.
2. sin(22.5) = √( (1 - √2/2)/2 )  (positive since 22.5 in QI).
3. Simplify: √( (2-√2)/4 ) = √(2-√2)/2 .

### Common Mistakes

- **Half-angle sign choice**: Always determine the quadrant of the half-angle before picking + or - in front of the square root.
- **Forgetting the 2 in denominator**: (1 - cos)/2 inside the sqrt, not (1-cos)/sqrt2.
- **Using for double instead**: These solve for half; double angle goes the other way.
- **tan half two forms**: Both equal; choose the one avoiding division by small number.
- **Domain**: θ/2 must be in domain where original defined, and sqrt arg ≥0 which it is by identities.
- **Calculator for 22.5 without exact**: Loses the nice radical form.

## Sum-to-product identity

<!-- section: sum-to-product -->

These identities collapse sums of sines or cosines into products. Magic for solving equations and simplifying waves (beats).

### Sum-to-Product and Product-to-Sum Identities

Sum-to-product:

$\sin A + \sin B = 2 \sin(\frac{A+B}{2}) \cos(\frac{A-B}{2})$

$\sin A - \sin B = 2 \cos(\frac{A+B}{2}) \sin(\frac{A-B}{2})$

$\cos A + \cos B = 2 \cos(\frac{A+B}{2}) \cos(\frac{A-B}{2})$

$\cos A - \cos B = -2 \sin(\frac{A+B}{2}) \sin(\frac{A-B}{2})$

These turn sums into products, helpful for solving equations and simplifying.

**ELI5**

- Instead of adding two sine waves of different phases, the identity collapses them into a single sine or cosine times a slow envelope — the math behind "beats" in sound.

**Worked Example:**

**Evaluate sin 75° + sin 15° exactly**

1. A=75, B=15: 2 sin(45) cos(30) = 2 * (√2/2) * (√3/2) = √2 * √3 / 2 = √6 / 2.

### Common Mistakes

- **Sum-to-product arithmetic**: (A+B)/2 and (A-B)/2 must be computed carefully; mixing up which is which ruins the identity application.
- **Sign in cos A - cos B**: Has the minus in front of the sin sin term.
- **Forgetting product-to-sum the other way**: Sometimes needed too.
- **Using when A or B make arg undefined**: Check domains.
- **Calculator for exact**: Loses the simplified radical.
- **Confusing with double angle**: Similar looking but different (A=B case recovers double).

## Sum and difference formula

<!-- section: sum-difference -->

The addition formulas let us compute trig for sums and differences of known angles, giving exact values for 15°, 75°, etc., and are the basis for many identities.

### Sum and Difference Formulas

Addition formulas:

$\sin(A \pm B) = \sin A \cos B \pm \cos A \sin B$

$\cos(A \pm B) = \cos A \cos B \mp \sin A \sin B$

$\tan(A \pm B) = \frac{\tan A \pm \tan B}{1 \mp \tan A \tan B}$

These are proved using distance formula on unit circle points or using Euler's formula with complex exponentials.

They let us find exact values for angles like 15°=45-30 and expand expressions.

**ELI5**

- The formulas tell you the sine or cosine of a "combined angle" if you know the pieces. Like knowing the trig for 45 and 30 lets you get exact 15° or 75° without memorizing more.

**Worked Example:**

**Exact value of sin(15°)**

1. sin(45-30) = sin45 cos30 - cos45 sin30.
2. (√2/2)(√3/2) - (√2/2)(1/2) = (√6 - √2)/4.

### Common Mistakes

- **Sum/difference sign errors**: The minus in cos(A-B) = cosA cosB + sinA sinB is frequently flipped.
- **For tan(A+B) the denominator sign**: 1 - tanA tanB for + ; 1 + for - .
- **Forgetting mp signs**: In cos(A±B) the sin term flips sign with the ± .
- **Using for half/double instead**: These are for arbitrary A B; double is special case.
- **Domain where tan defined in tan formula**: If 1 - tanA tanB =0 the sum hits asymptote.
- **Calculator in wrong mode for exact problems**: Use exact values of 30 45 60, not decimal approx.

## Law of sines

<!-- section: law-of-sines -->

The law of sines generalizes the right-triangle ratios to any triangle, allowing us to solve for missing sides or angles when we have AAS, ASA or the tricky SSA.

### Law of Sines

In any triangle ABC with sides a,b,c opposite angles A,B,C respectively:

$\frac{a}{\sin A} = \frac{b}{\sin B} = \frac{c}{\sin C} = 2R$

where R is the circumradius.

This is derived by dropping an altitude and using area formulas or by using the unit circle extended definition.

The law is most powerful in AAS, ASA, and SSA (ambiguous case) situations.

**ELI5**

- The Law of Sines says bigger sides face bigger angles, and the ratios are constant. It lets you find missing sides or angles when you know two angles and a side (or two sides and a non-included angle).

**Worked Example:**

**Solving a triangle: angle A=40°, side a=8, angle B=60°. Find side b and angle C.**

1. Angle C = 180° -40° -60° =80°.
2. a / sin A = b / sin B => 8 / sin40 = b / sin60.
3. b = 8 * sin60 / sin40 ≈ 8 * 0.8660 / 0.6428 ≈ 10.77.
4. (Use calculator for decimals in practice; keep exact when possible.)

### Common Mistakes

- **Ambiguous case (SSA)**: When using Law of Sines with two sides and angle opposite one of them, there may be zero, one, or two possible triangles. Always check the height h = b sin A against the given side.
- **Calculator mode**: Degrees or radians must match the problem (usually degrees in triangle problems).
- **Assuming unique triangle**: SSA can be ambiguous; draw the picture.
- **Forgetting to find the third angle first**: In AAS/ASA you get third angle from 180 sum before applying law.
- **Rounding too early**: Keep precision until final answer.
- **Using law of sines for SAS or SSS**: Those are for law of cosines.

## Law of cosines

<!-- section: law-of-cosines -->

The law of cosines is Pythagoras with a correction term for the included angle. It lets us solve SAS and SSS triangles.

### Law of Cosines

For any triangle:

$c^2 = a^2 + b^2 - 2ab \cos C$

(and cyclic permutations).

When C=90°, cos C=0, recovers Pythagoras.

Useful for SAS (two sides + included angle) to find third side, or SSS to find an angle via rearranged cos C = (a² + b² - c²)/(2ab).

**ELI5**

- The Law of Cosines is Pythagoras with a "correction term" -2ab cos C that accounts for the angle not being right. When the angle is acute the correction is positive (side c smaller); when obtuse, cos negative, c larger.

**Worked Example:**

**Find angle C given sides a=3, b=4, c=6.**

1. cos C = (a² + b² - c²) / (2ab) = (9 + 16 - 36) / (2*3*4) = (-11)/24 ≈ -0.4583.
2. C = arccos(-0.4583) ≈ 117.3°.
3. Check: 117° is obtuse, and 6²=36 > 3²+4²=25, consistent with obtuse.

### Common Mistakes

- **Law of Cosines for angle**: When solving for angle, the numerator a²+b²-c² can be negative (obtuse); arccos handles it correctly if you keep the sign.
- **Using wrong angle**: The cos C is for the angle opposite side c (included between a and b).
- **Forgetting the 2ab**: The formula has the 2.
- **Mixing with law of sines**: Use cos for SAS/SSS, sines for AAS/ASA/SSA.
- **Calculator in rad vs deg for arccos**: arccos returns radians or degrees per mode; convert if needed for answer.
- **Rounding intermediate**: Keep exact or high precision for arccos input.

## Inverse trigonometric functions

<!-- section: inverse-trig -->

Inverse trig recover the angle from a known sine, cosine or tangent value. Their ranges are restricted to make them functions (one-to-one).

### Inverse Trigonometric Functions

The inverse trig functions recover an angle from a known trig value within a restricted principal range where the function is one-to-one.

- $\arcsin x$ : domain [-1,1], range [-π/2, π/2]
- $\arccos x$ : domain [-1,1], range [0, π]
- $\arctan x$ : domain all reals, range (-π/2, π/2)
- $\text{arccsc}, \text{arcsec}, \text{arccot}$ defined similarly with their own conventions.

These are essential for solving trig equations ("what angle has this sine?") and in calculus for integrals and derivatives.

**ELI5**

- arcsin(x) is "the angle whose sine is x", but we pick the answer from a standard "principal" slice of the circle so the machine always gives one consistent answer.

**Worked Example:**

**Compute arctan(1) + arcsin(0.5)**

1. arctan(1) = π/4 (45°).
2. arcsin(0.5) = π/6 (30°).
3. Sum: π/4 + π/6 = 5π/12.

### Common Mistakes

- **Range of inverse functions**: arcsin range is [-π/2,π/2]; arccos [0,π]. Using the wrong branch or forgetting the range produces answers outside the principal value.
- **Calculator mode**: arccos etc return in current angle mode; check.
- **arcsin(sin x) ≠ x for all x**: Only true inside the principal range; outside it gives the co-terminal in range.
- **Domain restrictions**: arcsin and arccos only defined for [-1,1]; arctan all reals.
- **Confusing arctan with 1/tan**: arctan is inverse function, not reciprocal (which is cot).
- **Quadrant when solving**: After arcsin you may need to add π - result for other quadrant solutions in equations.

## Trigonometric equation

<!-- section: trig-equations -->

Solving trig equations requires identities to reduce to basic forms, then inverse trig plus the periodic general solution, followed by checking for extraneous roots.

### Trigonometric Equations

To solve trig equations:

1. Use identities to rewrite in terms of a single function.
2. Solve the basic equation sin x = k or similar (use inverse + general solution).
3. Find all solutions in the desired interval by adding the period(s).
4. Check for extraneous solutions introduced by squaring or other operations.

General solutions:
- sin x = k : x = (-1)^m arcsin(k) + mπ , or the two families.
- cos x = k : x = ± arccos(k) + 2mπ.
- tan x = k : x = arctan(k) + mπ.

**ELI5**

- Solving trig equations is like "find all the times on the clock when the sine wave is at height 0.5." Because waves repeat, there are infinitely many answers; we list the pattern using + period.

**Worked Example:**

**Solve 2 sin² x - sin x - 1 = 0 for x in [0, 2π).**

1. Let u = sin x: 2u² - u -1 =0.
2. (2u +1)(u -1)=0 => u=1 or u=-1/2.
3. sin x =1 => x= π/2.
4. sin x =-1/2 => x= 7π/6 , 11π/6.
5. Solutions: π/2, 7π/6, 11π/6. (3 solutions)

### Common Mistakes

- **General solution omissions**: Forgetting the ± or the period when writing the general solution from inverse trig leads to missing families of solutions.
- **Quadrant when solving equations**: After taking arcsin or arccos you must add the other angle in the correct quadrant where the function has the same value.
- **Calculator mode**: Solving trig equations numerically without ensuring degree/radian mode matches the problem statement.
- **Extraneous solutions**: Squaring both sides of a trig equation (to get sin² or similar) can introduce solutions that do not satisfy the original.
- **Not using identities first**: Trying to solve without reducing to single function leads to harder equations.
- **Forgetting to check solutions in original**: Especially after squaring or multiplying by expressions that could be zero.

## Trigonometric substitution

<!-- section: trig-sub -->

Although more fully developed in calculus, trig substitution is the technique of writing x = a tan θ, x = a sin θ, or x = a sec θ to simplify expressions involving √(a² - x²), √(x² + a²), or √(x² - a²).

### Trigonometric Substitution (Preview)

Although more fully developed in calculus, trig substitution is the technique of writing x = a tan θ, x = a sin θ, or x = a sec θ to simplify expressions involving √(a² - x²), √(x² + a²), or √(x² - a²).

After substitution, trig identities simplify the radical to a single trig function, the integral becomes easier, and back-substitution using the inverse trig or a reference triangle recovers the answer in x.

**ELI5**

- When an expression under a square root looks like a Pythagorean triple missing one leg, introduce an angle whose trig ratios "label" the sides of that triangle. The radical disappears and you integrate a power of sine or cosine instead.

**Worked Example:**

**Form of substitution for ∫ dx / √(9 - x²)**

1. Form √(a² - x²) suggests x = 3 sin θ, dx = 3 cos θ dθ.
2. √(9 - 9 sin²θ) = 3 cos θ (assuming cos>0).
3. Integral becomes ∫ (3 cos θ dθ) / (3 cos θ) = ∫ dθ = θ + C.
4. Back: θ = arcsin(x/3), so arcsin(x/3) + C.

### Common Mistakes

- **Choosing the wrong sub for the radical form**: a²-x² -> sin; x²+a² -> tan; x²-a² -> sec.
- **Forgetting the dx in terms of dθ**: dx = a sec² dθ for tan sub etc; must substitute it too.
- **Not drawing the reference triangle for back-sub**: The triangle with opp=x, hyp=3 etc lets you express sinθ = x/3 etc without memorizing.
- **Assuming cos or tan always positive**: Choose the range of θ so the sub functions are positive in the interval, or use abs.
- **Leaving answer in θ**: Must back-sub to x (or arcsin(arctan etc)).
- **Forgetting +C in indefinite integral**: Always.
