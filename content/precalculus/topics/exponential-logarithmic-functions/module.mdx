# Exponential & Logarithmic Functions

Exponential and logarithmic rules, the natural log, logarithmic scales, and growth models.

## Exponential function

<!-- section: exponential-functions -->

Exponential functions grow (or decay) at a rate proportional to their current value. They appear everywhere in nature and finance.

### Exponential Functions

An **exponential function** has the form $f(x) = a \cdot b^x$ where $b > 0$, $b \ne 1$, and $a \ne 0$. The base b determines the growth or decay rate; the coefficient a is a vertical stretch or reflection.

Key properties:
- $b^{x+y} = b^x \cdot b^y$
- $(b^x)^y = b^{xy}$
- $b^0 = 1$
- For b > 1 the function is increasing; 0 < b < 1 it is decreasing.

The graph always passes through (0, a), has horizontal asymptote y=0, and is defined for all real x.

**ELI5**

- Exponential is "repeated multiplication by the same factor." Start with a, multiply by b, then by b again, forever. Small changes in the exponent produce huge changes in the result when |x| is large.

**Worked Example:**

**Solving a simple exponential equation**

Solve $3^{2x-1} = 81$.

1. Express 81 as a power of 3: 81 = 3^4.
2. So 3^{2x-1} = 3^4.
3. Equate exponents (one-to-one): 2x - 1 = 4.
4. 2x = 5, x = 5/2.

### Common Mistakes

- **Base confusion**: Writing b^x as x^b (power vs exponential).
- **Exponent rules with negatives**: (-2)^3 = -8 but (-2)^4 = +16; fractional exponents with even roots of negative bases are not real.
- **Growth vs decay**: For 0 < b < 1 the exponential b^x is decay (decreasing), not growth.
- **Forgetting b^0=1**: Always the y-intercept for a* b^x is a.
- **Domain of exponential**: Always all reals; students sometimes think like logs.
- **Continuous vs discrete**: e^{rt} is continuous limit; (1+r)^t discrete. They are close but not identical.

## Logarithm

<!-- section: logarithms -->

Logarithms are the inverse operation to exponentiation. They turn multiplication into addition and are essential for solving exponential equations and handling scales.

### Logarithms

The **logarithm** base b of a positive number a is the exponent to which b must be raised to produce a: $\log_b a = c \iff b^c = a$.

Logarithms are defined only for positive arguments and positive base ≠1.

Change of base formula: $\log_b a = \frac{\ln a}{\ln b}$ (or log10).

Log properties (the "log rules"):
- $\log_b (xy) = \log_b x + \log_b y$
- $\log_b (x/y) = \log_b x - \log_b y$
- $\log_b (x^y) = y \log_b x$
- $\log_b b = 1$, $\log_b 1 = 0$

**ELI5**

- A log is the "how many times did I multiply" counter. If you kept multiplying by 2 until you reached 32, you multiplied 5 times: log2(32)=5. It turns "power towers" back into ordinary numbers you can add and subtract.

**Worked Example:**

**Using log properties to simplify**

Simplify $\log_3 9 + \log_3 27 - \log_3 3$.

1. 9=3^2 so log3 9 =2; 27=3^3 log=3; log3 3=1.
2. Or without computing each: log3(9*27 / 3) = log3(729/3) = log3(243).
3. 243=3^5, so 5.
4. Direct: 2 + 3 - 1 =4? Wait recalc: 9*27=243, 243/3=81=3^4, log=4. Yes.

### Common Mistakes

- **Base confusion**: log_b a means "b to what power = a", not "a to what power = b". The base is always on the bottom of the log notation.
- **Domain of logs**: Students forget that log arguments must be positive; domain of log_b is (0, ∞).
- **Change of base arithmetic**: \log_b a = \ln a / \ln b , not \ln a - \ln b or other misapplications.
- **Log of sum**: There is no simple log(x + y) rule; logs turn products/quotients/powers into sums, not sums into anything simpler.
- **Forgetting log_b b =1 and log_b 1=0**: Basic values that simplify many expressions.
- **Negative base or arg**: Logs not defined for negative or zero in reals; b must >0 ≠1.

## Natural logarithm

<!-- section: natural-log -->

The natural log is the logarithm with base e, the special number that makes calculus clean: its derivative is itself, and ln's derivative is 1/x.

### The Natural Logarithm

The **natural logarithm** $\ln x = \log_e x$ uses the famous base e ≈ 2.718281828..., defined as the limit $(1 + 1/n)^n$ as n→∞.

The natural log and exponential base e are inverses: $\ln(e^x) = x$, $e^{\ln x} = x$ (x>0).

In calculus the natural log and e are special because $\frac{d}{dx} e^x = e^x$ and $\frac{d}{dx} \ln x = 1/x$.

All other logs are scalar multiples of the natural log via the change-of-base formula.

**ELI5**

- The natural log is the "pure" log that arises naturally from continuous growth and calculus. e is the number that makes the exponential curve match its own slope everywhere.

**Worked Example:**

**Solving with natural logs**

Solve $e^{2x} = 7$ for x.

1. Take natural log of both sides: $\ln(e^{2x}) = \ln 7$.
2. Simplifies to 2x = \ln 7.
3. x = (\ln 7)/2.
4. Numerically \ln 7 ≈ 1.94591, so x ≈ 0.97295.

### Common Mistakes

- **Base confusion with e**: ln means log_e , not log10 or log2; always natural in advanced math.
- **Domain of ln**: Only positive reals; ln of negative or zero undefined in reals.
- **Forgetting ln(e^x) =x exactly**: Simplifies many expressions.
- **Mixing ln with log in software**: Some languages log is ln, others log10; check docs.
- **Change of base with ln vs log10**: Either works; ln often preferred in calc because of derivative.
- **Approximating e or ln without calc**: e≈2.718, but use exact ln when possible.

## Logarithmic scale

<!-- section: logarithmic-scales -->

Log scales compress huge ranges into manageable visual space by making distance proportional to ratio, not difference.

### Logarithmic Scales

A **logarithmic scale** spaces numbers so that equal distances correspond to equal *ratios* (multiplicative factors) rather than additive differences.

Classic examples:
- Richter scale for earthquake magnitude (each whole number is 10× energy).
- Decibel scale for sound intensity (10× power per 10 dB).
- pH scale (each unit is 10× change in H+ concentration).
- Astronomical magnitude and stellar brightness.

On a log plot, exponential functions appear as straight lines — this is why semi-log paper is useful for spotting exponential behavior in data.

**ELI5**

- Log scales let your eyes handle numbers that differ by factors of millions without the big numbers squishing the small ones off the page. A jump from 1 to 10 looks the same size as 1,000,000 to 10,000,000.

**Worked Example:**

**Interpreting a decibel reading**

A sound at 60 dB is how many times more intense than one at 30 dB? (Assume power scale, 10 dB = 10× intensity.)

1. Difference = 30 dB.
2. 30 dB / 10 dB per factor = 3 factors of 10.
3. Therefore 10^3 = 1000 times more intense.

### Common Mistakes

- **Thinking log scale is just 'log of the numbers on axes'**: The spacing between ticks is logarithmic.
- **Confusing additive vs multiplicative**: +1 on Richter is *10 energy, not +1.
- **Forgetting that 0 may not appear or negative not shown**: Many log scales start at small positive.
- **Misreading pH**: pH7 is 10× less acidic than pH6 (lower pH more acidic).
- **Calculator log vs ln in scale calcs**: Use consistent base; the factor is the same.
- **Assuming all 'log plots' are log-log**: Semi-log (one axis log) straightens exponentials; log-log straightens power laws.

## Exponential growth

<!-- section: exponential-growth -->

When a quantity's rate of change is proportional to its size, the result is exponential growth or decay. This leads to the differential equation dP/dt = k P whose solution is P = P0 e^{kt}.

### Exponential Growth and Decay

**Exponential growth** occurs when a quantity changes at a rate proportional to its current size: dP/dt = k P with k>0 leads to P(t) = P0 e^{kt}.

**Exponential decay** uses k<0.

Discrete compounding: A = P (1 + r/n)^{nt} ; continuous limit n→∞ gives A = P e^{rt}.

Half-life (for decay): time for quantity to halve is t_{1/2} = \ln(2) / |k|.

These models appear in finance, biology, physics, and technology adoption curves.

**ELI5**

- Exponential growth is "the bigger you are, the faster you grow." Each step multiplies by the same factor >1, so the increases themselves get larger and larger — the opposite of linear "add the same amount every time."

**Worked Example:**

**Continuous compounding and doubling time**

$1000 is invested at 4% annual interest compounded continuously.

1. Formula: A(t) = 1000 e^{0.04 t}.
2. To find doubling time, set A=2000: 2000 = 1000 e^{0.04 t} => 2 = e^{0.04 t}.
3. Take ln: \ln 2 = 0.04 t => t = \ln 2 / 0.04 ≈ 0.693147 / 0.04 ≈ 17.33 years.
4. Rule of 72 approximation: 72 / 4 ≈ 18 years (close).

### Common Mistakes

- **Growth vs decay**: For 0 < b < 1 the exponential b^x is decay (decreasing), not growth.
- **Continuous vs discrete**: e^{rt} is the continuous limit; (1+r)^t is discrete annual compounding. They are close but not identical.
- **Half life depends on k not on P0**: Time to halve is independent of starting amount.
- **Rule of 72 is approximation**: 72/r% gives rough doubling years; exact is ln2 *100 / r .
- **Forgetting the e in continuous model**: Many default to (1+r)^t even when problem says continuous.
- **Sign of k**: k>0 growth, k<0 decay; |k| in half life.
