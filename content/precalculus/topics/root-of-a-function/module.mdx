# Root of a function

Roots (zeros) tell where the function crosses or touches the x-axis. For polynomials they reveal factors and are guaranteed by the Fundamental Theorem of Algebra.

## Roots of a Function

<!-- section: roots -->

A **root** (or zero) of a function $f$ is a value $r$ such that $f(r) = 0$. For polynomials, roots correspond to x-intercepts and linear factors.

By the Fundamental Theorem of Algebra, a degree-n polynomial has exactly n roots in the complex numbers, counting multiplicity. Over the reals, roots may be real or come in conjugate pairs for complex non-real roots.

Factoring helps find roots: if $(x-r)$ is a factor then r is a root (Factor Theorem).

**ELI5**

- Roots are the "sweet spots" where the output is exactly zero — the places the graph crosses or touches the x-axis. For a quadratic like a parabola, there are at most two such places.

**Worked Example:**

**Finding all roots of $x^3 - 6x^2 + 11x - 6 = 0$**

1. Possible rational roots by Rational Root Theorem: factors of 6 over 1: ±1,2,3,6.
2. Test x=1: 1-6+11-6=0. Yes, (x-1) is a factor.
3. Synthetic division or polynomial division yields (x-1)(x^2 -5x +6)=0.
4. x^2-5x+6=(x-2)(x-3).
5. Roots: 1,2,3 (all real, simple).

## Common Mistakes

- **Missing multiplicity**: When counting roots or factoring, repeated roots (x-r)^2 count as two roots for the Fundamental Theorem.
- **Rational Root Theorem misapplication**: Possible rational roots are p/q where p divides constant term, q divides leading coeff; sign ± must be considered.
- **Assuming all roots are real**: High degree polys often have complex roots; they come in conjugate pairs for real coeffs.
- **Forgetting Factor Theorem converse**: If r is root then (x-r) divides; useful for factoring.
- **Graphing without finding roots**: Roots help sketch; but multiplicity tells if it crosses or bounces.
- **Confusing root of poly with root of equation in general**: For non-polys, may have no closed form or infinitely many (trig).
