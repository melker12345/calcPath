# Law of sines

The law of sines generalizes the right-triangle ratios to any triangle, allowing us to solve for missing sides or angles when we have AAS, ASA or the tricky SSA.

## Law of Sines

<!-- section: law-of-sines -->

In any triangle ABC with sides a,b,c opposite angles A,B,C respectively:

$\frac{a}{\sin A} = \frac{b}{\sin B} = \frac{c}{\sin C} = 2R$

where R is the circumradius.

This is derived by dropping an altitude and using area formulas or by using the unit circle extended definition.

The law is most powerful in AAS, ASA, and SSA (ambiguous case) situations.

**ELI5**

- The Law of Sines says bigger sides face bigger angles, and the ratios are constant. It lets you find missing sides or angles when you know two angles and a side (or two sides and a non-included angle).

**Worked Example:**

**Solving a triangle: angle A=40°, side a=8, angle B=60°. Find side b and angle C.**

1. Angle C = 180° -40° -60° =80°.
2. a / sin A = b / sin B => 8 / sin40 = b / sin60.
3. b = 8 * sin60 / sin40 ≈ 8 * 0.8660 / 0.6428 ≈ 10.77.
4. (Use calculator for decimals in practice; keep exact when possible.)

## Common Mistakes

- **Ambiguous case (SSA)**: When using Law of Sines with two sides and angle opposite one of them, there may be zero, one, or two possible triangles. Always check the height h = b sin A against the given side.
- **Calculator mode**: Degrees or radians must match the problem (usually degrees in triangle problems).
- **Assuming unique triangle**: SSA can be ambiguous; draw the picture.
- **Forgetting to find the third angle first**: In AAS/ASA you get third angle from 180 sum before applying law.
- **Rounding too early**: Keep precision until final answer.
- **Using law of sines for SAS or SSS**: Those are for law of cosines.
