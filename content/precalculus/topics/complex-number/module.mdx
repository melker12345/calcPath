# Complex number

Complex numbers extend the reals to solve equations like x²+1=0 and provide algebra for rotations and oscillations.

## Complex Numbers

<!-- section: complex-numbers -->

A **complex number** is an expression z = a + bi where a, b are real numbers and i is the imaginary unit satisfying i² = -1.

- Real part Re(z) = a
- Imaginary part Im(z) = b
- Modulus |z| = √(a² + b²)
- Conjugate \bar z = a - bi (note \bar{(z w)} = \bar z \bar w, |z|² = z \bar z)

Arithmetic follows the distributive law, replacing i² with -1 whenever it appears.

The complex numbers form a field: every nonzero z has a multiplicative inverse 1/z = \bar z / |z|².

**ELI5**

- Complex numbers are "2D numbers": one real direction, one imaginary direction at right angles. Adding them is vector addition; multiplying scales and rotates.

**Worked Example:**

**Multiply (2 + 3i)(1 - i) and simplify**

1. Distribute: 2*1 + 2*(-i) + 3i*1 + 3i*(-i) = 2 -2i +3i -3 i².
2. -3 i² = -3(-1) = +3.
3. Combine: (2+3) + (-2i +3i) = 5 + i.

## Common Mistakes

- **i² = -1 arithmetic**: Forgetting to replace i² with -1 when multiplying complex numbers leads to wrong real/imaginary parts.
- **Inverse of complex**: 1/(a+bi) requires multiplying numerator and denominator by conjugate; forgetting yields non-real denominator.
- **Modulus of product**: |z w| = |z| |w| (magnitudes multiply); not |z + w|.
- **Conjugate of sum vs product**: \bar (z+w) = \bar z + \bar w always; same for product.
- **Writing j instead of i**: In some engineering contexts j is used, but in math i is standard.
- **Forgetting complex include the reals**: When b=0, z=a is real.
