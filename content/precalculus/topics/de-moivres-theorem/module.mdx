# De Moivre's theorem

De Moivre turns powers of complex in polar form into simple scaling of r and adding to the angle. It is the key to roots of unity and polynomial roots.

## De Moivre's Theorem

<!-- section: de-moivre -->

**De Moivre's theorem**: [r (cos θ + i sin θ)]^n = r^n (cos(nθ) + i sin(nθ)) for integer n.

Consequences:
- Powers are easy: just raise r and multiply angle.
- Roots: the n-th roots of re^{iθ} are √[n] r * exp( i (θ + 2π k)/n ) for k=0…n-1. They lie on a circle, equally spaced every 2π/n.

This is the key to finding roots of polynomials and understanding roots of unity.

**ELI5**

- Raising a complex number to a power is "stretch by r each time and turn by θ each time." After n times you have turned nθ and scaled r^n. The n-th roots are the n numbers that, when turned and stretched n times, land back on the starting number.

**Worked Example:**

**Find the three cube roots of 8 (i.e., solutions to z^3 = 8 = 8 + 0i)**

1. 8 = 8 cis 0.
2. Roots: 2 * cis( (0 + 2π k)/3 ) for k=0,1,2.
3. k=0: 2 cis 0 = 2
4. k=1: 2 cis(2π/3) = 2(-1/2 + i √3/2) = -1 + i√3
5. k=2: 2 cis(4π/3) = 2(-1/2 - i √3/2) = -1 - i√3

## Common Mistakes

- **De Moivre for fractional powers**: n must be integer for the basic statement; roots require the k=0 to n-1 branches.
- **Forgetting the n distinct roots**: There are always n roots counting multiplicity in complex; the formula gives all.
- **Arg not adding multiples of 2π**: The +2π k /n generates the different branches; k mod n.
- **r negative**: Take |r| and adjust arg by π.
- **Leaving roots in cis form when a+bi wanted**: Convert back using cos sin of the angles.
- **n=0 or negative**: Powers defined for negative integer via inverse; roots for positive integer n.
