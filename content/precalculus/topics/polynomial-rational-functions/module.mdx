# Polynomial & Rational Functions

Polynomial behavior, zeros and roots, rational functions, asymptotes, and partial fractions.

## Polynomial

<!-- section: polynomials -->

Polynomials are the building blocks of algebra. Their simple structure (finite sum of power terms) gives them nice properties: defined everywhere, smooth, and completely determined by finitely many coefficients.

### Polynomials

A **polynomial** is an expression of the form $p(x) = a_n x^n + a_{n-1} x^{n-1} + \dots + a_1 x + a_0$ where the $a_i$ are constants (coefficients) and $n$ is a nonnegative integer called the degree (provided $a_n \ne 0$).

The degree tells the highest power and governs the end behavior: for large |x|, the leading term dominates.

Polynomials are defined everywhere, continuous, and differentiable. They form a vector space and are dense in the continuous functions on closed intervals (Weierstrass approximation theorem).

**ELI5**

- A polynomial is a "power sum recipe": you take powers of your input, multiply by fixed numbers (the coefficients), and add them up. Higher degree means the graph can wiggle more.

**Worked Example:**

**Identifying degree, leading coefficient, and constant term**

For $p(x) = -3x^4 + 7x^2 - 5x + 9$:

1. Highest power with nonzero coeff is 4, so degree 4.
2. Leading coefficient $a_4 = -3$.
3. Constant term $a_0 = 9$.
4. Note that the $x^3$ term is missing (its coefficient is implicitly 0).

### Common Mistakes

- **Leading term mistakes**: Forgetting that the degree is determined only by the highest power with nonzero coefficient (lower terms do not affect degree or end behavior).
- **Missing implicit zero coefficients**: x^3 term absent means coeff 0; does not change degree.
- **Confusing degree with number of terms**: A degree 5 poly can have only 2 terms (e.g. x^5 + 1).
- **End behavior errors for negative leading coeff**: For even degree negative leading, both ends go to -∞.
- **Thinking constant is degree 0 or 1**: Nonzero constant poly has degree 0; the zero polynomial degree is often defined as -∞ or undefined.
- **Forgetting polynomials have no vertical asymptotes or holes**: They are defined and smooth on all reals.

## Root of a function

<!-- section: roots -->

Roots (zeros) tell where the function crosses or touches the x-axis. For polynomials they reveal factors and are guaranteed by the Fundamental Theorem of Algebra.

### Roots of a Function

A **root** (or zero) of a function $f$ is a value $r$ such that $f(r) = 0$. For polynomials, roots correspond to x-intercepts and linear factors.

By the Fundamental Theorem of Algebra, a degree-n polynomial has exactly n roots in the complex numbers, counting multiplicity. Over the reals, roots may be real or come in conjugate pairs for complex non-real roots.

Factoring helps find roots: if $(x-r)$ is a factor then r is a root (Factor Theorem).

**ELI5**

- Roots are the "sweet spots" where the output is exactly zero — the places the graph crosses or touches the x-axis. For a quadratic like a parabola, there are at most two such places.

**Worked Example:**

**Finding all roots of $x^3 - 6x^2 + 11x - 6 = 0$**

1. Possible rational roots by Rational Root Theorem: factors of 6 over 1: ±1,2,3,6.
2. Test x=1: 1-6+11-6=0. Yes, (x-1) is a factor.
3. Synthetic division or polynomial division yields (x-1)(x^2 -5x +6)=0.
4. x^2-5x+6=(x-2)(x-3).
5. Roots: 1,2,3 (all real, simple).

### Common Mistakes

- **Missing multiplicity**: When counting roots or factoring, repeated roots (x-r)^2 count as two roots for the Fundamental Theorem.
- **Rational Root Theorem misapplication**: Possible rational roots are p/q where p divides constant term, q divides leading coeff; sign ± must be considered.
- **Assuming all roots are real**: High degree polys often have complex roots; they come in conjugate pairs for real coeffs.
- **Forgetting Factor Theorem converse**: If r is root then (x-r) divides; useful for factoring.
- **Graphing without finding roots**: Roots help sketch; but multiplicity tells if it crosses or bounces.
- **Confusing root of poly with root of equation in general**: For non-polys, may have no closed form or infinitely many (trig).

## Rational function

<!-- section: rational-functions -->

Rational functions are ratios of polynomials. They bring asymptotes, holes, and richer end behavior than pure polynomials, while still being algebraic.

### Rational Functions

A **rational function** is a quotient $r(x) = \frac{p(x)}{q(x)}$ of two polynomials p and q (q not the zero polynomial).

After canceling common factors, any remaining zeros of q in the domain produce vertical asymptotes or holes (if canceled).

The end behavior is determined by comparing degrees of p and q:
- deg p < deg q : horizontal asymptote y=0
- deg p = deg q : horizontal asymptote y = leading_p / leading_q
- deg p = deg q +1 : oblique (slant) asymptote found by polynomial division
- higher: no horizontal, end behavior like the quotient polynomial.

**ELI5**

- Rational functions are like "division of two power-sum recipes." They usually have breaks (asymptotes) where the bottom recipe is zero, and their far-left and far-right behavior is predictable from the "sizes" (degrees) of top and bottom.

**Worked Example:**

**Analyzing $r(x) = \\frac{x^2 - 1}{x^2 - 4}$**

1. Factor: numerator (x-1)(x+1), denominator (x-2)(x+2).
2. No common factors, so vertical asymptotes at x=±2.
3. Degrees equal, so horizontal asymptote y=1.
4. Holes: none.
5. x-intercepts (roots): x=±1.
6. y-intercept: r(0)= (-1)/ (-4) = 1/4.

### Common Mistakes

- **Vertical asymptote vs hole**: If a factor cancels, the common root produces a hole, not an asymptote. Always simplify first.
- **Horizontal asymptote degree rule misremembered**: When numerator degree is larger by exactly 1 there is a slant asymptote, not "no asymptote."
- **End behavior for rationals**: Students sometimes think the horizontal asymptote is always y=0; it depends on the degrees.
- **Forgetting y-intercept or x-intercepts after asymptote analysis**: Full picture needs intercepts too.
- **Not checking if num and den have common factors before stating asymptotes**: Simplification changes the graph (holes vs asymptotes).
- **Oblique asymptote requires polynomial division**: Just knowing deg tells you it exists; you must divide to find the actual line equation.

## Asymptote

<!-- section: asymptotes -->

Asymptotes are "ghost lines" that the graph approaches at the edges or at forbidden points. They are key to understanding the long-term behavior of rational and other functions.

### Asymptotes

**Vertical asymptotes** occur where the function heads to ±∞ as x approaches a finite value c (usually where denominator →0 while numerator stays nonzero).

**Horizontal asymptotes** describe the limiting value of f(x) as x → ±∞.

**Oblique (slant) asymptotes** appear when the function behaves like a non-horizontal line at infinity (when deg p = deg q + 1).

To find vertical: solve q(x)=0 after simplification and check numerator.

To find horizontal/oblique: use degree comparison or divide and take limit of remainder term →0.

**ELI5**

- Asymptotes are "ghost lines" the graph gets closer and closer to but never quite touches (or crosses for some cases) as you zoom way out or approach a forbidden x value.

**Worked Example:**

**Finding all asymptotes of $f(x) = \\frac{2x^2 + 3x -1}{x - 1}$**

1. Vertical: denominator zero at x=1; numerator 2+3-1=4 ≠0, so vertical asymptote x=1.
2. Degrees: num 2, den 1 → deg p = deg q +1, so oblique asymptote.
3. Perform division: 2x^2 +3x -1 = (2x +5)(x-1) + 4 ? Long division: 2x +5 , remainder 4.
   (Check: (2x+5)(x-1)=2x^2+3x-2 , subtract from orig gives +1, wait remainder 1? Precise: remainder 1.
4. Thus f(x) = 2x + 5 + 1/(x-1). Oblique asymptote y=2x+5.

### Common Mistakes

- **Vertical asymptote vs hole**: If a factor cancels, the common root produces a hole, not an asymptote. Always simplify first.
- **Horizontal asymptote degree rule misremembered**: When numerator degree is larger by exactly 1 there is a slant asymptote, not "no asymptote."
- **End behavior for rationals**: Students sometimes think the horizontal asymptote is always y=0; it depends on the degrees.
- **Forgetting to check numerator when finding vertical**: If num also zero, it may cancel to hole.
- **Slant asymptote calculation errors**: Must perform the division correctly to get the exact linear expression.
- **Assuming oblique only for rationals**: Other functions (e.g. with logs or exp) can have slant or other asymptotic behavior too.

## Partial fraction decomposition

<!-- section: partial-fractions -->

Partial fractions turn a complicated rational into a sum of simpler pieces. This is the key technique for integrating rationals by hand and for many inverse transforms.

### Partial Fraction Decomposition

**Partial fraction decomposition** rewrites a rational function with factored denominator as a sum of simpler fractions. This is invaluable for integration and inverse Laplace transforms.

For distinct linear factors: $\frac{p(x)}{(x-r_1)\dots(x-r_k)} = \frac{A_1}{x-r_1} + \dots + \frac{A_k}{x-r_k}$.

For repeated factors: include higher powers, e.g. A/(x-r) + B/(x-r)^2 .

For irreducible quadratics: include (Bx + C)/(quadratic).

Solve for coefficients by clearing denominators and equating coefficients or using clever substitutions (cover-up method for simple cases).

**ELI5**

- Partial fractions is "undoing the addition of fractions with different bottoms" so you can work with each piece separately — like separating a mixed salad back into individual ingredients for easier digestion (integration).

**Worked Example:**

**Decompose $\\frac{5x + 7}{(x-1)(x+3)}$**

1. Write $\frac{5x+7}{(x-1)(x+3)} = \frac{A}{x-1} + \frac{B}{x+3}$.
2. Multiply both sides by (x-1)(x+3): 5x + 7 = A(x+3) + B(x-1).
3. Set x=1: 5(1)+7=12 = A(4) => A=3.
4. Set x=-3: 5(-3)+7=-8 = B(-4) => B=2.
5. Thus $\frac{3}{x-1} + \frac{2}{x+3}$.
6. Verify: common denominator gives (3(x+3) + 2(x-1)) / ... = (3x+9 +2x-2)=5x+7. Good.

### Common Mistakes

- **Partial fractions setup**: Forgetting to include terms for every factor (including powers for repeated linear factors) leads to underdetermined systems.
- **Not clearing the denominator completely**: When setting up the equation, multiply both sides by the full factored denom before plugging values or equating coeffs.
- **Cover-up method only for simple distinct linear**: For repeated or quadratic factors you need full system or derivatives for repeated.
- **Forgetting to factor the denominator first**: You must have linear and irreducible quad factors completely before starting.
- **Sign errors when plugging the roots**: When x=r_i , only the A_i term survives; easy arithmetic slip.
- **Not verifying the decomposition by recombining**: Always combine back over common denom to check coefficients match original.
