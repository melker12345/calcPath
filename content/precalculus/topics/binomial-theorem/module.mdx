# Binomial theorem

The binomial theorem gives a direct formula for expanding (x+y)^n without multiplying n times, using binomial coefficients.

## Binomial Theorem

<!-- section: binomial-theorem -->

The **binomial theorem** expands (x + y)^n for positive integer n:

(x + y)^n = Σ_{k=0}^n C(n,k) x^{n-k} y^k

where C(n,k) = n! / (k! (n-k)!) is the binomial coefficient ("n choose k").

Pascal's triangle gives a quick way to read the coefficients.

The theorem is proved by induction or by combinatorial counting (choosing k factors of y out of n).

**ELI5**

- Instead of multiplying (x+y) by itself n times, the theorem gives a direct formula for each term's coefficient. It's the "choose how many y's to pick" counting rule turned into algebra.

**Worked Example:**

**Expand (x + 2)^4 and identify the x^2 term**

1. C(4,0)x^4 * 2^0 = x^4
2. C(4,1)x^3 *2^1 = 4 x^3 *2 = 8 x^3
3. C(4,2)x^2 *2^2 = 6 x^2 *4 = 24 x^2
4. C(4,3)x *2^3 = 4 x *8 = 32 x
5. C(4,4)*16 = 16
6. Full: x^4 + 8x^3 + 24x^2 + 32x + 16. The x^2 coefficient is 24.

## Common Mistakes

- **Binomial coefficients**: C(n,k)=C(n,n-k); forgetting this or miscalculating factorials for larger n.
- **Off by one in k**: The term with y^k is C(n,k) x^{n-k} y^k ; power on x is n-k.
- **Forgetting the y^k power**: Even if y=1, the coeff is still C(n,k).
- **Negative or fractional n**: The basic theorem is for nonnegative integer n; generalized binomial for others.
- **Not using Pascal for small n**: Quick to build row by row instead of computing large factorials.
- **Sign errors with (x - y)**: Alternating signs from y^k with negative.
