# Trigonometric Functions

Sine, cosine, tangent, the unit circle, and core trigonometric identities.

## Trigonometric functions

<!-- section: trig-functions-overview -->

Trigonometric functions connect angles with ratios of lengths and are indispensable for describing periodic phenomena — waves, oscillations, circular motion, seasons, and alternating current.

### Trigonometric Functions Overview

The six trigonometric functions are defined for an acute angle θ in a right triangle as ratios of sides:

- $\sin \theta = \frac{\text{opposite}}{\text{hypotenuse}}$
- $\cos \theta = \frac{\text{adjacent}}{\text{hypotenuse}}$
- $\tan \theta = \frac{\text{opposite}}{\text{adjacent}}$
- $\csc \theta = \frac{1}{\sin \theta}$, $\sec \theta = \frac{1}{\cos \theta}$, $\cot \theta = \frac{1}{\tan \theta}$

These extend to all angles via the unit circle or reference angles. All six are periodic; sine and cosine have period $2\pi$, tangent has period $\pi$.

**ELI5**

- Trigonometry turns angles into numbers you can calculate with. In a right triangle the "steepness angle" tells you exactly how the sides relate via these fixed ratios.

**Worked Example:**

**Finding all six trig values for a 3-4-5 right triangle with acute angle opposite the side of length 3**

1. opposite=3, adjacent=4, hypotenuse=5.
2. sin = 3/5, cos=4/5, tan=3/4.
3. csc=5/3, sec=5/4, cot=4/3.

### Common Mistakes

- **Radians vs degrees**: Calculators and most advanced math use radians by default. sin(30) in radians is not 0.5; always check mode.
- **Reference angle signs**: Forgetting to apply the correct sign for sine/cosine/tangent in each quadrant leads to wrong signs.
- **Identity direction**: sin² + cos² =1 can be used to find one from the other, but you must take ± square root and choose sign from quadrant info.
- **Undefined tangent**: tan and sec are undefined at odd multiples of π/2; students sometimes plot them or solve equations as if defined there.
- **Unit circle memorization gaps**: Not knowing the 30-45-60 exact values forces calculator use and loses exact answers on exams.
- **Forgetting the three reciprocal functions**: csc sec cot are 1 over the primary three; useful in identities.

## Sine

<!-- section: sine -->

Sine is the fundamental periodic function, giving the vertical position as you travel around the unit circle.

### Sine

The **sine** function sin θ gives the y-coordinate of the point on the unit circle reached by traveling an arc length θ (in radians) counterclockwise from (1,0).

Properties: odd function (sin(-θ) = -sin θ), range [-1,1], period 2π, zeros at integer multiples of π.

Sine is the "vertical" component in circular motion and the imaginary part of e^{iθ}.

**ELI5**

- Sine tells you "how high or low you are" as you walk around a circle at constant speed. It starts at 0, goes up to 1 at 90°, back to 0 at 180°, down to -1, and repeats.

**Worked Example:**

**Graph key points of y = sin x over one period**

At x=0: sin0=0

x=π/2: 1

x=π: 0

x=3π/2: -1

x=2π: 0

The curve is a smooth wave oscillating between -1 and 1 with wavelength 2π.

### Common Mistakes

- **Radians vs degrees**: Calculators and most advanced math use radians by default. sin(30) in radians is not 0.5; always check mode.
- **Sign errors in quadrants**: sin positive in QI and QII, negative in QIII QIV.
- **Period of sin vs tan**: sin period 2π; tan π.
- **Forgetting sin is odd**: sin(-θ) = -sin θ; useful for symmetry.
- **Zeros at kπ not k π/2**: At π/2 sin=1, not zero.
- **Range always [-1,1]**: No matter the angle, |sin| ≤1.

## Cosine

<!-- section: cosine -->

Cosine gives the horizontal position on the unit circle. It is the even counterpart to sine and phase shifted from it.

### Cosine

**Cosine** cos θ is the x-coordinate on the unit circle after angle θ.

It is even (cos(-θ)=cos θ), range [-1,1], period 2π, zeros at π/2 + kπ.

Cosine is phase-shifted sine: cos θ = sin(θ + π/2).

It models the horizontal component of circular motion and the real part of e^{iθ} (Euler's formula).

**ELI5**

- Cosine is "how far left or right you are" on the circle. It starts at 1 when angle=0, drops to 0 at 90°, to -1 at 180°, etc. Same wave as sine but shifted left by 90°.

**Worked Example:**

**Using cosine to find adjacent side**

In a right triangle with hypotenuse 10 and angle 60°, find adjacent side.

1. cos 60° = 1/2 = adjacent / 10.
2. adjacent = 10 * (1/2) = 5.

### Common Mistakes

- **Radians vs degrees**: Always check calculator mode.
- **Even vs odd**: cos even, sin and tan odd.
- **Reference angle signs**: Cos positive in QI QIV, negative QII QIII.
- **Zeros at odd multiples of π/2**: Not the same as sin.
- **Confusing with sin in identities**: cos θ = sin( θ + π/2) not sin(θ + π/2) = cos.
- **Phase shift direction**: cos θ = sin(θ + π/2) is shift left (advance).

## Tangent

<!-- section: tangent -->

Tangent is the slope of the line from origin to the unit circle point. It has period π and vertical asymptotes.

### Tangent

**Tangent** tan θ = sin θ / cos θ = opposite/adjacent.

It has period π, range all reals, vertical asymptotes at odd multiples of π/2, zeros at integer multiples of π.

The tangent function is useful for slopes (rise over run) and appears in many trig substitution integrals.

**ELI5**

- Tangent is the slope of the line from origin to the point on the circle. As you approach 90° from left the slope shoots to +∞; just after 90° it comes from -∞.

**Worked Example:**

**Finding tan of 135°**

1. 135° = 180° - 45°, reference 45°.
2. In quadrant II sin positive, cos negative.
3. sin135= sin45=√2/2, cos135= -√2/2.
4. tan = (√2/2) / (-√2/2) = -1.

### Common Mistakes

- **Radians vs degrees**: Calculators and most advanced math use radians by default. sin(30) in radians is not 0.5; always check mode.
- **Undefined tangent**: tan and sec are undefined at odd multiples of π/2; students sometimes plot them or solve equations as if defined there.
- **Period π not 2π**: tan repeats every 180°, not 360°.
- **Sign in quadrants**: tan positive QI QIII, negative QII QIV.
- **tan = opp/adj only for acute**: For general angles use unit circle or reference with signs.
- **Confusing with arctan range**: arctan range (-π/2,π/2) not the same as tan's behavior.

## Unit circle

<!-- section: unit-circle -->

The unit circle is the dictionary for all trig values. Memorizing the key angles gives exact answers without calculator.

### The Unit Circle

The **unit circle** is the circle of radius 1 centered at origin. Points are (cos θ, sin θ) where θ is the arc length (radians) from positive x-axis.

Common angles and exact values:

| θ (radians) | θ (degrees) | sin θ     | cos θ     | tan θ     |
|-------------|-------------|-----------|-----------|-----------|
| 0           | 0°          | 0         | 1         | 0         |
| π/6         | 30°         | 1/2       | √3/2      | 1/√3      |
| π/4         | 45°         | √2/2      | √2/2      | 1         |
| π/3         | 60°         | √3/2      | 1/2       | √3        |
| π/2         | 90°         | 1         | 0         | undef     |
| π           | 180°        | 0         | -1        | 0         |
| 3π/2        | 270°        | -1        | 0         | undef     |
| 2π          | 360°        | 0         | 1         | 0         |

Memorizing these (and signs by quadrant) lets you evaluate trig functions exactly without calculator.

**ELI5**

- The unit circle is a "dictionary" that translates any angle directly into its sin and cos by walking the distance around the circle and reading the coordinates. Radians make the numbers match the actual arc length.

**Worked Example:**

**Exact value of cos(5π/3)**

1. 5π/3 = 300° is in quadrant IV (reference angle 60°).
2. cos positive in IV: cos(60°)=1/2.
3. Thus cos(5π/3) = +1/2.

### Common Mistakes

- **Radians vs degrees**: Calculators and most advanced math use radians by default. sin(30) in radians is not 0.5; always check mode.
- **Reference angle signs**: Forgetting to apply the correct sign for sine/cosine/tangent in each quadrant leads to wrong signs.
- **Unit circle memorization gaps**: Not knowing the 30-45-60 exact values forces calculator use and loses exact answers on exams.
- **Forgetting undef at π/2 +kπ for tan/sec/csc/cot**: Those points have cos or sin zero.
- **θ in degrees on unit circle table**: The table is for radians; convert if needed.
- **Negative angles**: Equivalent to clockwise; use even/odd or add 2π k to positive.

## Trigonometric identity

<!-- section: basic-identities -->

Identities are the algebra rules for trig. They let us rewrite expressions to simplify, solve, or integrate.

### Basic Trigonometric Identities

Fundamental identities:

1. Pythagorean: $\sin^2 \theta + \cos^2 \theta = 1$
   - Also: $1 + \tan^2 \theta = \sec^2 \theta$, $1 + \cot^2 \theta = \csc^2 \theta$

2. Reciprocal: $\csc \theta = 1/\sin$, etc.

3. Quotient: $\tan \theta = \sin/\cos$, $\cot = \cos/\sin$

4. Even/odd: cos even, sin and tan odd.

These allow simplification and are the starting point for proving more complex identities.

**ELI5**

- Identities are "always-true trig facts" you can use like algebra rules to rewrite messy expressions into simpler ones or to solve equations.

**Worked Example:**

**Prove that $\frac{\sin \theta}{\tan \theta} = \cos \theta$ (for tan defined)**

1. Right side: cos θ.
2. Left: sin θ / (sin θ / cos θ) = sin θ * (cos θ / sin θ) = cos θ.
3. Both sides equal, identity holds.

### Common Mistakes

- **Identity direction**: sin² + cos² =1 can be used to find one from the other, but you must take ± square root and choose sign from quadrant info.
- **Forgetting to state domain restrictions**: Identities hold where the expressions are defined (e.g. tan defined).
- **Using = when proving**: Start with one side, transform with = steps to other side; don't assume the thing to prove in middle.
- **Confusing identities with equations to solve**: Identities are true for all (in domain); equations are true for specific values.
- **Sign errors in even/odd**: cos even, sin tan odd.
- **Quotient vs reciprocal**: tan = sin/cos (quotient), csc=1/sin (reciprocal).
