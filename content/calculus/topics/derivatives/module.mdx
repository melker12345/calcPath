# Derivatives

The derivative is the central concept of differential calculus. It measures the instantaneous rate of change of a function — how fast the output is changing with respect to the input at a precise moment.

Geometrically, the derivative at a point is the slope of the tangent line to the curve at that point. The tangent line is the best linear approximation to the function near that point.

If $y = f(x)$, then $f'(x) = \lim_{h \to 0} \frac{f(x+h) - f(x)}{h}$ (when the limit exists). This is the definition that makes every other differentiation rule possible.

Derivatives let us solve optimization problems, describe motion, approximate functions, and understand how quantities change together.

## The definition and what it means

<!-- section: definition -->

The derivative is fundamentally a limit. It formalizes the idea of 'zooming in' on a point until the curve looks straight, then measuring the slope of that line.

The difference quotient $\frac{f(x+h)-f(x)}{h}$ is the average rate of change (slope of secant) over interval h. As h→0, it becomes the instantaneous rate (slope of tangent).

If the limit exists, f is differentiable at x (which implies continuous at x, but not conversely — |x| at 0 is continuous but not differentiable).

Notation: $f'(x)$, $\frac{dy}{dx}$, $\frac{df}{dx}$, $y'$, $D_x f$ all mean the same.

**ELI5**: The derivative tells you the 'speedometer reading' of the function at a point — not the average speed over a trip, but exactly how fast it's climbing or falling right now at this x.

**Example: Using the definition**

- Find $f'(x)$ for $f(x) = x^2$ from definition.
- $\frac{(x+h)^2 - x^2}{h} = \frac{2xh + h^2}{h} = 2x + h$.
- Limit as h→0: $2x$. So $f'(x) = 2x$.

## Core differentiation rules

<!-- section: rules -->

Power rule: $\frac{d}{dx} x^n = n x^{n-1}$ (for any real n, where defined).

Constant multiple: $\frac{d}{dx} [c f(x)] = c f'(x)$.

Sum/difference: $(f \pm g)' = f' \pm g'$.

These three let you differentiate any polynomial instantly.

**ELI5**: The power rule is the 'multiply by the old power, subtract one from the power' recipe. It's the single most used tool in calculus.

## Derivatives of common functions

<!-- section: common -->

- $\frac{d}{dx} e^x = e^x$ (the exponential is its own derivative — unique!)
- $\frac{d}{dx} \ln x = \frac{1}{x}$ (for x > 0)
- $\frac{d}{dx} \sin x = \cos x$
- $\frac{d}{dx} \cos x = -\sin x$
- $\frac{d}{dx} \tan x = \sec^2 x$
- $\frac{d}{dx} a^x = a^x \ln a$

Memorize these — they are the building blocks.

## Product rule

<!-- section: product -->

$(fg)' = f'g + fg'$

**ELI5**: The derivative of a product is not the product of the derivatives. Instead, it's 'first times derivative of second + second times derivative of first'. Like the two factors taking turns differentiating while the other stays still.

**Example**

- $\frac{d}{dx}(x^2 \sin x) = 2x \sin x + x^2 \cos x$

## Quotient rule

<!-- section: quotient -->

$\left( \frac{f}{g} \right)' = \frac{f'g - fg'}{g^2}$

**ELI5**: Similar to product, but with a minus and the denominator squared. Think of it as a special case of product rule on f * (1/g).

## Chain rule

<!-- section: chain -->

$\frac{d}{dx} f(g(x)) = f'(g(x)) \cdot g'(x)$

The chain rule is the most important and most used rule after the basics. It says the derivative of the outside function (evaluated at the inside) times the derivative of the inside.

**ELI5**: Rates multiply when one thing depends on another. If y depends on u and u depends on x, then dy/dx = (dy/du) * (du/dx). Like 'how fast y changes with u' times 'how fast u changes with x'.

**Example**: $\frac{d}{dx} e^{3x} = 3 e^{3x}$ (outside e^u ' rate is e^u, inside u=3x rate 3).

## Inverse trigonometric derivatives

<!-- section: invtrig -->

- $\frac{d}{dx} \arcsin x = \frac{1}{\sqrt{1-x^2}}$
- $\frac{d}{dx} \arccos x = -\frac{1}{\sqrt{1-x^2}}$
- $\frac{d}{dx} \arctan x = \frac{1}{1+x^2}$

These come from implicit differentiation on the inverse relations.

## Implicit differentiation

<!-- section: implicit -->

When y is defined implicitly as a function of x by an equation F(x,y)=0, differentiate both sides w.r.t. x, treating y as y(x), and solve for y'.

Every time you hit a y, you multiply by y' (chain rule).

**ELI5**: Instead of solving for y first (which may be impossible), you differentiate the relationship directly and the y' terms appear naturally.

**Example**: For $x^2 + y^2 = 25$, diff: 2x + 2y y' = 0 ⇒ y' = -x/y.

## Logarithmic differentiation

<!-- section: logdiff -->

For complicated products/powers, take ln of both sides, differentiate implicitly, solve for y'.

Especially useful for $y = x^x$ or variable bases/exponents.

**Example**: $y = x^x$, ln y = x ln x, (1/y) y' = ln x + 1, y' = x^x (ln x + 1).

## Higher-order derivatives

<!-- section: higherorder -->

$f''(x) = \frac{d}{dx} f'(x)$, the second derivative measures how the slope itself is changing (concavity).

Higher orders appear in Taylor series and differential equations.

## Tangent lines and linearization

<!-- section: tangent -->

The tangent line at x=a is y = f(a) + f'(a)(x - a).

This is the linear approximation (linearization) of f near a: f(x) ≈ f(a) + f'(a)(x-a).

The error is approximately (1/2)f''(a)(x-a)^2 for small (x-a).

**ELI5**: Near a point, every smooth curve looks almost like its tangent line. The linearization is the 'best straight-line guess' for the function value a little distance away.

## Interpreting the derivative graphically

<!-- section: graphical -->

f' > 0 ⇒ f increasing; f' < 0 ⇒ decreasing.

f'' > 0 ⇒ concave up (holds water); f'' < 0 ⇒ concave down.

Critical points where f'=0 or undefined (candidates for max/min).

## Hyperbolic functions and their derivatives

<!-- section: hyperbolic -->

$\sinh x = \frac{e^x - e^{-x}}{2}$, $\cosh x = \frac{e^x + e^{-x}}{2}$

$\frac{d}{dx} \sinh x = \cosh x$, $\frac{d}{dx} \cosh x = \sinh x$

Analogous to trig but with exponentials; useful in catenary curves, relativity, etc.

## Common Mistakes

- Forgetting the chain rule when differentiating composites (the #1 error).
- Misapplying product/quotient rules (sign error in quotient, or treating as product of derivatives).
- Confusing f'(a) (the number, slope at one point) with f'(x) (the function).
- Forgetting to use the definition when asked explicitly, or using rules when definition is required.
- In implicit diff, forgetting the y' multiplier on every term containing y.
- Thinking |x| is differentiable at 0 (it's not; corner).
- Losing the constant of integration when going backwards (but that's for integrals).
