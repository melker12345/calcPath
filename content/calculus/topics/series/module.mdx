# Series & Sequences

A sequence is an infinite list of numbers. A series is the sum of a sequence. Most of the time the sum is infinite, so the central question is: does it converge to a finite number, or diverge to infinity (or oscillate)?

Series are the language of Taylor approximations, Fourier analysis, probability (infinite trials), and numerical methods.

## Sequences and their limits

<!-- section: sequences -->

A sequence a_n has limit L if as n→∞ (n integer), a_n → L.

Same ε-N definition as functions, but discrete.

Monotonic + bounded ⇒ converges (key theorem).

## Series, partial sums, and convergence

<!-- section: partialsums -->

The series ∑ a_n from n=1 to ∞ converges to S if the sequence of partial sums s_N = a_1 + ... + a_N → S as N→∞.

Otherwise diverges.

The terms a_n must → 0 for convergence (necessary but not sufficient).

**ELI5**: Adding infinitely many numbers. The running total has to settle down to a single number, not keep growing or oscillating forever.

## Geometric series

<!-- section: geometric -->

∑ ar^{n-1} = a / (1-r) for |r| < 1. Diverges otherwise.

Partial sum s_N = a (1-r^N)/(1-r).

Every repeating decimal is a geometric series.

**ELI5**: Each term is a fixed fraction of the previous. If the fraction |r|<1, the leftovers get smaller fast enough that the infinite sum is finite.

## Telescoping series

<!-- section: telescoping -->

Most terms cancel when you write out partial sums. Classic: ∑ (1/n - 1/(n+1)) = 1 (harmonic telescope).

## The divergence test (nth-term test)

<!-- section: divergence -->

If a_n does not → 0, then ∑ a_n diverges.

(If a_n → 0, says nothing — could go either way.)

## p-series and the harmonic series

<!-- section: pseries -->

∑ 1/n^p converges iff p > 1.

Harmonic (p=1) diverges (very slowly, like ln N).

Important benchmark for comparison tests.

## The integral test

<!-- section: integraltest -->

If f positive, continuous, decreasing, then ∑ f(n) converges ⇔ ∫_1^∞ f(x) dx converges.

The integral and the series either both converge or both diverge.

Error estimate from integral remainder.

## Comparison and limit comparison tests

<!-- section: comparison -->

Direct comparison: 0 ≤ a_n ≤ b_n and ∑b converges ⇒ ∑a converges; if ∑a diverges then ∑b diverges.

Limit comparison: if a_n / b_n → positive finite L, then ∑a and ∑b behave the same (both conv or both div).

Choose b_n as a p-series or geometric you know.

## Ratio and root tests

<!-- section: ratioroot -->

Ratio test: look at lim |a_{n+1}/a_n|. If <1 converges absolutely; >1 diverges; =1 inconclusive.

Root test: lim sup |a_n|^{1/n} — same thresholds.

Great for factorials and exponentials (e.g. power series radius).

## Alternating series test

<!-- section: alternating -->

For alternating signs, decreasing terms →0: the series converges (conditionally if not absolutely).

Error less than first omitted term, and same sign.

**ELI5**: The partial sums bounce back and forth across the target, getting closer each pair of terms. The leftover is smaller than the next single term.

## Absolute vs conditional convergence

<!-- section: absconv -->

Absolute convergence: ∑ |a_n| converges ⇒ ∑ a_n converges (and you can rearrange freely).

Conditional: ∑ a_n converges but ∑ |a_n| diverges (rearrangements can change the sum — Riemann rearrangement theorem).

## Power series

<!-- section: power -->

∑ c_n (x-a)^n . Has a radius of convergence R (0 to ∞).

Inside |x-a|<R converges absolutely; outside diverges; at endpoints check separately.

Differentiate or integrate term-by-term inside radius (preserves convergence).

**ELI5**: A power series is like an infinite polynomial. It behaves like a function inside its 'interval of convergence' and blows up outside.

## Taylor and Maclaurin series

<!-- section: taylor -->

Taylor series of f around a: ∑ [f^{(n)}(a)/n!] (x-a)^n

Maclaurin = a=0.

Common: e^x = ∑ x^n / n!

sin x = ∑ (-1)^k x^{2k+1} / (2k+1)!

cos x = ∑ (-1)^k x^{2k} / (2k)!

1/(1-x) = ∑ x^n (geometric)

## Taylor remainder and error bounds

<!-- section: remainder -->

Lagrange form of remainder: |R_n(x)| ≤ M |x-a|^{n+1} / (n+1)! where M bounds |f^{(n+1)}| on interval.

Tells you how many terms needed for desired accuracy.

## Building new series from known ones

<!-- section: building -->

Substitute, differentiate, integrate, multiply known series.

E.g. arctan x = ∫ 1/(1+t^2) dt = series by integrating geometric.

e^{-x^2} from e^u sub.

## Choosing the right convergence test strategy

<!-- section: strategy -->

- Terms →0? No → divergence test.
- Looks geometric or p? Try those.
- Factorials/exponentials? Ratio/root.
- Alternating? Alternating test.
- Positive terms, compare to known? Comparison or limit comp or integral.
- At endpoint of power series? Plug in and test the resulting numerical series.

**ELI5**: Series convergence is detective work. Look at the 'shape' of the general term and pick the tool that matches.

## Common Mistakes

- Using divergence test backwards (a_n→0 does NOT imply convergence).
- Forgetting to check endpoints of power series interval.
- Wrong choice of u or dv in parts for series? (more for integrals).
- Rearranging conditionally convergent series (sum can become anything).
- Confusing radius R with interval (endpoints matter).
- In Taylor, using wrong center a or forgetting factorial.
- Not using remainder to justify truncation error.
