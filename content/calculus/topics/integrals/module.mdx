# Integrals

The integral is the other pillar of calculus. While the derivative measures instantaneous change, the integral measures accumulation — total distance, total area, total work, total probability mass.

The Fundamental Theorem of Calculus ties them together: integration and differentiation are inverse operations.

## The big idea: Riemann sums

<!-- section: riemann -->

The definite integral $\int_a^b f(x)\,dx$ is the limit of Riemann sums: divide [a,b] into n subintervals, pick sample point x_i* in each, sum f(x_i*) Δx, let n→∞ (Δx→0).

Left, right, midpoint, trapezoidal rules are approximations.

**ELI5**: To find the area under a wiggly curve, chop it into thin rectangles (or trapezoids), add their areas, then make the slices thinner and thinner until the error disappears. That's the integral.

## The Fundamental Theorem of Calculus (FTC)

<!-- section: ftc -->

FTC Part 1: If F'(x) = f(x), then $\int_a^b f(x)\,dx = F(b) - F(a)$.

FTC Part 2: $\frac{d}{dx} \int_a^x f(t)\,dt = f(x)$ (the upper limit variable appears in the integrand via chain rule).

This is why we have antiderivatives — they let us evaluate definite integrals exactly without limits of sums.

**ELI5**: The integral from a to b is just the 'net change' in the antiderivative between a and b. The area under the rate curve is the total accumulated quantity.

## Net area vs total area

<!-- section: netarea -->

$\int_a^b f(x)\,dx$ gives signed (net) area: positive above x-axis, negative below.

Total area (geometric) is $\int_a^b |f(x)|\,dx$.

## Antiderivatives and the constant of integration

<!-- section: antideriv -->

$\int f(x)\,dx = F(x) + C$ where F' = f.

The +C is crucial: the general antiderivative is a whole family of functions differing by constants. Definite integrals pin it down.

**ELI5**: Going backwards from derivative to function loses information about vertical shift. The C represents all the possible 'starting heights'.

## Common integrals: your core toolbox

<!-- section: toolbox -->

Power: $\int x^n dx = \frac{x^{n+1}}{n+1} + C$ (n ≠ -1)

$\int \frac{1}{x} dx = \ln|x| + C$

$\int e^x dx = e^x + C$

$\int \sin x = -\cos x$, $\int \cos x = \sin x$

$\int \sec^2 x = \tan x$, etc.

Memorize + know how to verify by differentiating back.

## Substitution (reverse chain rule)

<!-- section: usub -->

For $\int f(g(x)) g'(x) dx$, let u = g(x), du = g' dx, becomes $\int f(u) du$.

The hardest part is spotting the inside function and its derivative (possibly with a constant factor you pull out).

**ELI5**: Substitution undoes the chain rule. If the integrand looks like 'outside function of something' times 'derivative of that something', the something is u.

## Integration by parts (reverse product rule)

<!-- section: parts -->

$\int u dv = uv - \int v du$

Choose u to be something that simplifies when differentiated, dv easy to integrate.

LIATE heuristic: Log, Inverse trig, Algebraic, Trig, Exp — choose u from left side.

Classic for $\int x e^x dx$, $\int \ln x dx$, $\int \arcsin x dx$.

## Trigonometric integrals

<!-- section: trigint -->

Powers of sin/cos: use identities (power-reduce, half-angle) or save one sin/cos for du and express rest in the other.

For $\int \sin^m x \cos^n x dx$, if one odd, save it for du.

## Trigonometric substitution

<!-- section: trigsub -->

For $\sqrt{a^2 - x^2}$ use x = a sin θ or a cos θ.

For $\sqrt{x^2 + a^2}$ use x = a tan θ.

For $\sqrt{x^2 - a^2}$ use x = a sec θ.

Draw the triangle to convert back to x.

## Partial fractions

<!-- section: partial -->

For rational functions where deg(num) < deg(den), factor den into linear/quadratic factors, decompose into A/(x-r) + (Bx+C)/(quadratic) etc.

Solve for coefficients by clearing denominator and equating coeffs (or plug in roots).

## Improper integrals

<!-- section: improper -->

Infinite limits or discontinuities inside interval.

Evaluate as limit: $\lim_{b\to\infty} \int_a^b f = $ the improper integral (converges if limit finite).

p-integrals: $\int_1^\infty \frac{1}{x^p} dx$ converges for p>1.

Comparison test for convergence.

## Which technique should I use? Decision tree

<!-- section: technique -->

1. Is it a basic form or obvious sub? Use basic or u-sub.
2. Product of different types? Parts.
3. Rational? Partial fractions (after poly div if needed).
4. Trig powers or sqrt of quadratics? Trig id or trig sub.
5. None obvious? Try parts or sub, or numerical.

**ELI5**: Integration is pattern matching. Train your eye to recognize the 'shape' of the integrand and match it to the right undo-button (sub, parts, partials...).

## Area between curves and volumes preview

<!-- section: areaprev -->

$\int_a^b [top(x) - bottom(x)] dx$ for area between curves.

Leads naturally into volumes by slicing (disk/washer, shells) in the next major topic.

## Common Mistakes

- Forgetting +C on indefinite integrals.
- Wrong u in substitution (not including the du factor or forgetting to change limits for definite).
- Sign errors in by-parts (uv - integral).
- For partial fractions, forgetting repeated factors or irreducible quadratics.
- Treating improper integrals as proper (must take the limit).
- Mixing up net vs total area.
- Not checking if antiderivative is valid on the whole interval (logs, abs values, domains).
