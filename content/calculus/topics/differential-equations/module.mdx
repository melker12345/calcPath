# Differential Equations

A differential equation (DE) is any equation that involves a function and its derivatives. Solving a DE means finding the function itself. This is the reverse of what you've been doing: instead of 'given f, find f'', now it's 'given a relationship involving f' (and maybe f''), find f'.

Differential equations model nearly everything that changes: population growth, radioactive decay, temperature cooling, spring motion, electrical circuits, and fluid flow. If something evolves over time, there's a DE behind it.

## What is a differential equation?

<!-- section: intro -->

A differential equation (DE) is any equation that relates a function y(x) to one or more of its derivatives. The unknown is the function itself — you're solving for y, not a number.

The order of a DE is the highest derivative that appears. First-order: y' = 2xy. Second-order: y'' + 4y = 0.

A solution is a function y(x) that, when substituted, makes the equation true.

General solution has arbitrary constants (one per order). Initial value problem (IVP) uses y(x0)=y0 etc to pin down constants.

Linear vs nonlinear.

**ELI5**: In algebra you solve for a number. In DEs you solve for a whole function. The equation gives a rule about how the function's rate of change relates to its value.

## Direction fields (slope fields)

<!-- section: slopefields -->

Visual tool for first-order DE y' = f(x,y): at each point draw a short segment with slope f(x,y). Solution curves are tangent to the segments.

Gives qualitative behavior instantly (equilibria, increasing/decreasing regions) without solving.

**ELI5**: A field of tiny arrows showing which way solutions must flow at every point. Drop a 'particle' anywhere and follow the arrows to see the solution shape.

## Separable equations

<!-- section: separable -->

dy/dx = g(x) h(y). Separate: dy/h(y) = g(x) dx, integrate both sides.

Most first-order solvable in calc 1 are separable.

**ELI5**: If the rate factors into 'something of x times something of y', you can move all y stuff to left, x to right, and integrate.

## Exponential growth and decay

<!-- section: expgrowth -->

y' = k y ⇒ y = C e^{kt}.

k>0 growth, k<0 decay. Half-life, doubling time = ln2 / |k|.

Models: populations (unconstrained), radioactive decay, compound interest (continuous).

## Newton's law of cooling

<!-- section: cooling -->

dT/dt = -k (T - T_a) where T_a ambient.

Solution: T(t) = T_a + (T0 - T_a) e^{-kt}.

The object approaches ambient exponentially.

## Linear first-order equations

<!-- section: linear -->

y' + P(x) y = Q(x).

Integrating factor μ = e^{∫P dx}. Multiply: (μ y)' = μ Q. Integrate.

Standard technique that always works for linear 1st order.

## Logistic growth

<!-- section: logistic -->

y' = k y (1 - y/M) (M carrying capacity).

Solution: y = M / (1 + C e^{-kt}).

S-shaped curve: slow start, rapid middle, levels off at M. Better model than pure exponential for real populations.

## Bernoulli equations

<!-- section: bernoulli -->

y' + P y = Q y^n (n≠0,1).

Substitute v = y^{1-n}, reduces to linear.

## Second-order linear homogeneous equations

<!-- section: secondorder -->

y'' + p y' + q y = 0.

Characteristic equation r^2 + p r + q = 0.

Roots real distinct: e^{r1 x}, e^{r2 x}

Repeated: e^{rx}, x e^{rx}

Complex: e^{αx} (cos βx, sin βx)

## Non-homogeneous equations and particular solutions

<!-- section: nonhomog -->

y'' + p y' + q y = g(x).

General = homogeneous general + particular.

Guess particular: polynomial, exp, sin/cos matching g (undetermined coeffs). Variation of parameters always works.

## Applications: springs and oscillations

<!-- section: springs -->

m y'' + c y' + k y = 0 (or forced).

Models harmonic motion, damped, driven oscillators. Resonance when driving freq matches natural.

## Initial-value problems (IVPs)

<!-- section: ivp -->

DE + enough initial conditions to determine all constants uniquely.

Existence/uniqueness theorems guarantee a unique solution in a neighborhood for nice coefficients.

**ELI5**: The DE describes the law of motion; initial position and velocity (or temp etc.) pick out which particular solution from the family actually happens in your experiment.

## Common Mistakes

- Forgetting the constant(s) in general solution.
- Wrong characteristic equation (signs or order).
- In separation, forgetting the constant after integrating both sides.
- For linear, wrong integrating factor or forgetting to multiply the Q term too.
- In logistic, confusing M with initial or max.
- Not checking that your proposed solution actually satisfies the original DE.
- For second order, missing that complex roots give sin/cos pair.
