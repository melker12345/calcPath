# Applications of Derivatives

Derivatives are not just theoretical — they solve real problems: finding maximum profit, minimum cost, fastest route, when two ships are closest, how fast a ladder is sliding, the best dimensions for a can, and predicting motion.

This topic shows the power of the derivative as a tool for optimization, approximation, and understanding change in the sciences.

## Critical points and the first derivative test

<!-- section: critical -->

Critical points: where f'(x) = 0 or f' undefined.

The first derivative test: sign chart of f' around a critical point tells if it's a local max (f' changes + to -), local min (- to +), or neither (no sign change).

**ELI5**: Critical points are where the slope is zero (flat) or undefined (corner/cusp). The first derivative test is like checking the 'wind direction' before and after the point to see if you crested a hill or hit a valley.

## The second derivative test

<!-- section: secondderiv -->

At a critical point where f'' exists: if f'' > 0 then local min (concave up); f'' < 0 local max (concave down); f''=0 inconclusive.

Faster than first deriv test when it works.

## Concavity and inflection points

<!-- section: concavity -->

f'' > 0: concave up; f'' < 0: concave down.

Inflection point: where concavity changes (f'' changes sign).

The second derivative tells you the 'bend' of the curve.

## Absolute extrema on closed intervals; Extreme Value Theorem

<!-- section: extrema -->

On a closed interval [a,b], a continuous function attains its absolute max and min.

They occur at critical points inside or at the endpoints. Evaluate f at those candidates and compare.

**ELI5**: On a closed hike (start and end fixed), the highest and lowest points must be either at the start/end or where your path is locally flat (critical point). The Extreme Value Theorem guarantees you hit both.

## Curve sketching: putting it all together

<!-- section: curvesketch -->

Use: domain, intercepts, symmetry, asymptotes, critical points, sign of f'/f'', inflection points, end behavior.

Build a complete picture of the graph from the derivatives.

## Optimization problems

<!-- section: optimization -->

Classic: max area for fixed perimeter, min surface for fixed volume, best price for profit, etc.

Steps: 1. Draw picture & define variables. 2. Write primary equation (what to optimize) and constraint. 3. Solve constraint for one var, substitute. 4. Differentiate, find critical points. 5. Verify max/min with second test or endpoints. 6. Answer in context.

**ELI5**: Turn a word problem into a function of one variable, then use calculus to find its peak or valley. The answer is usually the 'just right' size or price.

**Example**: Maximize area of rectangle with perimeter 100. Let width x, length (50-x), A=x(50-x). A'=50-2x=0 ⇒ x=25. Max area 625 at square.

## Related rates

<!-- section: relatedrates -->

Two (or more) quantities changing with time, related by an equation. Find how fast one is changing when you know the other.

Steps: 1. Draw & label with variables. 2. Write equation relating the vars. 3. Differentiate w.r.t. time t (chain rule everywhere). 4. Plug in known values at the instant of interest. 5. Solve for the unknown rate.

**ELI5**: 'If this balloon is inflating at 3 cm³/s, how fast is its radius growing when r=5?' The equation V=4/3 π r³ connects them; differentiate to get the rates connected by dV/dt and dr/dt.

## Motion along a line

<!-- section: motion -->

Position s(t), velocity v(t)=s'(t), acceleration a(t)=v'(t)=s''(t).

Speed = |v(t)|.

When v and a same sign: speeding up; opposite: slowing down.

Total distance traveled = integral of speed, not net displacement.

## Linearization and differentials

<!-- section: linearization -->

The differential dy = f'(x) dx is the approximate change in y for small dx.

Used for error propagation and approximations: f(x+Δx) ≈ f(x) + f'(x)Δx.

## The Mean Value Theorem

<!-- section: mvt -->

If f continuous on [a,b], differentiable on (a,b), then there exists c in (a,b) with f'(c) = [f(b)-f(a)]/(b-a).

Says the instantaneous rate equals the average rate somewhere.

Rolle's Theorem is special case when f(a)=f(b).

**ELI5**: On any trip, your average speed was some number. At some instant, your speedometer must have read exactly that average speed.

## Newton's method

<!-- section: newton -->

Iterative root finder: x_{n+1} = x_n - f(x_n)/f'(x_n).

Uses tangent line to guess better root. Fast when close, but can fail (cycles, divergence).

## Rates of change in the sciences

<!-- section: ratesci -->

Biology: population models (logistic), spread of disease.

Physics: velocity, acceleration, related rates (ladder, shadow, melting snowball).

Economics: marginal cost/revenue = derivatives of cost/revenue functions.

Chemistry: reaction rates.

**ELI5**: Derivatives measure 'how fast something is happening right now' — whether it's a population growing, a shadow lengthening, or the cost of making one more widget.

## Common Mistakes

- Forgetting to check endpoints in absolute extrema on closed intervals.
- In related rates, differentiating w.r.t. the wrong variable or forgetting chain rule on every term.
- Using first derivative test when second is easier, or vice versa.
- Confusing displacement (net) with total distance (always positive).
- In optimization, not verifying that the critical point is actually in the domain or is a max.
- Newton's method: starting too far or at a flat point (f'=0).
- Forgetting units in rates/optimization answers.
