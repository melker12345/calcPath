# Applications of Integration

Once you can integrate, the integral becomes a Swiss Army knife for geometry and physics: areas, volumes, lengths, work against forces, pressure on dams, balancing points (center of mass), average values, and more.

Every application is the same idea: chop the quantity into tiny pieces, approximate each piece with a simple formula (rectangle, disk, shell, etc.), sum (integrate), take limit.

## Areas between curves

<!-- section: areacurves -->

A = ∫_a^b [f(x) - g(x)] dx where f ≥ g.

Vertical or horizontal slices; sometimes easier in terms of y.

Sketch first! Find intersection points for limits.

## Volumes of solids of revolution: disk and washer methods

<!-- section: diskwasher -->

Disk: V = π ∫ [R(x)]^2 dx (around x-axis, solid).

Washer: V = π ∫ ([R_outer]^2 - [R_inner]^2) dx.

Cross sections perpendicular to axis of rotation are disks/washers.

**ELI5**: Spin a region around an axis. Each thin slice becomes a coin or ring (washer). Volume of coin = π r^2 thickness. Integrate.

## Volumes by cylindrical shells

<!-- section: shells -->

V = ∫ 2π (radius) (height) (thickness) — usually 2π x f(x) dx when rotating around y-axis.

Often easier when washer would require solving for x in terms of y.

Complements disk/washer; choose the easier one.

## Arc length

<!-- section: arclength -->

L = ∫_a^b √(1 + [f'(x)]^2) dx

The 'speed' of the curve: hypotenuse of dx and dy.

Many nice functions give non-elementary integrals (e.g. ellipse perimeter involves elliptic integrals).

## Parametric equations: curves in motion (review + integrals)

<!-- section: parametric -->

Area, arc length, surface area all have parametric versions (see parametric-polar topic).

x(t), y(t): replace dx with x'(t) dt etc.

## Surface area of revolution

<!-- section: surfacearea -->

S = ∫ 2π y √(1 + (dy/dx)^2) dx (rotate y=f(x) around x-axis)

Like arc length but each segment generates a 'band' whose area is circumference 2π y times slant length ds.

## Work and physical applications

<!-- section: work -->

Work = ∫ F(x) dx (variable force).

Examples: spring (Hooke's law F=kx, work = ∫ kx dx), pumping liquid out of tank (force = weight density × volume of slice × distance lifted), lifting a chain/rope.

**ELI5**: Work is force times distance, but when either changes, you integrate the product along the path.

## Average value of a function

<!-- section: averagevalue -->

Avg = (1/(b-a)) ∫_a^b f(x) dx

By FTC or mean value theorem for integrals, there is c where f(c) equals the average.

**ELI5**: The average height of the curve is the height of the rectangle with same base and same area as under the curve.

## Hydrostatic force

<!-- section: hydrostatic -->

Force on a submerged plate: F = ∫ ρ g depth(y) × width(y) × dy (or slice).

Pressure increases linearly with depth. Integrate pressure × area element.

Classic: dam face, triangular window, etc.

## Center of mass and moments

<!-- section: centerofmass -->

For lamina (thin plate): moments M_x = ∫ y ρ dA, etc.

Center of mass (x̄, ȳ) = (M_y / m, M_x / m) where m = total mass.

For uniform density, geometric centroid.

For revolution solids or wires, use theorems of Pappus (volume = area × distance traveled by centroid).

**ELI5**: The balance point. Imagine the shape as made of tiny weights; the center of mass is the weighted average position. For symmetric shapes it's on the axis of symmetry.

## Common Mistakes

- Forgetting to square the radius in disk/washer volumes.
- Using wrong radius or height in shells (distance from axis of rotation).
- In work problems, wrong distance each slice travels or forgetting density.
- In hydrostatic, using wrong depth (measure from surface, not bottom) or mixing units (lb vs N, ft vs m).
- In center of mass, confusing moments with coordinates.
- Not sketching the region or solid first — leads to wrong bounds or method.
- Arc length: forgetting the 1 + (f')^2 under square root.
- Mixing disk vs shell when one is clearly easier.
