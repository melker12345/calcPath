# Ideals and Quotient Rings

Ideals are the ring-theoretic analogue of normal subgroups. They are the subsets you can "mod out by" to form quotient rings while preserving the ring operations.

The construction of quotient rings $R/I$ generalizes $\mathbb{Z}/n\mathbb{Z}$ and is the foundation for all of commutative algebra and algebraic geometry (where ideals correspond to varieties).

Ring homomorphisms have kernels that are ideals, and the isomorphism theorems carry over beautifully.

## Ideals {#ideals}

Ideals generalise the notion of "normal subgroups" from group theory to rings: they are the subsets you can safely "divide by" to form quotient structures while keeping the ring operations well-defined. They are indispensable in commutative algebra, algebraic number theory, and algebraic geometry, where they correspond to geometric objects called varieties.

The definition is deliberately asymmetric at first (left vs. right) because rings need not be commutative. The absorption property (multiplying an ideal element by anything from the ring stays inside the ideal) is what makes the quotient construction work. In commutative rings the distinction disappears and we simply speak of ideals.

In this section you will learn the precise axioms for left, right, and two-sided ideals, see the basic examples that appear everywhere, and understand why kernels of ring homomorphisms are always ideals.

Let $R$ be a ring. A subset $I \subseteq R$ is a **left ideal** if:

- $(I, +)$ is a subgroup of $(R, +)$
- For all $r \in R$, $a \in I$, $r a \in I$ (absorption)

Right ideal: $a r \in I$.

A **two-sided ideal** (or just "ideal" when R commutative) satisfies both.

In a commutative ring, left = right = two-sided.

The whole ring R and {0} are always ideals.

**ELI5**

- An ideal is a "black hole" inside the ring: once something falls in (by multiplying from outside), it stays in. You can add and subtract inside it freely.
- It is the right kind of subset to collapse to zero when forming quotients.

### Worked Examples

**Worked Example:**

**Even integers $2\mathbb{Z}$ is an ideal in $\mathbb{Z}$**

For any $k \in \mathbb{Z}$, $k \cdot (2m) = 2(km) \in $2\mathbb{Z}$. Additive subgroup obvious.

## Principal ideals {#principal-ideals}

In many familiar rings the ideals are particularly simple: they are all generated by a single element. Such ideals are called principal, and the rings in which every ideal is principal (PIDs) are among the nicest rings for factorization and divisibility theory.

The construction is straightforward once you have a ring with identity: just take all multiples of a fixed element a. This immediately gives you the ideals (n) inside the integers, and the same pattern works in polynomial rings over fields. Not every ring is a PID—polynomials in two variables already provide counter-examples—but the principal ones are the ones where the Euclidean algorithm and unique factorization behave best.

In this section you will learn the definition, see why Z is a PID while Z[x] is not for the ideal (2,x), and connect principal ideals to the broader hierarchy of Euclidean domains, PIDs, and UFDs that appears in the next topic.

In a commutative ring R with 1, the **principal ideal** generated by a is $(a) = \{ r a \mid r \in R \}$ (all multiples of a).

More generally $(a_1,\dots,a_k)$ is the ideal of all $r_1 a_1 + \cdots + r_k a_k$.

In $\mathbb{Z}$, every ideal is principal: $(n) = n\mathbb{Z}$.

In $\mathbb{Z}[x]$, $(2, x)$ is not principal.

A ring in which every ideal is principal is a **principal ideal ring** (PIR); if also a domain, a **PID**.

**ELI5**

- The principal ideal (a) is everything you can "make" by multiplying a by arbitrary ring elements — like all multiples on a number line.

### Worked Examples

**Worked Example:**

<strong>In $\mathbb{Z}/12\mathbb{Z}$, the ideal generated by 4</strong>

(4) = {0,4,8} (multiples of 4 mod 12). It is also (8) since 8= -4 mod12, and has 3 elements.

## Quotient rings {#quotient-rings}

Quotient rings are the ring-theoretic version of modular arithmetic: once you have an ideal I, you can "set everything in I to zero" and obtain a new ring R/I whose elements are cosets. This construction is exactly how we obtain Z/nZ from Z, and it is the central tool that lets us move between rings and study their structure via homomorphic images.

The key technical point is that the ideal absorption property guarantees the operations on cosets are independent of the choice of representatives. Without that, the multiplication table would be ambiguous. Every ring homomorphism factors through the quotient by its kernel, so quotients classify the "views" of a ring.

In this section you will learn how to define the operations on R/I, verify they are well-defined, recognise the universal property of the quotient map, and see the first isomorphism theorem in action.

Given an ideal I of R, the **quotient ring** R/I is the set of cosets {r + I}, with operations:

(r + I) + (s + I) = (r+s) + I

(r + I)(s + I) = (rs) + I

These are well-defined precisely because I is an ideal (absorption makes multiplication independent of representatives).

The map R → R/I , r ↦ r+I is a surjective ring homomorphism with kernel I.

**ELI5**

- Quotienting by I means "declare everything in I to be zero." Because of the absorption property, this doesn't break the multiplication rule.
- Classic example: $\mathbb{Z}/(n) \cong \mathbb{Z}/n\mathbb{Z}$.

### Worked Examples

**Worked Example:**

<strong>$\mathbb{Z}[x] / (x^2 + 1) \cong \mathbb{C}$ (intuitively)</strong>

The ideal (x²+1) consists of polynomials with x²+1 factor. In the quotient, x² +1 ≡0 so x² ≡ -1, and the image of x behaves exactly like i. The cosets are a + b x + (x²+1), a,b real (or rational), giving complex numbers.

## The ring homomorphism theorems {#homomorphism-theorems}

The isomorphism theorems for rings are the direct analogues of the three isomorphism theorems for groups. They give precise relationships between a ring, its ideals (kernels), its homomorphic images, and the quotients you can form. Mastering them is essential for any serious work in algebra; almost every structural result about rings ultimately reduces to one of these theorems or the lattice isomorphism (correspondence) theorem for ideals.

Just as in groups, the first theorem says that the image of any homomorphism is isomorphic to the quotient by its kernel. The higher theorems let you manipulate "fractions" of ideals and rings with the same algebraic rules you use for numbers. The correspondence theorem tells you that ideals of a quotient R/I are in one-to-one order-preserving correspondence with ideals of R that contain I.

In this section you will learn the statements of the three isomorphism theorems plus the correspondence theorem, see why kernels are ideals, and work through the universal examples that appear in every textbook and research paper.

For a ring homomorphism $\phi: R \to S$:

- $\ker \phi$ is an ideal of R.
- $\operatorname{im} \phi$ is a subring of S.
- **First isomorphism theorem for rings**: $R / \ker\phi \cong \operatorname{im}\phi$ (as rings).

There are second and third isomorphism theorems and a correspondence theorem: ideals of R/I are in bijection with ideals of R containing I.

**ELI5**

- Kernels of ring maps are ideals, not just additive subgroups — multiplication by anything in R must stay in the kernel.

### Worked Examples

**Worked Example:**

**The evaluation map**

Fix a ∈ R. Eval at a: $\phi: R[x] \to R$, f ↦ f(a). Kernel is the ideal of polynomials vanishing at a, i.e. (x-a) when R is a field or integral domain. Image is R. So R[x]/(x-a) ≅ R.

## Common Mistakes

- Forgetting that ideals must be additive subgroups (closed under subtraction).
- In non-commutative rings, confusing left, right, and two-sided ideals.
- Trying to form R/I when I is only a subring, not an ideal — multiplication of cosets fails to be well-defined.
- Assuming every ideal is principal (false in most rings; e.g. (2,x) in Z[x]).
- Confusing the ideal (2) in Z[x] (even constant term? no: all polys with even coefficients) with the ideal generated by 2 and x.
- Forgetting the correspondence: there is a lattice isomorphism between ideals containing I and ideals of R/I.
