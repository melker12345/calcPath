# Group Homomorphisms and Isomorphisms

Homomorphisms are the "structure-preserving maps" between groups. They are the morphisms in the category of groups. The most important invariants attached to a homomorphism are its kernel (elements sent to the identity) and image (elements hit).

The three isomorphism theorems (plus the correspondence theorem) are the fundamental tools for relating subgroups, quotients, and homomorphic images. They let you move information back and forth between a group and its quotients or images.

Isomorphisms are the "relabelings" that show two groups are essentially the same. Automorphisms are the symmetries of a single group.

## Group homomorphisms {#homomorphisms}

Homomorphisms are the structure-preserving functions between groups—the only maps that deserve to be called "group maps." They are the arrows in the category of groups and are the device that lets us compare different groups, transport theorems from one to another, and classify groups up to isomorphism.

The homomorphism property $\phi(ab)=\phi(a)\phi(b)$ is deceptively simple: it automatically forces preservation of the identity and inverses, so you only ever have to check one identity. The kernel and image of any such map carry enormous information; they are the main invariants used throughout the rest of group theory.

In this section you will learn the definition and its immediate consequences, the most common examples you will meet in every course, and how kernels and images organise the landscape of homomorphisms.

A map $\phi: G \to H$ between groups is a **homomorphism** if $\phi(ab) = \phi(a)\phi(b)$ for all $a,b \in G$.

It automatically preserves identity: $\phi(e_G) = e_H$, and inverses: $\phi(a^{-1}) = \phi(a)^{-1}$.

Examples:

- The map $\phi: \mathbb{Z} \to \mathbb{Z}/n\mathbb{Z}$, $k \mapsto k \bmod n$ (reduction mod n).
- Determinant $\det: GL_n(\mathbb{R}) \to \mathbb{R}^\times$.
- Sign homomorphism $\operatorname{sgn}: S_n \to \{\pm 1\}$.

**ELI5**

- A homomorphism is a translation of operations: doing the operation in G and then translating gives the same result as translating first and then operating in H. It respects the "multiplication table."

### Worked Examples

**Worked Example:**

**The exponential map**

$\exp: (\mathbb{R}, +) \to (\mathbb{R}^>0, \times)$, $x \mapsto e^x$ is a homomorphism: $e^{x+y} = e^x e^y$.

## Kernel and image {#kernel-image}

Every homomorphism comes with two canonical subgroups attached to it: the kernel (what gets crushed to the identity) and the image (what actually gets hit in the target). These two subgroups control almost everything you can say about the map—injectivity, surjectivity, and the isomorphism type of the image.

The kernel is always normal (a fact that only becomes obvious after you check the conjugation criterion), while the image is always a subgroup of the codomain. The first isomorphism theorem then identifies the image with the quotient of the domain by the kernel, giving the fundamental dictionary between homomorphisms, normal subgroups, and quotients.

In this section you will learn the definitions, the four fundamental facts that follow immediately, and why these two subgroups are the most important pieces of data attached to any group homomorphism you will ever write down.

The **kernel** of $\phi: G\to H$ is $\ker \phi = \{g \in G \mid \phi(g) = e_H \}$.

The **image** is $\operatorname{im} \phi = \{\phi(g) \mid g \in G \} \subseteq H$.

**Fundamental facts**:

- $\ker \phi$ is always a normal subgroup of $G$.
- $\operatorname{im} \phi$ is always a subgroup of $H$.
- $\phi$ is injective iff $\ker \phi = \{e\}$.
- $\phi$ is surjective iff $\operatorname{im} \phi = H$.

By the first isomorphism theorem, $G / \ker \phi \cong \operatorname{im} \phi$.

**ELI5**

- Kernel measures "how much is crushed to the identity" — the ambiguity or loss of information.
- Image measures "how much of the target you actually reach."

### Worked Examples

**Worked Example:**

**Kernel of reduction mod 2**

$\phi: \mathbb{Z} \to \mathbb{Z}/2\mathbb{Z}$, $k\mapsto k \bmod 2$. $\ker \phi = 2\mathbb{Z}$ (evens). Image is all of $\mathbb{Z}/2\mathbb{Z}$.

## The isomorphism theorems {#isomorphism-theorems}

The three isomorphism theorems (together with the correspondence theorem) are the central structural results of group theory. They tell you exactly how subgroups, quotients, and homomorphic images of a group are related, and they let you translate statements about one into statements about the others with almost no extra work.

The first theorem is the most used: it identifies every homomorphic image with a quotient by the kernel. The second and third let you "cancel" subgroups in fractions of groups the way you cancel common factors in ordinary fractions. The correspondence theorem says that the subgroup lattice of a quotient is the interval in the original lattice lying above the kernel.

In this section you will learn the precise statements, the short proofs that rely only on the first isomorphism theorem plus the isomorphism theorems for the ambient group, and why these four results are the workhorses that appear in virtually every argument in the subject.

**First Isomorphism Theorem**: $G / \ker\phi \cong \operatorname{im}\phi$.

**Second Isomorphism Theorem**: If $H,K \leq G$ and $H$ normalizes $K$ (or one is normal), then $HK / K \cong H / (H \cap K)$.

**Third Isomorphism Theorem**: If $N \trianglelefteq G$ and $M/N \trianglelefteq G/N$ (i.e. $N \leq M \trianglelefteq G$), then $(G/N) / (M/N) \cong G/M$.

These, together with the lattice isomorphism (correspondence) theorem, are the workhorses of group theory.

**ELI5**

- The first says: "the group you get after crushing the kernel is exactly the picture you painted in the image."
- The others let you manipulate "fractions" of groups the same way you cancel in fractions of numbers.

### Worked Examples

**Worked Example:**

<strong>$\mathbb{Z}/12\mathbb{Z} / \langle 4 \rangle \cong \mathbb{Z}/4\mathbb{Z}$? Wait, order check.</strong>

$|\mathbb{Z}/12| =12$, $\langle4\rangle=\{0,4,8\}$ order 3. 12/3=4. The quotient has order 4 and is cyclic, hence $\cong \mathbb{Z}/4\mathbb{Z}$.

## Automorphisms {#automorphisms}

An **automorphism** of $G$ is an isomorphism $\phi: G \to G$.

The set of all automorphisms forms a group $\operatorname{Aut}(G)$ under composition.

An **inner automorphism** is one of the form $\phi_g(x) = g x g^{-1}$ (conjugation by fixed $g$).

The inner automorphisms form a normal subgroup $\operatorname{Inn}(G) \trianglelefteq \operatorname{Aut}(G)$, and $\operatorname{Aut}(G)/\operatorname{Inn}(G) \cong \operatorname{Out}(G)$ (outer automorphism group).

For abelian groups, all inner autos are trivial (conjugation does nothing).

Examples:

- $\operatorname{Aut}(\mathbb{Z}/n\mathbb{Z}) \cong (\mathbb{Z}/n\mathbb{Z})^\times$, order $\phi(n)$.
- $\operatorname{Aut}(S_3) \cong S_3$ (all autos inner).

**ELI5**

- An automorphism is a way to relabel the elements of the group so that the multiplication table looks identical — a symmetry of the group's own structure.
- Inner ones come from "rotating the labels using the group itself."

### Worked Examples

**Worked Example:**

**Automorphisms of $\mathbb{Z}/6\mathbb{Z}$**

Generators of the additive group are elements coprime to 6: 1 and 5. Sending generator 1 to 1 or to 5 gives two distinct autos. $\operatorname{Aut} \cong \mathbb{Z}/2\mathbb{Z}$.

## Common Mistakes

- Forgetting to prove that a proposed map preserves the operation (the homomorphism property is the only thing you need to check; the rest follows).
- Claiming a map is an isomorphism just because it is bijective — you must verify it is a homomorphism (or its inverse is).
- Confusing kernel with the set of elements mapped to zero when the groups are additive.
- Thinking every homomorphism is surjective or injective. Most interesting ones are neither.
- Forgetting that kernels are normal (this is why we can form $G/\ker\phi$).
- Assuming $\operatorname{Aut}(G)$ is always isomorphic to $G$ (false in general; e.g. for prime p, $\operatorname{Aut}(\mathbb{Z}/p\mathbb{Z})$ has order p-1, not p).
