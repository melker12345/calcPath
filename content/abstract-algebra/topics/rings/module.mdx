# Rings

Rings generalize the arithmetic of integers and polynomials. They have two operations — addition and multiplication — that interact via the distributive laws. Addition always forms an abelian group; multiplication is associative and distributes over addition, but need not have inverses or even an identity.

Rings are the setting in which we study factorization, ideals, and modules. Many of the deepest results in algebra (unique factorization, Hilbert's basis theorem, etc.) live in ring theory.

This topic covers the axioms, basic examples, units, zero-divisors, and subrings.

## Ring axioms and definition {#definition}

Rings capture the essential algebraic properties of familiar number systems (and many others) using just two operations. To ensure addition and multiplication "play nicely" together — so that we can expand, factor, and manipulate expressions reliably — we impose a short list of axioms. These are the minimal requirements; many extra properties (such as commutativity or inverses) may or may not hold.

A **ring** $(R, +, \cdot)$ is a set $R$ with two binary operations satisfying:

1. $(R, +)$ is an abelian group.
2. Multiplication is associative: $(ab)c = a(bc)$.
3. Distributivity: $a(b+c) = ab + ac$ and $(b+c)a = ba + ca$.

A ring with **multiplicative identity** (or unity) 1 such that $1r = r1 = r$ for all r is called a **ring with 1** (or unital ring). We usually assume rings have 1 unless stated otherwise.

A ring is **commutative** if $ab=ba$ for all a,b.

**ELI5**

- Think of a ring as "numbers you can add and multiply" but the multiplication might not let you divide. Addition is always nice and reversible; multiplication may have zero divisors or lack units.
- The distributive law is what makes multiplication "compatible" with addition — the heart of algebra.

### Worked Examples

**Worked Example:**

**The integers $\mathbb{Z}$ form a commutative ring with 1**

Addition and multiplication are the usual ones. Identity 1. No zero divisors.

## Important examples of rings {#examples}

- $\mathbb{Z}$, $\mathbb{Q}$, $\mathbb{R}$, $\mathbb{C}$ — commutative rings with 1.
- $\mathbb{Z}/n\mathbb{Z}$ — commutative ring with 1 (not a domain if n composite).
- $M_n(\mathbb{R})$ — matrix rings, non-commutative, with 1 (the identity matrix).
- Polynomial ring $R[x]$ over any ring R — if R commutative with 1 then so is $R[x]$.
- The zero ring (one element, 0=1) — usually excluded in definitions that require 1 ≠ 0.

**ELI5**

- Polynomial rings let you treat $x$ formally as a variable and do algebra with coefficients from another ring.
- Matrix rings show that non-commutative multiplication arises naturally when composing linear transformations.

### Worked Examples

**Worked Example:**

<strong>$\mathbb{Z}/6\mathbb{Z}$ is a ring with zero divisors</strong>

Elements 0,1,2,3,4,5 mod 6. Note 2·3=6≡0, but neither is 0. So zero divisors.

## Units and zero-divisors {#units}

In a ring R with 1:

- A **unit** is an element u with a multiplicative inverse v (uv=vu=1). The set of units R^× forms a group under multiplication.
- A **zero-divisor** is a nonzero a such that there exists nonzero b with ab=0 or ba=0.
- A **nilpotent** element satisfies a^k =0 for some k>0.

In an integral domain (no zero-divisors), the only nilpotent is 0.

In $\mathbb{Z}/n\mathbb{Z}$, the units are exactly the classes coprime to n; there are $\phi(n)$ of them.

**ELI5**

- Units are the "invertible numbers" inside the ring — the ones you can divide by while staying inside the ring.
- Zero-divisors are "poison" elements that kill other nonzero things when multiplied — like 2 and 3 in mod 6: their product disappears.

### Worked Examples

**Worked Example:**

**Units in $\mathbb{Z}/8\mathbb{Z}$**

Units: 1,3,5,7 (odd numbers). 3·3=9≡1, 3·5=15≡7, 3·7=21≡5, 5·5=25≡1, etc. The unit group is isomorphic to the Klein four-group C2 × C2.

## Subrings {#subrings}

A subset S of a ring R is a **subring** if it is closed under addition, subtraction (or negatives), and multiplication, and contains the same 1 (if we require rings to have 1).

The subring test is analogous to the subgroup test.

Examples: $\mathbb{Z} \subset \mathbb{Q} \subset \mathbb{R} \subset \mathbb{C}$.

**ELI5**

- A subring is a smaller "number system" living inside a bigger one that is still closed under + and ×.

## Common Mistakes

- Forgetting that addition must form an abelian group (including commutativity of + and additive inverses).
- Assuming every ring has a 1, or that 1 ≠ 0 (the zero ring is degenerate).
- Confusing "commutative ring" with "field" — fields require multiplicative inverses for nonzero elements.
- Thinking matrix rings are commutative.
- Forgetting distributivity when checking if a set is a ring (the hard part is usually verifying the two distributive laws or that multiplication stays inside).
- In $\mathbb{Z}/n\mathbb{Z}$ thinking every nonzero element is a unit (only true when n prime).
