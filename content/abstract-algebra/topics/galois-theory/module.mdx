# Field Extensions and Galois Theory

Galois theory is the jewel of classical abstract algebra. It establishes a deep dictionary between field extensions and groups (the Galois groups), allowing one to translate hard questions about solvability of polynomials into questions about finite groups.

The crowning achievement is the proof that there is no general formula by radicals for solving polynomial equations of degree 5 or higher — the general quintic is not solvable by radicals — because symmetric groups S_n for n ≥ 5 are not solvable groups.

This topic introduces the language of extensions, algebraic elements, splitting fields, Galois groups, and the fundamental theorem that gives the lattice anti-isomorphism between subfields and subgroups.

## Field extensions {#field-extensions}

Field extensions are the algebraic setting in which we adjoin new elements (roots, transcendental quantities) to a base field while keeping all the field operations. They are the direct generalisation of "number systems built on top of the rationals" and are the objects Galois theory ultimately classifies by their symmetry groups.

The degree [K : F] measures the "size" of the extension as a vector-space dimension; when the degree is finite the extension is called finite. Not every finite extension is obtained by adjoining a single element, but many important ones are. Transcendental extensions (such as the reals over the rationals) have infinite degree and lie outside the reach of classical Galois theory.

In this section you will learn the basic vocabulary of extensions and degree, the distinction between algebraic and transcendental elements, and the notion of a simple extension that prepares the ground for the Galois correspondence.

A **field extension** K/F is a field K containing F as a subfield.

The **degree** [K : F] is the dimension of K as a vector space over F.

- If finite degree, K is algebraic over F? Not necessarily (transcendental extensions exist).
- Simple extension: K = F(α) for some α in K.
- If α algebraic over F of minimal polynomial degree n, then [F(α):F] = n, with power basis of size n.

Examples: R/Q degree infinite (transcendental), C/R degree 2, F_p(α) where α root of irr poly.

**ELI5**

- A field extension is a bigger "number world" that contains your original field. The degree tells you how many "independent directions" you need to describe the new numbers using the old ones (like basis).

### Worked Examples

**Worked Example:**

<strong>$\mathbb{Q}(i)$ over $\mathbb{Q}$ has degree 2</strong>

Minimal poly of i is x²+1, irreducible over Q. Basis {1, i}. Every element a + b i with a,b rational.

## Algebraic extensions {#algebraic}

An element is algebraic over a field F when it satisfies a polynomial equation with coefficients in F. The algebraic numbers (roots of polynomials over Q) form an enormous field that is the algebraic closure of Q inside C; most explicit numbers you meet in algebra are algebraic.

The minimal polynomial is the monic polynomial of least degree that annihilates the element; it is irreducible and unique. Adjoining one algebraic element produces a finite extension whose degree equals the degree of that minimal polynomial. Not every algebraic extension is finite (the algebraic closure of Q is algebraic but infinite), but every finite extension is algebraic.

In this section you will learn the definitions of algebraic element and algebraic extension, the existence and uniqueness of minimal polynomials, and the relationship between degree and the splitting behaviour of polynomials that prepares for Galois groups.

An element α ∈ K is **algebraic** over F if it is a root of some nonzero polynomial in F[x].

The **minimal polynomial** m(x) ∈ F[x] is the monic irr poly of least degree that α satisfies. It is unique.

K/F is an **algebraic extension** if every element of K is algebraic over F.

- Finite extensions are algebraic.
- Algebraic extensions need not be finite (e.g. algebraic closure of Q).
- If α algebraic, F(α)/F is finite (and algebraic).

Splitting field of a poly f over F is the smallest extension in which f factors completely into linears.

**ELI5**

- Algebraic numbers are roots of polynomial equations with rational (or integer) coefficients. Transcendental like π and e are not.
- The splitting field is the smallest world in which your polynomial "factors all the way" into (x - root) terms.

### Worked Examples

**Worked Example:**

**Splitting field of x^3 + x + 1 over Q (sketch)**

This cubic is irreducible over Q (no rational roots by rational root thm). Its splitting field has degree dividing 6 (Gal embeds in S3), and in this case turns out to be 6.

## Galois groups {#galois-group}

Let K/F be a finite normal separable extension (Galois extension).

The **Galois group** Gal(K/F) = Aut(K/F) = {field automorphisms of K that fix F pointwise}.

- It is a finite group of order [K:F].
- For splitting field of separable poly, the extension is Galois.
- Action on roots: Gal permutes the roots of any irr poly over F.

For quadratic x² - d, Gal ≅ Z/2Z (the conjugation sending √d to -√d).

For cyclotomic fields, Gal(Q(ζ_n)/Q) ≅ (Z/nZ)^× .

**ELI5**

- A Galois group is the collection of all "symmetries" of the big field that don't move the base field. These symmetries must send roots of a polynomial to other roots.
- The group "measures the ambiguity" in how the roots can be permuted while respecting all algebraic relations over the base.

### Worked Examples

**Worked Example:**

**Gal(Q(i)/Q) = {id, σ} where σ(a+bi)=a-bi**

Order 2, matches degree 2. The only nontrivial auto (complex conjugation).

## The fundamental theorem of Galois theory {#fundamental-theorem}

Let K/F be Galois with G = Gal(K/F).

There is a lattice-reversing bijection:

Subfields E with F ⊆ E ⊆ K   ↔   subgroups H ≤ G

Given by E ↔ Gal(K/E), and H ↔ fixed field K^H = {x in K | σ(x)=x for all σ in H}.

Properties:

- [K:E] = |Gal(K/E)|
- [E:F] = [G : Gal(K/E)]
- E/F normal (Galois) iff Gal(K/E) normal in G; then Gal(E/F) ≅ G / Gal(K/E)

This lets you read off all intermediate fields from the subgroup lattice of G.

**ELI5**

- The bigger the subfield, the smaller the group of symmetries that fix it (more things fixed ⇒ fewer autos).
- Normal subgroups correspond to "nice" intermediate fields that are themselves Galois over the base.

### Worked Examples

**Worked Example:**

**Quadratic case**

G = {1, σ} ≅ C2. Subgroups: {1} and G.

Fixed fields: K^{{1}} = K, K^G = F.

Intermediate fields: only F and K. Corresponds to only trivial and whole group.

## Solvability by radicals and quintics (sketch) {#solvable}

A poly is solvable by radicals if its roots can be expressed using +,−,×,÷ and nth roots starting from the coefficients.

This corresponds to the Galois group of the splitting field being a **solvable group** (has composition series with abelian factors).

- S_n for n≤4 are solvable ⇒ quadratics, cubics, quartics have radical formulas (Cardano, Ferrari).
- S_n for n≥5 are not solvable (have simple non-abelian composition factor A_n) ⇒ the general quintic is not solvable by radicals.

This is the historical origin of Galois theory (Galois, Abel, Ruffini).

**ELI5**

- For degree 5, the symmetries of the five roots are too entangled (the alternating group A_5 has no normal subgroups to "peel off" layer by layer) to allow an expression using only root extractions in general.

## Common Mistakes

- Confusing algebraic with finite: an algebraic extension can be infinite (union of all finite ones).
- Forgetting separability: in char p there are inseparable extensions; Galois theory in full generality requires normal + separable.
- Assuming every extension is Galois (e.g. the splitting field of an irreducible cubic may be degree 6 while a subextension by one root has degree 3 and is not normal).
- Mixing up fixed fields and Galois correspondence direction: larger field ↔ smaller group.
- Thinking the Galois group order is always the degree of the minimal polynomial of one primitive element (it is the degree of the whole extension).
- Claiming all quintics are unsolvable — only the general one; many specific quintics have solvable Gal groups (e.g. x^5 - x -1 may or may not, but x^5-1 is cyclotomic solvable).
