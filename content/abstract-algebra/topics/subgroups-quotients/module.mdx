# Subgroups, Cosets, and Quotient Groups

Once you have a group, the next natural questions are: what are its "sub-collections" that are still groups? How do you "divide" a group by a subgroup? These ideas lead directly to Lagrange's theorem (one of the most useful counting results in algebra), normal subgroups, and quotient groups — the group-theoretic analogue of modular arithmetic.

Quotient groups let you "mod out" by a normal subgroup exactly as you mod out by $n$ in $\mathbb{Z}/n\mathbb{Z}$. This construction is ubiquitous: it produces factor groups, simplifies symmetry, and appears in the isomorphism theorems and Galois theory.

## Subgroups {#subgroups}

Not every subset of a group forms a group on its own. The subsets that do — closed under the operation and inverses, and containing the identity — are called subgroups. They are the "symmetry sub-systems" inside a larger symmetry group. The one-step subgroup test gives an efficient way to check the axioms without verifying all four separately.

A subset $H \subseteq G$ is a **subgroup** of $G$ if $H$ is itself a group under the same operation as $G$.

The **subgroup test** (one-step): $H$ is a subgroup iff $H$ is nonempty and for all $a,b \in H$, $a b^{-1} \in H$.

Trivial subgroups: $\{e\}$ and $G$ itself. Proper subgroups are neither.

Examples:

- $2\mathbb{Z}$ (even integers) is a subgroup of $(\mathbb{Z}, +)$.
- The alternating group $A_n$ of even permutations is a subgroup of $S_n$ (index 2, hence normal).
- In $(\mathbb{Z}/12\mathbb{Z}, +)$, the set $\{0,3,6,9\}$ is a subgroup.

**ELI5**

- A subgroup is a "club inside the bigger club" that is closed under the club's rules and has its own "do nothing" and "undo" moves that work inside the club.
- If the big group is all dance moves, a subgroup might be the moves that preserve a certain pattern (e.g. only rotations, no reflections).

### Worked Examples

**Worked Example:**

**Checking $3\mathbb{Z}$ is a subgroup of $\mathbb{Z}$ under +**

Take $a=3m$, $b=3n$. Then $a - b = 3(m-n) \in 3\mathbb{Z}$. Nonempty (contains 0). Hence subgroup.

## Cosets and Lagrange's theorem {#lagrange}

Once you have a subgroup, the next natural objects are its cosets—the "parallel copies" of the subgroup obtained by translating it by elements of the big group. Cosets partition the group into equal-sized pieces; their number (the index) is therefore a divisor of the group order when the group is finite. This simple counting fact is Lagrange's theorem, one of the most frequently applied results in all of finite group theory.

The proof is almost immediate once you observe that left (or right) multiplication by a fixed element is a bijection, so every coset has exactly the same cardinality as the subgroup. The consequences for element orders, possible subgroup sizes, and the structure of finite groups are immediate and powerful.

In this section you will learn the definition of left and right cosets, prove they partition the group, derive Lagrange's theorem together with its standard corollaries, and see how the index controls the size of quotient sets even before you have a group structure on them.

Let $H \leq G$ and $g \in G$. The **left coset** of $H$ containing $g$ is $gH = \{gh \mid h \in H\}$. Right coset $Hg$.

Cosets partition $G$: every element is in exactly one left (resp. right) coset. The number of distinct left cosets is the **index** $[G:H]$.

**Lagrange's theorem**: If $G$ is finite and $H \leq G$, then $|H|$ divides $|G|$, and $[G:H] = |G|/|H|$.

Consequences:

- Order of any element divides $|G|$.
- $|G|$ is divisible by the order of every subgroup.
- Possible subgroup orders are the divisors of $|G|$.

**ELI5**

- Cosets are like "parallel copies" of the subgroup. If you take the evens inside integers, the cosets are the evens and the odds. Lagrange says the size of any such copy divides the total size — you can't have a club whose size doesn't fit evenly into the whole population.

### Worked Examples

**Worked Example:**

**Subgroups of $\mathbb{Z}/6\mathbb{Z}$**

Divisors of 6: 1,2,3,6.

Subgroup of order 1: $\{0\}$

Order 2: $\{0,3\}$

Order 3: $\{0,2,4\}$

Order 6: whole group.

## Normal subgroups and quotient groups {#quotients}

Not every subgroup can be "divided out" to produce a quotient group; only the normal ones can. Normality is the precise condition that makes left and right cosets coincide, so that the set of cosets inherits a well-defined group operation from G. Quotient groups are the group-theoretic version of modular arithmetic and are the source of almost all the groups you meet in nature (Z/nZ, symmetry groups of objects, Galois groups, etc.).

A subgroup N is normal when it is invariant under conjugation by every element of G. In abelian groups every subgroup is automatically normal; in non-abelian groups normality is a strong symmetry condition. Once you have a normal subgroup the quotient map is a surjective homomorphism whose kernel is exactly N, and the first isomorphism theorem applies.

In this section you will learn the definition and equivalent characterisations of normality, how to form the quotient group G/N, verify the operation is well-defined, and see the universal example of Z/nZ arising as Z modulo the normal subgroup nZ.

A subgroup $N \leq G$ is **normal** ($N \trianglelefteq G$) if $gNg^{-1} = N$ for all $g \in G$ (left and right cosets coincide: $gN = Ng$).

Equivalently: $g n g^{-1} \in N$ for all $g,n$.

The **quotient group** $G/N$ is the set of (left) cosets of $N$, with operation $(gN)(hN) = (gh)N$. This is well-defined precisely when $N$ is normal.

The quotient map $\pi: G \to G/N$, $g \mapsto gN$ is a surjective homomorphism with kernel $N$.

**ELI5**

- Normal subgroups are "symmetric" with respect to conjugation by the whole group — you can "move them around" with group elements and they look the same.
- The quotient is like "identifying" all elements that differ by something in $N$. In $\mathbb{Z}/n\mathbb{Z}$ we are quotienting $\mathbb{Z}$ by the normal subgroup $n\mathbb{Z}$.

### Worked Examples

**Worked Example:**

<strong>$n\mathbb{Z} \trianglelefteq \mathbb{Z}$</strong>

For any positive integer $n$, the subgroup $n\mathbb{Z}$ of multiples of $n$ is normal in $(\mathbb{Z}, +)$ (since $\mathbb{Z}$ is abelian, all subgroups are normal). The quotient $\mathbb{Z}/n\mathbb{Z}$ is the cyclic group of residues modulo $n$.

## Sylow theorems and simple groups {#sylow}

For finite groups the Sylow theorems give extraordinarily precise counting information about the p-subgroups (subgroups whose order is the highest power of a given prime p dividing |G|). They are the main tool for proving existence of normal subgroups in finite groups and for classifying groups of small order.

The three theorems together say that Sylow p-subgroups exist, that they are all conjugate (hence "the same up to relabelling"), and that the number of them satisfies strong congruence and divisibility conditions. A group with no nontrivial normal subgroups is called simple; the classification of finite simple groups is one of the great achievements of twentieth-century mathematics, and the Sylow theorems are indispensable in the proof.

In this section you will learn the statements of the Sylow theorems, the definition of simple groups, and how these results are used to force normal subgroups or to prove that certain groups must be semidirect products.

Let $G$ be finite of order $p^k m$ with $p \nmid m$.

A **Sylow $p$-subgroup** is a subgroup of order $p^k$ (maximal $p$-power order).

**Sylow theorems**:

1. Sylow $p$-subgroups exist.
2. All Sylow $p$-subgroups are conjugate.
3. The number $n_p$ of Sylow $p$-subgroups satisfies $n_p \equiv 1 \pmod{p}$ and $n_p$ divides $m$.

A group is **simple** if it has no nontrivial normal subgroups.

- $A_5$ is the smallest non-abelian simple group (order 60).
- Simple groups are the "building blocks" in the classification of finite groups (Jordan–Hölder).

**ELI5**

- Sylow theorems are powerful counting tools that often force the existence of normal subgroups or prove a group must be a semidirect product.
- A simple group has no "normal pieces" you can quotient by — it is atomic from the normal-subgroup viewpoint.

### Worked Examples

**Worked Example:**

**Sylow 2-subgroups of $S_3$**

$|S_3|=6=2\cdot 3$. Sylow 2-subgroups have order 2: they are $\{e,(12)\}$, $\{e,(13)\}$, $\{e,(23)\}$. Number $n_2=3 \equiv 1 \pmod 2$ and divides 3. They are conjugate.

## Common Mistakes

- Confusing "subgroup" with "subset closed under the operation" — you must also have the identity and inverses (or use the one-step test correctly).
- Assuming every subgroup is normal. $H = \{e, (12)\}$ is not normal in $S_3$.
- Forgetting that Lagrange gives a necessary condition on orders but not sufficient (there may be no subgroup of a given divisor order, e.g. no subgroup of order 6 in $A_4$).
- Writing $G/H$ without checking normality first.
- Thinking all groups of prime order are cyclic — they are, but that's a consequence of Lagrange + cyclic generation.
