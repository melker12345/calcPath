# Integral Domains and Fields

Integral domains are the "number rings" without zero-divisors — the natural home of unique factorization ideas. Fields are the rings in which you can divide by every nonzero element; they are the analogues of the rational, real, and complex numbers.

The relationship is tight: every field is an integral domain, and the quotient of a domain by a maximal ideal is a field. The field of fractions construction lets you "localize" any domain into a field (like Z to Q).

Characteristic is a fundamental invariant that controls the arithmetic inside the structure (e.g. 1+1+...+1 =0 in finite fields).

## Integral domains {#domains}

Within rings, some are "well-behaved" for multiplication in the sense that you cannot have two nonzero elements whose product is zero. Such rings are called integral domains; they are the natural setting in which unique factorization and divisibility theory can be developed without extra complications.

An **integral domain** is a commutative ring with 1 that has no zero-divisors (i.e., ab=0 implies a=0 or b=0) and usually 1 ≠ 0.

Examples: Z, any field, and polynomial rings over domains.

Non-examples include rings with zero-divisors such as Z/6Z or matrix rings.

Cancellation is valid in integral domains (nonzero factors can be canceled).

**ELI5**

- An integral domain is a place where multiplication behaves like ordinary integers: no "weird" nonzero things that multiply to zero and destroy information.
- You can safely cancel nonzero factors.

### Worked Examples

**Worked Example:**

**Polynomial rings k[x] over a field k are integral domains**

If two polynomials multiply to the zero polynomial, all coefficients of product are zero. By looking at lowest degree terms (leading coefficients multiply), if both nonzero then product nonzero. Hence no zero divisors.

## Fields {#fields}

Fields are the rings in which division by any nonzero element is always possible inside the structure. They are the algebraic counterparts of the rational, real, and complex numbers, and they underpin linear algebra, algebraic geometry, and much of number theory.

A **field** is a commutative ring with 1 in which every nonzero element has a multiplicative inverse (i.e. the nonzero elements form a group under multiplication).

Equivalently: a commutative division ring, or an integral domain in which the only ideals are {0} and the whole ring.

Examples: Q, R, C, Z/pZ for p prime (finite fields F_p), function fields, etc.

Fields are special kinds of integral domains (every field is a domain, but not conversely, e.g. Z is a domain that is not a field).

**ELI5**

- In a field you can always "divide" (multiply by inverse) as long as you don't divide by zero — just like fractions, decimals, or complex numbers.
- Finite fields exist and are used heavily in coding theory and cryptography.

### Worked Examples

**Worked Example:**

**In F_3 = Z/3Z, the inverse of 2 is 2**

Because 2·2=4≡1 mod 3. Every nonzero has unique inverse.

## Characteristic {#characteristic}

The **characteristic** of a ring R (with 1) is the smallest positive integer n such that n·1 = 1+1+...+1 (n times) equals 0 in R; or 0 if no such n exists.

- char(Z) = char(R) = 0.
- char(Z/pZ) = p.
- If R has characteristic n >0 then n annihilates every element: n r =0 for all r.
- In a domain (hence in fields), char is either 0 or prime.

The prime subfield of a field of char p is F_p; of char 0 is Q.

**ELI5**

- Characteristic tells you the "smallest number of 1's that add up to zero." In clock arithmetic mod p, p ones make a full cycle back to zero.
- Char 0 means you can add 1 as many times as you want and never hit zero — like the integers.

**Worked Example:**

**Characteristic of Z/12Z is 12**

12·1 =0 mod12, and no smaller positive k works (k*1 mod12 =0 only for multiples of 12? Wait 6*1=6≠0, yes smallest is 12, even though not prime and has zero divisors.

## Prime and maximal ideals; field of fractions {#fractions}

In a commutative ring R:

- I is **prime** if I ≠ R and ab ∈ I ⇒ a∈I or b∈I.
- I is **maximal** if I ≠ R and no ideal strictly between I and R.

Every maximal ideal is prime. In Z, (p) for p prime is maximal (and prime); (0) is prime but not maximal; (n) for composite n>1 is neither.

The **field of fractions** (or quotient field) of an integral domain D is the smallest field containing D (up to iso), constructed as fractions a/b with b≠0, modulo the equivalence (a/b ~ c/d iff ad=bc).

For D=Z we recover Q.

**ELI5**

- Prime ideals are the ones whose quotients still have no zero-divisors.
- Maximal ideals are the largest proper ideals; their quotients have special properties.

### Worked Examples

**Worked Example:**

**Z/(p) is a field for p prime**

(p) is maximal because Z is a PID and (p) is generated by irreducible = prime element.

## Common Mistakes

- Assuming every domain is a field (Z is the counter-example everyone knows).
- Confusing "no zero-divisors" with "every nonzero is a unit".
- Forgetting that characteristic is defined using the multiplicative identity 1, not arbitrary elements.
- In finite rings, thinking char must be prime (Z/4Z and Z/6Z have composite char).
- Claiming (0) is maximal in Z (it's prime but Z/(0) ≅ Z is not a field).
- Forgetting to check 1 ≠ 0 when defining domains/fields (the zero ring is usually excluded).
- In field of fractions, writing fractions without reducing or forgetting the equivalence relation ad=bc.
