# Polynomial Rings and Unique Factorization

Polynomial rings are the most important examples of rings beyond the integers. Over a field, F[x] is a Euclidean domain — the gold standard for "nice" factorization.

The hierarchy is: Euclidean domain (ED) ⇒ principal ideal domain (PID) ⇒ unique factorization domain (UFD). Each implication is strict in general.

Understanding these classes tells you exactly when you have unique factorization into irreducibles (up to units and order), and when you have a Euclidean algorithm for gcds.

## Polynomial rings over fields {#polynomial-rings}

Polynomial rings F[x] over a field F are the direct generalisation of the integers that lets us do "arithmetic with indeterminates." They are the setting in which we prove unique factorization for polynomials, run the Euclidean algorithm, and build field extensions by adjoining roots. Almost everything that works for Z also works for F[x] (and vice versa), which is why they are the prototype for all Euclidean domains.

The key extra structure is the degree function, which plays the role of absolute value and makes division with remainder possible. Because F is a field, there are no zero-divisors among the leading coefficients, so the degree of a product is exactly the sum of the degrees. This turns F[x] into a Euclidean domain and therefore into a PID and a UFD.

In this section you will learn the basic arithmetic of F[x], the division algorithm, the fact that it is a Euclidean domain, and how irreducibles behave like primes.

Let F be a field. Then F[x] is the set of polynomials with coefficients in F.

- It is a commutative ring with 1.
- It is an integral domain.
- Degree function: deg(fg) = deg f + deg g (when F field, no zero divisors).
- Division algorithm: for any f,g with g≠0 there exist unique q,r with f = q g + r and deg r < deg g (or r=0).

Irreducible polynomials play the role of "primes".

**ELI5**

- Polynomials over a field behave almost exactly like the integers: you can divide with remainder, gcds exist via Euclidean algorithm, and you get unique factorization into "prime" (irreducible) polynomials.
- Only the nonzero constant polynomials are units (have multiplicative inverses) in F[x].

## Euclidean domains {#euclidean-domains}

A Euclidean domain is an integral domain equipped with a Euclidean function that lets you perform division with strictly smaller remainders, exactly as you do with integers or with polynomials. This single extra piece of structure is enough to guarantee that gcds exist via the Euclidean algorithm and that every ideal is principal.

The two classical examples are the integers (with N(n) = |n|) and polynomial rings over fields (with N(f) = deg f). A few quadratic integer rings also qualify, but most number rings do not; the Euclidean property is rare and precious.

In this section you will learn the definition, verify that both Z and F[x] satisfy it, see the Euclidean algorithm at work, and understand why every Euclidean domain is automatically a PID (which sets up the next section on unique factorization).

A domain D is a **Euclidean domain** if there is a function N: D\{0} → nonnegative integers (Euclidean function) such that for any a,b≠0 there exist q,r with a = q b + r where either r=0 or N(r) < N(b).

Classic examples:

- Z with N(n) = |n|
- F[x] with N(f) = deg f
- Certain quadratic integer rings (e.g. the Gaussian integers)

In an ED you can run the Euclidean algorithm to compute gcds, and every ED is a PID.

**ELI5**

- A Euclidean domain is a domain with a "size" function that lets you do long division with strictly smaller remainders. This powers the Euclidean algorithm.

### Worked Examples

**Worked Example:**

**gcd in Z using Euclidean algorithm**

gcd(252, 198): 252=1*198 +54; 198=3*54 +36; 54=1*36+18; 36=2*18+0. gcd=18.

## PIDs and UFDs {#pids-ufds}

The chain Euclidean domain ⇒ PID ⇒ UFD organises the rings in which "unique factorization" makes sense. A PID is the middle ground where every ideal is generated by one element; a UFD is the weaker setting where factorization into irreducibles is still unique (up to units and order) even if not every ideal is principal.

Every Euclidean domain is a PID (the Euclidean function produces a generator for any ideal via the algorithm), and every PID is a UFD (the usual proof by Noetherian induction plus the fact that irreducibles are prime). The converses fail: Z[x] is a UFD but not a PID, and there are PIDs that are not Euclidean (rare).

In this section you will learn the precise definitions, the theorem relating the three classes, the counter-examples that separate them, and why in a UFD the notions of prime and irreducible coincide.

A **PID** is an integral domain in which every ideal is principal.

A **UFD** is an integral domain in which every nonzero non-unit element factors into irreducibles, and the factorization is unique up to units and ordering.

**Theorem**: Every PID is a UFD. Every ED is a PID.

Counter-examples the other way: polynomial rings in multiple variables show UFDs that fail to be PIDs (e.g. the ideal generated by 2 and x in Z[x] is not principal). There exist UFDs that are not PIDs, and PIDs that are not EDs (rare).

In a UFD, "prime" and "irreducible" coincide.

In a PID, every nonzero prime ideal is maximal (a key fact used in factorization).

**ELI5**

- PID: every ideal is generated by a single element (like in Z, every ideal nZ).
- UFD: you have unique prime factorization, the fundamental theorem of arithmetic generalized.

### Worked Examples

**Worked Example:**

**An example of a UFD that is not a PID**

The ideal I = (2, x) in Z[x] consists of all polys with even constant term. Suppose I=(f). Then f divides 2 and x. Degree arguments and content show no single generator works.

## Factorization in F[x] {#factorization}

Over a field F, F[x] is a Euclidean domain, hence PID and UFD.

- A polynomial is irreducible over F iff it cannot be written as product of lower degree non-units.
- For F=Q or finite fields, there are irreducibility tests (Eisenstein, reduction mod p, checking no roots for low deg).
- Unique factorization: f = c · p1^{e1} ... pk^{ek} where c unit (leading coeff or constant), pi monic irreducibles.

Gauss's lemma relates factorization over Z and over Q.

**ELI5**

- Over the reals or complexes you can factor completely into linears (fundamental thm of algebra). Over rationals many polynomials stay irreducible (e.g. x^2+1).

**Worked Example:**

**Factor x^4 + 4 over Q**

## Common Mistakes

- Forgetting that in F[x] the units are the nonzero constants, not just ±1.
- Applying Euclidean algorithm without a Euclidean function (only works in EDs).
- Claiming Z[x] is a PID because Z and x are PIDs — product of PIDs need not be (and isn't).
- Confusing irreducible with prime (they coincide in UFDs, but definition of prime is stronger in general rings).
- Thinking every UFD is a PID (e.g. certain polynomial rings provide counterexamples).
- In polynomial division, forgetting that the coefficients must live in a field to guarantee the division algorithm works (leading coeff must be invertible).
- Assuming unique factorization always holds — it fails dramatically in some rings (e.g. Z[√-5]).
