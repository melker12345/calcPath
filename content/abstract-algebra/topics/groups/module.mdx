# Groups

Groups are the most fundamental algebraic structure in abstract algebra. A group captures the idea of symmetry and reversible operations in a precise, axiomatic way. Understanding groups lets you see the common patterns behind addition of integers, multiplication of nonzero reals, rotations of a square, and permutations of a set.

The power of the group concept is abstraction: once you prove a theorem about groups in general, it applies automatically to every concrete realization (numbers, matrices, symmetries, functions). This is why group theory underpins large parts of physics, chemistry, cryptography, and pure mathematics.

This topic introduces the definition, the four axioms, basic examples, abelian and cyclic groups, and the notion of order.

## The definition of a group {#definition}

Groups formalize the notion of symmetry and reversible actions. The definition isolates exactly the properties needed so that every element has an "undo" and combinations of actions behave predictably (independent of parenthesization). Once these four axioms are satisfied, a rich theory follows that applies uniformly to numbers, permutations, matrices, and geometric transformations.

A **group** is a set $G$ together with a binary operation $\cdot : G \times G \to G$ satisfying four axioms:

1. **Closure**: For all $a, b \in G$, $a \cdot b \in G$.
2. **Associativity**: For all $a, b, c \in G$, $(a \cdot b) \cdot c = a \cdot (b \cdot c)$.
3. **Identity**: There exists an element $e \in G$ (the identity) such that for all $a \in G$, $a \cdot e = e \cdot a = a$.
4. **Inverses**: For every $a \in G$ there exists $a^{-1} \in G$ such that $a \cdot a^{-1} = a^{-1} \cdot a = e$.

The operation is often written multiplicatively, but it can be additive (as in $(\mathbb{Z}, +)$). The identity is then $0$ and the inverse of $a$ is $-a$.

Note that we do **not** require commutativity ($a \cdot b = b \cdot a$). If the operation is commutative, the group is called **abelian**.

**ELI5**

- Imagine a set of dance moves that you can combine. Closure says any two moves produce another valid move. Associativity says the order of combining three moves doesn't matter for the final position. Identity is the "do nothing" move. Inverses mean every move has an "undo" move that brings you back to start.
- Groups describe "things you can do and then undo" while staying inside the allowed actions. The axioms guarantee you never get stuck or produce nonsense.

### Worked Examples

**Worked Example:**

**Verifying the group axioms for $(\mathbb{Z}, +)$**

1. Closure: sum of two integers is an integer.
2. Associativity: addition of numbers is associative.
3. Identity: $0$, since $n + 0 = n$.
4. Inverse: $-n$, since $n + (-n) = 0$.

**Verifying $(\mathbb{Z}, \cdot)$ is not a group**

Multiplication on all integers fails inverses: the only elements with multiplicative inverses in $\mathbb{Z}$ are $\pm 1$. Most integers have no inverse inside $\mathbb{Z}$.

## Common examples of groups {#examples}

Familiar groups you will meet constantly:

- $(\mathbb{Z}, +)$, $(\mathbb{Q}, +)$, $(\mathbb{R}, +)$, $(\mathbb{C}, +)$ — infinite abelian groups.
- $(\mathbb{Z}/n\mathbb{Z}, +)$ — the integers modulo $n$, cyclic of order $n$.
- $(\mathbb{Z}/n\mathbb{Z})^\times$ — the multiplicative group of units modulo $n$ (elements coprime to $n$). Order $\phi(n)$.
- $S_n$ — the symmetric group of all permutations of $n$ elements, order $n!$, non-abelian for $n \geq 3$.
- $GL_n(\mathbb{R})$ — the general linear group of invertible $n \times n$ real matrices under multiplication.
- The Klein four-group $V_4 = \{e, a, b, c\}$ with all non-identity elements order 2.
- Any vector space over a field, with operation vector addition (the additive group of the vector space).

**ELI5**

- The integers under addition form a group because you can always add, the order of adding doesn't matter, zero does nothing, and every number has a negative that cancels it.
- Permutations of three objects (like rotating and flipping a triangle) form the smallest non-abelian group $S_3$. Swapping A then B is different from B then A.

### Worked Examples

**Worked Example:**

**The group $\mathbb{Z}/6\mathbb{Z}$ under addition**

Elements: $\{0,1,2,3,4,5\}$. Operation: addition mod 6.

Identity: 0. Inverse of 1 is 5 (1+5=6≡0), inverse of 2 is 4, etc. It is cyclic, generated by 1.

## Abelian groups {#abelian}

A group is **abelian** (or commutative) if $ab = ba$ for all $a,b \in G$.

Most number-based groups are abelian. Symmetric groups $S_n$ for $n\geq 3$ and matrix groups are the classic non-abelian examples.

All cyclic groups (see next) are abelian. The converse is false: the Klein four-group is abelian but not cyclic.

**ELI5**

- In an abelian group the order of operations never matters — like adding numbers. In non-abelian groups, sequence matters — like putting on socks and shoes (different from shoes then socks).

## Cyclic groups {#cyclic}

A group $G$ is **cyclic** if there exists $g \in G$ such that every element of $G$ can be written as a power (or multiple) of $g$. We write $G = \langle g \rangle$.

- $\mathbb{Z}$ is cyclic, generated by $1$ (or $-1$).
- $\mathbb{Z}/n\mathbb{Z}$ is cyclic of order $n$, generated by $1 \bmod n$.
- For $n \geq 3$, $S_n$ is not cyclic.

If $G$ is finite cyclic of order $n$, then for each divisor $d$ of $n$ there is exactly one subgroup of order $d$, and it is also cyclic.

**ELI5**

- A cyclic group is like a clock: everything is just repeated steps of a single basic move (the generator). After $n$ steps you are back where you started.

### Worked Examples

**Worked Example:**

**Generators of $\mathbb{Z}/12\mathbb{Z}$**

The generators are the elements coprime to 12: 1,5,7,11.

$\langle 1 \rangle = \{0,1,2,\dots,11\}$

$\langle 5 \rangle = \{0,5,10,3,8,1,6,11,4,9,2,7\}$ (full group).

$\langle 2 \rangle = \{0,2,4,6,8,10\}$ (only even numbers, proper subgroup).

## Order of elements and the group {#order}

The **order** of an element $g \in G$, written $|g|$ or $o(g)$, is the smallest positive integer $k$ such that $g^k = e$ (if it exists; otherwise infinite).

The **order of the group** $|G|$ is the number of elements (cardinality).

Key facts:

- If $|g| = k$, then $g^m = e$ iff $k$ divides $m$.
- In a finite group, the order of every element divides the order of the group (Lagrange's theorem, proved via subgroups).
- In $\mathbb{Z}/n\mathbb{Z}$ under addition, order of $a$ is $n / \gcd(a,n)$.

**ELI5**

- Order of an element tells you after how many repeated applications of that "move" you return to the starting point. On a clock with 12 hours, adding 3 each time: 4 steps bring you back (order 4).

### Worked Examples

**Worked Example:**

**Orders in $\mathbb{Z}/8\mathbb{Z}$ under +**

- Order of 1: 8 (1+1+... 8 times =0 mod 8)
- Order of 2: 4 (2+2+2+2=8≡0)
- Order of 4: 2
- Order of 0: 1 (the identity)

## Common Mistakes

- Forgetting that the identity must work on both sides (left and right). In groups it automatically follows from the other axioms that left and right identities coincide, but you must still verify both in examples.
- Assuming every group is abelian. Symmetric groups $S_n$ ($n \geq 3$) provide counter-examples (e.g. $S_3$).
- Confusing the order of an element with the order of the group.
- Thinking the operation must be the usual + or ×. The operation can be function composition, matrix multiplication, or even "exclusive or" on bit strings.
- Forgetting to check closure when verifying a subset is a group (though for subgroups there is a shortcut test).
