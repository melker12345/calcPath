# Tychonoff's theorem

Tychonoff's theorem asserts that an arbitrary (possibly uncountable) product of compact topological spaces is again compact in the product topology.

It is one of the most powerful and widely used theorems in topology and is equivalent to the axiom of choice over ZF.

## The Statement

<!-- section: statement-ty -->

Let $\{X_i\}_{i \in I}$ be any family of compact topological spaces. Then the product space
\[
\prod_{i \in I} X_i
\]
equipped with the product topology is compact.

The product topology is the coarsest topology making all the projection maps $\pi_j : \prod X_i \to X_j$ continuous. Its basic open sets are finite intersections of sets of the form $\pi_j^{-1}(U_j)$ where $U_j$ open in $X_j$.

**ELI5**

- Each factor is "small" in the sense that any cover by open sets has a finite subcover.
- When you take the product (all possible combinations of points, one from each factor), the theorem says the whole thing is still "small" in the same sense, provided you use the product topology (which only looks at finitely many coordinates at a time for basic open sets).

**Worked Example:**

The unit interval $[0,1]$ is compact. By Tychonoff the countable product $[0,1]^\mathbb{N}$ (the Hilbert cube) is compact in the product topology. Likewise $\{0,1\}^\mathbb{R}$ (all functions from reals to {0,1}) is compact, etc.

## Proof Ideas

<!-- section: proof-ideas -->

There are several proofs, all ultimately relying on some form of the axiom of choice.

One standard proof uses Zorn's lemma on the poset of families of closed sets that have the finite intersection property and contain the complements of the open sets from a given cover; a maximal such family must have nonempty total intersection (by compactness of the factors), yielding a point in every set of the cover, a contradiction.

Another approach uses the ultrafilter lemma (equivalent to the Boolean prime ideal theorem) or Alexander's subbase theorem to reduce the problem to subbasic covers.

**ELI5**

- You assume you have a cover of the product with no finite subcover. You build a "maximal" collection of "bad" closed sets that still has the finite intersection property. By the compactness of each coordinate you can find a point that lies in all of them, which would have to be covered, contradiction.
- Choice is used to guarantee the existence of the maximal collection or the ultrafilter.

**Worked Example:**

Suppose $\{U_\alpha\}$ is an open cover of $\prod X_i$ with no finite subcover. Consider the complements $F_\alpha = \prod X_i \setminus U_\alpha$. The family of all finite intersections of the $F_\alpha$ that are nonempty has the finite intersection property. Extend to a maximal family with this property (Zorn). Projecting to each coordinate and using compactness of $X_i$ produces a point whose every neighborhood meets the family, hence the point lies in every $F_\alpha$, contradicting that the $U_\alpha$ cover.

## Importance and Applications

<!-- section: importance -->

Tychonoff is the key tool that makes the product topology useful for compactness arguments.

Classic applications:
- The Hilbert cube is compact.
- Stone–Čech compactification exists (as a closed subset of a product of intervals).
- Alaoglu's theorem (unit ball of the dual of a Banach space is weak*-compact) — the dual ball is a product of intervals.
- Many universal spaces in topology are compact products or closed subsets thereof.

**ELI5**

- Once you know arbitrary products of compacts are compact, you can embed or represent many complicated objects as subsets of "big but still compact" products, and then use compactness to extract limits, fixed points, etc.

**Worked Example:**

To construct the Stone–Čech compactification $\beta X$ of a discrete space $X$, embed $X$ into the compact product $[0,1]^{C_b(X)}$ (all bounded continuous functions to [0,1]) via evaluation maps, then take the closure. Tychonoff supplies the compactness of the big product.

## Weaker Versions and Choice Strength

<!-- section: weaker-versions -->

The finite Tychonoff theorem (finite products of compacts are compact) is provable in ZF with no choice.

The countable Tychonoff theorem requires some countable choice (or DC) but is strictly weaker than full AC.

The full Tychonoff theorem for arbitrary products is equivalent to AC.

For compact Hausdorff spaces, Tychonoff is equivalent to the Boolean prime ideal theorem (BPI), which is weaker than full AC but still not provable in ZF.

**ELI5**

- For two or three compact spaces you can prove the product is compact by hand (or with very little choice).
- For countably many you need to make countably many choices of finite subcovers.
- For uncountably many families you need the full strength of being able to choose from arbitrarily large collections.

**Worked Example:**

In models of ZF + ¬AC it is consistent that the product of two compact spaces is not compact, or that the countable product of copies of {0,1} (Cantor space) fails to be compact. This shows that even "obvious" compactness of products is not choice-free.

## Common Mistakes

- **Thinking Tychonoff is 'obvious' from the definition of compactness**: The product topology has many more open sets than the 'box' topology; covers can be very large and the finite subcover must be extracted using choice.
- **Confusing the product topology with the box topology**: In the box topology (all coordinates open at once) Tychonoff fails even for two spaces in some cases.
- **Assuming Tychonoff holds in ZF**: It does not; the full statement is equivalent to AC.
- **Forgetting that the theorem is for *arbitrary* products**: The countable case is much weaker and is what is usually needed in analysis.
- **Believing the theorem produces a 'constructive' compactness**: Like AC, the finite subcover whose existence is asserted may not be explicitly describable.
