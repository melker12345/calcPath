# Independence & Forcing

Independence results, forcing, proper forcing, inner models, and Gödel's constructible universe.

A statement φ is *independent* of a formal theory T (assumed consistent) if neither φ nor its negation ¬φ can be proved from the axioms of T.

In other words, both T + φ and T + ¬φ are consistent (relative to the consistency of T).

Independence results show that certain mathematical questions cannot be settled by a given list of axioms, no matter how long one works inside the theory.

## Definition and Basic Examples

<!-- section: definition-independence -->

By Gödel's completeness theorem, φ is independent of T if and only if there exists a model of T + φ and a model of T + ¬φ.

Classic examples in set theory:
- The continuum hypothesis (CH) is independent of ZFC.
- The axiom of choice (AC) is independent of ZF.
- The existence of a measurable cardinal is independent of ZFC (in the consistency-strength sense).

**ELI5**

- The axioms are like the rules of a game. Independence means that the rules do not force the answer to a particular question; both "yes" and "no" are still possible without breaking the rules.
- You need a bigger or different rulebook (extra axioms) to decide the question.

**Worked Example:**

ZFC does not prove CH and does not prove ¬CH. There are models of ZFC + CH (Gödel's L) and models of ZFC + ¬CH (Cohen's forcing extensions). Hence CH is independent of ZFC.

## Methods for Proving Independence

<!-- section: methods-for-independence -->

The two main techniques are:
1. *Inner models*: Construct a definable sub-universe (such as Gödel's constructible universe L) inside any model of the base theory, in which the statement holds (or fails).
2. *Forcing / outer models*: Starting from a model M of the base theory, adjoin new generic objects via a poset to produce an extension M[G] in which the statement holds (or fails) while the base theory remains true.

For statements of high consistency strength (large cardinals), one also uses *consistency-strength comparisons*: showing that the statement implies Con(T + ψ) for some ψ, while the negation does not, or vice versa.

**ELI5**

- Inner models are like "looking inside the current mathematical universe for a smaller, better-behaved sub-universe where the statement becomes true."
- Forcing is like "carefully adding new objects from outside to make the statement true, without breaking the old rules."

**Worked Example:**

To show Con(ZF) ⇒ Con(ZF + ¬AC): start with a model of ZF (or ZFC), use a symmetric extension or permutation model in which there is a countable collection of pairwise disjoint nonempty sets with no choice function. The resulting model satisfies ZF + "there is a Dedekind-finite infinite set" (hence ¬AC).

## Examples in Set Theory

<!-- section: examples-in-set-theory -->

Besides CH and AC, independence abounds:
- Suslin's hypothesis (there is no Suslin tree) is independent of ZFC.
- The Whitehead problem (every Whitehead group is free) is independent.
- Many statements about the structure of the real line under measure or category (e.g., "all sets of reals have the property of Baire") are independent.
- The existence of 0^# (a real coding truth about L) is independent of ZFC; its existence implies that L is not the whole universe in a strong sense.
- Large cardinal axioms are independent of ZFC in the sense that their consistency strength strictly exceeds that of ZFC.

**ELI5**

- Set theory is extremely rich; once you have the basic rules for sets, there is still enormous freedom in how "wide" or "tall" the universe of sets is allowed to be.
- Different choices produce different answers to questions about the continuum, about trees, about definable sets of reals, etc.

**Worked Example:**

The existence of a measurable cardinal implies that 0^# exists and that every uncountable cardinal in L is inaccessible, Mahlo, etc., in L. If there is no measurable, then in L there may be no such large cardinals. The statement "there is a measurable" is therefore independent of ZFC + "there is no measurable" in a strong consistency-strength sense.

## Philosophical Significance

<!-- section: philosophical-significance -->

Independence results, especially for "natural" mathematical statements such as CH, have been interpreted in several ways:
- The ZFC axioms are incomplete; we need to adopt additional axioms (large cardinals, forcing axioms, determinacy, etc.) to decide them.
- There may be no single "true" universe of sets; different legitimate universes give different answers.
- Some questions (especially those of very high consistency strength) may remain permanently undecided by any "reasonable" extension of ZFC.

Gödel himself believed that CH is false and that new axioms (large cardinals) would eventually decide it negatively. Modern research has produced many candidates for such axioms, some implying CH, others implying ¬CH.

**ELI5**

- Independence shows that the current rulebook is not powerful enough to answer every question we can meaningfully ask.
- Mathematicians then debate which extra rules to add. Different extra rules give different answers and different mathematics downstream.

**Worked Example:**

Large cardinals (measurable, Woodin, supercompact, etc.) decide the truth value of many statements about projective sets of reals (projective determinacy) and about the fine structure of L. Forcing axioms such as PFA decide that 2^ℵ₀ = ℵ₂ and that many combinatorial objects on ω₁ do not exist. Both are "natural" in different communities and decide CH differently.

## Common Mistakes

- **Thinking that independence means 'we can never know the answer'**: It means only that the *current* axioms do not decide it. Stronger axioms often do decide it.
- **Confusing independence with undecidability in the sense of Gödel's incompleteness for arithmetic**: Independence in set theory is usually proved by exhibiting models on both sides, not merely by a diagonal argument.
- **Believing that if a statement is independent then both it and its negation are 'equally true'**: In practice one may have strong reasons (from inner-model theory, from maximality considerations, from consequences for other fields) to prefer one side.
- **Assuming every interesting statement is independent of ZFC**: Many statements *are* decided by ZFC (e.g., the basic properties of ordinals and cardinals, the existence of non-constructible reals if 0^# exists is decided in certain ways, etc.).
- **Forgetting consistency-strength hierarchies**: "Con(ZFC + φ)" can be strictly stronger than "Con(ZFC)"; independence in that case is not symmetric.

Forcing is a powerful technique invented by Paul Cohen in 1963 to prove independence results in set theory. It allows one to start from a model of ZFC and adjoin new "generic" sets in a controlled way, producing a larger model in which previously undecidable statements become true or false.

Cohen used forcing to show that the continuum hypothesis and the axiom of choice are independent of ZF (more precisely, that ¬CH and ¬AC are consistent with ZF, assuming ZF itself is consistent).

## What Forcing Is

<!-- section: what-is-forcing -->

Given a ground model M of (a fragment of) ZFC and a partial order (poset) P ∈ M that M thinks is a forcing notion, one constructs a *generic filter* G ⊆ P that is "generic over M" (intersects every dense subset of P that belongs to M).

One then builds the *generic extension* M[G] consisting of all sets that can be "named" by P-names in M and interpreted using G.

The extension M[G] is again a model of ZFC (if M was), and it contains new sets (the generic object decoded from G) while preserving a great deal of the structure of M.

**ELI5**

- Think of P as a collection of "partial descriptions" or "promises" about a new object you want to add (e.g., finite pieces of a new real number).
- A generic G is an infinite coherent collection of these promises that "answers every question" that the ground model M can ask (by meeting all dense sets M can define).
- M[G] is the universe you get by adding the object described by G and closing under the set-theoretic operations that M already knew.

**Worked Example:**

The simplest forcing: Cohen forcing to add one new real. Let P be the set of all finite functions from ω to 2 (finite binary sequences), ordered by extension. A generic filter G corresponds to an infinite binary sequence (the union of the conditions in G) that is not in the ground model M. In M[G] there is a new subset of ω (the Cohen real).

## Posets and Generic Filters

<!-- section: posets-and-generics -->

A poset P is a partial order (usually assumed separative or with a maximum element 1). Stronger conditions give more information.

A filter G on P is *M-generic* if for every dense D ⊆ P with D ∈ M we have G ∩ D ≠ ∅.

Density means that below any condition there is a stronger condition in D; genericity says G "decides" every question M can pose.

**ELI5**

- Conditions are like "finite approximations to the object being added."
- Dense sets are "requirements" or "questions" that M can express ("make sure the real is not equal to this particular old real", "make sure this ordinal is collapsed or preserved", etc.).
- Genericity guarantees every requirement is eventually satisfied.

**Worked Example:**

In Cohen forcing, the dense set D_r = {p ∈ P | p is incompatible with the characteristic function of a particular ground-model real r} forces the generic real to differ from r at some point. Genericity ensures the generic real differs from every ground-model real, hence is new.

## Using Forcing to Prove Independence

<!-- section: forcing-to-prove-independence -->

To show that a statement φ is not provable from ZFC (assuming consistency):
- Start with a model M ⊨ ZFC.
- Find a poset P ∈ M such that M thinks "there is a condition that forces ¬φ" (or that in M[G] we will have ¬φ).
- Show that the forcing preserves enough of ZFC (or at least the consistency strength) so that M[G] ⊨ ZFC + ¬φ.

Cohen's original argument for ¬CH: force with a poset that adds ℵ₂ many new reals (or a product/iteration that makes 2^ℵ₀ ≥ ℵ₂) while preserving cardinals (using ccc posets). In the extension the old ℵ₁ is still uncountable but there are at least ℵ₂ many reals.

For ¬AC one uses symmetric extensions or permutation models (or class forcing) in which a Dedekind-finite infinite set of reals or a countable family of pairs without a choice function appears.

**ELI5**

- You design a "recipe" (the poset) for adding exactly the kind of new object that will make the statement you want to be independent become true (or false).
- You prove that the recipe does not destroy the axioms of set theory and does not accidentally make the statement true in the old model.

**Worked Example:**

To make 2^ℵ₀ = ℵ₂ (or larger), use the finite-support product of ℵ₂ many copies of Cohen forcing (or the Lévy collapse, or random forcing, etc.). The ccc property ensures no cardinals are collapsed, so the old ℵ₁ remains ℵ₁ in the extension, but many new reals are added, pushing the continuum up.

## Preservation Theorems

<!-- section: preservation-theorems -->

A central part of forcing technology is proving that certain posets preserve cardinals, cofinalities, or other properties:
- ccc (countable chain condition) posets preserve all cardinals and cofinalities ≥ ℵ₁.
- Proper posets preserve ℵ₁ and stationary subsets of ω₁.
- More generally, one classifies posets by how much they destroy (or preserve) of the ground model.

The forcing relation p ⊩ φ ("p forces φ") is defined in the ground model by a recursive definition and is the key tool for proving what will be true in the extension without having the generic yet.

**ELI5**

- Not every "recipe" for adding new sets is harmless; some posets collapse cardinals or destroy stationary sets.
- Preservation theorems tell you which posets are safe for which purposes (e.g., "if you only care about not collapsing ℵ₁, you may use any proper poset").

**Worked Example:**

A poset is ccc if it has no uncountable antichain. Cohen forcing is ccc. By a delta-system argument, any uncountable set of conditions has two compatible ones, so no new countable sequence of ground-model ordinals can be added; hence ℵ₁ is preserved.

## Common Mistakes

- **Thinking forcing 'adds' sets directly to the true universe V**: Forcing is a relative consistency technique inside a model M; the extension M[G] is another model, not necessarily a subclass of the true V.
- **Confusing the poset with the generic object**: The poset P lives in the ground model and describes possible approximations; the generic G is the actual infinite coherent object (usually not in M).
- **Believing every forcing destroys cardinals**: Many important posets (ccc, proper, semi-proper) are carefully designed *not* to collapse cardinals or to collapse only specific ones.
- **Forgetting the definability of the forcing relation**: All the combinatorial work (density arguments, preservation) is done inside the ground model using the definable relation ⊩; you never need the generic to be present to reason about what it will force.
- **Assuming forcing is the only way to obtain independence**: There are other methods (inner models, large cardinals, symmetric extensions, etc.), but forcing is the most flexible for "making the continuum large" or "adding counterexamples to choice."

*Proper forcing* is a class of partial orders introduced by Shelah that is large enough to include many useful posets (including all ccc and all countably closed posets) while still preserving ω₁ and stationary subsets of ω₁ in a controlled way.

The Proper Forcing Axiom (PFA) is the natural forcing axiom for this class at the level of ℵ₁ many dense sets. It has extremely strong consequences, including 2^ℵ₀ = ℵ₂ and the failure of square principles at all cardinals.

Proper forcing and PFA form one of the central pillars of modern set theory of the reals and of ω₁.

## Definition of Properness

<!-- section: what-is-proper -->

A poset P is *proper* if for all sufficiently large regular θ, for every countable elementary submodel N ≺ H_θ with P ∈ N, and for every p ∈ P ∩ N, there exists q ≤ p such that q is (N, P)-generic: for every dense set D ∈ N, q forces that the generic filter intersects D (i.e., the interpretation of the name for the generic meets D ∩ N).

Equivalently (by a standard characterization), P does not collapse ω₁ when forced over any countable transitive model containing it.

Properness is preserved by countable support iterations (under mild conditions), which makes it a very useful iteration class.

**ELI5**

- Countable elementary submodels N are like "countable approximations to the universe" that contain the poset.
- A master condition q for N is a condition below p that "promises" that the generic will be generic over N — it will meet all the dense sets that N thinks are dense.
- Proper posets are those for which you can always find such master conditions; they respect countable models without collapsing ω₁.

**Worked Example:**

Any ccc poset is proper (the master condition can be p itself, because antichains are countable and N sees them all). Any countably closed poset is proper (you can build a descending sequence meeting all the countably many dense sets in N below p). Many tree forcings, Namba forcing (under CH or not), and club-shooting posets are proper or semiproper.

## Proper Forcing Axiom (PFA)

<!-- section: proper-forcing-axiom -->

PFA: For every proper poset P and every collection of ℵ₁ many dense subsets of P, there exists a filter G ⊆ P that intersects all of them.

PFA is a very strong forcing axiom. It implies MA (since ccc posets are proper) and much more.

**ELI5**

- Instead of only ccc posets (as in MA), PFA lets you find filters generic for ℵ₁ many dense sets in *any* proper poset.
- This is a powerful "maximality" principle: the universe is closed under generic constructions coming from a very large class of posets, as long as you only have ℵ₁ many requirements.

**Worked Example:**

The poset for shooting a club through a fat stationary set on ω₁ (or for threading a square sequence) can be made proper. PFA applied to it and ℵ₁ dense sets produces the club or thread, which can be used to derive contradictions with square or to force reflection.

## Major Consequences of PFA

<!-- section: consequences-of-pfa -->

PFA has dramatic global effects:
- 2^ℵ₀ = ℵ₂.
- Failure of □_κ for all κ (no square sequences at any cardinal).
- NS_ω₁ is ℵ₂-saturated.
- Every stationary subset of ω₁ reflects or is killed in a controlled way; many combinatorial principles on ω₁ fail.
- The non-stationary ideal on ω₁ is precipitous, etc.
- Many consequences for topology, algebra, and analysis on uncountable objects (e.g., no S-spaces or L-spaces under certain strengthenings, automatic continuity results, etc.).

The proof that PFA implies 2^ℵ₀ = ℵ₂ usually proceeds by showing that if the continuum were larger, there would be a proper poset (a club-shooting or collapse poset) whose application under PFA would force the continuum down to ℵ₂, a contradiction.

**ELI5**

- PFA is so strong that it pins down the exact size of the continuum and destroys most "pathological" combinatorial objects on ω₁ that can exist under CH or in L.
- It is one of the strongest "anti-CH" principles that is still consistent (relative to a supercompact cardinal).

**Worked Example:**

Suppose 2^ℵ₀ > ℵ₂. Then there is a stationary costationary set S ⊆ ω₁ that codes (in a suitable way) a collapse of all cardinals between ℵ₁ and the continuum down to ℵ₁. There is a proper poset that shoots a club through S. PFA applied to this poset and ℵ₁ many dense sets (one for each possible name or dense set in the bookkeeping) produces the club, which in turn forces the collapse, contradicting that the continuum was larger than ℵ₂. Hence PFA ⇒ 2^ℵ₀ = ℵ₂.

## Proper versus Semiproper Forcing

<!-- section: proper-vs-semiproper -->

*Semiproper* posets are a larger class: they preserve stationary subsets of ω₁ (or at least do not collapse ω₁) but may collapse ω₂ or higher.

The semiproper forcing axiom (SPFA) is the analogue of PFA for semiproper posets. It also implies 2^ℵ₀ = ℵ₂ and is consistent relative to a supercompact cardinal (by revised countable support iterations).

Many important posets (Namba forcing, Prikry-type forcings, certain Radin forcings) are semiproper but not proper.

The distinction is important for preservation theorems in iterated forcing.

**ELI5**

- Proper posets are "very respectful" of countable models and preserve a lot.
- Semiproper posets are a bit wilder; they may change ω₂ but still keep ω₁ from collapsing.
- SPFA is the corresponding maximality principle for this larger class and has similar strong global effects.

**Worked Example:**

Namba forcing (changing the cofinality of ℵ₂ to ω while preserving ℵ₁) is semiproper but not proper. SPFA applied to it can be used to force the failure of CH or to thread squares at ℵ₂, etc.

## Consistency Strength

<!-- section: role-modern -->

The consistency of PFA (or SPFA) requires a supercompact cardinal (roughly):
- If there is a supercompact, one can force PFA by a (revised) countable support iteration of length the supercompact, preserving enough supercompactness to apply the axiom.
- Conversely, PFA implies (via core model theory or the existence of certain inner models with Woodin cardinals or stronger) the consistency of ZFC + "there is a supercompact cardinal."

Thus PFA sits at a high but precisely calibrated level in the consistency-strength hierarchy.

**ELI5**

- PFA is not a "mild" axiom like MA; it has substantial large-cardinal strength.
- The supercompact is used to "prepare" the universe so that the iteration that forces PFA does not destroy the very cardinals needed to carry out the bookkeeping and preservation.

**Worked Example:**

The standard consistency proof uses a Laver diamond or a master condition preparation at a supercompact κ to iterate all proper posets of size < κ while preserving the supercompactness of κ long enough that at the end one can argue that the generic extension satisfies PFA (because any proper poset in the extension was considered at some stage, and the master conditions ensure the generics were added).

## Role in Modern Set Theory

<!-- section: proper-vs-semiproper -->

Proper and semiproper forcing, together with their associated axioms PFA/SPFA and Martin's Maximum, are the main tools for obtaining "maximal" or "saturated" combinatorial behavior at ω₁ while controlling the continuum.

They compete with (and are often incompatible with) very large cardinals in inner models, but are compatible with moderate large cardinals.

They have settled many classical questions from topology, algebra, and infinitary combinatorics (existence of Countryman lines, automatic continuity of homomorphisms, reflection principles, etc.).

**ELI5**

- If you want the continuum to be ℵ₂ and you want almost no "bad" combinatorial objects on ω₁, PFA or SPFA is the axiom to assume.
- The price is a supercompact in the background for consistency, and the loss of some very large cardinals that would contradict the forcing axiom.

**Worked Example:**

Under PFA there are no ℵ₁-Aronszajn trees that are special in certain ways, no square sequences, the non-stationary ideal is saturated, every two ℵ₁-dense ideals on ω₁ are isomorphic after forcing, etc. These are theorems that calibrate exactly what "ω₁ combinatorics looks like" when the universe is maximal with respect to proper forcing.

## Common Mistakes

- **Confusing proper with ccc**: All ccc posets are proper, but many proper posets are not ccc (e.g., Namba forcing under CH, club shooting through fat stationary sets).
- **Thinking PFA is consistent relative to ZFC alone**: It requires a supercompact for the upper bound; the lower bound is also high (inner models with Woodins or supercompacts).
- **Assuming PFA decides everything**: It decides the continuum (ℵ₂) and kills square, but many questions about higher cardinals or about arbitrary sets of reals remain open or require stronger axioms.
- **Forgetting that properness is preserved by countable support iterations**: This is the key technical fact that makes iterated proper forcing possible and is what allows the consistency proofs.
- **Believing that semiproper is the same as proper**: Semiproper is strictly larger; the difference appears when one wants to change cofinalities of ℵ₂ or higher while preserving ω₁.

An *inner model* is a transitive class M ⊆ V that contains all the ordinals and satisfies (at least) the axioms of ZF. Inner models are "universes inside the universe" that are well-behaved and often satisfy extra axioms (AC, GCH, large cardinals) that the full universe V may not satisfy.

They are the primary tool for proving relative consistency results and for measuring the "distance" between V and L-like universes.

## Definition and Basic Properties

<!-- section: what-is-inner-model -->

A class M is an inner model of ZF if:
- M is transitive (x ∈ y ∈ M ⇒ x ∈ M),
- Ord ⊆ M (M contains every ordinal),
- (M, ∈) ⊨ ZF.

If M also satisfies the axiom of choice we say it is an inner model of ZFC.

Because M is transitive, the ordinals and the membership relation are computed absolutely: α is an ordinal in M iff it is an ordinal in V, and x ∈^M y iff x ∈ y.

**ELI5**

- An inner model is like a "sub-universe" that is closed under the set-theoretic operations and sees the same ordinals you do.
- Because it is transitive it does not have "fake" ordinals or wrong membership; it is an honest initial segment of the true universe in the membership sense.

**Worked Example:**

The constructible universe L is the smallest inner model of ZFC: if M is any inner model of ZF then L ⊆ M. This is because the definition of "constructible" only uses definability, which any model of ZF is closed under.

## The Constructible Universe L as the Prototypical Inner Model

<!-- section: examples-of-inner-models -->

L is built by iterating the definable power set operation along the ordinals. It always satisfies ZFC + GCH + V = L (inside itself).

Other canonical examples:
- L[U] for a normal measure U on a measurable cardinal (models ZFC + "there is a measurable").
- HOD, the class of hereditarily ordinal-definable sets (always models ZFC, even if V ⊨ ZF + ¬AC).
- Core models K (Jensen, Dodd-Jensen, Mitchell-Steel, etc.) that attempt to capture as much of V as possible while retaining fine structure and a definable well-order.

**ELI5**

- L is the "minimal" inner model: it contains only what you are forced to add by explicit definitions from the ordinals.
- HOD is the "definable from ordinals" part of V; it is always as nice as L (has AC and GCH in many cases) even when V is not.
- Core models are "the L of V" relative to the large cardinals that exist in V.

**Worked Example:**

Even if V ⊨ ZF + "there is a Dedekind-finite infinite set" (hence ¬AC), the class HOD computed in V still satisfies ZFC + GCH. This shows that the failure of AC, if it occurs, must be witnessed by sets that are not hereditarily ordinal-definable.

## Uses in Relative Consistency

<!-- section: uses-in-consistency -->

Inner models are the standard vehicle for relative consistency proofs of the form:

"Con(T + φ) ⇒ Con(T + ψ)"

where T is usually ZFC or ZF.

One constructs (inside a model of T + φ) an inner model of T + ψ.

Classic example: Gödel showed that if M ⊨ ZF then L^M ⊨ ZFC + GCH. Hence Con(ZF) ⇒ Con(ZFC + GCH).

Another: if there is a measurable cardinal then there is an inner model L[U] with a measurable cardinal, and in L[U] there are many inaccessibles below it. Hence the existence of a measurable implies Con(ZFC + "there is a proper class of inaccessibles").

**ELI5**

- To show that a strong assumption φ does not lead to contradiction with a weaker theory, you build a "safe sub-universe" inside a model of φ that satisfies the weaker theory plus the desired conclusion ψ.
- The safer and more canonical the sub-universe, the stronger the consistency result.

**Worked Example:**

If κ is measurable in V with normal measure U, then the inner model L[U] satisfies "κ is measurable" and, by the reflection properties of the ultrapower embedding, L[U] thinks there are stationarily many inaccessibles below κ. Hence Con(ZFC + measurable) ⇒ Con(ZFC + "there is a proper class of inaccessibles").

## Core Models and Covering Lemmas

<!-- section: core-models-and-covering -->

When there are no inner models with very large cardinals, one can often prove that V is "close" to a canonical core model K (L, L[U], K^c, etc.).

Jensen's covering lemma (for L): if 0^# does not exist, then for every uncountable set X of ordinals there is a constructible Y ⊇ X with |Y| = |X|.

Modern generalizations (Mitchell-Steel, Schimmerling, etc.) give covering lemmas for core models below Woodin cardinals, with consequences such as the singular cardinals hypothesis (SCH), square principles, etc.

Failure of covering implies the existence of an inner model with a Woodin cardinal (or stronger).

**ELI5**

- If the universe is not too "fat" (no inner models with Woodins), then it cannot deviate too much from the constructible or core-model universe; every large set of ordinals is covered by a "nice" set from the core model of the same size.
- This lets one transfer combinatorial principles from L (or K) up to V.

**Worked Example:**

If there is no inner model with a Woodin cardinal, then the singular cardinals hypothesis holds: if κ is singular strong limit then 2^κ = κ^+. This follows from the covering lemma plus the fine structure of the core model.

## HOD and Other Definability-Based Inner Models

<!-- section: role-modern -->

HOD is always an inner model of ZFC. It is used to:
- Show that failures of choice, if they occur, must involve non-ordinal-definable sets.
- Study the "definable" part of the universe even when large cardinals are present (HOD may or may not contain the large cardinals).
- In the context of forcing axioms or determinacy, one studies HOD of L(ℝ) or of other models; often HOD of L(ℝ) under AD is a model of ZFC + "there is a Woodin cardinal."

Other definability notions (HOD of L(ℝ), generic HOD, etc.) are active areas of research connecting determinacy, large cardinals, and forcing.

**ELI5**

- HOD is "what you can define from ordinals." Even in a wild universe, the definable-from-ordinals part is always tame (satisfies choice).
- Under determinacy assumptions, the HOD of L(ℝ) turns out to be a very interesting inner model that itself contains large cardinals.

**Worked Example:**

Under AD^{L(ℝ)}, the model HOD^{L(ℝ)} satisfies ZFC + "there is a Woodin cardinal" (in fact a proper class of them, under stronger determinacy). This is a theorem of Woodin; it shows that determinacy produces inner models with large cardinals.

## Common Mistakes

- **Thinking every inner model is L**: L is the smallest; there are many others (L[U], HOD, core models, etc.) that can be larger and can satisfy stronger axioms.
- **Confusing inner models with forcing extensions**: Inner models are *subclasses* (usually proper) of V; forcing extensions are usually *larger* than the ground model.
- **Assuming that if M is an inner model then V ⊨ 'M ⊨ ZFC' is the same as M ⊨ ZFC**: Because M is transitive the satisfaction is absolute for most formulas, but one must be careful with class quantification and with parameters.
- **Forgetting that HOD always models ZFC**: This is true even when V does not; it is one of the most robust ways to recover choice inside ZF.
- **Believing that core models always exist**: Core models exist only below certain large cardinal thresholds; above Woodin cardinals (or supercompacts, depending on the technology) the core model theory is still incomplete. The non-existence of a core model is itself a strong statement (implies inner models with Woodins or stronger).

Gödel's constructible universe L is the smallest inner model of ZFC. It is built in a definable, "minimal" way from the ordinals by iteratively taking only the sets that are first-order definable over previous levels.

Inside L one can prove the axiom of choice and the generalized continuum hypothesis. Therefore the consistency of ZF implies the consistency of ZFC + GCH.

L is the prototype of an "L-like" inner model and remains central to modern set theory even though it cannot accommodate very large cardinals.

## Definition of L

<!-- section: definition-l -->

We define a cumulative hierarchy of constructible levels by transfinite recursion on ordinals:
- L₀ = ∅
- L_{α+1} = Def(L_α), the collection of all subsets of L_α that are definable over the structure (L_α, ∈) by a first-order formula with parameters from L_α.
- For limit λ, L_λ = ⋃_{α<λ} L_α.

Then L = ⋃_{α ∈ Ord} L_α.

A set is *constructible* if it belongs to L.

**ELI5**

- Start with nothing.
- At each successor stage, add only those new sets that you can explicitly define using a formula and parameters you already have.
- At limit stages, just collect everything built so far.
- L is the "minimal" universe you get by always adding only what you are forced to add by definitions.

**Worked Example:**

L_ω is the set of all hereditarily finite constructible sets (essentially HF, the same as V_ω). L_{ω+1} contains all subsets of ω that are definable over (L_ω, ∈), i.e., all arithmetic sets of naturals (in fact more, but still only countably many). L_{ω+2} contains definable subsets of those, and so on. By L_{ω₁} one already has all constructible reals.

## Why L Is an Inner Model

<!-- section: why-constructible -->

L is transitive (by construction), contains all ordinals, and satisfies all the axioms of ZF. Moreover, one can prove *inside L* that every set is constructible (the statement "V = L").

From V = L one derives both AC (there is a definable global well-order) and GCH (the number of definable subsets at each level is controlled by the size of the previous level).

**ELI5**

- L is "made of sets" in exactly the same way the true universe is, so it obeys the same rules (the ZF axioms).
- Inside L, the only sets that exist are the ones that were explicitly defined along the way; this minimality lets one count them carefully enough to prove GCH and to define a well-order.

**Worked Example:**

To see AC in L: the construction gives a definable well-order <^L of the entire class L (by ordering first by the stage α at which a set appears, then by the Gödel number of the defining formula, then by the parameters). Any nonempty constructible set therefore has a <^L-least element, giving a uniform choice function.

## Proof of GCH in L

<!-- section: gch-in-l -->

Gödel showed that if κ is infinite and constructible, then the number of constructible subsets of κ is at most κ⁺ (the next cardinal after κ in L).

The argument uses the fact that any constructible subset of L_α is definable over some L_β (β ≥ α) with parameters. By taking Skolem hulls or using condensation lemmas, one shows that the "witness" β need not be much larger than α; hence there are only |L_{α⁺}| many such subsets.

Thus in L we have 2^κ = κ⁺ for every infinite cardinal κ (GCH).

**ELI5**

- When you only add definable sets, you cannot add "too many" at each stage; the number of possible definitions is limited by the size of the level you are working over.
- This keeps the power set from jumping over the next cardinal, yielding GCH.

**Worked Example:**

For κ = ℵ₀, any constructible real appears by stage ω₁ (because a definition over a countable level can be coded by a real). There are only ℵ₁ many countable ordinals, hence only ℵ₁ many constructible reals in L. Thus 2^ℵ₀ = ℵ₁ in L.

## Limitations of L

<!-- section: limitations-of-l -->

L cannot contain very large cardinals that are consistent with ZFC. If there is a measurable cardinal in V, then 0^# exists and no cardinal remains measurable in L.

More generally, L is "thin": it has a definable well-order, satisfies GCH, and lacks the generic objects or embeddings that large cardinals or forcing axioms produce in V.

Modern inner-model theory attempts to build L-like models that can accommodate Woodin cardinals, supercompacts, etc., while retaining a definable well-order and (a form of) GCH. The "Ultimate L" program of Woodin aims at a canonical inner model compatible with all large cardinals that would decide many independent statements (including CH) in a canonical way.

**ELI5**

- L is the smallest possible universe; it is too "skinny" to contain the very large cardinals or the "fat" generic objects that forcing can add.
- If you believe in very large cardinals or in maximality principles, then L cannot be the whole true universe V.

**Worked Example:**

If κ is measurable in V with normal measure U, then in L the measure U ∩ L is not a measure on κ (because L is missing many subsets), and in fact 0^# exists, so there are non-constructible reals and L ≠ V.

## Role in Modern Set Theory

<!-- section: role-modern -->

Even when L ≠ V, the study of L and its generalizations (L[U], L[ℰ], hod mice, etc.) is central:
- They give lower bounds on consistency strength.
- They decide many statements about "small" sets (projective determinacy, etc.) when large cardinals exist.
- Condensation and covering lemmas for L-like models give powerful combinatorial information about V.

Gödel's construction remains the template for all inner-model theory.

**ELI5**

- L is the "core" or "skeleton" that is always present. Even if the true universe is much larger, the constructible sets inside it still behave nicely and can be used to prove theorems or to measure how much "extra stuff" exists outside L.

**Worked Example:**

Jensen's covering lemma says that if 0^# does not exist, then every uncountable set of ordinals in V is covered by a constructible set of the same size. This has many consequences for singular cardinals and stationary sets.

## Common Mistakes

- **Thinking L is the whole universe V**: L may be a proper subclass; 0^# or a measurable cardinal witnesses that V is larger than L.
- **Confusing L with the cumulative hierarchy V_α**: V_α uses the *full* power set at each stage; L uses only the *definable* power set.
- **Believing GCH holds in V because it holds in L**: L ⊨ GCH always (if it exists), but V may satisfy 2^ℵ₀ = anything consistent.
- **Assuming L contains all large cardinals**: Large cardinals "destroy" L in the sense that their existence implies L is properly smaller than V and that they are not large in L.
- **Forgetting that L is always a model of ZFC + GCH**: Even if you start from a model of ZF + ¬AC, the L computed inside it will still satisfy full ZFC + GCH.
