# Zorn's lemma

Zorn's lemma is a maximality principle equivalent to the axiom of choice. It says that a partially ordered set in which every chain has an upper bound must possess at least one maximal element.

It is one of the most frequently applied forms of choice in algebra, analysis, and topology because it turns "every ascending chain can be continued" into "there is a maximal object."

## The Statement

<!-- section: statement-zorn -->

Let $(P, \le)$ be a nonempty partially ordered set. A *chain* in $P$ is a totally ordered subset. An *upper bound* for a chain $C$ is an element $u \in P$ such that $c \le u$ for all $c \in C$.

Zorn's lemma: If every chain in $P$ has an upper bound in $P$, then $P$ has a maximal element (an element $m$ such that no element of $P$ is strictly greater than $m$).

**ELI5**

- Imagine a poset as a collection of boxes with "is contained in or equal" arrows.
- If whenever you have a totally ordered stack of boxes you can find a box that sits above the whole stack, then there must be at least one box that has nothing above it.

**Worked Example:**

Take $P$ to be the set of all proper ideals of a ring $R$, ordered by inclusion. The union of any chain of proper ideals is still proper (if it contained 1 then 1 would already be in some member of the chain). By Zorn there is a maximal proper ideal — a maximal ideal.

## Equivalence to the Axiom of Choice

<!-- section: equivalents-zorn -->

Over ZF, Zorn's lemma is equivalent to AC (and therefore to the well-ordering theorem, Tychonoff, "every vector space has a basis," etc.).

One direction: from AC well-order the poset and build an ascending chain by transfinite recursion, adding the least possible extension at each stage; it must stop at a maximal element.

The converse: given a family of nonempty sets, consider the poset of partial choice functions ordered by extension; chains have upper bounds (unions); a maximal one must be a total choice function.

**ELI5**

- AC lets you pick; Zorn lets you keep picking "as far as possible" until you cannot pick any more; the "farthest" point is maximal.
- The two are interchangeable in proofs.

**Worked Example:**

To derive AC from Zorn: let $X$ be a set of nonempty sets. Let $P$ be the set of all functions $f$ whose domain is a subset of $X$ and $f(A) \in A$ for $A$ in the domain. Order $P$ by extension. Any chain has upper bound the union function. A maximal $f$ must have domain all of $X$, otherwise pick an element from a missed set and extend.

## Typical Applications

<!-- section: typical-uses -->

- Every vector space has a basis (poset of linearly independent subsets).
- Every ring has a maximal ideal (poset of proper ideals).
- Every field has an algebraic closure (poset of algebraic extensions).
- Hahn-Banach theorem (poset of partial linear extensions of a functional that stay dominated by a seminorm).
- Existence of ultrafilters (poset of proper filters).

In each case one verifies the chain-upper-bound hypothesis and concludes a maximal object with the desired property.

**ELI5**

- "Keep adding stuff that is still independent / proper / dominated / filter-like until you cannot add any more." The thing you end up with is maximal and therefore has the strong property you want (basis, maximal ideal, ultrafilter...).

**Worked Example:**

For Hahn-Banach: start with a linear functional defined on a subspace that is bounded by a seminorm p. The poset of all linear extensions still bounded by p. Union of a chain of such extensions is still linear and bounded by p. A maximal extension must be defined on the whole space (otherwise extend by one more dimension using the usual one-dimensional Hahn-Banach lemma).

## Proof Ideas and Why the Hypothesis Is Necessary

<!-- section: proof-ideas -->

The usual proof from AC uses a well-ordering of $P$ and transfinite recursion to build a chain that cannot be properly extended.

The hypothesis is essential: the poset of all finite subsets of an infinite set, ordered by inclusion, has the property that every chain has an upper bound that is still finite, yet it has no maximal element (you can always add one more point).

**ELI5**

- Without the "every chain can be bounded" rule you can have infinite ascending staircases that never reach a top floor; the lemma would fail.
- The hypothesis guarantees that any staircase you climb has a landing you can step onto, so eventually you must reach a floor with no higher floor.

**Worked Example:**

In the finite-subsets poset, the chain $\emptyset \subset \{a_1\} \subset \{a_1,a_2\} \subset \dots$ (enumerating the infinite ground set) has upper bounds that are still finite (any finite initial segment), but you can keep going forever; there is no maximal finite set.

## Common Misunderstandings

<!-- section: common-misunderstandings -->

Some frequent mistakes when applying or recalling Zorn's lemma for the first time.

## Common Mistakes

- **Stating Zorn as 'every poset has a maximal element'**: Counterexamples are trivial (any poset with no maximal element, e.g., the integers under $<$ or the finite-subsets example).
- **Forgetting to verify the chain-upper-bound hypothesis**: Many students apply Zorn to a poset where some chains have no upper bound inside the poset.
- **Confusing maximal with maximum**: A maximal element need not be above everything; it just has nothing above *it*. A maximum (greatest element) is above everything.
- **Thinking Zorn is weaker or stronger than AC**: They are equivalent over ZF.
- **Using Zorn when a constructive or choice-free proof exists**: Many results that 'look like' they need Zorn can be proved with weaker choice or even in ZF (e.g., for countable objects).
