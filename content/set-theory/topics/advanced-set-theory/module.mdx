# Advanced Set Theory

Descriptive set theory, large cardinals, and Martin's axiom.

Descriptive set theory is the study of *definable* sets of real numbers (and, more generally, of Polish spaces) and the regularity properties they possess with respect to Lebesgue measure, Baire category, perfect subsets, and other notions.

It sits at the intersection of set theory, topology, measure theory, and recursion theory. Its classical results (Borel, analytic, and co-analytic sets) are provable in ZFC; higher levels of definability (projective sets, sets in L(ℝ)) lead to independence phenomena that are resolved by large cardinals or determinacy axioms.

## What "Definable" Means

<!-- section: what-is-descriptive -->

The basic pointclasses are:
- **Open** and **closed** sets.
- **Borel** sets: the σ-algebra generated by the open sets (closed under countable unions and complements).
- **Analytic** (Σ¹₁) sets: continuous images of Borel sets, or projections of Borel sets in the product space.
- **Projective** sets: the smallest pointclass containing the Borel sets and closed under projection (∃^ℝ) and complement.

One also studies sets definable from ordinals and reals (in L(ℝ), or boldface pointclasses with real parameters).

**ELI5**

- "Definable" means you can write down an explicit description (a formula, a code for a Borel set, a tree, etc.) that picks the set out from the reals.
- The more alternations of quantifiers over the reals you allow, the more complicated the sets become and the harder it is to prove they are "nice" (measurable, etc.).

**Worked Example:**

The set of all irrational numbers is open (hence Borel). The set of all continued-fraction expansions that are eventually periodic is countable (hence Borel). An analytic set might be "the set of reals that code a well-ordering of ω that is not isomorphic to any ordinal < ω₁^CK" (a complete analytic set).

## Classical Regularity for Borel and Analytic Sets

<!-- section: analytic-and-projective -->

Borel sets are Lebesgue measurable, have the Baire property, and if uncountable contain a perfect subset.

Analytic sets (projections of Borel sets) inherit these regularity properties (Souslin, Lusin, Novikov, etc.):
- Every analytic set is Lebesgue measurable.
- Every analytic set has the Baire property.
- Every uncountable analytic set contains a perfect subset (the perfect set property).

Co-analytic sets also have the perfect set property (by a classical theorem of Souslin).

These proofs are effective and do not require choice beyond what is needed for the basic theory of Polish spaces.

**ELI5**

- Even though analytic sets are "existential" over the reals, they are still tame: you cannot hide a Vitali set or a Bernstein set inside an analytic set.
- The proofs usually construct explicit measures or comeager sets or perfect trees that witness the regularity.

**Worked Example:**

To show an analytic set A has the perfect set property: if A is uncountable, one builds a tree of attempts to find two different reals in A that agree on longer and longer initial segments; the construction yields a perfect tree whose branches all lie in A.

## The Projective Hierarchy and Independence

<!-- section: regularity-properties -->

For projective sets the situation changes dramatically.

ZFC proves regularity properties for Σ¹₁ and Π¹₁ (analytic and co-analytic), and for some statements at the next level, but already at the Δ¹₂ or Σ¹₂ level one can have independence.

- It is consistent with ZFC that there is a projective set that is not Lebesgue measurable (e.g., by adding a Cohen real or by forcing a Δ¹₂ well-ordering of the reals).
- It is consistent with ZFC that all projective sets are Lebesgue measurable, have the Baire property, etc. (Solovay's model from an inaccessible cardinal, or from large cardinals + forcing).

Projective determinacy (PD) — the statement that all projective games are determined — implies all the classical regularity properties for all projective sets.

**ELI5**

- At low levels of definability, "explicit" sets of reals are forced to be nice by ZFC.
- At higher levels you can force "wild" projective sets to exist, or you can assume large cardinals that force all projective sets to be nice again.

**Worked Example:**

In L there is a Δ¹₂ well-ordering of the reals. From it one can construct a projective (in fact Δ¹₂) Vitali set that is not Lebesgue measurable. Hence in L projective regularity fails.

## Large Cardinals and Determinacy

<!-- section: forcing-and-descriptive -->

Large cardinals imply determinacy for definable sets of reals:
- A Woodin cardinal implies that every projective set is determined (PD).
- Infinitely many Woodins imply AD^{L(ℝ)} (determinacy for all sets in L(ℝ)).
- Determinacy in turn implies all the regularity properties (measurability, Baire property, perfect set property, uniformization, etc.) for the corresponding pointclasses.

Thus large cardinals "buy" regularity for definable sets.

Forcing can be used in the opposite direction: from a model with large cardinals one can force to make the continuum large while preserving the determinacy (or the regularity) coming from the cardinals, or one can force to destroy regularity at higher levels.

**ELI5**

- A large cardinal acts like a "force field" that prevents definable sets from being too wild.
- Forcing can be used either to protect that force field while changing the size of the continuum, or (if the cardinal is not strong enough) to break the regularity for more complicated definable sets.

**Worked Example:**

If there are infinitely many Woodin cardinals with a measurable above them, then AD^{L(ℝ)} holds. One can then force with a proper forcing (or a homogeneous forcing) that preserves the Woodins (or at least the derived determinacy) while making 2^ℵ₀ = ℵ₂, obtaining a model in which all sets of reals in L(ℝ) are regular and the continuum is ℵ₂.

## Connections to Other Areas

<!-- section: role-modern -->

Descriptive set theory interacts with:
- Recursion theory (hyperarithmetic sets, admissible ordinals, effective descriptive set theory).
- Ergodic theory and dynamical systems (definable equivalence relations, Borel reducibility).
- Model theory (Scott ranks, Vaught's conjecture for certain classes).
- Set theory of the reals (cardinal invariants of the continuum, forcing axioms, inner models for determinacy).

The modern theory of definable equivalence relations and Borel reducibility has become a major subfield ("descriptive set theory for equivalence relations").

**ELI5**

- Once you have a good theory of which definable sets are nice, you can ask the same questions for definable *equivalence relations* or *functions*: "Can you classify the orbits in a definable way?" "Is this equivalence relation reducible to that one by a Borel function?"
- These questions have turned out to be extremely rich and are calibrated by the same large-cardinal and determinacy hierarchy.

**Worked Example:**

The equivalence relation E₀ (eventual agreement of binary sequences) is a canonical "turbulent" equivalence relation. It is not smooth (not reducible to equality on the reals), yet it is "universal" among countable Borel equivalence relations in a precise sense. Its complexity is measured in the Borel reducibility hierarchy, which itself is independent of ZFC at higher levels.

## Common Mistakes

- **Thinking all 'definable' sets are Borel**: Borel sets are the lowest level; analytic and projective sets are properly larger pointclasses.
- **Assuming that 'all sets of reals are measurable' follows from ZFC**: It does not; it requires large cardinals (or an inaccessible in Solovay's model) and implies Con(ZFC + many inaccessibles).
- **Confusing classical descriptive set theory (ZFC theorems) with the independence phenomena at higher levels**: The beautiful ZFC theorems stop at co-analytic sets; beyond that one needs extra axioms.
- **Believing that large cardinals 'solve' all questions about the reals**: They decide many questions about *definable* sets; questions about *arbitrary* sets of reals remain independent or require forcing axioms or other principles.
- **Forgetting the role of forcing in producing counterexamples**: Even when large cardinals give regularity in L(ℝ), forcing can still produce models in which definable sets (at higher complexity) are wild, or in which the continuum is large while regularity is preserved.

A *large cardinal* is a cardinal number so big that its mere existence (in a model of set theory) implies the consistency of ZFC and usually of ZFC plus the existence of many smaller large cardinals.

Large cardinals form a rich hierarchy of consistency strength and are the primary tool for calibrating how "strong" a given mathematical statement or theory is.

They also decide many statements left undecided by ZFC alone (projective determinacy, regularity properties of definable sets of reals, etc.).

## What Makes a Cardinal "Large"?

<!-- section: what-are-large-cardinals -->

The smallest infinite cardinals (ℵ₀, ℵ₁, …) are "small" in the sense that their existence is provable in ZFC (or follows from the axiom of infinity plus replacement).

A cardinal κ becomes large when it is so big that the structure V_κ (or an ultrapower by a measure on κ) already satisfies ZFC or much stronger theories.

Typical first examples:
- Inaccessible cardinal
- Mahlo cardinal
- Weakly compact
- Ramsey
- Measurable
- Woodin, supercompact, huge, etc.

**ELI5**

- Think of the ordinals as a tall ladder. Most rungs are "small." A large cardinal is an extremely high rung whose height is so great that if you look only up to that rung you already see a whole universe that obeys all the rules of set theory (and more).
- The higher the rung, the stronger the "reflection" or "closure" properties it has, and the more consistency strength it carries.

**Worked Example:**

If κ is the least inaccessible cardinal, then V_κ ⊨ ZFC + "there are no inaccessible cardinals." Thus the existence of one inaccessible already proves Con(ZFC + "there are no inaccessibles"), a strictly stronger consistency statement than Con(ZFC).

## Inaccessible Cardinals and Beyond

<!-- section: inaccessible-and-beyond -->

A cardinal κ is (strongly) *inaccessible* if:
- κ is uncountable,
- κ is regular (cf(κ) = κ),
- κ is a strong limit (λ < κ ⇒ 2^λ < κ).

Then V_κ ⊨ ZFC.

A cardinal is *Mahlo* if it is inaccessible and the set of smaller inaccessibles is stationary in it. This implies there are stationarily many inaccessibles below it, and V_κ sees many inaccessibles.

Higher notions (weakly compact, Ramsey, etc.) are defined by partition properties, indescribability, or elementary embeddings.

**ELI5**

- Inaccessible = "so big that the universe below it already satisfies all of ZFC by itself."
- Mahlo = "so big that it is a limit of inaccessibles in a strong sense (you cannot avoid them by taking a club)."

**Worked Example:**

If κ is inaccessible then replacement holds in V_κ because any function f : α → V_κ with α < κ has range bounded below κ (by regularity + strong limit). Hence V_κ thinks "replacement holds for all sets."

## Measurable Cardinals and Elementary Embeddings

<!-- section: measurable-and-stronger -->

A cardinal κ is *measurable* if there is a non-principal κ-complete ultrafilter U on κ.

Equivalently (by Scott), there is a non-trivial elementary embedding j : V → M (M transitive) with critical point κ (j(κ) > κ).

The ultrapower construction produces such j from U.

Measurability implies that κ is the κ-th inaccessible, the κ-th Mahlo, etc. (in fact far stronger reflection).

**ELI5**

- A measurable cardinal is so big that you can "average" over it in a coherent way (the ultrafilter) and obtain an elementary embedding that moves κ but fixes everything smaller.
- The embedding "reflects" properties upward and shows that κ is a limit of many smaller large cardinals.

**Worked Example:**

If U is a normal measure on κ then the ultrapower embedding j satisfies j(α) = α for all α < κ, but j(κ) > κ. By elementarity, any property that holds of κ in M also holds of many smaller cardinals in V.

## The Consistency-Strength Hierarchy

<!-- section: consistency-strength-hierarchy -->

Large cardinal axioms are linearly ordered by consistency strength (at least for the well-studied ones).

If "there is a supercompact cardinal" is consistent, then "there is a measurable cardinal" is consistent (in fact there is a proper class of them in an inner model).

The hierarchy is used to measure the strength of any statement φ that implies Con(ZFC + large cardinals).

Many "concrete" mathematical statements (determinacy of projective games, regularity of definable sets of reals, the existence of certain combinatorial objects on ω₁, etc.) have been exactly located on this hierarchy.

**ELI5**

- Large cardinals form a ladder of increasing "power." Each rung proves the consistency of all the rungs below it.
- If a mathematical statement (about reals, or about trees, or about operators) requires a high rung for its consistency, we say it has high consistency strength.

**Worked Example:**

"Every projective set of reals is Lebesgue measurable + DC" is equiconsistent with "there are infinitely many Woodin cardinals." This is a theorem (Martin–Steel–Woodin); the large cardinals are necessary as well as sufficient.

## Role in Settling Independent Questions

<!-- section: role-in-settling -->

Large cardinals decide many statements left open by ZFC:
- They imply projective determinacy (PD), which settles the behavior of all projective sets of reals with respect to measure, category, perfect-set property, etc.
- They imply that 0^# exists (hence L ≠ V and there are non-constructible reals).
- They decide the truth of many statements in L about singular cardinals, stationary sets, etc.

Forcing axioms (which are in tension with some large cardinals) decide other statements, often implying ¬CH.

**ELI5**

- Once you assume a large cardinal, many questions about "definable" or "simple" infinite objects get definite answers (the objects must behave nicely).
- Different large cardinals or different maximality principles (forcing axioms) can give different answers to questions like CH.

**Worked Example:**

If there is a Woodin cardinal then every projective set is Lebesgue measurable, has the Baire property, and has the perfect set property if uncountable. These regularity properties are independent of ZFC alone.

## Common Mistakes

- **Thinking "large cardinal" just means "very big infinite cardinal"**: ℵ_ω is very big but singular and "small" in consistency strength. Large cardinals have strong closure or reflection properties.
- **Believing the existence of a large cardinal is a theorem of ZFC**: All large cardinal axioms (beyond inaccessible in some weak senses) are independent of ZFC; they have higher consistency strength.
- **Confusing the cardinal itself with the consistency statement**: "There is a measurable" proves Con(ZFC + many inaccessibles), but the cardinal is an object *inside* the model, not just a consistency assertion.
- **Assuming all large cardinals are compatible with each other**: Some combinations (e.g., certain forcing axioms + very large cardinals) are inconsistent or require careful calibration.
- **Forgetting that large cardinals live in V or in inner models**: When we say "assume there is a measurable," we usually mean in V; but one also studies what large cardinals hold in L, in HOD, in core models, etc.

Martin's axiom (MA) is a forcing axiom asserting that for ccc (countable chain condition) partial orders, one can find filters that are generic for any "small" (less than continuum many) collection of dense sets.

It is consistent with the continuum being arbitrarily large, and it implies many of the combinatorial and regularity consequences that were once associated with the continuum hypothesis, while allowing 2^ℵ₀ to be much larger than ℵ₁.

MA is one of the most useful "additional axioms" beyond ZFC for settling questions about the real line, cardinal invariants, and infinitary combinatorics.

## The Statement

<!-- section: statement-ma -->

MA(κ) (for a cardinal κ): If P is a ccc poset and D_α (α < κ) are dense subsets of P, then there exists a filter G ⊆ P such that G ∩ D_α ≠ ∅ for all α < κ.

Martin's axiom (MA): MA(κ) holds for every κ < 2^ℵ₀.

In other words, for ccc posets you can meet < continuum many dense sets simultaneously with a single filter.

**ELI5**

- A ccc poset is one in which you cannot find uncountably many pairwise incompatible conditions (a "wide" but "shallow" tree of possibilities).
- MA says that if you have fewer than continuum many "requirements" (dense sets) for such a poset, then the universe already contains a filter that satisfies all of them at once.
- This is a weak form of "genericity" that stops short of full choice or full forcing axioms.

**Worked Example:**

The poset for adding a Cohen real is ccc. The dense sets "the generic real differs from the α-th ground-model real at some coordinate" (one for each α < 2^ℵ₀) are dense. MA says there is a single real that differs from all ground-model reals of size < continuum; i.e., the covering number of the meager ideal is the continuum.

## Consistency Strength and Models

<!-- section: statement-ma -->

MA + ¬CH is consistent relative to ZFC. In fact, starting from a model with a large enough cardinal (or just ZFC if one is willing to make the continuum a weakly compact in an inner model, but usually one uses an inaccessible or just iterates), one can force MA + 2^ℵ₀ = λ for any regular λ ≥ ℵ₂ with uncountable cofinality by a finite-support ccc iteration of length λ that book-keeps all possible ccc posets and all possible collections of < λ dense sets.

The iteration itself remains ccc, so cardinals are preserved and the continuum becomes λ while MA holds in the extension.

MA is therefore a relatively "mild" additional axiom; it does not increase consistency strength beyond ZFC.

**ELI5**

- You can start with a universe in which the continuum is small or large, and then carefully add generics for all the ccc posets you care about, in a book-kept iteration, so that at the end the continuum is whatever size you want and every "small" collection of dense sets for ccc posets has already been met by a filter that was added along the way.
- No large cardinals are needed for the consistency of MA + ¬CH; ZFC alone suffices.

**Worked Example:**

To obtain MA + 2^ℵ₀ = ℵ₂, iterate all ccc posets of size ℵ₁ with finite support for ℵ₂ steps, dovetailing so that every possible ccc poset in intermediate models and every possible ℵ₁-sequence of dense sets appears as a stage. The whole iteration is ccc (by a delta-system argument on the supports), preserves ℵ₁ and ℵ₂, makes the continuum ℵ₂, and forces MA(ℵ₁), hence MA.

## Combinatorial Consequences

<!-- section: consequences-of-ma -->

MA has many pleasant consequences that "look like" consequences of CH but without forcing the continuum to be small:
- The union of < 2^ℵ₀ many meager sets is meager (additivity of the meager ideal = continuum).
- The union of < 2^ℵ₀ many null sets has measure zero (additivity of the null ideal = continuum).
- There are no Suslin trees.
- Every ccc poset of size < continuum has a filter meeting all dense sets of size < continuum (by definition).
- The real line cannot be the union of < continuum many countable sets in certain senses, etc.

In cardinal invariants of the continuum, MA implies that many invariants (add, cov, non, cof for both meager and null ideals) equal the continuum.

**ELI5**

- MA makes "< continuum" a "small" cardinal in the combinatorial sense: you cannot cover the reals with fewer than continuum many "small" (meager or null) sets.
- It also kills many "pathological" objects (Suslin trees) that can exist under CH or in L.

**Worked Example:**

Suppose {N_α | α < κ} with κ < 2^ℵ₀ are nowhere dense. To show their union is nowhere dense, consider the ccc poset of nonempty open intervals with rational endpoints, ordered by inclusion. The dense sets "the interval is disjoint from N_α" are dense for each α. MA gives a filter (i.e., a decreasing sequence of intervals) whose intersection point lies in no N_α and is in every basic open set we care about, showing the union is not dense in any interval.

## Relation to Other Axioms

<!-- section: relation-to-other-axioms -->

MA is strictly between ZFC and CH in strength for many purposes:
- MA + CH is consistent (in fact MA(ℵ₁) + CH is just CH plus a weak forcing axiom).
- MA + ¬CH is consistent and implies many consequences that CH also implies, but also some that CH contradicts (e.g., the non-existence of Suslin trees is compatible with both, but MA makes the additivity of ideals = continuum while CH makes it ℵ₁).

Stronger forcing axioms:
- The Proper Forcing Axiom (PFA) is the analogue of MA for all *proper* posets (a much larger class than ccc). PFA implies MA + 2^ℵ₀ = ℵ₂ and many structural statements about ω₁ (no square, every stationary set reflects or is killed in a controlled way, etc.).
- Martin's Maximum (MM) is the strongest reasonable forcing axiom (for stationary-set-preserving posets) and also implies 2^ℵ₀ = ℵ₂.

MA is implied by PFA and MM.

**ELI5**

- MA is the "ccc version" of forcing axioms: it only talks about the 'tame' ccc posets.
- PFA and MM talk about much wilder posets and therefore have much stronger consequences (in particular they pin the continuum exactly at ℵ₂).

**Worked Example:**

PFA implies that there are no ℵ₁-Suslin trees (because the tree poset is proper) and that 2^ℵ₀ = ℵ₂ (by a proper forcing that shoots a club through a stationary set coding a collapse or by other bookkeeping). MA alone does not decide the exact value of the continuum (only that it is > ℵ₁ and regular of uncountable cofinality in the usual models).

## Philosophical Status

<!-- section: philosophical-status -->

MA is often viewed as a "maximality" or "anti-CH" principle in a weak sense:
- It says that the universe cannot avoid generic filters for ccc posets when only < continuum many dense sets are involved.
- It makes many objects "small" in the cardinal-invariant sense without forcing the actual size of the continuum to be small.
- It is compatible with a wide range of values for 2^ℵ₀ and with the existence of large cardinals.

In the context of the "set theory of the real line," MA (and stronger forcing axioms) are competing with CH and with large-cardinal + determinacy approaches as candidates for "what the continuum is like."

**ELI5**

- Under CH the continuum is small, so many things collapse to ℵ₁-sized combinatorics.
- Under MA the continuum may be large, but you still get "ℵ₁-like" or "small" behavior for anything of size < continuum, because the forcing axioms guarantee generic filters for the relevant posets.
- It is a way of saying "the continuum is large, but combinatorially it still behaves as if it were the 'next' cardinal after ℵ₁ in many respects."

**Worked Example:**

Under CH there can be a Suslin tree (an ℵ₁-tree with no uncountable chains or antichains). MA kills all such trees because the tree poset is ccc and one can take the dense sets that "kill a particular node on level α" for each α < 2^ℵ₀. A filter meeting all of them would give an uncountable antichain, contradicting that it is a Suslin tree. Hence MA ⇒ "there are no Suslin trees."

## Common Mistakes

- **Thinking MA implies CH or ¬CH**: MA is compatible with both (though the interesting models usually take MA + ¬CH).
- **Confusing MA with full AC or with the existence of generics for arbitrary posets**: MA only concerns ccc posets and only < continuum many dense sets.
- **Assuming MA is a theorem of ZFC**: It is not; it is independent (consistent with ZFC + CH and with ZFC + ¬CH + large continuum).
- **Forgetting that MA has no extra consistency strength**: Unlike large cardinals, MA does not prove Con(ZFC + stronger statements); it is a purely combinatorial/ forcing axiom whose consistency is equiconsistent with ZFC.
- **Believing that MA decides the exact value of the continuum**: It forces the continuum to be regular and > ℵ₁, but the exact value (ℵ₂, ℵ₃, ℵ_{ω+1}, …) can vary in different models of MA. Stronger axioms (PFA, MM) pin it at ℵ₂.
