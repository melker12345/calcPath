# Independence (mathematical logic)

A statement φ is *independent* of a formal theory T (assumed consistent) if neither φ nor its negation ¬φ can be proved from the axioms of T.

In other words, both T + φ and T + ¬φ are consistent (relative to the consistency of T).

Independence results show that certain mathematical questions cannot be settled by a given list of axioms, no matter how long one works inside the theory.

## Definition and Basic Examples

<!-- section: definition-independence -->

By Gödel's completeness theorem, φ is independent of T if and only if there exists a model of T + φ and a model of T + ¬φ.

Classic examples in set theory:
- The continuum hypothesis (CH) is independent of ZFC.
- The axiom of choice (AC) is independent of ZF.
- The existence of a measurable cardinal is independent of ZFC (in the consistency-strength sense).

**ELI5**

- The axioms are like the rules of a game. Independence means that the rules do not force the answer to a particular question; both "yes" and "no" are still possible without breaking the rules.
- You need a bigger or different rulebook (extra axioms) to decide the question.

**Worked Example:**

ZFC does not prove CH and does not prove ¬CH. There are models of ZFC + CH (Gödel's L) and models of ZFC + ¬CH (Cohen's forcing extensions). Hence CH is independent of ZFC.

## Methods for Proving Independence

<!-- section: methods-for-independence -->

The two main techniques are:
1. *Inner models*: Construct a definable sub-universe (such as Gödel's constructible universe L) inside any model of the base theory, in which the statement holds (or fails).
2. *Forcing / outer models*: Starting from a model M of the base theory, adjoin new generic objects via a poset to produce an extension M[G] in which the statement holds (or fails) while the base theory remains true.

For statements of high consistency strength (large cardinals), one also uses *consistency-strength comparisons*: showing that the statement implies Con(T + ψ) for some ψ, while the negation does not, or vice versa.

**ELI5**

- Inner models are like "looking inside the current mathematical universe for a smaller, better-behaved sub-universe where the statement becomes true."
- Forcing is like "carefully adding new objects from outside to make the statement true, without breaking the old rules."

**Worked Example:**

To show Con(ZF) ⇒ Con(ZF + ¬AC): start with a model of ZF (or ZFC), use a symmetric extension or permutation model in which there is a countable collection of pairwise disjoint nonempty sets with no choice function. The resulting model satisfies ZF + "there is a Dedekind-finite infinite set" (hence ¬AC).

## Examples in Set Theory

<!-- section: examples-in-set-theory -->

Besides CH and AC, independence abounds:
- Suslin's hypothesis (there is no Suslin tree) is independent of ZFC.
- The Whitehead problem (every Whitehead group is free) is independent.
- Many statements about the structure of the real line under measure or category (e.g., "all sets of reals have the property of Baire") are independent.
- The existence of 0^# (a real coding truth about L) is independent of ZFC; its existence implies that L is not the whole universe in a strong sense.
- Large cardinal axioms are independent of ZFC in the sense that their consistency strength strictly exceeds that of ZFC.

**ELI5**

- Set theory is extremely rich; once you have the basic rules for sets, there is still enormous freedom in how "wide" or "tall" the universe of sets is allowed to be.
- Different choices produce different answers to questions about the continuum, about trees, about definable sets of reals, etc.

**Worked Example:**

The existence of a measurable cardinal implies that 0^# exists and that every uncountable cardinal in L is inaccessible, Mahlo, etc., in L. If there is no measurable, then in L there may be no such large cardinals. The statement "there is a measurable" is therefore independent of ZFC + "there is no measurable" in a strong consistency-strength sense.

## Philosophical Significance

<!-- section: philosophical-significance -->

Independence results, especially for "natural" mathematical statements such as CH, have been interpreted in several ways:
- The ZFC axioms are incomplete; we need to adopt additional axioms (large cardinals, forcing axioms, determinacy, etc.) to decide them.
- There may be no single "true" universe of sets; different legitimate universes give different answers.
- Some questions (especially those of very high consistency strength) may remain permanently undecided by any "reasonable" extension of ZFC.

Gödel himself believed that CH is false and that new axioms (large cardinals) would eventually decide it negatively. Modern research has produced many candidates for such axioms, some implying CH, others implying ¬CH.

**ELI5**

- Independence shows that the current rulebook is not powerful enough to answer every question we can meaningfully ask.
- Mathematicians then debate which extra rules to add. Different extra rules give different answers and different mathematics downstream.

**Worked Example:**

Large cardinals (measurable, Woodin, supercompact, etc.) decide the truth value of many statements about projective sets of reals (projective determinacy) and about the fine structure of L. Forcing axioms such as PFA decide that 2^ℵ₀ = ℵ₂ and that many combinatorial objects on ω₁ do not exist. Both are "natural" in different communities and decide CH differently.

## Common Mistakes

- **Thinking that independence means 'we can never know the answer'**: It means only that the *current* axioms do not decide it. Stronger axioms often do decide it.
- **Confusing independence with undecidability in the sense of Gödel's incompleteness for arithmetic**: Independence in set theory is usually proved by exhibiting models on both sides, not merely by a diagonal argument.
- **Believing that if a statement is independent then both it and its negation are 'equally true'**: In practice one may have strong reasons (from inner-model theory, from maximality considerations, from consequences for other fields) to prefer one side.
- **Assuming every interesting statement is independent of ZFC**: Many statements *are* decided by ZFC (e.g., the basic properties of ordinals and cardinals, the existence of non-constructible reals if 0^# exists is decided in certain ways, etc.).
- **Forgetting consistency-strength hierarchies**: "Con(ZFC + φ)" can be strictly stronger than "Con(ZFC)"; independence in that case is not symmetric.
