# Descriptive set theory

Descriptive set theory is the study of *definable* sets of real numbers (and, more generally, of Polish spaces) and the regularity properties they possess with respect to Lebesgue measure, Baire category, perfect subsets, and other notions.

It sits at the intersection of set theory, topology, measure theory, and recursion theory. Its classical results (Borel, analytic, and co-analytic sets) are provable in ZFC; higher levels of definability (projective sets, sets in L(ℝ)) lead to independence phenomena that are resolved by large cardinals or determinacy axioms.

## What "Definable" Means

<!-- section: what-is-descriptive -->

The basic pointclasses are:
- **Open** and **closed** sets.
- **Borel** sets: the σ-algebra generated by the open sets (closed under countable unions and complements).
- **Analytic** (Σ¹₁) sets: continuous images of Borel sets, or projections of Borel sets in the product space.
- **Projective** sets: the smallest pointclass containing the Borel sets and closed under projection (∃^ℝ) and complement.

One also studies sets definable from ordinals and reals (in L(ℝ), or boldface pointclasses with real parameters).

**ELI5**

- "Definable" means you can write down an explicit description (a formula, a code for a Borel set, a tree, etc.) that picks the set out from the reals.
- The more alternations of quantifiers over the reals you allow, the more complicated the sets become and the harder it is to prove they are "nice" (measurable, etc.).

**Worked Example:**

The set of all irrational numbers is open (hence Borel). The set of all continued-fraction expansions that are eventually periodic is countable (hence Borel). An analytic set might be "the set of reals that code a well-ordering of ω that is not isomorphic to any ordinal < ω₁^CK" (a complete analytic set).

## Classical Regularity for Borel and Analytic Sets

<!-- section: analytic-and-projective -->

Borel sets are Lebesgue measurable, have the Baire property, and if uncountable contain a perfect subset.

Analytic sets (projections of Borel sets) inherit these regularity properties (Souslin, Lusin, Novikov, etc.):
- Every analytic set is Lebesgue measurable.
- Every analytic set has the Baire property.
- Every uncountable analytic set contains a perfect subset (the perfect set property).

Co-analytic sets also have the perfect set property (by a classical theorem of Souslin).

These proofs are effective and do not require choice beyond what is needed for the basic theory of Polish spaces.

**ELI5**

- Even though analytic sets are "existential" over the reals, they are still tame: you cannot hide a Vitali set or a Bernstein set inside an analytic set.
- The proofs usually construct explicit measures or comeager sets or perfect trees that witness the regularity.

**Worked Example:**

To show an analytic set A has the perfect set property: if A is uncountable, one builds a tree of attempts to find two different reals in A that agree on longer and longer initial segments; the construction yields a perfect tree whose branches all lie in A.

## The Projective Hierarchy and Independence

<!-- section: regularity-properties -->

For projective sets the situation changes dramatically.

ZFC proves regularity properties for Σ¹₁ and Π¹₁ (analytic and co-analytic), and for some statements at the next level, but already at the Δ¹₂ or Σ¹₂ level one can have independence.

- It is consistent with ZFC that there is a projective set that is not Lebesgue measurable (e.g., by adding a Cohen real or by forcing a Δ¹₂ well-ordering of the reals).
- It is consistent with ZFC that all projective sets are Lebesgue measurable, have the Baire property, etc. (Solovay's model from an inaccessible cardinal, or from large cardinals + forcing).

Projective determinacy (PD) — the statement that all projective games are determined — implies all the classical regularity properties for all projective sets.

**ELI5**

- At low levels of definability, "explicit" sets of reals are forced to be nice by ZFC.
- At higher levels you can force "wild" projective sets to exist, or you can assume large cardinals that force all projective sets to be nice again.

**Worked Example:**

In L there is a Δ¹₂ well-ordering of the reals. From it one can construct a projective (in fact Δ¹₂) Vitali set that is not Lebesgue measurable. Hence in L projective regularity fails.

## Large Cardinals and Determinacy

<!-- section: forcing-and-descriptive -->

Large cardinals imply determinacy for definable sets of reals:
- A Woodin cardinal implies that every projective set is determined (PD).
- Infinitely many Woodins imply AD^{L(ℝ)} (determinacy for all sets in L(ℝ)).
- Determinacy in turn implies all the regularity properties (measurability, Baire property, perfect set property, uniformization, etc.) for the corresponding pointclasses.

Thus large cardinals "buy" regularity for definable sets.

Forcing can be used in the opposite direction: from a model with large cardinals one can force to make the continuum large while preserving the determinacy (or the regularity) coming from the cardinals, or one can force to destroy regularity at higher levels.

**ELI5**

- A large cardinal acts like a "force field" that prevents definable sets from being too wild.
- Forcing can be used either to protect that force field while changing the size of the continuum, or (if the cardinal is not strong enough) to break the regularity for more complicated definable sets.

**Worked Example:**

If there are infinitely many Woodin cardinals with a measurable above them, then AD^{L(ℝ)} holds. One can then force with a proper forcing (or a homogeneous forcing) that preserves the Woodins (or at least the derived determinacy) while making 2^ℵ₀ = ℵ₂, obtaining a model in which all sets of reals in L(ℝ) are regular and the continuum is ℵ₂.

## Connections to Other Areas

<!-- section: role-modern -->

Descriptive set theory interacts with:
- Recursion theory (hyperarithmetic sets, admissible ordinals, effective descriptive set theory).
- Ergodic theory and dynamical systems (definable equivalence relations, Borel reducibility).
- Model theory (Scott ranks, Vaught's conjecture for certain classes).
- Set theory of the reals (cardinal invariants of the continuum, forcing axioms, inner models for determinacy).

The modern theory of definable equivalence relations and Borel reducibility has become a major subfield ("descriptive set theory for equivalence relations").

**ELI5**

- Once you have a good theory of which definable sets are nice, you can ask the same questions for definable *equivalence relations* or *functions*: "Can you classify the orbits in a definable way?" "Is this equivalence relation reducible to that one by a Borel function?"
- These questions have turned out to be extremely rich and are calibrated by the same large-cardinal and determinacy hierarchy.

**Worked Example:**

The equivalence relation E₀ (eventual agreement of binary sequences) is a canonical "turbulent" equivalence relation. It is not smooth (not reducible to equality on the reals), yet it is "universal" among countable Borel equivalence relations in a precise sense. Its complexity is measured in the Borel reducibility hierarchy, which itself is independent of ZFC at higher levels.

## Common Mistakes

- **Thinking all 'definable' sets are Borel**: Borel sets are the lowest level; analytic and projective sets are properly larger pointclasses.
- **Assuming that 'all sets of reals are measurable' follows from ZFC**: It does not; it requires large cardinals (or an inaccessible in Solovay's model) and implies Con(ZFC + many inaccessibles).
- **Confusing classical descriptive set theory (ZFC theorems) with the independence phenomena at higher levels**: The beautiful ZFC theorems stop at co-analytic sets; beyond that one needs extra axioms.
- **Believing that large cardinals 'solve' all questions about the reals**: They decide many questions about *definable* sets; questions about *arbitrary* sets of reals remain independent or require forcing axioms or other principles.
- **Forgetting the role of forcing in producing counterexamples**: Even when large cardinals give regularity in L(ℝ), forcing can still produce models in which definable sets (at higher complexity) are wild, or in which the continuum is large while regularity is preserved.
