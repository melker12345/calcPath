# Large cardinal

A *large cardinal* is a cardinal number so big that its mere existence (in a model of set theory) implies the consistency of ZFC and usually of ZFC plus the existence of many smaller large cardinals.

Large cardinals form a rich hierarchy of consistency strength and are the primary tool for calibrating how "strong" a given mathematical statement or theory is.

They also decide many statements left undecided by ZFC alone (projective determinacy, regularity properties of definable sets of reals, etc.).

## What Makes a Cardinal "Large"?

<!-- section: what-are-large-cardinals -->

The smallest infinite cardinals (ℵ₀, ℵ₁, …) are "small" in the sense that their existence is provable in ZFC (or follows from the axiom of infinity plus replacement).

A cardinal κ becomes large when it is so big that the structure V_κ (or an ultrapower by a measure on κ) already satisfies ZFC or much stronger theories.

Typical first examples:
- Inaccessible cardinal
- Mahlo cardinal
- Weakly compact
- Ramsey
- Measurable
- Woodin, supercompact, huge, etc.

**ELI5**

- Think of the ordinals as a tall ladder. Most rungs are "small." A large cardinal is an extremely high rung whose height is so great that if you look only up to that rung you already see a whole universe that obeys all the rules of set theory (and more).
- The higher the rung, the stronger the "reflection" or "closure" properties it has, and the more consistency strength it carries.

**Worked Example:**

If κ is the least inaccessible cardinal, then V_κ ⊨ ZFC + "there are no inaccessible cardinals." Thus the existence of one inaccessible already proves Con(ZFC + "there are no inaccessibles"), a strictly stronger consistency statement than Con(ZFC).

## Inaccessible Cardinals and Beyond

<!-- section: inaccessible-and-beyond -->

A cardinal κ is (strongly) *inaccessible* if:
- κ is uncountable,
- κ is regular (cf(κ) = κ),
- κ is a strong limit (λ < κ ⇒ 2^λ < κ).

Then V_κ ⊨ ZFC.

A cardinal is *Mahlo* if it is inaccessible and the set of smaller inaccessibles is stationary in it. This implies there are stationarily many inaccessibles below it, and V_κ sees many inaccessibles.

Higher notions (weakly compact, Ramsey, etc.) are defined by partition properties, indescribability, or elementary embeddings.

**ELI5**

- Inaccessible = "so big that the universe below it already satisfies all of ZFC by itself."
- Mahlo = "so big that it is a limit of inaccessibles in a strong sense (you cannot avoid them by taking a club)."

**Worked Example:**

If κ is inaccessible then replacement holds in V_κ because any function f : α → V_κ with α < κ has range bounded below κ (by regularity + strong limit). Hence V_κ thinks "replacement holds for all sets."

## Measurable Cardinals and Elementary Embeddings

<!-- section: measurable-and-stronger -->

A cardinal κ is *measurable* if there is a non-principal κ-complete ultrafilter U on κ.

Equivalently (by Scott), there is a non-trivial elementary embedding j : V → M (M transitive) with critical point κ (j(κ) > κ).

The ultrapower construction produces such j from U.

Measurability implies that κ is the κ-th inaccessible, the κ-th Mahlo, etc. (in fact far stronger reflection).

**ELI5**

- A measurable cardinal is so big that you can "average" over it in a coherent way (the ultrafilter) and obtain an elementary embedding that moves κ but fixes everything smaller.
- The embedding "reflects" properties upward and shows that κ is a limit of many smaller large cardinals.

**Worked Example:**

If U is a normal measure on κ then the ultrapower embedding j satisfies j(α) = α for all α < κ, but j(κ) > κ. By elementarity, any property that holds of κ in M also holds of many smaller cardinals in V.

## The Consistency-Strength Hierarchy

<!-- section: consistency-strength-hierarchy -->

Large cardinal axioms are linearly ordered by consistency strength (at least for the well-studied ones).

If "there is a supercompact cardinal" is consistent, then "there is a measurable cardinal" is consistent (in fact there is a proper class of them in an inner model).

The hierarchy is used to measure the strength of any statement φ that implies Con(ZFC + large cardinals).

Many "concrete" mathematical statements (determinacy of projective games, regularity of definable sets of reals, the existence of certain combinatorial objects on ω₁, etc.) have been exactly located on this hierarchy.

**ELI5**

- Large cardinals form a ladder of increasing "power." Each rung proves the consistency of all the rungs below it.
- If a mathematical statement (about reals, or about trees, or about operators) requires a high rung for its consistency, we say it has high consistency strength.

**Worked Example:**

"Every projective set of reals is Lebesgue measurable + DC" is equiconsistent with "there are infinitely many Woodin cardinals." This is a theorem (Martin–Steel–Woodin); the large cardinals are necessary as well as sufficient.

## Role in Settling Independent Questions

<!-- section: role-in-settling -->

Large cardinals decide many statements left open by ZFC:
- They imply projective determinacy (PD), which settles the behavior of all projective sets of reals with respect to measure, category, perfect-set property, etc.
- They imply that 0^# exists (hence L ≠ V and there are non-constructible reals).
- They decide the truth of many statements in L about singular cardinals, stationary sets, etc.

Forcing axioms (which are in tension with some large cardinals) decide other statements, often implying ¬CH.

**ELI5**

- Once you assume a large cardinal, many questions about "definable" or "simple" infinite objects get definite answers (the objects must behave nicely).
- Different large cardinals or different maximality principles (forcing axioms) can give different answers to questions like CH.

**Worked Example:**

If there is a Woodin cardinal then every projective set is Lebesgue measurable, has the Baire property, and has the perfect set property if uncountable. These regularity properties are independent of ZFC alone.

## Common Mistakes

- **Thinking "large cardinal" just means "very big infinite cardinal"**: ℵ_ω is very big but singular and "small" in consistency strength. Large cardinals have strong closure or reflection properties.
- **Believing the existence of a large cardinal is a theorem of ZFC**: All large cardinal axioms (beyond inaccessible in some weak senses) are independent of ZFC; they have higher consistency strength.
- **Confusing the cardinal itself with the consistency statement**: "There is a measurable" proves Con(ZFC + many inaccessibles), but the cardinal is an object *inside* the model, not just a consistency assertion.
- **Assuming all large cardinals are compatible with each other**: Some combinations (e.g., certain forcing axioms + very large cardinals) are inconsistent or require careful calibration.
- **Forgetting that large cardinals live in V or in inner models**: When we say "assume there is a measurable," we usually mean in V; but one also studies what large cardinals hold in L, in HOD, in core models, etc.
