# Inner model

An *inner model* is a transitive class M ⊆ V that contains all the ordinals and satisfies (at least) the axioms of ZF. Inner models are "universes inside the universe" that are well-behaved and often satisfy extra axioms (AC, GCH, large cardinals) that the full universe V may not satisfy.

They are the primary tool for proving relative consistency results and for measuring the "distance" between V and L-like universes.

## Definition and Basic Properties

<!-- section: what-is-inner-model -->

A class M is an inner model of ZF if:
- M is transitive (x ∈ y ∈ M ⇒ x ∈ M),
- Ord ⊆ M (M contains every ordinal),
- (M, ∈) ⊨ ZF.

If M also satisfies the axiom of choice we say it is an inner model of ZFC.

Because M is transitive, the ordinals and the membership relation are computed absolutely: α is an ordinal in M iff it is an ordinal in V, and x ∈^M y iff x ∈ y.

**ELI5**

- An inner model is like a "sub-universe" that is closed under the set-theoretic operations and sees the same ordinals you do.
- Because it is transitive it does not have "fake" ordinals or wrong membership; it is an honest initial segment of the true universe in the membership sense.

**Worked Example:**

The constructible universe L is the smallest inner model of ZFC: if M is any inner model of ZF then L ⊆ M. This is because the definition of "constructible" only uses definability, which any model of ZF is closed under.

## The Constructible Universe L as the Prototypical Inner Model

<!-- section: examples-of-inner-models -->

L is built by iterating the definable power set operation along the ordinals. It always satisfies ZFC + GCH + V = L (inside itself).

Other canonical examples:
- L[U] for a normal measure U on a measurable cardinal (models ZFC + "there is a measurable").
- HOD, the class of hereditarily ordinal-definable sets (always models ZFC, even if V ⊨ ZF + ¬AC).
- Core models K (Jensen, Dodd-Jensen, Mitchell-Steel, etc.) that attempt to capture as much of V as possible while retaining fine structure and a definable well-order.

**ELI5**

- L is the "minimal" inner model: it contains only what you are forced to add by explicit definitions from the ordinals.
- HOD is the "definable from ordinals" part of V; it is always as nice as L (has AC and GCH in many cases) even when V is not.
- Core models are "the L of V" relative to the large cardinals that exist in V.

**Worked Example:**

Even if V ⊨ ZF + "there is a Dedekind-finite infinite set" (hence ¬AC), the class HOD computed in V still satisfies ZFC + GCH. This shows that the failure of AC, if it occurs, must be witnessed by sets that are not hereditarily ordinal-definable.

## Uses in Relative Consistency

<!-- section: uses-in-consistency -->

Inner models are the standard vehicle for relative consistency proofs of the form:

"Con(T + φ) ⇒ Con(T + ψ)"

where T is usually ZFC or ZF.

One constructs (inside a model of T + φ) an inner model of T + ψ.

Classic example: Gödel showed that if M ⊨ ZF then L^M ⊨ ZFC + GCH. Hence Con(ZF) ⇒ Con(ZFC + GCH).

Another: if there is a measurable cardinal then there is an inner model L[U] with a measurable cardinal, and in L[U] there are many inaccessibles below it. Hence the existence of a measurable implies Con(ZFC + "there is a proper class of inaccessibles").

**ELI5**

- To show that a strong assumption φ does not lead to contradiction with a weaker theory, you build a "safe sub-universe" inside a model of φ that satisfies the weaker theory plus the desired conclusion ψ.
- The safer and more canonical the sub-universe, the stronger the consistency result.

**Worked Example:**

If κ is measurable in V with normal measure U, then the inner model L[U] satisfies "κ is measurable" and, by the reflection properties of the ultrapower embedding, L[U] thinks there are stationarily many inaccessibles below κ. Hence Con(ZFC + measurable) ⇒ Con(ZFC + "there is a proper class of inaccessibles").

## Core Models and Covering Lemmas

<!-- section: core-models-and-covering -->

When there are no inner models with very large cardinals, one can often prove that V is "close" to a canonical core model K (L, L[U], K^c, etc.).

Jensen's covering lemma (for L): if 0^# does not exist, then for every uncountable set X of ordinals there is a constructible Y ⊇ X with |Y| = |X|.

Modern generalizations (Mitchell-Steel, Schimmerling, etc.) give covering lemmas for core models below Woodin cardinals, with consequences such as the singular cardinals hypothesis (SCH), square principles, etc.

Failure of covering implies the existence of an inner model with a Woodin cardinal (or stronger).

**ELI5**

- If the universe is not too "fat" (no inner models with Woodins), then it cannot deviate too much from the constructible or core-model universe; every large set of ordinals is covered by a "nice" set from the core model of the same size.
- This lets one transfer combinatorial principles from L (or K) up to V.

**Worked Example:**

If there is no inner model with a Woodin cardinal, then the singular cardinals hypothesis holds: if κ is singular strong limit then 2^κ = κ^+. This follows from the covering lemma plus the fine structure of the core model.

## HOD and Other Definability-Based Inner Models

<!-- section: role-modern -->

HOD is always an inner model of ZFC. It is used to:
- Show that failures of choice, if they occur, must involve non-ordinal-definable sets.
- Study the "definable" part of the universe even when large cardinals are present (HOD may or may not contain the large cardinals).
- In the context of forcing axioms or determinacy, one studies HOD of L(ℝ) or of other models; often HOD of L(ℝ) under AD is a model of ZFC + "there is a Woodin cardinal."

Other definability notions (HOD of L(ℝ), generic HOD, etc.) are active areas of research connecting determinacy, large cardinals, and forcing.

**ELI5**

- HOD is "what you can define from ordinals." Even in a wild universe, the definable-from-ordinals part is always tame (satisfies choice).
- Under determinacy assumptions, the HOD of L(ℝ) turns out to be a very interesting inner model that itself contains large cardinals.

**Worked Example:**

Under AD^{L(ℝ)}, the model HOD^{L(ℝ)} satisfies ZFC + "there is a Woodin cardinal" (in fact a proper class of them, under stronger determinacy). This is a theorem of Woodin; it shows that determinacy produces inner models with large cardinals.

## Common Mistakes

- **Thinking every inner model is L**: L is the smallest; there are many others (L[U], HOD, core models, etc.) that can be larger and can satisfy stronger axioms.
- **Confusing inner models with forcing extensions**: Inner models are *subclasses* (usually proper) of V; forcing extensions are usually *larger* than the ground model.
- **Assuming that if M is an inner model then V ⊨ 'M ⊨ ZFC' is the same as M ⊨ ZFC**: Because M is transitive the satisfaction is absolute for most formulas, but one must be careful with class quantification and with parameters.
- **Forgetting that HOD always models ZFC**: This is true even when V does not; it is one of the most robust ways to recover choice inside ZF.
- **Believing that core models always exist**: Core models exist only below certain large cardinal thresholds; above Woodin cardinals (or supercompacts, depending on the technology) the core model theory is still incomplete. The non-existence of a core model is itself a strong statement (implies inner models with Woodins or stronger).
