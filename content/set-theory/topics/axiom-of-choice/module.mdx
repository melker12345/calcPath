# Axiom of Choice & Equivalents

The axiom of choice and its equivalents: Zorn's lemma, well-ordering theorems, and Tychonoff's theorem.

The axiom of choice (AC) asserts that for any collection of nonempty sets there exists a function that selects exactly one element from each set in the collection.

It is the most famous "extra" axiom in set theory: it is consistent with ZF, its negation is consistent with ZF, and it is equivalent to a host of powerful and sometimes counter-intuitive statements.

## The Statement

<!-- section: statement-ac -->

Let $X$ be a set whose elements are nonempty sets. Then there exists a function $f$ (a *choice function*) such that
\[
f(A) \in A \quad \text{for every } A \in X.
\]

No explicit rule or formula for choosing the elements is provided; the axiom merely guarantees that some such selector exists in the universe of sets.

**ELI5**

- You have an infinite shelf of boxes, each box nonempty. AC says there is a way to reach into every box and pull out one item from each, all at once, even if you have no uniform rule telling you which item to pick from a given box.
- For one box or finitely many boxes you do not need an axiom; you can just "pick." For infinitely many the axiom is required.

**Worked Example:**

Let $X$ be the set of all pairs of socks (assume infinitely many pairs, and the two socks in a pair are indistinguishable except by position). A choice function on $X$ picks, for each pair, one sock. There is no "left vs right" rule that works uniformly, so AC is what licenses the existence of the selection.

## Equivalents and Consequences

<!-- section: equivalents-and-consequences -->

Over ZF, AC is equivalent to:
- The well-ordering theorem (every set can be well-ordered).
- Zorn's lemma (a poset in which every chain has an upper bound has a maximal element).
- Every vector space has a basis.
- The product of any family of nonempty compact topological spaces is compact (Tychonoff).
- Every surjective function has a right inverse.
- etc.

Many everyday theorems in algebra, analysis, and topology rely on some form of choice.

**ELI5**

- AC is a "Swiss Army knife" that lets you perform infinitely many selections or maximality arguments in one go.
- Once you accept it, you get bases for weird vector spaces, ultrafilters, well-orderings of the reals, etc.

**Worked Example:**

To prove that if $f: A \twoheadrightarrow B$ is surjective then there is $g: B \to A$ with $f \circ g = \mathrm{id}_B$: for each $b \in B$ the preimage $f^{-1}(b)$ is nonempty; a choice function on the family of preimages gives the desired $g$.

## Why It Is Controversial

<!-- section: why-controversial -->

AC is non-constructive: the choice function is not given by any explicit description or algorithm.

Using AC one can prove the existence of highly pathological objects:
- Non-Lebesgue-measurable subsets of $\mathbb{R}$ (Vitali sets).
- Bases for $\mathbb{R}$ as a vector space over $\mathbb{Q}$ (Hamel bases) that are not continuous in any sense.
- Sets without the Baire property, etc.

Some mathematicians (especially in analysis or constructive mathematics) prefer to work in ZF + weaker choice principles or even ZF alone.

**ELI5**

- AC lets you "wave a magic wand" and assert selections exist without showing how to make them.
- The objects produced by the wand can be so weird that they have no explicit description and behave badly with respect to measure or category.

**Worked Example:**

Using a Hamel basis for $\mathbb{R}/\mathbb{Q}$ one can construct (highly discontinuous) solutions to Cauchy's functional equation $f(x+y)=f(x)+f(y)$ that are not of the form $f(x)=cx$. Such functions are not measurable and require AC for their existence.

## Weaker Forms of Choice

<!-- section: weaker-forms -->

- AC$_\omega$ (countable choice): choice functions exist for countable families.
- DC (dependent choice): given a relation $R$ on a nonempty set such that $\forall x \exists y\ xRy$, there is an infinite sequence $x_0 R x_1 R x_2 \dots$
- AC$_\kappa$ for specific cardinals, etc.

DC is sufficient for most of classical real analysis (Baire category theorem, sequential compactness arguments, etc.) and is strictly weaker than full AC. Many "countable" choice principles are enough for everyday mathematics while avoiding the worst pathologies.

**ELI5**

- Countable choice lets you make countably many independent picks.
- Dependent choice lets the pick at step n depend on what you picked before (like building an infinite path in a tree with no dead ends).
- Full AC lets you pick from arbitrarily large (even uncountable, non-well-orderable) families at once.

**Worked Example:**

The proof that a countable union of countable sets is countable requires (countable) choice to pick enumerations for each set in the union. In some models of ZF + ¬AC$_\omega$ this statement fails even for sets of reals.

## Common Mistakes

- **Thinking AC is needed to pick an element from a single nonempty set**: No axiom is required; the definition of "nonempty" already says there exists an element.
- **Believing AC is inconsistent with ZF**: Both ZF+AC and ZF+¬AC are consistent (relative to ZF).
- **Assuming every proof that uses AC can be rewritten without it**: Some results (e.g., "every vector space has a basis") are actually equivalent to AC over ZF.
- **Confusing AC with the continuum hypothesis**: CH is independent of ZFC; it can hold or fail whether or not AC holds.
- **Forgetting that many 'obvious' theorems require some choice**: "The union of countably many countable sets is countable", "every field has an algebraic closure", etc., need some fragment of AC.

Zorn's lemma is a maximality principle equivalent to the axiom of choice. It says that a partially ordered set in which every chain has an upper bound must possess at least one maximal element.

It is one of the most frequently applied forms of choice in algebra, analysis, and topology because it turns "every ascending chain can be continued" into "there is a maximal object."

## The Statement

<!-- section: statement-zorn -->

Let $(P, \le)$ be a nonempty partially ordered set. A *chain* in $P$ is a totally ordered subset. An *upper bound* for a chain $C$ is an element $u \in P$ such that $c \le u$ for all $c \in C$.

Zorn's lemma: If every chain in $P$ has an upper bound in $P$, then $P$ has a maximal element (an element $m$ such that no element of $P$ is strictly greater than $m$).

**ELI5**

- Imagine a poset as a collection of boxes with "is contained in or equal" arrows.
- If whenever you have a totally ordered stack of boxes you can find a box that sits above the whole stack, then there must be at least one box that has nothing above it.

**Worked Example:**

Take $P$ to be the set of all proper ideals of a ring $R$, ordered by inclusion. The union of any chain of proper ideals is still proper (if it contained 1 then 1 would already be in some member of the chain). By Zorn there is a maximal proper ideal — a maximal ideal.

## Equivalence to the Axiom of Choice

<!-- section: equivalents-zorn -->

Over ZF, Zorn's lemma is equivalent to AC (and therefore to the well-ordering theorem, Tychonoff, "every vector space has a basis," etc.).

One direction: from AC well-order the poset and build an ascending chain by transfinite recursion, adding the least possible extension at each stage; it must stop at a maximal element.

The converse: given a family of nonempty sets, consider the poset of partial choice functions ordered by extension; chains have upper bounds (unions); a maximal one must be a total choice function.

**ELI5**

- AC lets you pick; Zorn lets you keep picking "as far as possible" until you cannot pick any more; the "farthest" point is maximal.
- The two are interchangeable in proofs.

**Worked Example:**

To derive AC from Zorn: let $X$ be a set of nonempty sets. Let $P$ be the set of all functions $f$ whose domain is a subset of $X$ and $f(A) \in A$ for $A$ in the domain. Order $P$ by extension. Any chain has upper bound the union function. A maximal $f$ must have domain all of $X$, otherwise pick an element from a missed set and extend.

## Typical Applications

<!-- section: typical-uses -->

- Every vector space has a basis (poset of linearly independent subsets).
- Every ring has a maximal ideal (poset of proper ideals).
- Every field has an algebraic closure (poset of algebraic extensions).
- Hahn-Banach theorem (poset of partial linear extensions of a functional that stay dominated by a seminorm).
- Existence of ultrafilters (poset of proper filters).

In each case one verifies the chain-upper-bound hypothesis and concludes a maximal object with the desired property.

**ELI5**

- "Keep adding stuff that is still independent / proper / dominated / filter-like until you cannot add any more." The thing you end up with is maximal and therefore has the strong property you want (basis, maximal ideal, ultrafilter...).

**Worked Example:**

For Hahn-Banach: start with a linear functional defined on a subspace that is bounded by a seminorm p. The poset of all linear extensions still bounded by p. Union of a chain of such extensions is still linear and bounded by p. A maximal extension must be defined on the whole space (otherwise extend by one more dimension using the usual one-dimensional Hahn-Banach lemma).

## Proof Ideas and Why the Hypothesis Is Necessary

<!-- section: proof-ideas -->

The usual proof from AC uses a well-ordering of $P$ and transfinite recursion to build a chain that cannot be properly extended.

The hypothesis is essential: the poset of all finite subsets of an infinite set, ordered by inclusion, has the property that every chain has an upper bound that is still finite, yet it has no maximal element (you can always add one more point).

**ELI5**

- Without the "every chain can be bounded" rule you can have infinite ascending staircases that never reach a top floor; the lemma would fail.
- The hypothesis guarantees that any staircase you climb has a landing you can step onto, so eventually you must reach a floor with no higher floor.

**Worked Example:**

In the finite-subsets poset, the chain $\emptyset \subset \{a_1\} \subset \{a_1,a_2\} \subset \dots$ (enumerating the infinite ground set) has upper bounds that are still finite (any finite initial segment), but you can keep going forever; there is no maximal finite set.

## Common Misunderstandings

<!-- section: common-misunderstandings -->

Some frequent mistakes when applying or recalling Zorn's lemma for the first time.

## Common Mistakes

- **Stating Zorn as 'every poset has a maximal element'**: Counterexamples are trivial (any poset with no maximal element, e.g., the integers under $<$ or the finite-subsets example).
- **Forgetting to verify the chain-upper-bound hypothesis**: Many students apply Zorn to a poset where some chains have no upper bound inside the poset.
- **Confusing maximal with maximum**: A maximal element need not be above everything; it just has nothing above *it*. A maximum (greatest element) is above everything.
- **Thinking Zorn is weaker or stronger than AC**: They are equivalent over ZF.
- **Using Zorn when a constructive or choice-free proof exists**: Many results that 'look like' they need Zorn can be proved with weaker choice or even in ZF (e.g., for countable objects).

The well-ordering theorem states that every set admits a well-ordering: a total ordering of its elements such that every nonempty subset has a least element.

It is one of the most powerful and controversial consequences of the axiom of choice, and is fully equivalent to AC over ZF.

## The Statement

<!-- section: statement-wot -->

For any set $X$ there exists a binary relation $<$ on $X$ that is a strict total (linear) order and is well-founded: every nonempty $Y \subseteq X$ has a $<$-least element.

Equivalently, there is an ordinal $\alpha$ and a bijection $f: X \to \alpha$ such that the pulled-back order on $X$ is a well-ordering.

**ELI5**

- No matter how wild or large your collection of objects is, you can line them up so that there is always a "first" one in any subcollection.
- The lining-up may have nothing to do with any ordering you already had in mind.

**Worked Example:**

For a finite set the usual counting order works. For the naturals the usual order works. For the reals or for an arbitrary vector space the theorem guarantees some (highly non-constructive) lining-up exists.

## Equivalence to the Axiom of Choice

<!-- section: equivalents -->

Over ZF the following are equivalent:
- The axiom of choice.
- The well-ordering theorem.
- Zorn's lemma.
- Every vector space has a basis.
- Tychonoff's theorem for compact spaces.
- etc.

Proofs in both directions are standard but non-trivial; they usually go through transfinite recursion or Zorn.

**ELI5**

- AC says you can make infinitely many choices at once.
- Well-ordering says you can line everything up so that "choose the least remaining" becomes a definite rule that works for all subcollections at once.
- They are secretly the same power.

**Worked Example:**

From a well-ordering of the union of a family of nonempty sets, define the choice function by picking, from each set, the least element according to the global well-order. This uses no further choice.

## Important Consequences

<!-- section: consequences -->

- Every set has a cardinality that is an aleph (an initial ordinal).
- Every vector space has a Hamel basis.
- The reals (and any set) can be well-ordered (though not definably in general).
- Many "obvious" statements in algebra and analysis become provable only after assuming this or an equivalent.

**ELI5**

- Once everything is lined up, you can systematically pick "the smallest" to build bases, maximal ideals, ultrafilters, etc.
- Without the lining-up some of these constructions get stuck.

**Worked Example:**

To prove every vector space V has a basis: well-order V. By transfinite recursion include a vector v if it is linearly independent from all previously included vectors. At the end the collected set is a basis (spans by the well-order ensuring every vector was considered; independent by construction).

## Common Pitfalls and Independence

<!-- section: common-pitfalls -->

The well-ordering whose existence is asserted is usually completely non-constructive and has no relation to any "natural" order on the set.

The theorem is *not* provable in ZF alone. Gödel showed its consistency relative to ZF; Cohen showed its independence (there are models of ZF + "there is a set that cannot be well-ordered").

In such models many classical theorems fail (some vector spaces have no basis, etc.).

**ELI5**

- The theorem does not hand you an explicit list or formula for the ordering; it only says one exists somewhere in the universe of sets.
- In some (non-standard) universes of sets the theorem fails for some collections.

**Worked Example:**

It is consistent with ZF that the reals cannot be well-ordered. In such models there is no choice function on the family of nonempty subsets of $\\mathbb{R}$, and many analytic statements that rely on selecting representatives become unprovable or false.

## Common Mistakes

- **Thinking the usual order on the reals is a well-ordering**: It is a total order but (0,1) has no least element.
- **Believing the well-ordering theorem produces a definable or 'nice' ordering**: The proof via AC gives no explicit description.
- **Confusing it with the well-ordering of the ordinals themselves**: The ordinals are well-ordered by definition; the theorem is about *arbitrary* sets.
- **Assuming it is needed for all of mathematics**: Much of mathematics can be done in ZF alone or with weaker choice principles (dependent choice, countable choice); only when arbitrary families appear does full AC become necessary.
- **Forgetting that its negation is also consistent**: There are models where AC fails badly, yet ZF holds and a surprising amount of mathematics still works.

In the context of the axiom of choice, the well-ordering theorem asserts that every set can be well-ordered: its elements can be arranged in a total order so that every nonempty subset has a least element.

This is fully equivalent to AC over ZF and is one of the most direct expressions of the "power to make simultaneous choices."

## The Statement in the Context of AC

<!-- section: statement-wot2 -->

For any set $X$ there exists a relation $<$ on $X$ that is a strict linear order and is well-founded (every nonempty subset of $X$ has a $<$-least element).

Equivalently, there is an ordinal $\alpha$ and a bijection between $X$ and $\alpha$ that pulls back the ordinal ordering to a well-ordering of $X$.

**ELI5**

- No matter what $X$ is (reals, functions, sets of sets, ...), you can line its elements up like 0 < 1 < 2 < ... possibly going on past all the finite numbers, so that there is always a first element in any subcollection.
- The lining-up is guaranteed by AC but is usually not the 'natural' order you might have expected.

**Worked Example:**

For $X = \mathbb{R}$ the usual order is not a well-order (open intervals have no least element). The theorem says some other total well-order on $\mathbb{R}$ exists. The proof gives no formula for it.

## Equivalence with the Axiom of Choice

<!-- section: equivalence-ac -->

From AC to well-ordering: use a choice function on the nonempty subsets of $X$ to build the ordering by transfinite recursion — at each stage pick the "next" element from the remaining ones.

From a well-ordering of the union of a family of nonempty sets back to AC: from each set pick its least element according to the global well-order. This is a definite choice function; no further choice is required.

**ELI5**

- AC lets you pick "the next one" from whatever is left, over and over, possibly transfinitely many times; the result is a well-ordering.
- If you already have a well-ordering, picking the "first" according to it gives you the choices for free.

**Worked Example:**

Given a family $\{A_i\}_{i \in I}$ of nonempty sets, let $U = \bigcup A_i$ and let $<$ be a well-ordering of $U$ (exists by the theorem). Define $f(A_i)$ to be the $<$-least element of $A_i$. Then $f$ is a choice function.

## Uses in Mathematics

<!-- section: uses-in-math -->

- Every vector space has a basis (well-order the vectors; add each if independent of previous).
- Dimension is well-defined (any two bases have the same cardinality, proved by comparing via the well-orderings).
- Many existence proofs in algebra and set theory become possible once arbitrary selections or maximality arguments are licensed.

**ELI5**

- "Line everything up, then go through in order and keep what still satisfies the independent / maximal / whatever property." The result at the end is the desired object.
- Without the lining-up you cannot systematically decide what to keep when there are infinitely many candidates.

**Worked Example:**

Proof that every vector space V has a basis: well-order V. Define B by transfinite recursion: put v into B if v is not in the linear span of the elements already placed in B earlier in the well-ordering. B is linearly independent by construction and spans because every vector was considered at its stage.

## Pitfalls and Non-constructivity

<!-- section: pitfalls -->

The well-ordering guaranteed by the theorem is almost always non-constructive. It does not respect any topology, measure, or definable structure on the set.

In particular, a well-ordering of $\mathbb{R}$ produced via AC can be used to construct non-measurable sets, and the ordering relation itself, viewed as a subset of the plane, will typically fail to be Lebesgue measurable or to have the Baire property.

This shows that "existence" via AC does not imply "tame" or "explicit" or "measurable."

**ELI5**

- The theorem says a lining-up exists somewhere in the mathematical universe. It does not hand you a list or a rule you can write down or compute with.
- The lining-up can be so wild that it creates sets that cannot be measured or described nicely.

**Worked Example:**

Using a well-ordering of $\mathbb{R}$ one can pick, from each equivalence class of $\mathbb{R}/\mathbb{Q}$ (Vitali classes), the least element according to the well-ordering. The resulting Vitali set is non-Lebesgue-measurable. The construction relies on the well-ordering existing but gives no concrete description of the set.

## Common Mistakes

- **Thinking the theorem says the usual order on a set is a well-ordering**: For the reals, the integers, etc., the usual order is not well-founded in the required sense.
- **Believing the well-ordering is unique or canonical**: There are usually many; the theorem only asserts existence of at least one.
- **Assuming the well-ordering is definable or Borel or continuous**: In general it is none of these.
- **Forgetting that the theorem is equivalent to full AC**: Results that use the well-ordering theorem cannot in general be proved in ZF alone or with only countable choice.
- **Confusing ordinal well-ordering with arbitrary set well-ordering**: The ordinals are well-ordered by definition; the theorem is the non-trivial statement that *every* set can be made to look like an ordinal via a bijection.

Tychonoff's theorem asserts that an arbitrary (possibly uncountable) product of compact topological spaces is again compact in the product topology.

It is one of the most powerful and widely used theorems in topology and is equivalent to the axiom of choice over ZF.

## The Statement

<!-- section: statement-ty -->

Let $\{X_i\}_{i \in I}$ be any family of compact topological spaces. Then the product space
\[
\prod_{i \in I} X_i
\]
equipped with the product topology is compact.

The product topology is the coarsest topology making all the projection maps $\pi_j : \prod X_i \to X_j$ continuous. Its basic open sets are finite intersections of sets of the form $\pi_j^{-1}(U_j)$ where $U_j$ open in $X_j$.

**ELI5**

- Each factor is "small" in the sense that any cover by open sets has a finite subcover.
- When you take the product (all possible combinations of points, one from each factor), the theorem says the whole thing is still "small" in the same sense, provided you use the product topology (which only looks at finitely many coordinates at a time for basic open sets).

**Worked Example:**

The unit interval $[0,1]$ is compact. By Tychonoff the countable product $[0,1]^\mathbb{N}$ (the Hilbert cube) is compact in the product topology. Likewise $\{0,1\}^\mathbb{R}$ (all functions from reals to {0,1}) is compact, etc.

## Proof Ideas

<!-- section: proof-ideas -->

There are several proofs, all ultimately relying on some form of the axiom of choice.

One standard proof uses Zorn's lemma on the poset of families of closed sets that have the finite intersection property and contain the complements of the open sets from a given cover; a maximal such family must have nonempty total intersection (by compactness of the factors), yielding a point in every set of the cover, a contradiction.

Another approach uses the ultrafilter lemma (equivalent to the Boolean prime ideal theorem) or Alexander's subbase theorem to reduce the problem to subbasic covers.

**ELI5**

- You assume you have a cover of the product with no finite subcover. You build a "maximal" collection of "bad" closed sets that still has the finite intersection property. By the compactness of each coordinate you can find a point that lies in all of them, which would have to be covered, contradiction.
- Choice is used to guarantee the existence of the maximal collection or the ultrafilter.

**Worked Example:**

Suppose $\{U_\alpha\}$ is an open cover of $\prod X_i$ with no finite subcover. Consider the complements $F_\alpha = \prod X_i \setminus U_\alpha$. The family of all finite intersections of the $F_\alpha$ that are nonempty has the finite intersection property. Extend to a maximal family with this property (Zorn). Projecting to each coordinate and using compactness of $X_i$ produces a point whose every neighborhood meets the family, hence the point lies in every $F_\alpha$, contradicting that the $U_\alpha$ cover.

## Importance and Applications

<!-- section: importance -->

Tychonoff is the key tool that makes the product topology useful for compactness arguments.

Classic applications:
- The Hilbert cube is compact.
- Stone–Čech compactification exists (as a closed subset of a product of intervals).
- Alaoglu's theorem (unit ball of the dual of a Banach space is weak*-compact) — the dual ball is a product of intervals.
- Many universal spaces in topology are compact products or closed subsets thereof.

**ELI5**

- Once you know arbitrary products of compacts are compact, you can embed or represent many complicated objects as subsets of "big but still compact" products, and then use compactness to extract limits, fixed points, etc.

**Worked Example:**

To construct the Stone–Čech compactification $\beta X$ of a discrete space $X$, embed $X$ into the compact product $[0,1]^{C_b(X)}$ (all bounded continuous functions to [0,1]) via evaluation maps, then take the closure. Tychonoff supplies the compactness of the big product.

## Weaker Versions and Choice Strength

<!-- section: weaker-versions -->

The finite Tychonoff theorem (finite products of compacts are compact) is provable in ZF with no choice.

The countable Tychonoff theorem requires some countable choice (or DC) but is strictly weaker than full AC.

The full Tychonoff theorem for arbitrary products is equivalent to AC.

For compact Hausdorff spaces, Tychonoff is equivalent to the Boolean prime ideal theorem (BPI), which is weaker than full AC but still not provable in ZF.

**ELI5**

- For two or three compact spaces you can prove the product is compact by hand (or with very little choice).
- For countably many you need to make countably many choices of finite subcovers.
- For uncountably many families you need the full strength of being able to choose from arbitrarily large collections.

**Worked Example:**

In models of ZF + ¬AC it is consistent that the product of two compact spaces is not compact, or that the countable product of copies of {0,1} (Cantor space) fails to be compact. This shows that even "obvious" compactness of products is not choice-free.

## Common Mistakes

- **Thinking Tychonoff is 'obvious' from the definition of compactness**: The product topology has many more open sets than the 'box' topology; covers can be very large and the finite subcover must be extracted using choice.
- **Confusing the product topology with the box topology**: In the box topology (all coordinates open at once) Tychonoff fails even for two spaces in some cases.
- **Assuming Tychonoff holds in ZF**: It does not; the full statement is equivalent to AC.
- **Forgetting that the theorem is for *arbitrary* products**: The countable case is much weaker and is what is usually needed in analysis.
- **Believing the theorem produces a 'constructive' compactness**: Like AC, the finite subcover whose existence is asserted may not be explicitly describable.
