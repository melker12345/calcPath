# Axiom of choice

The axiom of choice (AC) asserts that for any collection of nonempty sets there exists a function that selects exactly one element from each set in the collection.

It is the most famous "extra" axiom in set theory: it is consistent with ZF, its negation is consistent with ZF, and it is equivalent to a host of powerful and sometimes counter-intuitive statements.

## The Statement

<!-- section: statement-ac -->

Let $X$ be a set whose elements are nonempty sets. Then there exists a function $f$ (a *choice function*) such that
\[
f(A) \in A \quad \text{for every } A \in X.
\]

No explicit rule or formula for choosing the elements is provided; the axiom merely guarantees that some such selector exists in the universe of sets.

**ELI5**

- You have an infinite shelf of boxes, each box nonempty. AC says there is a way to reach into every box and pull out one item from each, all at once, even if you have no uniform rule telling you which item to pick from a given box.
- For one box or finitely many boxes you do not need an axiom; you can just "pick." For infinitely many the axiom is required.

**Worked Example:**

Let $X$ be the set of all pairs of socks (assume infinitely many pairs, and the two socks in a pair are indistinguishable except by position). A choice function on $X$ picks, for each pair, one sock. There is no "left vs right" rule that works uniformly, so AC is what licenses the existence of the selection.

## Equivalents and Consequences

<!-- section: equivalents-and-consequences -->

Over ZF, AC is equivalent to:
- The well-ordering theorem (every set can be well-ordered).
- Zorn's lemma (a poset in which every chain has an upper bound has a maximal element).
- Every vector space has a basis.
- The product of any family of nonempty compact topological spaces is compact (Tychonoff).
- Every surjective function has a right inverse.
- etc.

Many everyday theorems in algebra, analysis, and topology rely on some form of choice.

**ELI5**

- AC is a "Swiss Army knife" that lets you perform infinitely many selections or maximality arguments in one go.
- Once you accept it, you get bases for weird vector spaces, ultrafilters, well-orderings of the reals, etc.

**Worked Example:**

To prove that if $f: A \twoheadrightarrow B$ is surjective then there is $g: B \to A$ with $f \circ g = \mathrm{id}_B$: for each $b \in B$ the preimage $f^{-1}(b)$ is nonempty; a choice function on the family of preimages gives the desired $g$.

## Why It Is Controversial

<!-- section: why-controversial -->

AC is non-constructive: the choice function is not given by any explicit description or algorithm.

Using AC one can prove the existence of highly pathological objects:
- Non-Lebesgue-measurable subsets of $\mathbb{R}$ (Vitali sets).
- Bases for $\mathbb{R}$ as a vector space over $\mathbb{Q}$ (Hamel bases) that are not continuous in any sense.
- Sets without the Baire property, etc.

Some mathematicians (especially in analysis or constructive mathematics) prefer to work in ZF + weaker choice principles or even ZF alone.

**ELI5**

- AC lets you "wave a magic wand" and assert selections exist without showing how to make them.
- The objects produced by the wand can be so weird that they have no explicit description and behave badly with respect to measure or category.

**Worked Example:**

Using a Hamel basis for $\mathbb{R}/\mathbb{Q}$ one can construct (highly discontinuous) solutions to Cauchy's functional equation $f(x+y)=f(x)+f(y)$ that are not of the form $f(x)=cx$. Such functions are not measurable and require AC for their existence.

## Weaker Forms of Choice

<!-- section: weaker-forms -->

- AC$_\omega$ (countable choice): choice functions exist for countable families.
- DC (dependent choice): given a relation $R$ on a nonempty set such that $\forall x \exists y\ xRy$, there is an infinite sequence $x_0 R x_1 R x_2 \dots$
- AC$_\kappa$ for specific cardinals, etc.

DC is sufficient for most of classical real analysis (Baire category theorem, sequential compactness arguments, etc.) and is strictly weaker than full AC. Many "countable" choice principles are enough for everyday mathematics while avoiding the worst pathologies.

**ELI5**

- Countable choice lets you make countably many independent picks.
- Dependent choice lets the pick at step n depend on what you picked before (like building an infinite path in a tree with no dead ends).
- Full AC lets you pick from arbitrarily large (even uncountable, non-well-orderable) families at once.

**Worked Example:**

The proof that a countable union of countable sets is countable requires (countable) choice to pick enumerations for each set in the union. In some models of ZF + ¬AC$_\omega$ this statement fails even for sets of reals.

## Common Mistakes

- **Thinking AC is needed to pick an element from a single nonempty set**: No axiom is required; the definition of "nonempty" already says there exists an element.
- **Believing AC is inconsistent with ZF**: Both ZF+AC and ZF+¬AC are consistent (relative to ZF).
- **Assuming every proof that uses AC can be rewritten without it**: Some results (e.g., "every vector space has a basis") are actually equivalent to AC over ZF.
- **Confusing AC with the continuum hypothesis**: CH is independent of ZFC; it can hold or fail whether or not AC holds.
- **Forgetting that many 'obvious' theorems require some choice**: "The union of countably many countable sets is countable", "every field has an algebraic closure", etc., need some fragment of AC.
