# Imaginary unit

The imaginary unit i is the number defined by the equation i² = -1.

It is not a "real" number in the usual sense, but a new mathematical object that we adjoin to the reals to form the complex numbers. With i we can take square roots of negative numbers and solve every polynomial equation.

Although called "imaginary," i has profound real-world applications in physics, engineering, and mathematics.

## Definition and Powers of i

<!-- section: definition-and-powers-of-i -->

i is defined so that i * i = -1.

Higher powers of i follow a simple 4-cycle because i^4 = (i^2)^2 = (-1)^2 = 1, and then the pattern repeats:

i^1 = i

i^2 = -1

i^3 = -i

i^4 = 1

i^5 = i

...

To compute i^k for large k, reduce the exponent modulo 4.

**ELI5**

- i is a "90-degree turn" button in number land. Pressing it twice gets you to -1.
- The cycle of 4 comes from going around the circle of directions: up, left, down, right, repeat.

**Worked Example:**

1. $i^7 = i^{4+3} = i^4 \cdot i^3 = 1 \cdot (-i) = -i$.

**Worked Example:**

1. $i^{100}$: $100 \mod 4 = 0$, so $i^{100} = (i^4)^{25} = 1^{25} = 1$.

## Geometric Interpretation

<!-- section: geometric-interpretation -->

In the complex plane, the real numbers lie on the horizontal axis and pure imaginaries on the vertical axis.

Multiplying any complex number z by i rotates z counterclockwise by 90 degrees around the origin, without changing its distance from zero.

Multiplying by i again rotates another 90° (total 180°, which multiplies by -1), and so on.

**ELI5**

- i itself sits at (0,1) — one unit straight up.
- Multiplying by i is like turning the arrow representing the number 90° left (counterclockwise).

**Worked Example:**

1. Start with $1$ (point $(1,0)$). $\cdot i \to i$ (point $(0,1)$). $\cdot i \to -1$ (point $(-1,0)$). $\cdot i \to -i$ (point $(0,-1)$). $\cdot i \to 1$ again.

**Worked Example:**

1. Take $2 + 3i$ (arrow to $(2,3)$). Multiply by $i$: $i(2 + 3i) = 2i + 3 i^2 = -3 + 2i$ (arrow to $(-3,2)$ — rotated $90^\circ$ CCW).

## Role in the Complex Numbers

<!-- section: role-in-complex -->

Every complex number can be written using real coefficients times 1 and i: a + b i.

The introduction of i lets us solve x^2 + 1 = 0 (roots ±i) and, more generally, makes the complex numbers algebraically closed.

i appears in Euler's formula e^{i θ} = cos θ + i sin θ, which connects exponentials, trig, and complex numbers.

**ELI5**

- With i we "complete" the number system so nothing is missing when we try to solve equations.
- It is the secret ingredient that makes many formulas in science and engineering work beautifully.

**Worked Example:**

1. Solve $x^2 + 9 = 0$: $x^2 = -9$, so $x = \pm 3i$.

**Worked Example:**

1. $i$ appears in the formula for roots of unity and in Fourier analysis.

## Common Mistakes

- **Thinking i is a variable**: It is a fixed constant, like π or e.
- **Computing i^2 as +1**: The whole point is that it equals -1.
- **Forgetting the cycle when reducing large powers**: Always mod 4.
- **Writing √(-4) = ± 2i without specifying principal**: The principal square root is the one with non-negative real part or the convention 2i.
- **Assuming all powers of i are imaginary**: i^4=1 is real, i^2=-1 is real.
- **Confusing i with the engineering j**: Same thing, different letter in some fields.
- **Trying to order complex numbers on the line**: They live in a plane; there is no total order compatible with the arithmetic like on the reals.
