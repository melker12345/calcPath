# Factorization

Factorization (or factoring) is the process of rewriting a polynomial as a product of simpler polynomials (or constants). It is the reverse of expanding.

Factoring is one of the most powerful tools in algebra: it lets us solve polynomial equations, simplify rational expressions, find zeros of functions, and recognize patterns that would otherwise be hidden. The goal is always to factor completely over the reals (or as far as possible).

## Greatest Common Factor (GCF)

<!-- section: greatest-common-factor -->

The first step with any polynomial is to factor out the greatest common factor of all its terms, if it exists and is not 1.

For the GCF: take the GCF of the numerical coefficients and the lowest power of each variable that appears in every term.

After pulling out the GCF, the remaining polynomial should have no common factor greater than 1 in all terms.

**ELI5**

- The GCF is the biggest "chunk" that divides every term evenly. Pull it out front like a common multiplier.
- What is left inside is the "reduced" version of the expression.

**Worked Example:**

1. For $12x^4 - 8x^3 + 4x^2$, GCF of coeffs is 4, lowest x power is $x^2$, so $4x^2$. Inside: $3x^2 - 2x + 1$. Thus $4x^2 (3x^2 - 2x + 1)$.

**Worked Example:**

1. $6a^2 b^3 - 9 a b^4 + 3 a b^2 = 3 a b^2 (2 a b - 3 b^2 + 1)$.

## Factoring by Grouping

<!-- section: grouping -->

When a polynomial has four or more terms, try grouping them in pairs (or sometimes first three + last) so that a common binomial factor appears.

After factoring each group, a common factor should emerge that can be pulled out.

If grouping does not produce a common factor, try a different grouping or conclude it does not factor nicely that way.

**ELI5**

- Grouping is like pairing socks so that each pair has something in common; then the pairs themselves share a bigger pattern.
- It turns a messy four-term expression into two factors.

**Worked Example:**

1. $x^3 + x^2 + 4x + 4 = (x^3 + x^2) + (4x + 4) = x^2(x + 1) + 4(x + 1) = (x^2 + 4)(x + 1)$.

**Worked Example:**

1. $2x^3 - 6x^2 - 5x + 15 = (2x^3 - 6x^2) + (-5x + 15) = 2x^2(x - 3) - 5(x - 3) = (2x^2 - 5)(x - 3)$.

## Factoring Trinomials

<!-- section: factoring-trinomials -->

For a quadratic trinomial $ax^2 + bx + c$ (a ≠ 0):

- If a = 1: look for two numbers m, n such that m n = c and m + n = b. Then (x + m)(x + n).
- If a > 1: either use trial and error with factors of a and of c, or the "ac method": find factors of a c that add to b, split the middle term, then group.

Always check by expanding the proposed factors.

Many trinomials do not factor nicely over the integers (prime over the integers).

**ELI5**

- You are looking for two binomials whose "outer" and "inner" cross terms will add up to the middle coefficient.
- It's like a number puzzle: find the right pair that multiplies to the constant and adds to the linear coeff.

**Worked Example:**

1. Factor $x^2 - 5x + 6$. Numbers that multiply to 6 and add to -5: -2 and -3. So $(x - 2)(x - 3)$.

**Worked Example:**

1. Factor $3x^2 + 10x - 8$. $ac = 3 \cdot (-8) = -24$. Factors of -24 that add to 10: 12 and -2. Split middle: $3x^2 +12x -2x -8$. Group: $3x(x + 4) -2(x + 4) = (3x - 2)(x + 4)$.

## Using Special Identities

<!-- section: using-special-identities -->

Memorize and recognize:

- Difference of squares: $a^2 - b^2 = (a - b)(a + b)$
- Perfect square trinomials: $a^2 + 2ab + b^2 = (a + b)^2$, $a^2 - 2ab + b^2 = (a - b)^2$
- Sum and difference of cubes (see dedicated lessons): $a^3 + b^3 = (a + b)(a^2 - ab + b^2)$, $a^3 - b^3 = (a - b)(a^2 + ab + b^2)$

These allow instant factoring when the polynomial matches the left-hand side exactly.

**ELI5**

- These are the "cheat codes" of factoring. If it looks exactly like one of the patterns, you get the factors for free.
- Always check the signs and the middle cross term (or its absence).

**Worked Example:**

1. $9x^2 - 25 = (3x)^2 - 5^2 = (3x - 5)(3x + 5)$.

**Worked Example:**

1. $ x^2 + 6x + 9 = (x + 3)^2 $ (perfect square, notice the middle is twice $3 \cdot x$).

## Common Mistakes

- **Forgetting to factor out the GCF first**: You may miss the complete factorization or make numbers harder.
- **Wrong sign pairs when factoring trinomials**: If product positive but sum negative, both numbers negative.
- **Stopping too early**: "Factor completely" means keep going until no factor can be factored further over the reals.
- **Assuming every quadratic factors**: Many quadratics are irreducible (discriminant negative or not perfect square for integer coeffs).
- **Mishandling the ac-method split**: The two numbers must multiply to a*c and add to b; then attach the correct original a coefficient to the groups.
- **Confusing sum of squares with difference of squares**: x^2 + 4 cannot be factored over the reals (no real b such that b^2 = -4).
- **Forgetting to check by multiplying back**: Always expand your factors to verify they equal the original polynomial.
