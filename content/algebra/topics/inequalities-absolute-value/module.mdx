# Inequalities & Absolute Value

Absolute value, linear and quadratic inequalities, and compound inequality techniques.

The absolute value of a real number x, written |x|, is its distance from zero on the number line. Because distance is never negative, |x| ≥ 0 for every real x, and |x| = 0 only when x = 0.

Absolute value is one of the first functions students meet that is not a straight line or simple power; its graph is a V and it appears in distance formulas, error analysis, and defining piecewise functions.

## Definition by Cases

<!-- section: definition -->

For any real number x:

|x| = x   if x ≥ 0

|x| = −x  if x < 0

This piecewise definition captures the idea that absolute value "folds" the negative half of the number line onto the positive side.

**ELI5**

- Absolute value is the "no negatives allowed" rule for distance.
- Negative numbers are on the left of zero; absolute value measures how far left or right without caring about direction.

**Worked Example:**

|−4| = 4 because −4 is four steps left of zero; distance is 4.

|3.5| = 3.5 because it is already on the right side.

## Important Properties

<!-- section: properties -->

- |x| ≥ 0, with equality only at x = 0.
- |−x| = |x|.
- |x y| = |x| |y|.
- |x / y| = |x| / |y| (y ≠ 0).
- |x + y| ≤ |x| + |y| (triangle inequality).

These make absolute value a norm and a useful tool in analysis.

**ELI5**

- Flipping the sign of x never changes its distance from zero.
- Distances multiply and divide nicely; the triangle inequality says the direct path is never longer than going via an extra point.

**Worked Example:**

| (−3) × 4 | = |−12| = 12, and |−3| × |4| = 3 × 4 = 12, matching the product rule.

## Distance Interpretation

<!-- section: distance-interpretation -->

The expression |x − a| gives the distance between the points x and a on the real line.

This interpretation turns many absolute-value equations and inequalities into statements about "points that are a certain distance from a center."

**ELI5**

- |x − 5| = 2 means "x is exactly two units away from 5," so x = 7 or x = 3.
- It is the number-line version of "how far is the house from the school?"

**Worked Example:**

| x − 10 | = 4 means the points exactly 4 units from 10: x = 14 and x = 6.

## Graphs of Absolute Value Functions

<!-- section: graphs -->

y = |x| is the basic V with vertex (0, 0), opening upward.

y = |x − h| + k is the same V shifted h units horizontally and k units vertically. The vertex is at (h, k) and the minimum value is k.

**ELI5**

- The V is like a valley; the bottom is the closest you can get to the x-axis.
- Shifting the V moves the "bottom point" around the plane.

**Worked Example:**

y = |x − 3| + 2 has vertex at (3, 2). It touches y = 2 at x = 3 and rises with slope ±1 on either side.

## Common Mistakes

- **Thinking |−5| = −5**: Absolute value is never negative.
- **Writing |x| = x as an identity**: True only for x ≥ 0.
- **Forgetting the triangle inequality direction**: |x + y| can be smaller than |x| + |y| when x and y have opposite signs.
- **Confusing |x| with x²**: |x| gives distance; x² gives area of square or squared distance.
- **Dropping the bars incorrectly when solving**: |x| = 3 becomes x = 3 or x = −3; you cannot simply remove bars without considering cases.

An inequality compares two expressions and tells which is larger, smaller, or that they are not equal. The four symbols <, >, ≤, ≥ (and ≠) turn expressions into statements that are true for ranges of values rather than single points.

Solving inequalities follows the same steps as equations with the single extra rule about reversing direction when multiplying or dividing by negatives.

## Solving Linear Inequalities

<!-- section: solving-techniques -->

Apply inverse operations to isolate the variable, exactly as with equations.

The direction of the inequality symbol stays the same when you:

- Add or subtract any number.
- Multiply or divide by a positive number.

**ELI5**

- Adding the same amount to two piles never changes which pile is bigger.
- Multiplying by a positive number keeps the order; the piles just get scaled the same way.

**Worked Example:**

Solve 5x − 3 > 12.

Add 3: 5x > 15.

Divide by 5: x > 3.

## The Flipping Rule

<!-- section: flipping-rule -->

When you multiply or divide both sides by a negative number, the inequality symbol must be reversed.

This is the only operation that changes the direction. Always check the sign of the number you are multiplying or dividing by.

**ELI5**

- Multiplying by a negative is like turning the number line around; left becomes right.
- The "greater than" relationship flips when everything is mirrored.

**Worked Example:**

−4x < 12.

Divide by −4 and flip: x > −3.

Verification: pick x = −2 (> −3). −4(−2) = 8 which is indeed < 12. Pick x = −4 (< −3). −4(−4) = 16 which is not < 12. Correct flip.

## Interval Notation

<!-- section: interval-notation -->

Interval notation gives a compact symbolic description of solution sets:

- (a, b) open interval: a < x < b
- [a, b] closed interval: a ≤ x ≤ b
- (a, b] half-open, etc.
- (−∞, b) or [a, ∞) for unbounded rays.
- ∪ for union of disjoint intervals.

**ELI5**

- Parentheses say "not including the fence post"; square brackets say "including the fence post."
- Infinity symbols mean the solution goes on forever in that direction.

**Worked Example:**

The solution x ≤ 4 or x > 7 is written (−∞, 4] ∪ (7, ∞).

## Graphing on the Number Line

<!-- section: graphing -->

Every solution set of a one-variable inequality can be pictured on the real number line.

Use open circles for strict endpoints, closed circles for included endpoints, and arrows or heavy shading for the included region.

**ELI5**

- The number line is a road; circles are gates that are open or locked, and the shading tells which stretch of road is allowed.

**Worked Example:**

x ≥ −1 is drawn with a filled circle at −1 and shading extending right to +∞.

## Common Mistakes

- **Forgetting to flip when multiplying or dividing by a negative**: The most frequent and costly error.
- **Using a closed circle for a strict inequality in the graph**: < and > never include the endpoint.
- **Writing the answer with the variable on the right side**: 5 > x is correct but x < 5 is preferred for readability.
- **Treating compound "and" inequalities as separate solves without intersecting the results**: Both conditions must hold simultaneously.
- **Dividing by an expression containing the variable without knowing its sign**: You cannot decide the flip without cases.

An absolute value equation contains the absolute value of an expression set equal to a number or another expression. Because absolute value "folds" the number line, these equations typically produce two solutions, one on each side of the "fold" point.

The case method (splitting into positive and negative branches) or the property |A| = |B| ⇔ A = B or A = −B are the standard tools.

## The Case Method

<!-- section: case-method -->

To solve |expression| = k (k ≥ 0):

Case 1: expression = k

Case 2: expression = −k

Solve each linear equation and collect the solutions.

If the right side is negative, there are no solutions because | | ≥ 0.

**ELI5**

- The absolute value bars hide whether the inside is positive or negative; we must consider both possibilities explicitly.
- It's like the expression could be the "positive twin" or the "negative twin" of the right-hand side.

**Worked Example:**

1. Solve $|x - 2| = 3$.
2. Case 1: $x - 2 = 3 \to x = 5$.
3. Case 2: $x - 2 = -3 \to x = -1$.
4. Both check: $|5 - 2| = 3$ and $|-1 - 2| = 3$.

## Using Absolute Value Properties

<!-- section: properties-method -->

When both sides are absolute values, |A| = |B| is equivalent to A = B or A = −B.

This often avoids writing full cases and is especially clean when the expressions are linear.

**ELI5**

- Two distances from zero are equal when the numbers themselves are the same or exact opposites.
- You still end up with two linear equations, but the setup is quicker.

**Worked Example:**

1. Solve $|x + 1| = |x - 4|$.
2. $x + 1 = x - 4$ (no solution) or $x + 1 = -(x - 4) \to 2x = 3 \to x = 3/2$.
3. Check: $|3/2 + 1| = 2.5$ and $|3/2 - 4| = 2.5$. Good.

## Extraneous Solutions

<!-- section: extraneous-solutions -->

Although the case method rarely introduces extraneous roots, students frequently square both sides of |A| = B to obtain A² = B² and then must verify.

Any candidate that makes the original inside expression produce a negative on the right side, or that fails substitution, is extraneous.

**ELI5**

- Squaring is a "blunt instrument" that can create extra answers that only work after you squared.
- Always plug every candidate back into the equation that still has the bars.

**Worked Example:**

1. $|x| = x + 1$. Squaring gives $x^2 = (x + 1)^2 \to x^2 = x^2 + 2x + 1 \to 0 = 2x + 1 \to x = -1/2$.
2. But $|-1/2| = 1/2$ and $-1/2 + 1 = 1/2$, wait it works? Bad example. Classic: $|x| = x - 1$ has no solution because left $\geq 0$ implies right $\geq 0$ so $x \geq 1$, but then $|x| = x$ contradicts $x - 1$. After squaring you may get $x = 1/2$ which fails original.

## Examples

<!-- section: examples -->

Practice with a variety of right-hand sides and expressions inside the bars builds fluency.

Always consider whether the right-hand side can possibly equal an absolute value (must be ≥ 0).

**ELI5**

- Draw the number line, mark the "center" inside the bars, and measure the given distance on both sides.
- The two points symmetric about the center are the solutions.

**Worked Example:**

1. Solve $|2x + 1| = 5$.
2. $2x + 1 = 5 \to 2x = 4 \to x = 2$.
3. $2x + 1 = -5 \to 2x = -6 \to x = -3$.
4. Verify both.

## Common Mistakes

- **Forgetting that |A| = k has no solution when k < 0**: Absolute value cannot equal a negative.
- **Solving only one case**: There are almost always two solutions; both branches must be solved.
- **Not checking after squaring**: x = 1/2 is a common extraneous root for equations like |x| = x − 1.
- **Dropping the bars without writing the ± case**: |x − 3| = 4 is not the same as x − 3 = 4.
- **Confusing absolute value equations with inequalities**: The solution sets look different (points vs intervals).

Absolute value inequalities describe intervals or unions of intervals on the number line. They translate the idea "the distance from a center is less than / greater than a certain amount" into algebraic statements.

The solutions are either a single bounded interval ("and") or two unbounded rays ("or"), depending on whether the inequality is < / ≤ or > / ≥.

## Case or Property Approach

<!-- section: case-or-property -->

For |A| < k (k > 0) rewrite as −k < A < k (the "and" form).

For |A| > k (k > 0) rewrite as A < −k or A > k (the "or" form).

The same logic applies to ≤ and ≥, simply including the endpoints.

**ELI5**

- Less than means "inside the fence" around the center: both left fence and right fence conditions must be true at once ("and").
- Greater than means "outside the fence": you are either far left or far right ("or").

**Worked Example:**

1. $|x - 4| \leq 1$ means $-1 \leq x - 4 \leq 1 \to 3 \leq x \leq 5$. The closed interval centered at 4 with radius 1.

## "And" versus "Or" Logic

<!-- section: and-vs-or -->

Strictly less/greater than or equal produces the compound connector:

- |A| < k or |A| ≤ k → compound "and" (intersection) → one interval.
- |A| > k or |A| ≥ k → compound "or" (union) → two rays.

When k = 0 the behavior is special: |A| > 0 is all reals except 0; |A| ≥ 0 is all reals.

**ELI5**

- "Inside the distance limit" requires being on the correct side of both boundaries.
- "Outside the distance limit" is satisfied as soon as you cross either boundary.

**Worked Example:**

1. $|x| > 2$ is $x < -2$ or $x > 2$. Two separate pieces.

## Graphing the Solutions

<!-- section: graphing-solutions -->

Translate the algebraic solution set directly onto the number line using the same circle and shading conventions as ordinary inequalities.

- < or > : open circles.
- ≤ or ≥ : closed circles.
- "And" interval gets a single shaded segment.
- "Or" gets two shaded rays.

**ELI5**

- The graph is the picture of the "allowed zone" described by the distance condition.
- The center of the absolute value expression is the center of symmetry of the solution set.

**Worked Example:**

1. $|x + 1| \geq 3 \to x \leq -4$ or $x \geq 2$. Open circles at $-4$ and 2? Wait $\geq$ so closed at $-4$ and 2, rays outward.

## Examples

<!-- section: examples -->

A few varied centers and radii build recognition of the pattern.

Always remember that the right-hand side of a < or > absolute value inequality must be non-negative for real solutions to exist in the usual way (negative right side yields empty or all-reals depending on direction).

**ELI5**

- Draw the center point first, mark the radius distance on both sides, then decide whether you want the inside strip or the two outer pieces.

**Worked Example:**

1. Solve $|2x - 3| > 5$.
2. $2x - 3 > 5$ or $2x - 3 < -5$.
3. $2x > 8 \to x > 4$ or $2x < -2 \to x < -1$.
4. Solution: $(-\infty, -1) \cup (4, \infty)$.

## Common Mistakes

- **Using "and" for a > inequality**: Greater absolute value means the two outer regions, which is "or".
- **Forgetting to flip both inequality signs when multiplying a compound by a negative**: −3 < 2x < 6 becomes −3/2 > x > −3 after dividing? No: divide all by 2 keeps, but if negative coefficient you must reverse all three parts.
- **Writing a single interval for a > problem**: That would be the "inside" solution, the opposite of what > asks.
- **Ignoring that |A| < 0 has no solution**: Absolute value cannot be negative.
- **Placing the circles at the wrong locations**: Solve for the boundary points first by setting |A| = k, then decide open/closed and direction of shading from the compound.

A quadratic inequality is an inequality that can be written with a quadratic expression on one side and zero on the other. The solution is usually an interval or a union of two intervals on the real line.

Because a quadratic changes sign only at its roots (when they exist), we locate the roots and then determine the sign in each interval they create.

## Moving to Standard Form and Factoring

<!-- section: factoring-first -->

The first step is almost always to bring every term to the left side so the inequality reads quadratic expression  ?  0.

Then factor (or use quadratic formula for roots) to find the critical numbers where the expression is zero.

**ELI5**

- Getting everything on one side lets you ask a simple question: "where is this expression positive or negative?"
- Factoring turns the expression into a product whose sign is easy to read from the factors.

**Worked Example:**

x² − 3x − 10 > 0 becomes (x − 5)(x + 2) > 0 after factoring.

## The Sign Chart (or Test-Point) Method

<!-- section: sign-chart -->

1. Find the roots and draw them on a number line; they divide the line into intervals.
2. Pick one convenient test point in each open interval.
3. Plug the test point into the quadratic (or evaluate the factored form) and record the sign.
4. Mark the intervals where the sign matches the inequality.
5. Decide whether to include the roots based on ≤ or ≥.

**ELI5**

- The roots are the "fence posts." Between posts the expression keeps the same "mood" (positive or negative).
- One quick plug per room tells you which rooms are allowed.

**Worked Example:**

For (x − 5)(x + 2) > 0 the posts are −2 and 5.

Test x = −3: (+) (negative) = negative.

Test x = 0: (−)(+) = negative.

Test x = 6: (+)(+) = positive.

Solution: x < −2 or x > 5.

## Leading Coefficient and Parabola Direction

<!-- section: test-points -->

The sign of the leading coefficient tells the end behavior of the parabola:

Positive a → opens up → positive far left and far right.

Negative a → opens down → negative far left and far right.

This lets you fill the outer intervals without testing once you know one middle interval.

**ELI5**

- If the bowl opens upward the far ends are high (positive). The middle may dip below zero between the roots.
- Downward bowl starts negative at both infinities.

**Worked Example:**

−x² + 5x + 6 ≤ 0 has negative leading coefficient. After factoring or testing you discover the solution is everywhere except the interval between the roots (or including depending on symbol).

## Writing the Solution in Interval Notation

<!-- section: solution-in-interval -->

Translate the marked intervals on the sign chart into interval notation, using ∪ for separate pieces.

Include square brackets at roots only when the inequality allows equality.

**ELI5**

- The chart picture becomes the compact math sentence that a computer or another person can read without the drawing.

**Worked Example:**

From the earlier chart: (−∞, −2) ∪ (5, ∞).

If the inequality had been ≥ the solution would be (−∞, −2] ∪ [5, ∞).

## Common Mistakes

- **Testing the roots themselves for sign**: At a root the value is exactly zero; you already know the sign change happens there.
- **Forgetting to consider the leading coefficient when predicting outer signs**: Saves time and catches errors.
- **Including or excluding roots incorrectly**: < and > never include; ≤ and ≥ do.
- **Not bringing all terms to one side first**: Mixed signs on right side make test points confusing.
- **Assuming every quadratic inequality has solutions**: When the parabola never crosses the required side there may be no solution or the entire line.
