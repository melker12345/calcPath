# Rational equation

A rational equation sets two rational expressions equal (or a rational expression equal to a number). The presence of variables in denominators means we must exclude values that make any denominator zero; those values can never be solutions.

Clearing denominators by multiplication produces a polynomial equation that is easier to solve, but the resulting candidates must be checked in the original equation.

## Definition and Domain Restrictions

<!-- section: definition -->

A rational expression is a quotient of two polynomials. A rational equation contains at least one such expression.

The domain of the equation (allowed values of the variable) excludes every root of every denominator.

**ELI5**

- You cannot divide by zero, so any number that would put a zero in a denominator is forbidden from the start.
- The equation only "exists" for the other numbers.

**Worked Example:**

In 2/(x − 3) = 1/x the variable cannot be 3 or 0.

## Clearing Denominators

<!-- section: clearing-denominators -->

Multiply every term on both sides by the least common multiple of all denominators appearing in the equation.

This produces an equivalent equation (for values in the domain) that contains no fractions.

**ELI5**

- Multiplying by the "magic number" that is a multiple of every bottom turns every fraction into a whole number or polynomial.
- You are allowed to do this only because you already excluded the zeros.

**Worked Example:**

Solve 1/x + 1/3 = 1/2.

LCM of denominators is 6x.

Multiply through: 6 + 2x = 3x.

6 = x.

Check x = 6 is not 0; it works.

## Extraneous Roots

<!-- section: extraneous-roots -->

Any root of the cleared polynomial equation that makes a denominator zero in the original is extraneous and must be discarded.

Even when the multiplication step itself does not introduce them, substitution is the only way to be sure a candidate is valid.

**ELI5**

- The multiplication step can "hide" the fact that we divided by zero in disguise.
- Checking is the honesty step that keeps only the answers that were legal all along.

**Worked Example:**

1. Solve $x/(x - 2) = 2/(x - 2) + 1$.
2. Multiply by $(x - 2)$: $x = 2 + (x - 2) \to 0 = 0$, identity.
3. But original is undefined at $x = 2$, so solution is all reals except 2.

## Examples

<!-- section: examples -->

Practice includes proportions, equations with several fractions, and equations that become quadratic after clearing.

Always state excluded values first, solve, then filter.

**ELI5**

- Write the "forbidden list" before you start so you remember to reject them at the end.
- The algebra gives candidates; the original equation is the final judge.

**Worked Example:**

1. Solve $1/(x + 1) - 1/(x - 1) = 1/x$.
2. LCM = $x(x + 1)(x - 1)$.
3. After clearing and simplifying: $x = -2$ (after quadratic or linear steps).
4. Check: denominators $-2+1=-1$, $-2-1=-3$, $-2$ all nonzero. Good.

## Common Mistakes

- **Forgetting to list excluded values before multiplying**: You will not know which roots to throw away later.
- **Multiplying only one side or only some terms**: Every term on both sides must be multiplied by the LCM.
- **Keeping a root that zeros a denominator**: It never was a solution; the original equation was not defined there.
- **Canceling a variable factor without considering it could be zero**: That is exactly when extraneous roots appear.
- **Assuming the cleared equation has the same domain as the original**: The polynomial equation is defined everywhere; the rational one is not.
