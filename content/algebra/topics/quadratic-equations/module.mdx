# Quadratic Equations

Quadratic equations, the quadratic formula, the discriminant, and rational equations.

A quadratic equation contains a squared term as its highest power of the variable. In standard form it is ax² + bx + c = 0 with a ≠ 0.

Because the graph of the related function is a parabola, a quadratic equation can have zero, one, or two real solutions. This richer behavior compared with linear equations makes quadratics central to algebra.

## Standard Form and Terminology

<!-- section: standard-form -->

ax² + bx + c = 0 is called standard form.

a is the leading coefficient, b the linear coefficient, c the constant term.

The solutions are often called roots, zeros, or x-intercepts.

**ELI5**

- The x² term makes the sides of the "bowl" curve; the higher the power the faster it grows.
- a tells whether the bowl opens upward (a > 0) or downward (a < 0).

**Worked Example:**

x² − 5x + 6 = 0 has a = 1, b = −5, c = 6. It factors nicely and has two roots.

## Solving by Factoring

<!-- section: solving-by-factoring -->

When a quadratic factors into (px + q)(rx + s) = 0, the zero-product property immediately gives the two roots by setting each factor to zero.

Many quadratics are factorable over the integers after a little practice with looking for factor pairs of c that add to b.

**ELI5**

- Factoring turns a single hard puzzle into two easy linear puzzles.
- If two numbers multiply to zero, one of them has to be the zero.

**Worked Example:**

x² − 7x + 12 = 0 factors as (x − 3)(x − 4) = 0.

Therefore x − 3 = 0 → x = 3 or x − 4 = 0 → x = 4.

## Graph and Roots Relationship

<!-- section: graph-and-roots -->

The real solutions of ax² + bx + c = 0 are exactly the x-values where the parabola y = ax² + bx + c crosses or touches the x-axis.

If the parabola opens upward and the vertex is above the x-axis there are no real roots; if it touches there is one (repeated) root; if it crosses there are two.

**ELI5**

- Picture throwing a ball; the times it is at ground level are the roots of the height equation.
- The picture of the parabola immediately tells you how many times it hits the ground.

**Worked Example:**

For y = x² − 4 the parabola crosses x-axis at x = −2 and x = 2, matching the equation x² − 4 = 0.

## Real-World Applications

<!-- section: applications -->

Quadratic equations appear in physics (projectile motion), geometry (areas, Pythagorean problems), business (profit maximization), and whenever a quantity has a squared relationship.

Translate the situation, form the equation, solve (factoring, completing the square, or quadratic formula), discard any physically meaningless roots, and state the answer in context.

**ELI5**

- Many "how far?" or "when?" questions involving acceleration or area produce a squared term.
- The parabola shape often matches the real curve of the situation.

**Worked Example:**

A rectangular garden has area 60 m². One side is 4 m longer than the other. Let width = x, length = x + 4. Then x(x + 4) = 60 → x² + 4x − 60 = 0. Factoring gives (x + 10)(x − 6) = 0. Positive width x = 6 m, length 10 m.

## Common Mistakes

- **Forgetting that a must be nonzero**: If a = 0 the equation collapses to linear.
- **Stopping after finding one root**: Quadratics frequently have two roots; check both.
- **Sign errors when factoring**: The pair of numbers that multiply to c must add to b; watch negatives.
- **Not checking by substitution**: Especially important when using the quadratic formula later.
- **Assuming every quadratic has two real roots**: The discriminant can be negative (no real roots) or zero (one real root).

The quadratic formula gives the solutions to any quadratic equation ax² + bx + c = 0 (a ≠ 0):

x = \frac{ -b \pm \sqrt{b^2 - 4ac} }{2a}

It is one of the most famous and useful formulas in algebra. Derived by completing the square on the general quadratic, it works whether the roots are real or complex and whether or not the quadratic factors nicely over the integers.

## Derivation and Statement

<!-- section: derivation-and-statement -->

Start with ax² + bx + c = 0.

Divide by a: x² + (b/a)x + c/a = 0.

Move constant: x² + (b/a)x = -c/a .

Add (b/(2a))² to both sides to complete the square:

x² + (b/a)x + (b/(2a))² = -c/a + b²/(4a²)

(x + b/(2a))² = ( -4 a c + b² ) / (4 a² ) = (b² - 4 a c)/(4 a²)

Take square roots:

x + b/(2a) = ± √(D) / (2a)   where D = b² - 4ac

x = -b/(2a) ± √D / (2a) = [ -b ± √D ] / (2a)

This is the quadratic formula.

**ELI5**

- Completing the square on the general case produces this closed-form expression that always works.
- The ± is there because when you take square root you get two possibilities (positive and negative).

**Worked Example:**

1. For $x^2 - 5x + 6 =0$ ($a=1$, $b=-5$, $c=6$), $D=25-24=1$, $x=[5 \pm 1]/2 \to 3$ or $2$. (Factors $(x-2)(x-3)$.)

## Using the Formula

<!-- section: using-the-formula -->

Identify a, b, c carefully (including signs).

Compute the discriminant D = b² - 4ac first; it tells you the nature of the roots before you finish.

Plug into the formula. Simplify the square root when possible.

If D < 0 the roots involve i (see complex lessons).

Always verify by plugging solutions back into the original equation (arithmetic errors are common).

**ELI5**

- a is the coefficient of x² (the "curvy" part), b of x, c the constant.
- D is your "preview": positive two real, zero one real (touch), negative two complex.

**Worked Example:**

1. $2x^2 - 3x - 2 =0$. $a=2$, $b=-3$, $c=-2$.
2. $D=9 +16=25$. $x=[3 \pm 5]/(4) \to (8)/4=2$ or $(-2)/4=-0.5$.
3. Check: $2(4) -3(2) -2=8-6-2=0$; $2(0.25) -3(-0.5) -2=0.5 +1.5 -2=0$. Good.

## Number and Nature of Roots (via Discriminant)

<!-- section: number-and-nature-of-roots -->

D = b² - 4ac :

- D > 0: two distinct real roots.
- D = 0: one real root (repeated, multiplicity two).
- D < 0: two complex conjugate roots (no real roots).

When coefficients are real, complex roots always appear in conjugate pairs.

The sum of roots = -b/a, product = c/a (Vieta's formulas).

**ELI5**

- The discriminant measures whether the parabola crosses, touches, or misses the x-axis.
- Positive D: crosses twice; zero: touches at vertex; negative: entirely above or below (never crosses).

**Worked Example:**

1. $x^2 + 1 =0$. $D=0-4=-4<0$. Roots $\pm i$. No real roots, graph never crosses x-axis.

**Worked Example:**

1. $x^2 - 2x +1=0$. $D=4-4=0$. Root $x=1$ (double). $(x-1)^2=0$.

## Common Mistakes

- **Sign errors on b (the formula has -b)**: Forgetting the minus in front of b is the most common slip.
- **Computing 4ac as 4a + c or 4*a*c wrong order**: Must be b squared minus 4*a*c.
- **Dividing by 2a only at the end incorrectly**: The entire numerator is divided by 2a.
- **Assuming two real roots always**: Check D first; many problems are designed with D<0 or 0.
- **Not simplifying √D when it is a perfect square**: Leaves ugly answers.
- **Plugging a,b,c into the formula without writing them down first**: Easy to grab the wrong number.
- **Forgetting that if a is negative the parabola opens down, but the formula still works the same**.
- **Using the formula when the equation is not quadratic (a=0)**: It is not valid; solve as linear instead.

The discriminant of the quadratic equation ax² + bx + c = 0 is the expression D = b² − 4 a c that appears under the square root in the quadratic formula.

Although it looks like a minor piece of the formula, the sign and value of D completely determine the number and nature of the solutions without having to compute the roots themselves. It is a powerful diagnostic tool.

## Definition and Role in the Formula

<!-- section: definition-and-role -->

D = b² - 4 a c .

In the quadratic formula the term √D tells us:

- Whether we stay in the reals or must enter the complex numbers.
- Whether the two potential roots are the same or different.

D is also equal to (root1 - root2)² * a² (up to sign), so |D| measures how far apart the roots are.

**ELI5**

- D is the "decider" inside the formula: positive → two different real answers; zero → exactly one; negative → complex pair.
- You can look at D first and know what kind of answer the whole formula will give.

**Worked Example:**

1. For $x^2 - 3x + 2 =0$, $D=9-8=1 >0 \to$ two real roots (indeed 1 and 2).
2. For $x^2 - 3x + 3 =0$, $D=9-12=-3 <0 \to$ two complex roots.

## Interpreting the Sign of D

<!-- section: interpreting-the-sign -->

Assuming a, b, c real and a ≠ 0:

- D > 0 : two distinct real roots. The parabola crosses the x-axis twice.
- D = 0 : exactly one real root (a double root). The parabola touches the x-axis at its vertex.
- D < 0 : no real roots; two complex conjugate roots. The parabola lies entirely above or entirely below the x-axis (depending on the sign of a).

The value of a tells the direction the parabola opens; D tells whether and how it meets the axis.

**ELI5**

- Positive D: the "mystery" under the root is a real number you can take ± of → two hits on the x-axis.
- Zero: exactly touches once.
- Negative: the square root is imaginary → the curve never reaches the x-axis in the real world.

**Worked Example:**

1. $2x^2 + 4x + 2 =0 \to D=16-16=0$. Root $x=-1$ (double). Graph touches at one point.

**Worked Example:**

1. $x^2 + 1 =0$, $D=-4<0$. No real roots. Graph of $y=x^2+1$ never crosses x-axis.

## Using the Discriminant Without Fully Solving

<!-- section: using-without-solving -->

Often we only need to know how many real solutions exist or whether the quadratic expression is always positive/negative.

Compute D only; no need for the full ± formula.

For inequalities (e.g. ax² + bx + c > 0) the sign of D plus the sign of a tells the solution set without finding the roots.

Vieta's formulas (sum and product of roots) can be used together with D for other relations.

**ELI5**

- D is like a quick test: "Will this quadratic equation have real answers?" before doing all the arithmetic.
- For "is this quadratic always above the x-axis?" D and a give the answer instantly.

**Worked Example:**

1. Does $3x^2 - 5x + 1 =0$ have real solutions? $D=25 - 12=13 >0 \to$ yes, two.

**Worked Example:**

1. For which $k$ does $x^2 + k x + 9 =0$ have exactly one real root? Set $D=0$: $k^2 - 36 =0 \to k=\pm 6$.

## Common Mistakes

- **Computing 4ac incorrectly (order of operations or sign)**: Must be b² minus (4 times a times c).
- **Forgetting that D=0 still counts as "real roots" (one of them)**: It does have a real solution (repeated).
- **Assuming D tells the roots themselves**: D only classifies; you still need the formula or factoring to find the actual numbers.
- **Ignoring a when describing the graph**: D= positive with a>0 still crosses, but the shape (width) depends on a.
- **Using D for non-quadratic equations**: Only valid when the equation really is quadratic (a≠0).
- **Sign error when D appears in inequalities**: The test intervals depend on both D and sign(a).
- **Thinking complex roots mean "no solutions at all"**: They are perfectly valid solutions in the complex numbers; just not real.

A rational equation sets two rational expressions equal (or a rational expression equal to a number). The presence of variables in denominators means we must exclude values that make any denominator zero; those values can never be solutions.

Clearing denominators by multiplication produces a polynomial equation that is easier to solve, but the resulting candidates must be checked in the original equation.

## Definition and Domain Restrictions

<!-- section: definition -->

A rational expression is a quotient of two polynomials. A rational equation contains at least one such expression.

The domain of the equation (allowed values of the variable) excludes every root of every denominator.

**ELI5**

- You cannot divide by zero, so any number that would put a zero in a denominator is forbidden from the start.
- The equation only "exists" for the other numbers.

**Worked Example:**

In 2/(x − 3) = 1/x the variable cannot be 3 or 0.

## Clearing Denominators

<!-- section: clearing-denominators -->

Multiply every term on both sides by the least common multiple of all denominators appearing in the equation.

This produces an equivalent equation (for values in the domain) that contains no fractions.

**ELI5**

- Multiplying by the "magic number" that is a multiple of every bottom turns every fraction into a whole number or polynomial.
- You are allowed to do this only because you already excluded the zeros.

**Worked Example:**

Solve 1/x + 1/3 = 1/2.

LCM of denominators is 6x.

Multiply through: 6 + 2x = 3x.

6 = x.

Check x = 6 is not 0; it works.

## Extraneous Roots

<!-- section: extraneous-roots -->

Any root of the cleared polynomial equation that makes a denominator zero in the original is extraneous and must be discarded.

Even when the multiplication step itself does not introduce them, substitution is the only way to be sure a candidate is valid.

**ELI5**

- The multiplication step can "hide" the fact that we divided by zero in disguise.
- Checking is the honesty step that keeps only the answers that were legal all along.

**Worked Example:**

1. Solve $x/(x - 2) = 2/(x - 2) + 1$.
2. Multiply by $(x - 2)$: $x = 2 + (x - 2) \to 0 = 0$, identity.
3. But original is undefined at $x = 2$, so solution is all reals except 2.

## Examples

<!-- section: examples -->

Practice includes proportions, equations with several fractions, and equations that become quadratic after clearing.

Always state excluded values first, solve, then filter.

**ELI5**

- Write the "forbidden list" before you start so you remember to reject them at the end.
- The algebra gives candidates; the original equation is the final judge.

**Worked Example:**

1. Solve $1/(x + 1) - 1/(x - 1) = 1/x$.
2. LCM = $x(x + 1)(x - 1)$.
3. After clearing and simplifying: $x = -2$ (after quadratic or linear steps).
4. Check: denominators $-2+1=-1$, $-2-1=-3$, $-2$ all nonzero. Good.

## Common Mistakes

- **Forgetting to list excluded values before multiplying**: You will not know which roots to throw away later.
- **Multiplying only one side or only some terms**: Every term on both sides must be multiplied by the LCM.
- **Keeping a root that zeros a denominator**: It never was a solution; the original equation was not defined there.
- **Canceling a variable factor without considering it could be zero**: That is exactly when extraneous roots appear.
- **Assuming the cleared equation has the same domain as the original**: The polynomial equation is defined everywhere; the rational one is not.
