# Quadratic formula

The quadratic formula gives the solutions to any quadratic equation ax² + bx + c = 0 (a ≠ 0):

x = \frac{ -b \pm \sqrt{b^2 - 4ac} }{2a}

It is one of the most famous and useful formulas in algebra. Derived by completing the square on the general quadratic, it works whether the roots are real or complex and whether or not the quadratic factors nicely over the integers.

## Derivation and Statement

<!-- section: derivation-and-statement -->

Start with ax² + bx + c = 0.

Divide by a: x² + (b/a)x + c/a = 0.

Move constant: x² + (b/a)x = -c/a .

Add (b/(2a))² to both sides to complete the square:

x² + (b/a)x + (b/(2a))² = -c/a + b²/(4a²)

(x + b/(2a))² = ( -4 a c + b² ) / (4 a² ) = (b² - 4 a c)/(4 a²)

Take square roots:

x + b/(2a) = ± √(D) / (2a)   where D = b² - 4ac

x = -b/(2a) ± √D / (2a) = [ -b ± √D ] / (2a)

This is the quadratic formula.

**ELI5**

- Completing the square on the general case produces this closed-form expression that always works.
- The ± is there because when you take square root you get two possibilities (positive and negative).

**Worked Example:**

1. For $x^2 - 5x + 6 =0$ ($a=1$, $b=-5$, $c=6$), $D=25-24=1$, $x=[5 \pm 1]/2 \to 3$ or $2$. (Factors $(x-2)(x-3)$.)

## Using the Formula

<!-- section: using-the-formula -->

Identify a, b, c carefully (including signs).

Compute the discriminant D = b² - 4ac first; it tells you the nature of the roots before you finish.

Plug into the formula. Simplify the square root when possible.

If D < 0 the roots involve i (see complex lessons).

Always verify by plugging solutions back into the original equation (arithmetic errors are common).

**ELI5**

- a is the coefficient of x² (the "curvy" part), b of x, c the constant.
- D is your "preview": positive two real, zero one real (touch), negative two complex.

**Worked Example:**

1. $2x^2 - 3x - 2 =0$. $a=2$, $b=-3$, $c=-2$.
2. $D=9 +16=25$. $x=[3 \pm 5]/(4) \to (8)/4=2$ or $(-2)/4=-0.5$.
3. Check: $2(4) -3(2) -2=8-6-2=0$; $2(0.25) -3(-0.5) -2=0.5 +1.5 -2=0$. Good.

## Number and Nature of Roots (via Discriminant)

<!-- section: number-and-nature-of-roots -->

D = b² - 4ac :

- D > 0: two distinct real roots.
- D = 0: one real root (repeated, multiplicity two).
- D < 0: two complex conjugate roots (no real roots).

When coefficients are real, complex roots always appear in conjugate pairs.

The sum of roots = -b/a, product = c/a (Vieta's formulas).

**ELI5**

- The discriminant measures whether the parabola crosses, touches, or misses the x-axis.
- Positive D: crosses twice; zero: touches at vertex; negative: entirely above or below (never crosses).

**Worked Example:**

1. $x^2 + 1 =0$. $D=0-4=-4<0$. Roots $\pm i$. No real roots, graph never crosses x-axis.

**Worked Example:**

1. $x^2 - 2x +1=0$. $D=4-4=0$. Root $x=1$ (double). $(x-1)^2=0$.

## Common Mistakes

- **Sign errors on b (the formula has -b)**: Forgetting the minus in front of b is the most common slip.
- **Computing 4ac as 4a + c or 4*a*c wrong order**: Must be b squared minus 4*a*c.
- **Dividing by 2a only at the end incorrectly**: The entire numerator is divided by 2a.
- **Assuming two real roots always**: Check D first; many problems are designed with D<0 or 0.
- **Not simplifying √D when it is a perfect square**: Leaves ugly answers.
- **Plugging a,b,c into the formula without writing them down first**: Easy to grab the wrong number.
- **Forgetting that if a is negative the parabola opens down, but the formula still works the same**.
- **Using the formula when the equation is not quadratic (a=0)**: It is not valid; solve as linear instead.
