# Absolute value inequality

Absolute value inequalities describe intervals or unions of intervals on the number line. They translate the idea "the distance from a center is less than / greater than a certain amount" into algebraic statements.

The solutions are either a single bounded interval ("and") or two unbounded rays ("or"), depending on whether the inequality is < / ≤ or > / ≥.

## Case or Property Approach

<!-- section: case-or-property -->

For |A| < k (k > 0) rewrite as −k < A < k (the "and" form).

For |A| > k (k > 0) rewrite as A < −k or A > k (the "or" form).

The same logic applies to ≤ and ≥, simply including the endpoints.

**ELI5**

- Less than means "inside the fence" around the center: both left fence and right fence conditions must be true at once ("and").
- Greater than means "outside the fence": you are either far left or far right ("or").

**Worked Example:**

1. $|x - 4| \leq 1$ means $-1 \leq x - 4 \leq 1 \to 3 \leq x \leq 5$. The closed interval centered at 4 with radius 1.

## "And" versus "Or" Logic

<!-- section: and-vs-or -->

Strictly less/greater than or equal produces the compound connector:

- |A| < k or |A| ≤ k → compound "and" (intersection) → one interval.
- |A| > k or |A| ≥ k → compound "or" (union) → two rays.

When k = 0 the behavior is special: |A| > 0 is all reals except 0; |A| ≥ 0 is all reals.

**ELI5**

- "Inside the distance limit" requires being on the correct side of both boundaries.
- "Outside the distance limit" is satisfied as soon as you cross either boundary.

**Worked Example:**

1. $|x| > 2$ is $x < -2$ or $x > 2$. Two separate pieces.

## Graphing the Solutions

<!-- section: graphing-solutions -->

Translate the algebraic solution set directly onto the number line using the same circle and shading conventions as ordinary inequalities.

- < or > : open circles.
- ≤ or ≥ : closed circles.
- "And" interval gets a single shaded segment.
- "Or" gets two shaded rays.

**ELI5**

- The graph is the picture of the "allowed zone" described by the distance condition.
- The center of the absolute value expression is the center of symmetry of the solution set.

**Worked Example:**

1. $|x + 1| \geq 3 \to x \leq -4$ or $x \geq 2$. Open circles at $-4$ and 2? Wait $\geq$ so closed at $-4$ and 2, rays outward.

## Examples

<!-- section: examples -->

A few varied centers and radii build recognition of the pattern.

Always remember that the right-hand side of a < or > absolute value inequality must be non-negative for real solutions to exist in the usual way (negative right side yields empty or all-reals depending on direction).

**ELI5**

- Draw the center point first, mark the radius distance on both sides, then decide whether you want the inside strip or the two outer pieces.

**Worked Example:**

1. Solve $|2x - 3| > 5$.
2. $2x - 3 > 5$ or $2x - 3 < -5$.
3. $2x > 8 \to x > 4$ or $2x < -2 \to x < -1$.
4. Solution: $(-\infty, -1) \cup (4, \infty)$.

## Common Mistakes

- **Using "and" for a > inequality**: Greater absolute value means the two outer regions, which is "or".
- **Forgetting to flip both inequality signs when multiplying a compound by a negative**: −3 < 2x < 6 becomes −3/2 > x > −3 after dividing? No: divide all by 2 keeps, but if negative coefficient you must reverse all three parts.
- **Writing a single interval for a > problem**: That would be the "inside" solution, the opposite of what > asks.
- **Ignoring that |A| < 0 has no solution**: Absolute value cannot be negative.
- **Placing the circles at the wrong locations**: Solve for the boundary points first by setting |A| = k, then decide open/closed and direction of shading from the compound.
