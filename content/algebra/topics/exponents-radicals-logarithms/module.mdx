# Exponents, Radicals & Logarithms

Exponent rules, roots, rational exponents, exponential functions, logarithms, and radical equations.

Exponentiation is the operation of raising a base to an exponent (power). The notation a^n means a multiplied by itself n times when n is a positive integer.

It is one of the most important operations in mathematics because it appears in growth models, scientific notation, polynomials, and almost every branch of higher math. The basic rules of exponents let us simplify expressions without expanding everything.

## Definition for Positive Integers

<!-- section: definition-and-positive-integers -->

For positive integer n, a^n = a * a * ... * a (n factors).

a^1 = a.

Bases can be any real (or complex) number; the exponent tells how many times to multiply the base by itself.

**ELI5**

- Exponent is a shorthand for repeated multiplication, just like multiplication is shorthand for repeated addition.
- 2^5 is 2 used as a factor five times: 2*2*2*2*2 = 32.

**Worked Example:**

1. Compute $3^4 = 3 \times 3 \times 3 \times 3 = 81$.
2. Compute $(-2)^3 = (-2) \times (-2) \times (-2) = -8$. (Odd power keeps the negative sign.)

**Worked Example:**

1. Compute $(1/2)^2 = 1/4$.

## Zero and Negative Exponents

<!-- section: zero-and-negative-exponents -->

For a ≠ 0:

a^0 = 1 (by convention to preserve the product rule a^m * a^0 = a^m).

a^{-n} = 1 / a^n .

These definitions make the exponent rules work uniformly for all integers.

Negative exponents move the factor to the denominator (or numerator if already in denominator) and flip the sign of the exponent.

**ELI5**

- Anything (nonzero) to the zero power is "one copy of the empty product," which is defined as 1.
- Negative power means "one over" the positive power; it is a reciprocal.

**Worked Example:**

1. $5^0 = 1$, $(x y)^0 = 1$ (for $x y \neq 0$).
2. $7^{-1} = 1/7$, $x^{-2} = 1/x^2$.

**Worked Example:**

1. $(2/3)^{-2} = (3/2)^2 = 9/4$.

## Properties and Order of Operations

<!-- section: properties-and-order-of-operations -->

Key rules (for appropriate bases):

- a^m * a^n = a^{m+n}
- (a^m)^n = a^{m n}
- (a b)^n = a^n b^n
- a^m / a^n = a^{m-n}   (or 1/a^{n-m})
- (a/b)^n = a^n / b^n

When evaluating, follow order of operations: parentheses first, then exponents (right-to-left for a^{b^c}), then * and / left to right, then + -.

**ELI5**

- When multiplying same base, add the exponents (like combining lengths of repeated groups).
- Power of a power: multiply the exponents (layers of repetition).
- Everything distributes over multiplication in the base.

**Worked Example:**

1. $x^3 \cdot x^5 = x^{3+5} = x^8$.
2. $(y^2)^4 = y^{2 \cdot 4} = y^8$.
3. $(2x)^3 = 2^3 x^3 = 8x^3$.

**Worked Example:**

1. $(3^2 \cdot 3^3) / 3^4 = 3^{2+3} / 3^4 = 3^5 / 3^4 = 3^{5-4} = 3^1 = 3$.

## Common Mistakes

- **Treating a^n as a * n**: Exponent is repeated multiplication, not scaling by the exponent.
- **Adding exponents on multiplication of different bases**: 2^3 * 3^3 = (2*3)^3 = 6^3, not 2^3 * 3^3 by adding.
- **Forgetting a^0 = 1 for nonzero a**: 100^0 is 1, not 0 or 100.
- **Sign errors with negative bases and even/odd powers**: (-2)^4 is positive, (-2)^3 is negative.
- **Misapplying the power rule to sums**: (a + b)^2 ≠ a^2 + b^2 (the binomial theorem gives the cross term).
- **Negative exponent on the whole fraction without flipping**:  (a/b)^{-1} = b/a , not 1/(a/b) incorrectly handled.
- **Evaluating left to right for stacked exponents**: 2^{3^2} = 2^9 = 512, not (2^3)^2 = 64. Right-associative.

The power rules (or laws of exponents) are the algebraic shortcuts that let us manipulate expressions containing exponents without expanding them into long products.

The two core ideas are the product rule and the power-of-a-power rule; from them the others follow. Mastering these rules is essential for simplifying algebraic expressions, solving exponential equations, and working with polynomials and scientific notation.

## Product Rule

<!-- section: product-rule -->

a^m * a^n = a^{m + n}   (same base).

When multiplying powers that have the identical base, keep the base and add the exponents.

This works because you are simply counting the total number of times the base is used as a factor.

**ELI5**

- Same base? Add the "how many times" numbers.
- x^2 * x^3 is x used 2 times then 3 more times = x used 5 times total.

**Worked Example:**

1. $x^5 \cdot x^3 = x^{5+3} = x^8$.
2. $y \cdot y^4 \cdot y^2 = y^{1+4+2} = y^7$.

**Worked Example:**

1. $(2^3) \cdot (2^2) = 2^{3+2} = 2^5 = 32$. (8 * 4 = 32, same result.)

## Power of a Power and Power of a Product

<!-- section: power-of-power-and-power-of-product -->

(a^m)^n = a^{m n} .

(a b)^n = a^n * b^n .

Raising a power to another power multiplies the exponents. Raising a product to a power raises each factor to that power.

These rules let you "peel" exponents inward or outward.

**ELI5**

- A power of a power is like repeating the repetition: multiply the counts.
- Power on a product "distributes" the exponent to each piece inside the parentheses.

**Worked Example:**

1. $(x^3)^4 = x^{3 \cdot 4} = x^{12}$.
2. $(3^2)^5 = 3^{2 \cdot 5} = 3^{10}$.

**Worked Example:**

1. $(2 x y^2)^3 = 2^3 \cdot x^3 \cdot (y^2)^3 = 8 x^3 y^6$.

## Quotient Rule and Combined Use

<!-- section: quotient-rule-and-combined -->

a^m / a^n = a^{m - n}   (a ≠ 0).

(a / b)^n = a^n / b^n .

When the rules are combined with negative and zero exponents, almost any expression with integer powers can be simplified to a single power or a fraction of powers with positive exponents.

**ELI5**

- Dividing same base: subtract the exponents (cancel the overlapping factors).
- Power on a fraction distributes to num and den separately.

**Worked Example:**

1. $x^7 / x^2 = x^{7-2} = x^5$.
2. $(x^4 / y^3)^2 = x^{4 \cdot 2} / y^{3 \cdot 2} = x^8 / y^6$.

**Worked Example:**

1. $(a^3 b^{-2})^2 / (a^{-1} b^4) = (a^6 / b^4) / (1/(a b^4))$. First simplify: numerator $a^6 b^{-4}$, divide by $a^{-1} b^4 = a^6 b^{-4} \cdot a^1 / b^4 = a^7 b^{-8} = a^7 / b^8$.

## Common Mistakes

- **Adding exponents when bases differ**: 2^3 * 3^2 is not 2^5 or 6^something; compute separately or factor.
- **Multiplying exponents on product of powers with same exponent**: a^2 * b^2 = (a b)^2 , not a^4 b^something unless using other rules.
- **Forgetting parentheses when bases have coefficients**: 2x^3 squared is (2x)^3? No,  (2 x^3 )^2 vs 2 (x^3)^2.
- **Subtracting exponents on multiplication**: Product adds, quotient subtracts.
- **Applying rules to sums or differences**: (a + b)^n does not equal a^n + b^n in general.
- **Ignoring the condition a ≠ 0 for negative or zero exponents**: 0^0 and negative powers of zero are undefined.
- **Wrong association on stacked powers**: 2^{3^2} = 2^9 not 8^2 (exponentiation is right-associative).

The (principal) square root of a non-negative real number x, written √x or x^{1/2}, is the unique non-negative real number y such that y^2 = x.

Square roots are the inverse operation to squaring. They appear in the quadratic formula, distance formula, standard deviation, and countless geometric and physical formulas. Understanding the domain (x ≥ 0), the principal root convention, and how to simplify and solve with them is fundamental.

## Definition and the Principal Root

<!-- section: definition-and-principal-root -->

For x ≥ 0, √x is defined to be the y ≥ 0 with y^2 = x.

Every positive x has two real square roots, +y and -y, but the symbol √x always returns the non-negative one (the principal root). The negative root is written -√x.

√0 = 0. Negative numbers have no real square root (they have imaginary roots involving i).

**ELI5**

- Squaring forgets the sign; the square root symbol "chooses" the positive answer to make functions well-defined.
- √9 = 3, not -3, even though both square to 9.

**Worked Example:**

1. $\sqrt{25} = 5$ (the principal). The equation $x^2 = 25$ has solutions $\pm 5$.

**Worked Example:**

1. $\sqrt{1/4} = 1/2$.

## Properties and Simplifying Square Roots

<!-- section: properties-and-simplifying -->

For a, b ≥ 0:

√(a b) = √a * √b

√(a / b) = √a / √b   (b > 0)

√(a^2) = |a|   (the absolute value appears because the principal root is non-negative).

To simplify √N where N is not a perfect square, factor out the largest perfect square factor.

**ELI5**

- Roots distribute over multiplication and division (like exponents).
- But √(a^2) "folds" the negative back to positive, hence absolute value.

**Worked Example:**

1. $\sqrt{50} = \sqrt{25 \cdot 2} = 5 \sqrt{2}$.
2. $\sqrt{12 x^3}$ for $x \geq 0 = \sqrt{4 \cdot 3 x^2 \cdot x} = 2 |x| \sqrt{3 x} = 2 x \sqrt{3 x}$.

**Worked Example:**

1. $\sqrt{18} + \sqrt{8} = 3\sqrt{2} + 2\sqrt{2} = 5\sqrt{2}$.

## Operations and Solving Equations

<!-- section: operations-and-solving -->

When multiplying or adding roots, simplify first then combine like terms.

To solve equations with square roots, isolate the root, square both sides, solve the resulting equation, and check every candidate in the original (squaring can introduce extraneous solutions).

The domain restrictions must be respected throughout.

**ELI5**

- Simplifying first makes adding or multiplying easier (like reducing fractions before operating).
- Squaring to solve is powerful but "lies" sometimes; always verify.

**Worked Example:**

1. Solve $\sqrt{3x + 1} = 5$.
2. Square: $3x + 1 = 25 \to 3x = 24 \to x = 8$.
3. Check: $\sqrt{24 + 1} = \sqrt{25} = 5$. Good.

**Worked Example:**

1. Solve $\sqrt{x - 2} = x - 4$ (note right side must be $\geq 0$ so $x \geq 4$).
2. Square: $x - 2 = (x - 4)^2 = x^2 - 8x + 16$.
3. $0 = x^2 - 9x + 18 = (x-3)(x-6)$.
4. Candidates $x=3,6$. Only $x=6$ satisfies original ($x=3$ gives $\sqrt{1} = -1$ which is false and right side negative). $x=6$: $\sqrt{4}=2$, $6-4=2$. Good.

## Common Mistakes

- **Writing √(a + b) = √a + √b**: False in general (e.g. √(4+9)=√13 ≠ 2+3).
- **Forgetting the absolute value in √(x^2) = |x|**: For negative x the principal root is positive.
- **Ignoring domain when simplifying or solving**: √(x^2 - 1) requires |x| ≥ 1.
- **Not checking solutions after squaring**: Extraneous roots appear when both sides were not non-negative or when squaring changed the equality set.
- **Confusing the symbol √x with "both roots"**: The symbol is the principal (≥0) root only; use ± when solving x^2 = k.
- **Dividing by zero or taking roots of negative in real context**: Always state restrictions.
- **Simplifying √(x^2 + 2x + 1) as x + 1 without | |**: It is |x + 1|.

The nth root of a number x, written √[n]x or x^{1/n}, is a number y such that y^n = x.

When n=2 it is the square root; n=3 the cube root, etc. Odd roots are defined for all real x and return one real value (positive or negative). Even roots are defined only for x ≥ 0 and return the non-negative principal root.

nth roots generalize square roots and appear in solving polynomial equations, in radical expressions, and when working with rational exponents.

## Definition and Even vs Odd Roots

<!-- section: definition-and-even-odd-roots -->

For integer n ≥ 2:

- If n odd: for any real x there is exactly one real y with y^n = x. y can be negative if x is.
- If n even: only for x ≥ 0 is there a real y; we take the principal y ≥ 0.

The symbol √[n]x always denotes the principal (non-negative when it makes sense) root.

**ELI5**

- Odd roots (cube, 5th, ...) are like "undo" for any sign; they preserve sign information.
- Even roots are like square roots: they "forget" sign and only accept non-negative inputs in reals.

**Worked Example:**

1. $\sqrt[3]{-8} = -2$ because $(-2)^3 = -8$.
2. $\sqrt[4]{81} = 3$ (principal), even though $(-3)^4 =81$ also.

**Worked Example:**

1. $\sqrt[5]{32} = 2$.

## Properties of nth Roots

<!-- section: properties -->

For a, b where the expressions are defined in reals:

√[n](a b) = √[n]a * √[n]b

√[n](a / b) = √[n]a / √[n]b   (b ≠ 0)

√[n](a^n) = |a| if n even; = a if n odd.

These mirror the square root properties but with care for the even/odd distinction on signs and domains.

**ELI5**

- Roots "distribute" over multiplication and division when everything stays real.
- For even roots the absolute value appears on √[n](a^n) because the root returns positive.

**Worked Example:**

1. $\sqrt[3]{8 \cdot 27} = \sqrt[3]{8} \cdot \sqrt[3]{27} = 2 \cdot 3 = 6$.
2. $\sqrt[4]{16 x^4} = \sqrt[4]{16} \cdot \sqrt[4]{(x^4)} = 2 |x|$ (for even root).

**Worked Example:**

1. $\sqrt[3]{x^3 / 8} = x / 2$ (for all real $x$).

## Simplifying and Solving with nth Roots

<!-- section: simplifying-and-solving -->

To simplify, factor the radicand to pull out perfect nth powers.

When solving equations with nth roots, raise both sides to the nth power. For odd n this is one-to-one and rarely introduces extraneous solutions. For even n (like square roots) always check candidates.

**ELI5**

- Pull out "perfect groups" of n identical factors, leaving the leftover under the root.
- Raising to n "undoes" the root, but even n can create sign ambiguity or extras.

**Worked Example:**

1. Simplify $\sqrt[3]{54 x^4} = \sqrt[3]{27 \cdot 2 x^3 \cdot x} = 3 x \sqrt[3]{2 x}$.

**Worked Example:**

1. Solve $\sqrt[4]{x - 1} = 2$.
2. Raise to 4th: $x - 1 = 16$, $x = 17$.
3. Check: $\sqrt[4]{16} = 2$ (principal), good. (Note $x-1$ must be $\geq 0$.)

## Common Mistakes

- **Taking even roots of negatives in real numbers**: Not defined (use complex if needed).
- **Writing √[n](a + b) = √[n]a + √[n]b**: Almost never true.
- **Forgetting | | for even roots of powers**: √[4](x^4) = |x|, not x.
- **Not checking after raising to even power**: Extraneous solutions common.
- **Confusing index n with the exponent on the variable**: ∛(x^3) = x, but √(x^2) = |x|.
- **Assuming all roots have two real solutions**: Only even powers in the equation typically do; odd root equations have one.
- **Domain slips when simplifying products inside even roots**: If a or b negative the product may still be positive, but individual roots may not be real.

A rational exponent is an exponent that is a fraction p/q in lowest terms. By definition, for a ≥ 0 (or appropriate domain),

a^{p/q} = (a^{1/q})^p = (a^p)^{1/q} .

Rational exponents provide a uniform way to express roots and powers together and extend the familiar integer exponent rules to fractional cases.

They are the bridge between radical notation and exponential notation and are heavily used in calculus, algebra, and modeling.

## Definition as Roots and Powers

<!-- section: definition-as-roots-and-powers -->

The denominator of the exponent indicates the root; the numerator indicates the power.

a^{m/n} means take the nth root of a, then raise to the m power (or raise first then take root; both agree when defined).

For negative bases, the expression is real when the root index (denominator in reduced form) is odd.

**ELI5**

- The fraction tells you "root first or power first" — two ways to the same place when it exists.
- 8^{2/3} is the cube root of 8, then squared (or 8 squared then cube root) = 4.

**Worked Example:**

1. $27^{2/3} = (27^{1/3})^2 = 3^2 = 9$.

**Worked Example:**

1. $(-32)^{3/5}$: 5th root of $-32$ is $-2$, then $^3 = -8$. (Denom 5 odd.)

## Properties of Rational Exponents

<!-- section: properties -->

All the integer power rules extend to rational exponents when the expressions are defined in the reals:

a^r * a^s = a^{r + s}

(a^r)^s = a^{r s}

(a b)^r = a^r b^r

a^r / a^s = a^{r - s}

etc.

This unifies roots and powers under one set of rules.

**ELI5**

- You can treat fractional exponents almost like integers for adding, multiplying, etc., as long as you don't step outside the domain.
- The arithmetic of the fractions does the combining of roots and powers.

**Worked Example:**

1. $x^{1/2} \cdot x^{3/4} = x^{1/2 + 3/4} = x^{5/4}$.
2. $(x^{2/3})^3 = x^2$.

**Worked Example:**

1. $(8 x^6)^{1/3} = 2 x^2$.

## Simplifying Expressions and Solving Equations

<!-- section: simplifying-and-solving -->

Rewrite rational exponents as radicals when helpful for simplification, or vice versa.

To solve equations with rational exponents, raise both sides to the reciprocal power (or clear by raising to lcm of denominators), then check solutions against the domain (especially non-negative requirements for even roots).

**ELI5**

- Convert to roots to see what is "inside" the root, or to exponents to use exponent arithmetic.
- Raising to the reciprocal "undoes" the fractional power, but domain and extraneous checks still needed.

**Worked Example:**

1. Simplify $(x^{3/2} y^{-1/2})^4 = x^{6} / y^{2}$.

**Worked Example:**

1. Solve $x^{3/2} = 8$.
2. Raise both sides to $2/3$: $x = 8^{2/3} = 4$. ($x \geq 0$ required.)
3. Check: $4^{3/2} = (4^{1/2})^3 = 2^3 = 8$. Good.

**Worked Example:**

1. Solve $x^{4/3} = 16$.
2. Raise to $3/4$: $x = 16^{3/4} = (2^4)^{3/4} = 2^3 = 8$.
3. (Or $(x^{1/3})^4 =16$, let $y=x^{1/3}$, $y^4=16$, $y=\pm 2$ (real), $x=\pm 8$. But wait for $4/3$ even num? Check: $(-8)^{4/3} = [(-8)^{1/3}]^4 = (-2)^4=16$. Yes both.)

## Common Mistakes

- **Using even denominator with negative base without checking**: (-4)^{1/2} not real; (-8)^{2/3} is real because denom 3 odd.
- **Adding exponents without common denominator**: 1/2 + 1/3 = 5/6, not 2/5 or 1/6.
- **Assuming (a^{p/q})^r always = a^{p r / q} without domain**: For negative a and certain fractions it may leave reals.
- **Forgetting to convert to common base or root when simplifying mixed radical/exponent forms**.
- **Raising both sides to a power without considering the principal root or sign**: Can miss solutions or add extras.
- **Writing a^{1/2 + 1/3} as a^{1/5}**: Wrong arithmetic on the exponent fraction.
- **Ignoring that 0 to a negative or zero fractional power is undefined**.

An exponential function has the form f(x) = a * b^x where a ≠ 0, b > 0, b ≠ 1, and the variable x appears in the exponent.

Exponential functions model phenomena with constant percent growth or decay: populations, compound interest, radioactive decay, cooling, and many natural processes. Their graphs are smooth curves that are always positive, have a horizontal asymptote, and grow (or decay) faster and faster.

## Definition and Basic Shape

<!-- section: definition-and-basic-shape -->

f(x) = a b^x .

- a is the initial value or y-intercept (f(0) = a).
- b is the growth/decay factor (the base).
- The graph is continuous, always above the x-axis, and has horizontal asymptote y=0.

For b > 0, b ≠ 1 the function is one-to-one, so it has an inverse (the logarithm).

**ELI5**

- Each time x increases by 1, the output is multiplied by b (not added).
- "Percent change is constant" is the key: +50% each step means *1.5, no matter the current size.

**Worked Example:**

1. $f(x) = 200 \cdot (1.1)^x$: starts at $200$ when $x=0$, grows $10\%$ per unit $x$.
2. At $x=1$: $220$; $x=2$: $242$, etc.

**Worked Example:**

1. $g(x) = 50 \cdot (0.8)^x$: starts at $50$, decays $20\%$ per unit time, approaches $0$.

## Growth versus Decay and the Role of the Base

<!-- section: growth-vs-decay-and-base -->

If b > 1: exponential growth. As x → +∞, f(x) → +∞ (if a>0); as x → -∞, f(x) → 0.

If 0 < b < 1: exponential decay. As x → +∞, f(x) → 0; as x → -∞, f(x) → +∞ (a>0).

The base b is often written 1 + r where r is the growth rate (r > -1, r ≠ 0).

**ELI5**

- b >1 is "more than one copy each time step" → explodes upward.
- b <1 is "less than one copy" → shrinks toward zero.

**Worked Example:**

1. Compound interest: $A = P (1 + r/n)^{n t}$ is exponential in $t$.

**Worked Example:**

1. Half-life: amount = initial $\cdot (1/2)^{t / \text{half-life}}$, $b=1/2 <1$, decay.

## Transformations of Exponential Functions

<!-- section: transformations -->

The usual transformations apply:

- f(x) + k : vertical shift.
- a f(x) : vertical stretch/compression/reflection (sign of a).
- f(x - h) : horizontal shift.
- f(c x) : horizontal stretch/compression (c affects the "speed" of growth).

The horizontal asymptote moves with vertical shifts. The y-intercept changes with a and shifts.

**ELI5**

- Shifting and stretching work the same as for other functions, but the exponential shape stays recognizable.
- Horizontal compression makes it grow faster.

**Worked Example:**

1. $g(x) = -2 \cdot 3^{x-1} + 5$: reflect over $x$-axis, stretch vert by 2, shift right 1, up 5. Asymptote $y=5$, $y$-intercept $g(0) = -2 \cdot 3^{-1} +5 = -2/3 +5 = 13/3$.

## Solving Basic Exponential Equations

<!-- section: solving-basic-equations -->

When both sides can be written as powers of the same base, equate the exponents.

Otherwise take logarithms (see logarithm lesson) or use numerical/graphical methods.

Always consider domain (expression defined) and check solutions.

**ELI5**

- If the bases match, the exponents must match (because exponential is one-to-one).
- "Undo" the exponential with a log when bases don't match.

**Worked Example:**

1. Solve $4^x = 16$.
2. $4^x = 4^2$ (since $16=4^2$), so $x=2$.

**Worked Example:**

1. Solve $5^{x-1} = 125$.
2. $125=5^3$, so $x-1=3$, $x=4$.

## Common Mistakes

- **Confusing exponential with polynomial (x in base vs exponent)**: 2^x grows much faster than any x^n.
- **Thinking b=1 or b<0 is allowed for real exponential functions in basic algebra**: Base must be positive and not 1 for the standard real exponential.
- **Forgetting the horizontal asymptote when sketching or transforming**: It moves only with vertical shifts.
- **Solving  b^x = c by taking xth root incorrectly**: Better to express c as power of b when possible.
- **Sign errors with negative a**: a can be negative, which reflects the graph over x-axis (still positive? No, then it goes negative, but b^x always >0 so sign follows a).
- **Ignoring that for 0<b<1 the growth/decay labels flip with direction of x**.
- **Writing (a b)^x = a^x * b^x correctly but then mishandling when a is the variable**.

A logarithm is the inverse operation of exponentiation. log_b(a) is the exponent to which the base b must be raised to produce a. In symbols: b^{log_b a} = a and log_b (b^c) = c.

Logarithms turn multiplication into addition, allow us to solve for variables in the exponent, measure orders of magnitude, and appear in the definition of many scales (decibels, Richter, pH) and in calculus as the natural log.

## Definition and Notation

<!-- section: definition-and-notation -->

log_b(a) = c  ⇔  b^c = a   (with a > 0, b > 0, b ≠ 1).

- b is the base.
- a is the argument.
- c is the logarithm (the exponent).

Special cases: log_b(1) = 0, log_b(b) = 1.

The expression is only defined for positive a.

**ELI5**

- Log asks "what power gets me there?" If 2^? =8, the answer is the log.
- It undoes the exponential, like square root undoes squaring.

**Worked Example:**

1. $\log_2(16) = 4$ because $2^4 = 16$.
2. $\log_{10}(0.001) = -3$ because $10^{-3} = 0.001$.

**Worked Example:**

1. $\log_5(1) = 0$, $\log_5(5) = 1$.

## Common Bases and Change of Base

<!-- section: common-bases-and-change-of-base -->

- Common log: log(x) usually means log_{10}(x).
- Natural log: ln(x) means log_e(x), where e ≈ 2.718 is the base of the natural exponential.
- Any base can be converted: log_b a = ln a / ln b   (or log10 a / log10 b).

Change of base lets us compute any log using calculators that have only ln or log10.

**ELI5**

- Base 10 is convenient for our decimal system and powers of 10.
- Base e arises naturally from continuous growth and calculus.
- Change of base is like using a universal translator between different "languages" of logs.

**Worked Example:**

1. $\log_2(10) \approx \ln(10)/\ln(2) \approx 2.302585 / 0.693147 \approx 3.3219$.

**Worked Example:**

1. $\log_3(81) = \log(81)/\log(3) = 4$ (exactly, since $3^4=81$).

## Properties of Logarithms

<!-- section: properties -->

log_b (m n) = log_b m + log_b n

log_b (m / n) = log_b m - log_b n

log_b (m^k) = k log_b m

These turn products/powers into sums, which is why logs linearize exponential relationships.

Also log_b (b) =1, log_b(1)=0, and the inverse properties above.

**ELI5**

- Log of a product is sum of logs (like how exponents add on multiplication).
- Power comes out front as a multiplier.

**Worked Example:**

1. $\log(100 x^3 / y) = \log 100 + 3 \log x - \log y = 2 + 3 \log x - \log y$ (base 10).

**Worked Example:**

1. $2 \log_5 3 + \log_5 4 = \log_5 (3^2) + \log_5 4 = \log_5 (9 \cdot 4) = \log_5 36$.

## Solving Exponential and Logarithmic Equations

<!-- section: solving-exponential-and-log-equations -->

To solve exponential: take log of both sides (any base), or rewrite with same base.

To solve logarithmic: exponentiate both sides (raise base to both sides), or use properties to combine.

Always check domain: arguments >0, bases valid.

**ELI5**

- To "get the variable out of the exponent," take a log.
- To get it out of the log, raise the base to the power of both sides.

**Worked Example:**

1. Solve $3^x = 27$.
2. Take log base 3: $x = \log_3 27 = 3$.
3. Or $3^x = 3^3$, $x=3$.

**Worked Example:**

1. Solve $\log_4 (x + 3) = 2$.
2. $4^2 = x + 3 \to 16 = x + 3 \to x=13$.
3. Check: $\log_4(16)=2$, yes.

**Worked Example:**

1. Solve $2 \ln x - \ln 3 = \ln 5$.
2. $\ln (x^2 / 3) = \ln 5 \to x^2 /3 =5 \to x^2 =15 \to x=\sqrt{15}$ (positive).

## Common Mistakes

- **Log of sum is not sum of logs**: log(a + b) ≠ log a + log b.
- **Forgetting domain restrictions**: log of negative or zero undefined; solutions that make arg ≤0 are invalid.
- **Base of log <0 or =1**: Not allowed in real numbers.
- **Confusing log_b a with a / b or b^a**: It is the exponent, not division or power swapped.
- **Dropping the base when using change of base**: Must divide logs of same base.
- **When solving, not checking after exponentiating or taking logs**: Extraneous can appear from domain.
- **Thinking ln means log base 10**: ln is specifically natural log base e.

A radical equation contains the variable inside a root (radical) symbol, such as √(x + 2) = 5 or ∛(2x) - 1 = 4.

The standard solution technique is to isolate the radical and raise both sides to the index of the root to "undo" it. Because raising to a power is not a one-to-one operation in the same way for even roots, extraneous solutions can appear and must be checked.

Radical equations arise when solving distances, times, or inverse power relationships.

## Isolating the Radical and Raising to a Power

<!-- section: isolating-and-raising -->

1. Isolate one radical on one side of the equation.
2. Raise both sides to the power equal to the root index (square for square roots, cube for cube roots, etc.).
3. If another radical remains, repeat the isolate-and-raise process.
4. Solve the resulting polynomial or simpler equation.

For odd roots the process is usually reversible without extras; for even roots extra caution is required.

**ELI5**

- Get the "trapped" variable alone under the root, then "peel off" the root by raising to the matching power.
- Like unwrapping layers; each raise removes one root.

**Worked Example:**

1. Solve $\sqrt{x - 1} = 3$.
2. Square both sides: $x - 1 = 9 \to x = 10$.
3. Check: $\sqrt{10-1} = 3$. Good.

**Worked Example:**

1. Solve $\sqrt[3]{x + 5} = 2$.
2. Cube both sides: $x + 5 = 8 \to x = 3$.
3. Check: $\sqrt[3]{8} = 2$. Good.

## Extraneous Solutions and Checking

<!-- section: extraneous-solutions-and-checking -->

After raising, every candidate must be substituted back into the original equation.

Reasons for extraneous roots:

- Squaring (or even powering) can turn a negative "would-be" value into a positive that satisfies the squared equation but not the original (principal root can't be negative).
- The isolated radical expression may have been negative, which is impossible for even roots.

Domain: any even root requires its radicand ≥ 0 in the original.

**ELI5**

- Squaring "hides the sign." A number and its negative both square to the same thing.
- The check is the honesty step that keeps only answers that were legal all along.

**Worked Example:**

1. Solve $\sqrt{x + 2} = x - 4$.
2. Square: $x + 2 = (x - 4)^2 = x^2 - 8x + 16$.
3. $0 = x^2 - 9x + 14 = (x-2)(x-7)$.
4. Candidates: $x=2$ or $x=7$.
5. Check $x=2$: $\sqrt{4} = 2$, $2-4=-2$. $2 \neq -2$. Discard.
6. Check $x=7$: $\sqrt{9}=3$, $7-4=3$. Equal. Good. Only $x=7$.

**Worked Example:**

1. Solve $\sqrt{2x - 3} + 4 = 1$.
2. Isolate: $\sqrt{2x-3} = -3$ (impossible for principal square root). No solution. (Squaring would produce extraneous $x=6$ that fails check.)

## Equations with Multiple Radicals

<!-- section: multiple-radicals -->

When two or more radicals are present:

- Move one radical to each side if helpful.
- Square (or raise) to eliminate one.
- The cross term usually creates a new radical; isolate it and raise again.
- Check all resulting candidates thoroughly.

Sometimes substitution (let u = √x or similar) simplifies.

**ELI5**

- Each squaring can create a "new monster radical" from the cross term; keep isolating and squaring until none remain.
- Verification is even more important with multiple layers.

**Worked Example:**

1. Solve $\sqrt{x + 3} + \sqrt{x} = 3$.
2. Isolate one: $\sqrt{x + 3} = 3 - \sqrt{x}$.
3. Square: $x + 3 = 9 - 6\sqrt{x} + x$.
4. $3 = 9 - 6\sqrt{x}$.
5. $6\sqrt{x} = 6$.
6. $\sqrt{x} = 1$.
7. $x = 1$.
8. Check: $\sqrt{1+3} + \sqrt{1} = 2 + 1 = 3$. Good.

**Worked Example:**

1. Solve $\sqrt{x + 7} - \sqrt{x - 5} = 2$. (Similar process yields $x=9$ after isolating twice and squaring twice; check passes.)

## Common Mistakes

- **Raising before isolating**: The cross terms become messy or you square extra junk.
- **Forgetting to check**: Many textbooks problems are designed to have extraneous roots.
- **Allowing negative under even root after squaring**: The candidate may make radicand negative.
- **Thinking odd-root equations can have extraneous**: They usually don't, but domain of other parts of equation may still exclude.
- **Not squaring the entire side**: When isolating one radical, the other side (which may contain another radical or constants) must be squared completely.
- **Losing solutions when there are two even roots**: Each may contribute ± but principal roots are non-neg; the ± comes from the algebra after.
- **Domain restrictions from original not carried forward**: Even after simplification, original even-root domains must hold.
