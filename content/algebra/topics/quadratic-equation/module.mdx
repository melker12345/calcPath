# Quadratic equation

A quadratic equation contains a squared term as its highest power of the variable. In standard form it is ax² + bx + c = 0 with a ≠ 0.

Because the graph of the related function is a parabola, a quadratic equation can have zero, one, or two real solutions. This richer behavior compared with linear equations makes quadratics central to algebra.

## Standard Form and Terminology

<!-- section: standard-form -->

ax² + bx + c = 0 is called standard form.

a is the leading coefficient, b the linear coefficient, c the constant term.

The solutions are often called roots, zeros, or x-intercepts.

**ELI5**

- The x² term makes the sides of the "bowl" curve; the higher the power the faster it grows.
- a tells whether the bowl opens upward (a > 0) or downward (a < 0).

**Worked Example:**

x² − 5x + 6 = 0 has a = 1, b = −5, c = 6. It factors nicely and has two roots.

## Solving by Factoring

<!-- section: solving-by-factoring -->

When a quadratic factors into (px + q)(rx + s) = 0, the zero-product property immediately gives the two roots by setting each factor to zero.

Many quadratics are factorable over the integers after a little practice with looking for factor pairs of c that add to b.

**ELI5**

- Factoring turns a single hard puzzle into two easy linear puzzles.
- If two numbers multiply to zero, one of them has to be the zero.

**Worked Example:**

x² − 7x + 12 = 0 factors as (x − 3)(x − 4) = 0.

Therefore x − 3 = 0 → x = 3 or x − 4 = 0 → x = 4.

## Graph and Roots Relationship

<!-- section: graph-and-roots -->

The real solutions of ax² + bx + c = 0 are exactly the x-values where the parabola y = ax² + bx + c crosses or touches the x-axis.

If the parabola opens upward and the vertex is above the x-axis there are no real roots; if it touches there is one (repeated) root; if it crosses there are two.

**ELI5**

- Picture throwing a ball; the times it is at ground level are the roots of the height equation.
- The picture of the parabola immediately tells you how many times it hits the ground.

**Worked Example:**

For y = x² − 4 the parabola crosses x-axis at x = −2 and x = 2, matching the equation x² − 4 = 0.

## Real-World Applications

<!-- section: applications -->

Quadratic equations appear in physics (projectile motion), geometry (areas, Pythagorean problems), business (profit maximization), and whenever a quantity has a squared relationship.

Translate the situation, form the equation, solve (factoring, completing the square, or quadratic formula), discard any physically meaningless roots, and state the answer in context.

**ELI5**

- Many "how far?" or "when?" questions involving acceleration or area produce a squared term.
- The parabola shape often matches the real curve of the situation.

**Worked Example:**

A rectangular garden has area 60 m². One side is 4 m longer than the other. Let width = x, length = x + 4. Then x(x + 4) = 60 → x² + 4x − 60 = 0. Factoring gives (x + 10)(x − 6) = 0. Positive width x = 6 m, length 10 m.

## Common Mistakes

- **Forgetting that a must be nonzero**: If a = 0 the equation collapses to linear.
- **Stopping after finding one root**: Quadratics frequently have two roots; check both.
- **Sign errors when factoring**: The pair of numbers that multiply to c must add to b; watch negatives.
- **Not checking by substitution**: Especially important when using the quadratic formula later.
- **Assuming every quadratic has two real roots**: The discriminant can be negative (no real roots) or zero (one real root).
