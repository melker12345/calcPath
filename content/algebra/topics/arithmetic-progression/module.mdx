# Arithmetic sequence

An arithmetic sequence (also called arithmetic progression) is a list of numbers in which each term after the first is obtained by adding a fixed constant d (the common difference) to the preceding term.

Examples: 2, 5, 8, 11, ... (d=3); 10, 7, 4, 1, ... (d=-3); the odd numbers 1, 3, 5, 7, ...

Arithmetic sequences model any situation with constant additive change: equal deposits, constant speed (distance each second), evenly spaced fence posts, etc. Their partial sums are arithmetic series.

## Definition and General Term

<!-- section: definition-and-general-term -->

An arithmetic sequence is determined by:

- The first term a (or a_1)
- The common difference d

The nth term is given by the explicit formula:

a_n = a + (n - 1) d

This is a linear function of n: the terms lie on an arithmetic (straight-line) progression when plotted against their index.

**ELI5**

- Each step you add exactly the same amount d, like climbing stairs of equal height.
- To find the 10th step you don't count one by one; you do first + 9 * d.

**Worked Example:**

Sequence: 4, 7, 10, 13, ... a=4, d=3.

a_5 = 4 + 4*3 = 16.

a_20 = 4 + 19*3 = 61.

**Worked Example:**

If a_1 = 100 and a_6 = 70, then d = (70-100)/5 = -6.

General term: 100 + (n-1)(-6) = 106 - 6n.

## Sum of a Finite Arithmetic Series

<!-- section: sum-of-finite-arithmetic-series -->

The sum of the first n terms S_n of an arithmetic series is:

S_n = n/2 * [2a + (n-1)d ]

or equivalently

S_n = n/2 * (first term + last term)

Derivation: write the sum forward and backward, add the two identical sums: each of the n pairs sums to (first + last), then divide by 2.

This formula lets us add long lists without adding term by term.

**ELI5**

- Pair the first and last (they add to the same constant), second and second-last, etc. There are n/2 such pairs.
- Multiply the pair sum by how many pairs.

**Worked Example:**

Sum of first 5 terms of 3, 5, 7, 9, 11.

First+last=3+11=14, n=5, S=5/2 *14=35.

(Or 5/2 * [2*3 +4*2]=5/2*(6+8)=35.)

**Worked Example:**

Sum of integers from 1 to 100: S=100/2 * (1+100)= 50*101=5050. (Gauss's famous shortcut.)

## Applications and Word Problems

<!-- section: applications-and-word-problems -->

Arithmetic sequences appear whenever a quantity increases or decreases by a constant amount per time period or per step:

- Savings: fixed amount deposited each month.
- Linear motion at constant velocity (position each second).
- Seats in a theater or bleachers with constant added per row.
- Number of handshakes or blocks in a pattern that grows linearly.

To solve word problems: identify a and d from the description, decide if you need a particular term or the sum, use the formulas, interpret the answer in context.

**ELI5**

- "Constant step size" is the signature of arithmetic.
- The formulas turn "how many steps" and "size of each step" into "where you are" or "total accumulated."

**Worked Example:**

A worker earns $50 on day 1 and gets a $3 raise each day. How much total after 30 days?

a=50, d=3, n=30. Last day pay=50+29*3=137.

Total S_30 =30/2 * (50+137)=15*187=2805.

**Worked Example:**

An arithmetic sequence of temperatures: starts at 20°C and drops 1.5° each hour. What is the temperature after 8 hours?

a_9 =20 +8*(-1.5)=20-12=8°C.

## Common Mistakes

- **Using n instead of (n-1) in the general term**: a_n = a + n d would be off by one (gives the (n+1)th term).
- **Forgetting the /2 in the sum formula**: Easy to double the sum.
- **Confusing sequence (the list of terms) with series (the sum of terms)**: The problem may ask for the 10th term or the sum of first 10.
- **Wrong sign for d**: If the sequence is decreasing, d is negative; the formulas still work.
- **Applying the arithmetic sum formula to a geometric sequence**: Different pattern (common ratio instead of difference).
- **Not identifying the first term correctly**: Sometimes the problem gives the second term or a later one; solve for a first.
- **Units or context errors**: The answer 2805 dollars makes sense; 2805 days probably does not.
