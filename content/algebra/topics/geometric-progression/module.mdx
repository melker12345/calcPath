# Geometric sequence

A geometric sequence (geometric progression) is a list in which each term after the first is obtained by multiplying the preceding term by a fixed constant r called the common ratio.

Examples: 2, 6, 18, 54, ... (r=3); 100, 50, 25, 12.5, ... (r=1/2); 3, -6, 12, -24, ... (r=-2).

Geometric sequences model multiplicative growth or decay: compound interest, population growth, bouncing balls (heights), half-life decay, and many fractal or scaling patterns. Their partial sums are geometric series; when |r|<1 the infinite sum converges to a finite value.

## Definition and General Term

<!-- section: definition-and-general-term -->

A geometric sequence is determined by:

- The first term a
- The common ratio r

The nth term has the explicit (closed-form) formula:

a_n = a * r^{n-1}

This is an exponential function of the index n.

If r > 1 the terms grow without bound (if a ≠ 0).

If 0 < r < 1 the terms shrink toward zero.

If r < 0 the signs alternate.

**ELI5**

- Each step you multiply by the same "growth factor" r, like a photocopier set to enlarge or shrink by a fixed percentage.
- The term number tells how many multiplications by r have happened since the start.

**Worked Example:**

3, 6, 12, 24, ... a=3, r=2.

a_6 = 3 * 2^5 = 3*32=96.

**Worked Example:**

A quantity starts at 1000 and is multiplied by 0.9 each day. After 10 days: 1000 * (0.9)^9 (for the 10th term, n-1=9).

## Sum of a Finite Geometric Series

<!-- section: sum-of-finite-geometric-series -->

The sum of the first n terms S_n of a geometric series (r ≠ 1) is:

S_n = a \frac{r^n - 1}{r - 1}

(or equivalently a \frac{1 - r^n}{1 - r} when written with 1-r in denominator).

Derivation: write S, multiply by r, subtract the two equations, solve for S.

When r=1 the sum is simply n a (all terms equal a).

**ELI5**

- The trick of multiplying the sum by r shifts every term; subtracting cancels most terms, leaving only the first and the "next after last".
- Dividing by (r-1) "normalizes" to give the closed form.

**Worked Example:**

Sum of first 5 terms of 4, 12, 36, ... (a=4, r=3).

S_5 =4*(3^5 -1)/(3-1) =4*(243-1)/2 =4*121=484.

**Worked Example:**

Sum of geometric series 1 + 1/2 + 1/4 + ... + 1/2^{9} (10 terms, a=1 r=1/2).

S= (1 - (1/2)^{10} ) / (1 - 1/2) = (1 - 1/1024)/(1/2) = 2*(1023/1024) ≈ 1.998 .

## Infinite Series and Applications

<!-- section: infinite-series-and-applications -->

When |r| < 1 the infinite geometric series sums to the finite value

S_∞ = a / (1 - r)

(This is the limit of the finite sum formula as n→∞, since r^n →0.)

This is the foundation for many repeating decimal representations and for the mathematics of fractals, probability (infinite trials), and physics (infinite reflections, damping).

**ELI5**

- If each step you keep only a fraction r <1 of the previous, the total "stuff" you ever get is finite even if you continue forever.
- The formula a/(1-r) is the "grand total" after all the infinite shrinking steps.

**Worked Example:**

0.3333... = 3/10 + 3/100 + 3/1000 + ... = (0.3) / (1 - 0.1) = 0.3 / 0.9 = 1/3.

**Worked Example:**

A ball dropped from 10 m bounces to 60% of previous height each time. Total distance traveled (down + up + down + ...):

First drop 10 m.

Then 2 * (6 + 6*0.6 + 6*0.6^2 + ...) = 2 * 6 / (1-0.6) = 12 / 0.4 = 30 m.

Grand total 10 + 30 = 40 m.

## Common Mistakes

- **Using the arithmetic sum formula on a geometric series (or vice versa)**: Different closed forms; check whether the problem adds or multiplies each step.
- **Forgetting the exponent is n-1**: a r^n would be the term after the nth.
- **Applying the infinite sum formula when |r| ≥ 1**: The series diverges (grows without bound or oscillates).
- **Sign errors with negative r**: The sum formula still holds, but terms alternate; the infinite sum may still converge if |r|<1.
- **Confusing the first term a with S_1**: S_1 = a, but the formula a(r^n-1)/(r-1) for n=1 gives a correctly only after algebra.
- **Writing the sum formula with r in the numerator instead of r^n**: a(r-1)/(r^n-1) is backwards.
- **Forgetting to multiply by 2 for up-and-down bounces in ball problems**: The infinite geometric after the first drop is usually doubled.
- **Domain for r=1 case**: Must handle separately; the general formula has division by zero.
