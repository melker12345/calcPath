# Foundations of Algebra

Variables, expressions, equations, inequalities, and order of operations — the language of symbolic math.

Algebra is the language of generalization in mathematics. Instead of working only with specific numbers like 3 + 5 = 8, algebra lets us write rules and solve problems that apply to whole families of numbers using symbols.

It forms the foundation for almost every branch of mathematics that follows: geometry with coordinates, calculus with rates of change, statistics with models, and physics with formulas. Mastering algebra means learning to think symbolically and manipulate relationships fluently.

## What is Algebra?

<!-- section: what-is-algebra -->

Algebra begins with the idea of replacing specific numbers with letters (or other symbols) so that statements become general. Arithmetic tells us 2 + 3 = 5; algebra lets us say x + 3 = 5 and then solve for the unknown x.

At its core algebra studies structures, relations, and operations on symbols. The same techniques solve problems in finance, science, engineering, and daily life.

**ELI5**

- Think of arithmetic as counting specific apples; algebra is writing a recipe that works no matter how many apples you have.
- Letters are like boxes that can hold different numbers; we open the box by solving.

**Worked Example:**

Suppose you know that a number plus 7 equals 12. In arithmetic you might guess, but algebra writes the equation x + 7 = 12. Subtract 7 from both sides: x = 12 − 7 = 5. The symbol x stood for 5.

## Variables and Symbolic Thinking

<!-- section: variables-symbols -->

A variable is a symbol (commonly x, y, n, or t) that represents an unknown or a quantity that can change. Constants are fixed numbers like 2 or π.

Expressions combine variables and constants with operations: 2x + 3y − 5. Equations set two expressions equal: 2x + 3 = 11.

Using variables we can write formulas that work universally, such as the area of a rectangle A = l w or the distance formula.

**ELI5**

- A variable is a placeholder, like a blank in a mad-lib that gets filled with a number later.
- Once you choose a value for x, the whole expression becomes a regular arithmetic problem.

**Worked Example:**

The perimeter of a rectangle is P = 2l + 2w. If l = 5 and w = 3, then P = 2(5) + 2(3) = 10 + 6 = 16. The same formula gives the perimeter for any length and width.

## Solving Problems Algebraically

<!-- section: solving-problems -->

The power of algebra appears when we translate real situations into symbols, solve the resulting equation or inequality, and translate the answer back.

Typical steps: assign a variable to the unknown, write an equation from the given relationships, perform inverse operations to isolate the variable, check the solution, and interpret.

Algebraic manipulation (adding the same to both sides, multiplying both sides by the same nonzero number) preserves equality and lets us rearrange any equation.

**ELI5**

- Solving is like unwrapping a present: each inverse operation removes one layer until the variable is revealed.
- Always check by plugging the answer back into the original statement.

**Worked Example:**

A number is doubled and then 4 is subtracted; the result is 10. Let the number be x. Then 2x − 4 = 10. Add 4: 2x = 14. Divide by 2: x = 7. Check: 2(7) − 4 = 14 − 4 = 10. Correct.

## Common Mistakes

- **Treating algebra like pure arithmetic without variables**: Forgetting to use letters when a problem asks for a general rule.
- **Changing the value of one side without doing the same to the other**: The golden rule "do the same to both sides" is broken.
- **Assuming a variable can only be a positive integer**: Variables can represent fractions, negatives, or even other expressions.
- **Skipping the check step**: Solutions that satisfy a manipulated equation may be extraneous if operations like squaring or dividing by variables were used.
- **Confusing expressions with equations**: 2x + 3 is an expression (has no =); 2x + 3 = 7 is an equation.

A variable is a symbol that stands for a number we do not yet know or that is allowed to change. It is the central tool that turns arithmetic into the powerful general language of algebra.

Without variables we could only solve one problem at a time with specific numbers. With variables we write once and solve for all similar problems.

## Definition of a Variable

<!-- section: definition -->

In mathematics a variable is a letter or other symbol that represents a quantity whose value is unknown, unspecified, or allowed to vary. Common choices include x, y, z, a, b, n, t.

Variables are distinct from constants (fixed numbers such as 2, −7, or π) and from the operators that combine them.

**ELI5**

- Imagine a box with a question mark on it; inside is a number, but we have not opened it yet. The box is the variable.
- Later we may discover the number inside is 4, or we may decide the box can hold any number we like.

**Worked Example:**

The sentence "a number plus four equals ten" becomes the equation x + 4 = 10. Here x is the variable standing for the unknown number.

## Uses of Variables in Mathematics

<!-- section: uses-in-math -->

Variables appear everywhere:

- To represent unknowns we want to solve for.
- To express general relationships and formulas (area, distance, interest).
- To define functions that map inputs to outputs.
- In statistics to represent data values or parameters.
- In science to stand for measurable quantities like time, mass, or temperature.

Using the same variable consistently within a problem keeps the meaning clear.

**ELI5**

- A single formula with variables is like a machine: drop in different numbers for the variables and it produces the correct answer every time.
- One equation can answer "what if the price is $5?" and "what if the price is $12?" without rewriting everything.

**Worked Example:**

The formula for the area of a triangle is A = (1/2) b h. Here A, b, and h are variables. If b = 8 and h = 5, then A = (1/2) × 8 × 5 = 20. The same formula works for any base and height.

## Notation and Conventions

<!-- section: notation-conventions -->

Certain letters carry conventional meanings that make reading mathematics easier:

- x, y, z: general unknowns or coordinates.
- a, b, c: coefficients or constants in equations.
- n, m, k: counting numbers, indices, or discrete quantities.
- t: time or a parameter.
- Greek letters (α, β, θ, π): often reserved for angles or special constants.

Coefficients are the numbers multiplying variables (the 2 in 2x). Subscripts (x_1, x_2) distinguish different variables of the same letter.

**ELI5**

- Mathematicians are a little lazy and very consistent; they reuse the same letters for the same kinds of jobs so readers instantly know what role each symbol plays.
- Subscripts are like name tags: x_1 is "the first x", x_2 is "the second x".

**Worked Example:**

In the quadratic equation ax² + bx + c = 0, a, b, and c are conventionally the coefficients. The solutions depend on the values we assign to those three variables.

## Common Mistakes

- **Using the same letter for two different unknowns in one problem**: This creates confusion; choose distinct letters.
- **Treating a variable as always positive or always an integer**: Variables can represent any real (or complex) number unless the context restricts them.
- **Forgetting that the variable name is arbitrary**: x + 3 = 7 is the same problem as w + 3 = 7; only the label changed.
- **Confusing the variable with its value**: The variable x is the symbol; after solving we may say "x equals 4" meaning the value that x represents.
- **Omitting the multiplication sign before a variable**: 3x means 3 × x; writing 3 × x is also correct but 3x is standard.

An algebraic expression is a phrase made of numbers, variables, and operation symbols. It has a value once the variables are given numbers, but it does not assert that anything equals anything else.

Expressions are the building blocks out of which equations, inequalities, and functions are constructed.

## Definition and Structure

<!-- section: definition -->

An expression is any finite combination of constants, variables, and the four arithmetic operations (and sometimes exponents, roots, etc.) that represents a single mathematical object.

Examples: 5, −x, 3a + 2b − 7, (x + 1)/(x − 2), 2x^3 − 5x + 4.

Expressions do not contain an equals sign, question mark, or the instruction "solve."

**ELI5**

- An expression is like a recipe or a shopping list: it tells you what to combine, but it is not yet a complete sentence claiming a fact.
- Once you fill in numbers for the variables you can calculate a single number result.

**Worked Example:**

The expression 4x − 9 tells us "take four times some number and subtract nine." It does not say it equals anything until we write an equation.

## Evaluating Expressions

<!-- section: evaluating -->

To evaluate an expression means to substitute specific numbers for each variable and then compute the resulting arithmetic value.

Order of operations (PEMDAS) must be respected. Parentheses first, then exponents, multiplication/division left to right, addition/subtraction left to right.

**ELI5**

- Plugging in numbers turns the abstract recipe into a concrete calculation you can do on a calculator or by hand.
- Every different choice of numbers for the variables generally gives a different numerical answer.

**Worked Example:**

Evaluate  x^2 − 3x + 2  when x = 4.

4² − 3(4) + 2 = 16 − 12 + 2 = 6.

When x = 0: 0 − 0 + 2 = 2.

## Simplifying Expressions

<!-- section: simplifying -->

Simplifying rewrites an expression into a shorter, clearer, or more convenient equivalent form without changing its value for any substitution.

Common techniques: combine like terms, factor out common factors, expand using the distributive property, cancel common factors in fractions (when defined).

**ELI5**

- Simplifying is housekeeping: you tidy up the expression so it is easier to read, compare, or use in the next step.
- 2x + 3x is the same as 5x, but 5x is neater.

**Worked Example:**

Simplify 5x + 2 − 3x + 7.

Group like terms: (5x − 3x) + (2 + 7) = 2x + 9.

The simplified expression 2x + 9 equals the original for every x.

## Expressions versus Equations

<!-- section: vs-equations -->

An expression is a phrase; an equation is a sentence that claims two expressions have the same value.

You can simplify or evaluate expressions. You solve equations (find values that make the sentence true).

An expression by itself is never "true" or "false"; it simply has a value.

**ELI5**

- "My age plus five" is an expression. "My age plus five equals 20" is an equation you can solve.
- You cannot "solve" an expression; you can only simplify it or plug numbers into it.

**Worked Example:**

The expression x + 5 can be simplified (it already is simple) or evaluated. The equation x + 5 = 12 can be solved: x = 7.

## Common Mistakes

- **Putting an equals sign after an expression when you only meant to simplify**: Write 2x + 3x = 5x only if you are stating an identity; otherwise just write the result 5x.
- **Forgetting to distribute the negative sign**: −(x + 2) = −x − 2, not −x + 2.
- **Evaluating without respecting order of operations**: Always do parentheses and exponents before multiplying.
- **Confusing "simplify" with "solve"**: Simplifying produces another expression; solving produces numbers that satisfy an equation.
- **Canceling terms that are not factors**: You can cancel common factors in a fraction but not common addends in a sum.

An equation is a complete mathematical sentence that says two expressions represent the same quantity. The equals sign = is the verb of the sentence.

Solving an equation means discovering the value or values of the variable that make the sentence true.

Equations are the primary tool we use to answer "what number satisfies this condition?" questions in every quantitative field.

## Definition and Components

<!-- section: definition -->

An equation consists of two expressions joined by an equals sign. The expressions on either side are called the left-hand side (LHS) and right-hand side (RHS).

Examples: 5 = 5 (identity, always true), x + 2 = 7 (conditional, true only for specific x), 2x = 2x (identity).

An equation may be true for all values (identity), for some values (conditional), or for none (contradiction).

**ELI5**

- Think of the = sign as a balance scale that is perfectly level only when the two sides weigh the same.
- The job of solving is to find which weight on the variable side makes the scale balance.

**Worked Example:**

The equation  x + 3 = 10  says "whatever x is, when you add three you get ten." The two sides are expressions that must be equal.

## Solving Linear Equations

<!-- section: solving-linear -->

The basic strategy is to use inverse operations to isolate the variable while keeping the equation balanced.

Allowed moves (that produce an equivalent equation):

- Add or subtract the same number from both sides.
- Multiply or divide both sides by the same nonzero number.
- Simplify expressions on either side.

**ELI5**

- Each move is like carefully removing one layer of wrapping without tilting the balance.
- You want the variable standing alone on one side and a number on the other.

**Worked Example:**

Solve 3x − 5 = 16.

Add 5 to both sides: 3x = 21.

Divide both sides by 3: x = 7.

## Properties that Preserve Equality

<!-- section: properties -->

The reflexive, symmetric, and transitive properties, plus the addition and multiplication properties of equality, guarantee that the steps we take do not change the solution set.

If a = b then a + c = b + c and a * c = b * c (c ≠ 0 when dividing).

These properties justify every legal manipulation.

**ELI5**

- If two piles of blocks are equal, adding the same number of blocks to each pile keeps them equal.
- The same holds for multiplying every block count by the same factor.

**Worked Example:**

Starting from x − 4 = 9, add 4: x = 13. Because we added the same amount to both sides, any x that worked before still works, and no new solutions appear.

## Checking Solutions

<!-- section: checking -->

After obtaining a candidate solution, substitute it back into the original equation. If both sides become equal, it is a valid solution.

Checking catches arithmetic errors and extraneous solutions introduced by operations such as multiplying by an expression containing the variable or raising to an even power.

**ELI5**

- Checking is your safety net. The algebra steps can sometimes create "fake" answers that only work after you changed the equation.
- Always plug the number back into the very first version you wrote.

**Worked Example:**

We solved 2x = 10 and got x = 5. Check in original: left side 2*5 = 10, right side 10. Equal, so x = 5 is correct.

## Common Mistakes

- **Moving a term to the other side without changing its sign**: Subtracting 5 is the same as adding −5; the sign must flip when crossing the equals sign.
- **Dividing by zero or by an expression that might be zero**: This is illegal and can lose or add solutions.
- **Forgetting to distribute when parentheses are present**: 3(x + 2) = 3x + 6, not 3x + 2.
- **Not checking the solution**: Many errors are revealed only at verification time.
- **Assuming every equation has exactly one solution**: Some have none, some have infinitely many (identities), some have two or more.

An inequality is a statement that one quantity is larger than, smaller than, or possibly equal to another. The symbols <, >, ≤, and ≥ replace the equals sign of an equation.

Solving inequalities is almost identical to solving equations, with one crucial extra rule: multiplying or dividing by a negative number reverses the inequality direction.

Inequalities describe ranges of values and appear in optimization, constraints, and describing intervals.

## Definition and Symbols

<!-- section: definition -->

The four basic inequality symbols are:

- a < b : a is less than b
- a > b : a is greater than b
- a ≤ b : a is less than or equal to b
- a ≥ b : a is greater than or equal to b

These comparisons can involve variables and are true for some numbers and false for others.

**ELI5**

- The symbols are like hungry alligators: the open mouth always points toward the bigger number.
- ≤ adds a little line that means "or equal," like including the endpoint on a fence.

**Worked Example:**

3 < 7 is true. −2 > 1 is false. x ≤ 5 is true for every number 5 and smaller.

## Solving Linear Inequalities

<!-- section: solving -->

The same inverse operations used for equations apply, except:

When you multiply or divide both sides by a negative number, you must flip the inequality symbol.

All other operations (add, subtract, multiply or divide by positive) keep the direction unchanged.

**ELI5**

- Multiplying by a negative is like looking in a mirror: left becomes right and the order reverses.
- Adding or subtracting never flips the order.

**Worked Example:**

Solve −2x + 1 ≥ 7.

Subtract 1: −2x ≥ 6.

Divide by −2 and flip: x ≤ −3.

## Graphing Solutions on the Number Line

<!-- section: graphing-on-number-line -->

The solution set of an inequality is an interval (or union of intervals) that can be shown on a number line.

- Open circle (○) for strict inequalities < or > (endpoint not included).
- Closed/filled circle (●) for ≤ or ≥ (endpoint included).
- Arrow or shading shows the direction of the solution ray or interval.

**ELI5**

- The number line becomes a "road" where only certain parts are open for travel.
- The circle tells you whether the exact endpoint house is allowed or off-limits.

**Worked Example:**

x > 2 is shown as an open circle at 2 and an arrow extending right to infinity. All numbers bigger than 2 are shaded.

## Compound Inequalities

<!-- section: compound-inequalities -->

Compound inequalities combine two inequalities with "and" (intersection) or "or" (union).

"And" statements such as a < x < b describe an interval between two numbers.

Solving often requires splitting into two separate inequalities and then intersecting or unioning the results.

**ELI5**

- "And" means both conditions must be true at the same time, like being taller than 5 feet AND shorter than 6 feet.
- "Or" means at least one is true.

**Worked Example:**

Solve −1 ≤ x + 2 < 5.

Subtract 2 from all three parts: −3 ≤ x < 3.

The solution is all x from −3 inclusive to 3 exclusive.

## Common Mistakes

- **Forgetting to flip the inequality when multiplying or dividing by a negative**: This is the single most common error and produces an interval on the wrong side of the number line.
- **Using a closed circle for a strict inequality**: < or > never include the endpoint.
- **Treating compound inequalities as two separate problems without recombining**: The final answer must satisfy both parts simultaneously for "and" statements.
- **Dividing by a variable without knowing its sign**: You cannot decide whether to flip without knowing the sign.
- **Writing the answer with the variable on the wrong side**: x < 5 is preferred over 5 > x for readability, though both are correct.

When an expression contains several different operations, we need an agreed-upon sequence so that everyone who reads it computes exactly the same value. That sequence is called the order of operations.

The most common mnemonic is PEMDAS (or BODMAS in some countries). The rules are universal in mathematics.

## The PEMDAS Rule

<!-- section: pemdas-rule -->

Evaluate expressions in this order:

1. Parentheses (or other grouping symbols: brackets, braces, fraction bars, absolute value bars).
2. Exponents (and roots, which are fractional exponents).
3. Multiplication and Division, performed left to right.
4. Addition and Subtraction, performed left to right.

Multiplication does not always come before division, nor addition before subtraction; they are peers and we scan left to right.

**ELI5**

- Parentheses are like a force field that says "do this part first no matter what."
- Exponents are powerful and happen early. Multiplication/division are next because they are repeated addition/subtraction. Plain + and − finish last.

**Worked Example:**

1. Start with $3 + 4 \times 2 − 1$. No exponents.
2. Multiplication first (left to right): $4 \times 2 = 8$.
3. Then addition and subtraction left to right: $3 + 8 = 11$, then $11 − 1 = 10$.

## Why a Standard Order Matters

<!-- section: why-order-matters -->

Without a fixed order the expression 3 + 4 × 2 could legitimately be interpreted as either 14 or 11. Ambiguity would make formulas, computer programs, and communication unreliable.

The order of operations is a social contract that lets us write compact expressions while guaranteeing a unique result.

**ELI5**

- Imagine traffic lights: everyone agrees red means stop. If every driver chose their own rule, crashes would be constant.
- The same for math symbols; the PEMDAS contract prevents crashes in calculations.

**Worked Example:**

1. Without order, $6 \div 2(1 + 2)$ has caused internet arguments.
2. With order: parentheses first → $6 \div 2 \times 3$.
3. Then left-to-right division and multiplication: $6 \div 2 \times 3 = 9$. The expression is deliberately written to be parsed one way.

## Parentheses and Exponents

<!-- section: parentheses-exponents -->

Parentheses override every other rule. They force early evaluation.

Exponents (powers) are performed before multiplication. Roots are also early because √x = x^{1/2}.

Nested parentheses are evaluated from the innermost outward.

**ELI5**

- Parentheses are the "do this NOW" boss of the expression.
- Exponents tell how many times to multiply a number by itself; they are stronger than ordinary multiplication so they come first.

**Worked Example:**

1. Start with $(2 + 3)^2 \times 4$.
2. Innermost parentheses: $2 + 3 = 5$.
3. Exponent: $5^2 = 25$.
4. Multiplication: $25 \times 4 = 100$.

## Common Expressions and Left-to-Right Rule

<!-- section: common-expressions -->

In real expressions multiplication and division often appear together, as do addition and subtraction. Always finish one pair left to right before moving to the next lower priority.

Fraction bars act as grouping symbols: the entire numerator and denominator are evaluated before the division occurs.

**ELI5**

- When two operations have the same "rank" (× and ÷, or + and −), read the sentence from left to right just as you read English.
- The fraction bar is like a big pair of parentheses around top and bottom.

**Worked Example:**

1. Start with $8 − 6 \div 2 + 3 \times 4$.
2. Division and multiplication left to right: $6 \div 2 = 3$, $3 \times 4 = 12$.
3. Addition and subtraction left to right: $8 − 3 = 5$, $5 + 12 = 17$.

## Common Mistakes

- **Doing operations strictly in the order the letters appear instead of left-to-right for MD and AS**: PEMDAS does not mean "multiplication always before division"; it means the MD group, then within the group left to right.
- **Ignoring parentheses**: 2 × (3 + 4) is 14, not 2 × 3 + 4 = 10.
- **Treating exponents as left to right with multiplication**: 2^3 × 2 = 16, not (2^3 × 2) wait, actually correct is 8 × 2 = 16, but 2^3^2 is right-associative in some contexts; for basic algebra keep simple.
- **Forgetting that a fraction bar groups the numerator and denominator**: (1+2)/3 is not 1 + 2/3.
- **Performing addition before multiplication just because it appears first on the page**: 2 + 3 × 4 = 14, never 20.
