# Quadratic inequality

A quadratic inequality is an inequality that can be written with a quadratic expression on one side and zero on the other. The solution is usually an interval or a union of two intervals on the real line.

Because a quadratic changes sign only at its roots (when they exist), we locate the roots and then determine the sign in each interval they create.

## Moving to Standard Form and Factoring

<!-- section: factoring-first -->

The first step is almost always to bring every term to the left side so the inequality reads quadratic expression  ?  0.

Then factor (or use quadratic formula for roots) to find the critical numbers where the expression is zero.

**ELI5**

- Getting everything on one side lets you ask a simple question: "where is this expression positive or negative?"
- Factoring turns the expression into a product whose sign is easy to read from the factors.

**Worked Example:**

x² − 3x − 10 > 0 becomes (x − 5)(x + 2) > 0 after factoring.

## The Sign Chart (or Test-Point) Method

<!-- section: sign-chart -->

1. Find the roots and draw them on a number line; they divide the line into intervals.
2. Pick one convenient test point in each open interval.
3. Plug the test point into the quadratic (or evaluate the factored form) and record the sign.
4. Mark the intervals where the sign matches the inequality.
5. Decide whether to include the roots based on ≤ or ≥.

**ELI5**

- The roots are the "fence posts." Between posts the expression keeps the same "mood" (positive or negative).
- One quick plug per room tells you which rooms are allowed.

**Worked Example:**

For (x − 5)(x + 2) > 0 the posts are −2 and 5.

Test x = −3: (+) (negative) = negative.

Test x = 0: (−)(+) = negative.

Test x = 6: (+)(+) = positive.

Solution: x < −2 or x > 5.

## Leading Coefficient and Parabola Direction

<!-- section: test-points -->

The sign of the leading coefficient tells the end behavior of the parabola:

Positive a → opens up → positive far left and far right.

Negative a → opens down → negative far left and far right.

This lets you fill the outer intervals without testing once you know one middle interval.

**ELI5**

- If the bowl opens upward the far ends are high (positive). The middle may dip below zero between the roots.
- Downward bowl starts negative at both infinities.

**Worked Example:**

−x² + 5x + 6 ≤ 0 has negative leading coefficient. After factoring or testing you discover the solution is everywhere except the interval between the roots (or including depending on symbol).

## Writing the Solution in Interval Notation

<!-- section: solution-in-interval -->

Translate the marked intervals on the sign chart into interval notation, using ∪ for separate pieces.

Include square brackets at roots only when the inequality allows equality.

**ELI5**

- The chart picture becomes the compact math sentence that a computer or another person can read without the drawing.

**Worked Example:**

From the earlier chart: (−∞, −2) ∪ (5, ∞).

If the inequality had been ≥ the solution would be (−∞, −2] ∪ [5, ∞).

## Common Mistakes

- **Testing the roots themselves for sign**: At a root the value is exactly zero; you already know the sign change happens there.
- **Forgetting to consider the leading coefficient when predicting outer signs**: Saves time and catches errors.
- **Including or excluding roots incorrectly**: < and > never include; ≤ and ≥ do.
- **Not bringing all terms to one side first**: Mixed signs on right side make test points confusing.
- **Assuming every quadratic inequality has solutions**: When the parabola never crosses the required side there may be no solution or the entire line.
