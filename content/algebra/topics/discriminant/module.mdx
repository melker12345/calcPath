# Discriminant

The discriminant of the quadratic equation ax² + bx + c = 0 is the expression D = b² − 4 a c that appears under the square root in the quadratic formula.

Although it looks like a minor piece of the formula, the sign and value of D completely determine the number and nature of the solutions without having to compute the roots themselves. It is a powerful diagnostic tool.

## Definition and Role in the Formula

<!-- section: definition-and-role -->

D = b² - 4 a c .

In the quadratic formula the term √D tells us:

- Whether we stay in the reals or must enter the complex numbers.
- Whether the two potential roots are the same or different.

D is also equal to (root1 - root2)² * a² (up to sign), so |D| measures how far apart the roots are.

**ELI5**

- D is the "decider" inside the formula: positive → two different real answers; zero → exactly one; negative → complex pair.
- You can look at D first and know what kind of answer the whole formula will give.

**Worked Example:**

1. For $x^2 - 3x + 2 =0$, $D=9-8=1 >0 \to$ two real roots (indeed 1 and 2).
2. For $x^2 - 3x + 3 =0$, $D=9-12=-3 <0 \to$ two complex roots.

## Interpreting the Sign of D

<!-- section: interpreting-the-sign -->

Assuming a, b, c real and a ≠ 0:

- D > 0 : two distinct real roots. The parabola crosses the x-axis twice.
- D = 0 : exactly one real root (a double root). The parabola touches the x-axis at its vertex.
- D < 0 : no real roots; two complex conjugate roots. The parabola lies entirely above or entirely below the x-axis (depending on the sign of a).

The value of a tells the direction the parabola opens; D tells whether and how it meets the axis.

**ELI5**

- Positive D: the "mystery" under the root is a real number you can take ± of → two hits on the x-axis.
- Zero: exactly touches once.
- Negative: the square root is imaginary → the curve never reaches the x-axis in the real world.

**Worked Example:**

1. $2x^2 + 4x + 2 =0 \to D=16-16=0$. Root $x=-1$ (double). Graph touches at one point.

**Worked Example:**

1. $x^2 + 1 =0$, $D=-4<0$. No real roots. Graph of $y=x^2+1$ never crosses x-axis.

## Using the Discriminant Without Fully Solving

<!-- section: using-without-solving -->

Often we only need to know how many real solutions exist or whether the quadratic expression is always positive/negative.

Compute D only; no need for the full ± formula.

For inequalities (e.g. ax² + bx + c > 0) the sign of D plus the sign of a tells the solution set without finding the roots.

Vieta's formulas (sum and product of roots) can be used together with D for other relations.

**ELI5**

- D is like a quick test: "Will this quadratic equation have real answers?" before doing all the arithmetic.
- For "is this quadratic always above the x-axis?" D and a give the answer instantly.

**Worked Example:**

1. Does $3x^2 - 5x + 1 =0$ have real solutions? $D=25 - 12=13 >0 \to$ yes, two.

**Worked Example:**

1. For which $k$ does $x^2 + k x + 9 =0$ have exactly one real root? Set $D=0$: $k^2 - 36 =0 \to k=\pm 6$.

## Common Mistakes

- **Computing 4ac incorrectly (order of operations or sign)**: Must be b² minus (4 times a times c).
- **Forgetting that D=0 still counts as "real roots" (one of them)**: It does have a real solution (repeated).
- **Assuming D tells the roots themselves**: D only classifies; you still need the formula or factoring to find the actual numbers.
- **Ignoring a when describing the graph**: D= positive with a>0 still crosses, but the shape (width) depends on a.
- **Using D for non-quadratic equations**: Only valid when the equation really is quadratic (a≠0).
- **Sign error when D appears in inequalities**: The test intervals depend on both D and sign(a).
- **Thinking complex roots mean "no solutions at all"**: They are perfectly valid solutions in the complex numbers; just not real.
