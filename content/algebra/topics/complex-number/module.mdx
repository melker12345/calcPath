# Complex number

A complex number is a number of the form a + b i, where a and b are real numbers and i is the imaginary unit satisfying i² = -1.

Complex numbers extend the real numbers so that every polynomial equation has solutions (Fundamental Theorem of Algebra). They are essential in electrical engineering, quantum mechanics, signal processing, and higher mathematics. We can add, subtract, multiply, and divide them just like reals, with a few new rules.

## Definition and the Imaginary Unit

<!-- section: definition-and-imaginary-unit -->

i is defined by i² = -1.

Any complex z = a + b i :

- a is the real part, Re(z).
- b is the imaginary part, Im(z).
- If b=0, z is real.
- If a=0 and b≠0, z is pure imaginary.

The complex numbers can be visualized as the plane with real axis horizontal, imaginary vertical.

**ELI5**

- i is a new "number" we invent so that negatives have square roots: √(-1)=i.
- A complex number is like a pair (real, imaginary) that we treat as a single entity with its own arithmetic.

**Worked Example:**

1. $3 + 4i$ has real part $3$, imaginary part $4$.
2. $-2i$ is $0 - 2i$, pure imaginary.
3. $7$ is $7 + 0i$, real.

## Arithmetic Operations

<!-- section: arithmetic-operations -->

Addition: (a + bi) + (c + di) = (a+c) + (b+d)i .

Subtraction similar.

Multiplication: (a + bi)(c + di) = ac + a di + b c i + b d i² = (ac - bd) + (ad + bc)i   (since i²=-1).

Division: multiply num and den by conjugate of den, then simplify.

**ELI5**

- Add and subtract like combining like terms (reals with reals, i's with i's).
- Multiplication uses the distributive law and replaces i² with -1.

**Worked Example:**

1. $(1 + 2i)(3 - i) = 1\cdot3 +1\cdot(-i) +2i\cdot3 +2i\cdot(-i) = 3 -i +6i -2 i^2 = 3 +5i +2 = 5 + 5i$.

**Worked Example:**

1. $(2 + i) / (1 - i) \cdot (1 + i)/(1 + i) = [(2+i)(1+i)] / (1+1) = (2 + 2i + i + i^2)/2 = (2 + 3i -1)/2 = (1 + 3i)/2 = 1/2 + (3/2)i$.

## Complex Conjugates and Magnitude

<!-- section: complex-conjugates-and-magnitude -->

The conjugate of z = a + bi is \bar z = a - bi .

Properties: z \bar z = |z|^2 (real and non-negative), z + \bar z = 2 Re(z), etc.

The modulus (magnitude) |z| = √(a² + b²) .

It satisfies |z w| = |z| |w| and |z / w| = |z| / |w| (w≠0).

Geometrically, |z| is the distance from origin in the complex plane.

**ELI5**

- Conjugate is the "mirror image" across the real axis.
- Modulus is how "far from zero" the number is, generalizing absolute value.

**Worked Example:**

1. For $z=3-4i$, $\bar z = 3+4i$, $|z|=5$, $z \bar z = 9+16=25=5^2$.

**Worked Example:**

1. $|(1+i)(2-i)| = |1+i| \cdot |2-i| = \sqrt{2} \cdot \sqrt{5} = \sqrt{10}$.

## Solving Equations with Complex Numbers

<!-- section: solving-with-complex -->

Quadratics with negative discriminant have complex roots that come in conjugate pairs (when coefficients real).

Higher degree polynomials factor completely into linears over the complexes.

To solve, use quadratic formula (it works for complex), or factor using complex numbers.

**ELI5**

- When the "discriminant under the square root" goes negative, the answers are no longer on the real number line; they have an imaginary component.
- The math still works; we just accept i as part of the answer.

**Worked Example:**

1. $x^2 + 4 = 0 \to x^2 = -4 \to x = \pm 2i$.

**Worked Example:**

1. Solve $x^2 - 2x + 5 = 0$.
2. $x = [2 \pm \sqrt{4 - 20}]/2 = [2 \pm \sqrt{-16}]/2 = [2 \pm 4i]/2 = 1 \pm 2i$.
3. Check: $(1+2i)^2 - 2(1+2i) + 5 = 1 + 4i + 4i^2 - 2 - 4i + 5 = (1-2+5) + (4i-4i) + 4(-1) = 4 - 4 = 0$. Yes.

## Common Mistakes

- **Writing i² = +1 or forgetting to replace i² with -1 in multiplication**: Leads to wrong real/imag parts.
- **Treating i as a variable**: i is a constant; you cannot "solve for i".
- **Forgetting the conjugate when dividing**: Denominator must be rationalized to a real number.
- **Assuming all complex solutions are pure imaginary**: Most have both real and imag parts.
- **Sign error on the imaginary part when finding conjugates or when distributing minus**.
- **Computing modulus without the square root**: |z| is the sqrt, not a² + b².
- **Forgetting that for real-coeff polynomials, complex roots come in conjugate pairs**: If 1+2i is a root, so is 1-2i.
