# Complex Numbers & Sequences

Complex numbers, the imaginary unit, and arithmetic and geometric sequences.

A complex number is a number of the form a + b i, where a and b are real numbers and i is the imaginary unit satisfying i² = -1.

Complex numbers extend the real numbers so that every polynomial equation has solutions (Fundamental Theorem of Algebra). They are essential in electrical engineering, quantum mechanics, signal processing, and higher mathematics. We can add, subtract, multiply, and divide them just like reals, with a few new rules.

## Definition and the Imaginary Unit

<!-- section: definition-and-imaginary-unit -->

i is defined by i² = -1.

Any complex z = a + b i :

- a is the real part, Re(z).
- b is the imaginary part, Im(z).
- If b=0, z is real.
- If a=0 and b≠0, z is pure imaginary.

The complex numbers can be visualized as the plane with real axis horizontal, imaginary vertical.

**ELI5**

- i is a new "number" we invent so that negatives have square roots: √(-1)=i.
- A complex number is like a pair (real, imaginary) that we treat as a single entity with its own arithmetic.

**Worked Example:**

1. $3 + 4i$ has real part $3$, imaginary part $4$.
2. $-2i$ is $0 - 2i$, pure imaginary.
3. $7$ is $7 + 0i$, real.

## Arithmetic Operations

<!-- section: arithmetic-operations -->

Addition: (a + bi) + (c + di) = (a+c) + (b+d)i .

Subtraction similar.

Multiplication: (a + bi)(c + di) = ac + a di + b c i + b d i² = (ac - bd) + (ad + bc)i   (since i²=-1).

Division: multiply num and den by conjugate of den, then simplify.

**ELI5**

- Add and subtract like combining like terms (reals with reals, i's with i's).
- Multiplication uses the distributive law and replaces i² with -1.

**Worked Example:**

1. $(1 + 2i)(3 - i) = 1\cdot3 +1\cdot(-i) +2i\cdot3 +2i\cdot(-i) = 3 -i +6i -2 i^2 = 3 +5i +2 = 5 + 5i$.

**Worked Example:**

1. $(2 + i) / (1 - i) \cdot (1 + i)/(1 + i) = [(2+i)(1+i)] / (1+1) = (2 + 2i + i + i^2)/2 = (2 + 3i -1)/2 = (1 + 3i)/2 = 1/2 + (3/2)i$.

## Complex Conjugates and Magnitude

<!-- section: complex-conjugates-and-magnitude -->

The conjugate of z = a + bi is \bar z = a - bi .

Properties: z \bar z = |z|^2 (real and non-negative), z + \bar z = 2 Re(z), etc.

The modulus (magnitude) |z| = √(a² + b²) .

It satisfies |z w| = |z| |w| and |z / w| = |z| / |w| (w≠0).

Geometrically, |z| is the distance from origin in the complex plane.

**ELI5**

- Conjugate is the "mirror image" across the real axis.
- Modulus is how "far from zero" the number is, generalizing absolute value.

**Worked Example:**

1. For $z=3-4i$, $\bar z = 3+4i$, $|z|=5$, $z \bar z = 9+16=25=5^2$.

**Worked Example:**

1. $|(1+i)(2-i)| = |1+i| \cdot |2-i| = \sqrt{2} \cdot \sqrt{5} = \sqrt{10}$.

## Solving Equations with Complex Numbers

<!-- section: solving-with-complex -->

Quadratics with negative discriminant have complex roots that come in conjugate pairs (when coefficients real).

Higher degree polynomials factor completely into linears over the complexes.

To solve, use quadratic formula (it works for complex), or factor using complex numbers.

**ELI5**

- When the "discriminant under the square root" goes negative, the answers are no longer on the real number line; they have an imaginary component.
- The math still works; we just accept i as part of the answer.

**Worked Example:**

1. $x^2 + 4 = 0 \to x^2 = -4 \to x = \pm 2i$.

**Worked Example:**

1. Solve $x^2 - 2x + 5 = 0$.
2. $x = [2 \pm \sqrt{4 - 20}]/2 = [2 \pm \sqrt{-16}]/2 = [2 \pm 4i]/2 = 1 \pm 2i$.
3. Check: $(1+2i)^2 - 2(1+2i) + 5 = 1 + 4i + 4i^2 - 2 - 4i + 5 = (1-2+5) + (4i-4i) + 4(-1) = 4 - 4 = 0$. Yes.

## Common Mistakes

- **Writing i² = +1 or forgetting to replace i² with -1 in multiplication**: Leads to wrong real/imag parts.
- **Treating i as a variable**: i is a constant; you cannot "solve for i".
- **Forgetting the conjugate when dividing**: Denominator must be rationalized to a real number.
- **Assuming all complex solutions are pure imaginary**: Most have both real and imag parts.
- **Sign error on the imaginary part when finding conjugates or when distributing minus**.
- **Computing modulus without the square root**: |z| is the sqrt, not a² + b².
- **Forgetting that for real-coeff polynomials, complex roots come in conjugate pairs**: If 1+2i is a root, so is 1-2i.

The imaginary unit i is the number defined by the equation i² = -1.

It is not a "real" number in the usual sense, but a new mathematical object that we adjoin to the reals to form the complex numbers. With i we can take square roots of negative numbers and solve every polynomial equation.

Although called "imaginary," i has profound real-world applications in physics, engineering, and mathematics.

## Definition and Powers of i

<!-- section: definition-and-powers-of-i -->

i is defined so that i * i = -1.

Higher powers of i follow a simple 4-cycle because i^4 = (i^2)^2 = (-1)^2 = 1, and then the pattern repeats:

i^1 = i

i^2 = -1

i^3 = -i

i^4 = 1

i^5 = i

...

To compute i^k for large k, reduce the exponent modulo 4.

**ELI5**

- i is a "90-degree turn" button in number land. Pressing it twice gets you to -1.
- The cycle of 4 comes from going around the circle of directions: up, left, down, right, repeat.

**Worked Example:**

1. $i^7 = i^{4+3} = i^4 \cdot i^3 = 1 \cdot (-i) = -i$.

**Worked Example:**

1. $i^{100}$: $100 \mod 4 = 0$, so $i^{100} = (i^4)^{25} = 1^{25} = 1$.

## Geometric Interpretation

<!-- section: geometric-interpretation -->

In the complex plane, the real numbers lie on the horizontal axis and pure imaginaries on the vertical axis.

Multiplying any complex number z by i rotates z counterclockwise by 90 degrees around the origin, without changing its distance from zero.

Multiplying by i again rotates another 90° (total 180°, which multiplies by -1), and so on.

**ELI5**

- i itself sits at (0,1) — one unit straight up.
- Multiplying by i is like turning the arrow representing the number 90° left (counterclockwise).

**Worked Example:**

1. Start with $1$ (point $(1,0)$). $\cdot i \to i$ (point $(0,1)$). $\cdot i \to -1$ (point $(-1,0)$). $\cdot i \to -i$ (point $(0,-1)$). $\cdot i \to 1$ again.

**Worked Example:**

1. Take $2 + 3i$ (arrow to $(2,3)$). Multiply by $i$: $i(2 + 3i) = 2i + 3 i^2 = -3 + 2i$ (arrow to $(-3,2)$ — rotated $90^\circ$ CCW).

## Role in the Complex Numbers

<!-- section: role-in-complex -->

Every complex number can be written using real coefficients times 1 and i: a + b i.

The introduction of i lets us solve x^2 + 1 = 0 (roots ±i) and, more generally, makes the complex numbers algebraically closed.

i appears in Euler's formula e^{i θ} = cos θ + i sin θ, which connects exponentials, trig, and complex numbers.

**ELI5**

- With i we "complete" the number system so nothing is missing when we try to solve equations.
- It is the secret ingredient that makes many formulas in science and engineering work beautifully.

**Worked Example:**

1. Solve $x^2 + 9 = 0$: $x^2 = -9$, so $x = \pm 3i$.

**Worked Example:**

1. $i$ appears in the formula for roots of unity and in Fourier analysis.

## Common Mistakes

- **Thinking i is a variable**: It is a fixed constant, like π or e.
- **Computing i^2 as +1**: The whole point is that it equals -1.
- **Forgetting the cycle when reducing large powers**: Always mod 4.
- **Writing √(-4) = ± 2i without specifying principal**: The principal square root is the one with non-negative real part or the convention 2i.
- **Assuming all powers of i are imaginary**: i^4=1 is real, i^2=-1 is real.
- **Confusing i with the engineering j**: Same thing, different letter in some fields.
- **Trying to order complex numbers on the line**: They live in a plane; there is no total order compatible with the arithmetic like on the reals.

An arithmetic sequence (also called arithmetic progression) is a list of numbers in which each term after the first is obtained by adding a fixed constant d (the common difference) to the preceding term.

Examples: 2, 5, 8, 11, ... (d=3); 10, 7, 4, 1, ... (d=-3); the odd numbers 1, 3, 5, 7, ...

Arithmetic sequences model any situation with constant additive change: equal deposits, constant speed (distance each second), evenly spaced fence posts, etc. Their partial sums are arithmetic series.

## Definition and General Term

<!-- section: definition-and-general-term -->

An arithmetic sequence is determined by:

- The first term a (or a_1)
- The common difference d

The nth term is given by the explicit formula:

a_n = a + (n - 1) d

This is a linear function of n: the terms lie on an arithmetic (straight-line) progression when plotted against their index.

**ELI5**

- Each step you add exactly the same amount d, like climbing stairs of equal height.
- To find the 10th step you don't count one by one; you do first + 9 * d.

**Worked Example:**

Sequence: 4, 7, 10, 13, ... a=4, d=3.

a_5 = 4 + 4*3 = 16.

a_20 = 4 + 19*3 = 61.

**Worked Example:**

If a_1 = 100 and a_6 = 70, then d = (70-100)/5 = -6.

General term: 100 + (n-1)(-6) = 106 - 6n.

## Sum of a Finite Arithmetic Series

<!-- section: sum-of-finite-arithmetic-series -->

The sum of the first n terms S_n of an arithmetic series is:

S_n = n/2 * [2a + (n-1)d ]

or equivalently

S_n = n/2 * (first term + last term)

Derivation: write the sum forward and backward, add the two identical sums: each of the n pairs sums to (first + last), then divide by 2.

This formula lets us add long lists without adding term by term.

**ELI5**

- Pair the first and last (they add to the same constant), second and second-last, etc. There are n/2 such pairs.
- Multiply the pair sum by how many pairs.

**Worked Example:**

Sum of first 5 terms of 3, 5, 7, 9, 11.

First+last=3+11=14, n=5, S=5/2 *14=35.

(Or 5/2 * [2*3 +4*2]=5/2*(6+8)=35.)

**Worked Example:**

Sum of integers from 1 to 100: S=100/2 * (1+100)= 50*101=5050. (Gauss's famous shortcut.)

## Applications and Word Problems

<!-- section: applications-and-word-problems -->

Arithmetic sequences appear whenever a quantity increases or decreases by a constant amount per time period or per step:

- Savings: fixed amount deposited each month.
- Linear motion at constant velocity (position each second).
- Seats in a theater or bleachers with constant added per row.
- Number of handshakes or blocks in a pattern that grows linearly.

To solve word problems: identify a and d from the description, decide if you need a particular term or the sum, use the formulas, interpret the answer in context.

**ELI5**

- "Constant step size" is the signature of arithmetic.
- The formulas turn "how many steps" and "size of each step" into "where you are" or "total accumulated."

**Worked Example:**

A worker earns $50 on day 1 and gets a $3 raise each day. How much total after 30 days?

a=50, d=3, n=30. Last day pay=50+29*3=137.

Total S_30 =30/2 * (50+137)=15*187=2805.

**Worked Example:**

An arithmetic sequence of temperatures: starts at 20°C and drops 1.5° each hour. What is the temperature after 8 hours?

a_9 =20 +8*(-1.5)=20-12=8°C.

## Common Mistakes

- **Using n instead of (n-1) in the general term**: a_n = a + n d would be off by one (gives the (n+1)th term).
- **Forgetting the /2 in the sum formula**: Easy to double the sum.
- **Confusing sequence (the list of terms) with series (the sum of terms)**: The problem may ask for the 10th term or the sum of first 10.
- **Wrong sign for d**: If the sequence is decreasing, d is negative; the formulas still work.
- **Applying the arithmetic sum formula to a geometric sequence**: Different pattern (common ratio instead of difference).
- **Not identifying the first term correctly**: Sometimes the problem gives the second term or a later one; solve for a first.
- **Units or context errors**: The answer 2805 dollars makes sense; 2805 days probably does not.

A geometric sequence (geometric progression) is a list in which each term after the first is obtained by multiplying the preceding term by a fixed constant r called the common ratio.

Examples: 2, 6, 18, 54, ... (r=3); 100, 50, 25, 12.5, ... (r=1/2); 3, -6, 12, -24, ... (r=-2).

Geometric sequences model multiplicative growth or decay: compound interest, population growth, bouncing balls (heights), half-life decay, and many fractal or scaling patterns. Their partial sums are geometric series; when |r|<1 the infinite sum converges to a finite value.

## Definition and General Term

<!-- section: definition-and-general-term -->

A geometric sequence is determined by:

- The first term a
- The common ratio r

The nth term has the explicit (closed-form) formula:

a_n = a * r^{n-1}

This is an exponential function of the index n.

If r > 1 the terms grow without bound (if a ≠ 0).

If 0 < r < 1 the terms shrink toward zero.

If r < 0 the signs alternate.

**ELI5**

- Each step you multiply by the same "growth factor" r, like a photocopier set to enlarge or shrink by a fixed percentage.
- The term number tells how many multiplications by r have happened since the start.

**Worked Example:**

3, 6, 12, 24, ... a=3, r=2.

a_6 = 3 * 2^5 = 3*32=96.

**Worked Example:**

A quantity starts at 1000 and is multiplied by 0.9 each day. After 10 days: 1000 * (0.9)^9 (for the 10th term, n-1=9).

## Sum of a Finite Geometric Series

<!-- section: sum-of-finite-geometric-series -->

The sum of the first n terms S_n of a geometric series (r ≠ 1) is:

S_n = a \frac{r^n - 1}{r - 1}

(or equivalently a \frac{1 - r^n}{1 - r} when written with 1-r in denominator).

Derivation: write S, multiply by r, subtract the two equations, solve for S.

When r=1 the sum is simply n a (all terms equal a).

**ELI5**

- The trick of multiplying the sum by r shifts every term; subtracting cancels most terms, leaving only the first and the "next after last".
- Dividing by (r-1) "normalizes" to give the closed form.

**Worked Example:**

Sum of first 5 terms of 4, 12, 36, ... (a=4, r=3).

S_5 =4*(3^5 -1)/(3-1) =4*(243-1)/2 =4*121=484.

**Worked Example:**

Sum of geometric series 1 + 1/2 + 1/4 + ... + 1/2^{9} (10 terms, a=1 r=1/2).

S= (1 - (1/2)^{10} ) / (1 - 1/2) = (1 - 1/1024)/(1/2) = 2*(1023/1024) ≈ 1.998 .

## Infinite Series and Applications

<!-- section: infinite-series-and-applications -->

When |r| < 1 the infinite geometric series sums to the finite value

S_∞ = a / (1 - r)

(This is the limit of the finite sum formula as n→∞, since r^n →0.)

This is the foundation for many repeating decimal representations and for the mathematics of fractals, probability (infinite trials), and physics (infinite reflections, damping).

**ELI5**

- If each step you keep only a fraction r <1 of the previous, the total "stuff" you ever get is finite even if you continue forever.
- The formula a/(1-r) is the "grand total" after all the infinite shrinking steps.

**Worked Example:**

0.3333... = 3/10 + 3/100 + 3/1000 + ... = (0.3) / (1 - 0.1) = 0.3 / 0.9 = 1/3.

**Worked Example:**

A ball dropped from 10 m bounces to 60% of previous height each time. Total distance traveled (down + up + down + ...):

First drop 10 m.

Then 2 * (6 + 6*0.6 + 6*0.6^2 + ...) = 2 * 6 / (1-0.6) = 12 / 0.4 = 30 m.

Grand total 10 + 30 = 40 m.

## Common Mistakes

- **Using the arithmetic sum formula on a geometric series (or vice versa)**: Different closed forms; check whether the problem adds or multiplies each step.
- **Forgetting the exponent is n-1**: a r^n would be the term after the nth.
- **Applying the infinite sum formula when |r| ≥ 1**: The series diverges (grows without bound or oscillates).
- **Sign errors with negative r**: The sum formula still holds, but terms alternate; the infinite sum may still converge if |r|<1.
- **Confusing the first term a with S_1**: S_1 = a, but the formula a(r^n-1)/(r-1) for n=1 gives a correctly only after algebra.
- **Writing the sum formula with r in the numerator instead of r^n**: a(r-1)/(r^n-1) is backwards.
- **Forgetting to multiply by 2 for up-and-down bounces in ball problems**: The infinite geometric after the first drop is usually doubled.
- **Domain for r=1 case**: Must handle separately; the general formula has division by zero.
