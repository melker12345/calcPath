# Inclusion–exclusion principle

When sets overlap, simply adding their sizes overcounts the elements that belong to multiple sets. Inclusion-exclusion corrects these overcounts with a precise alternating sum.

It is the systematic generalization of the two-set union formula to any number of sets and is indispensable for exact counting with overlaps.

## The Two-Set Case

<!-- section: two-sets -->

For any two sets:

$$|A \cup B| = |A| + |B| - |A \cap B|.$$

The intersection term is subtracted because those elements were added twice in the first sum; we want them counted only once.

**ELI5**

- People who like cats + people who like dogs counts the cat-and-dog lovers twice. Subtract them once and every lover is counted exactly once.
- If the sets are disjoint the intersection is empty and the formula collapses to plain addition.

**Worked Example:**

30 students. 18 play soccer, 12 play basketball, 5 play both.

Students playing at least one sport: 18 + 12 − 5 = 25.

Students playing neither: 30 − 25 = 5.

## Three or More Sets

<!-- section: three-or-more -->

For three sets:

$$|A \cup B \cup C| = |A| + |B| + |C| - |A∩B| - |A∩C| - |B∩C| + |A∩B∩C|.$$

In general, for a family of sets the term for the intersection of exactly k of them receives sign (−1)^{k+1}.

**ELI5**

- Triples were added three times then subtracted three times (once for each pair), netting zero — but they belong in the union, so we add them back at the end.
- The signs alternate + − + − … exactly like the principle of inclusion and exclusion.

**Worked Example:**

|A|=20, |B|=15, |C|=10; pairwise 5,4,3; triple 2.

Union = 20+15+10 − (5+4+3) + 2 = 45 − 12 + 2 = 35.

## Applications and Derivation Intuition

<!-- section: applications -->

The principle is derived by considering for each element how many times it is counted in the naive sum of singles, then in the corrections: an element in exactly r sets is overcounted in a way that the alternating sum leaves it with coefficient exactly 1 if r ≥ 1.

Classic uses: counting integers divisible by certain numbers (sieve), derangements, counting surjective functions, probability of unions, graph edge covers, etc.

**ELI5**

- "How many numbers ≤ 100 are divisible by 2, 3 or 5?" You add the three floors, subtract the pairwise lcms, add back the triple.
- It is the exact discrete version of "adding probabilities with overlaps corrected."

**Worked Example:**

Numbers from 1 to 100 divisible by 2, 3 or 5:

|A|=50, |B|=33, |C|=20, |A∩B|=16, |A∩C|=10, |B∩C|=6, |A∩B∩C|=3.

50+33+20 −16−10−6 +3 = 74.

## Common Mistakes

- **Getting the signs wrong**: + singles, − pairs, + triples, − quadruples… The pattern is (-1)^{k+1} for the k-fold intersections.
- **Forgetting to add back the triple (or higher) intersections**: After subtracting pairs the triple people have been added 3× and subtracted 3×, so they disappear unless you add them.
- **Using the formula when sets are already disjoint**: It still works (intersections zero) but you are doing unnecessary work.
- **Miscounting the intersections themselves**: |A ∩ B ∩ C| requires elements in *all three*, not just some pairs.
- **Applying to infinite sets without care**: The principle is for finite cardinalities; infinite cases need different tools.
- **Confusing union count with probability without dividing**: The count is useful, but probability requires dividing by the size of the universe.
