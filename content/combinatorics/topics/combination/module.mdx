# Combination

A combination is an unordered selection of objects. While permutations care about sequence, combinations care only about which items are chosen together — the set itself.

Combinations are the gateway to binomial coefficients, Pascal's triangle, and countless applications in probability and algebra.

## Unordered Selections

<!-- section: unordered-selections -->

To choose k distinct items from n where order does not matter, first choose an ordered selection in P(n,k) ways, then divide by the k! internal arrangements of each selection. This yields the combination formula:

$$\binom{n}{k} = \frac{P(n,k)}{k!} = \frac{n!}{k!(n-k)!}.$$

**ELI5**

- Picking a team of 5 players from 12: the team is the same set no matter who you list first on the roster.
- Contrast: if you then assign the 5 players to 5 distinct positions on the field, multiply back by 5! to get the permutations.

**Worked Example:**

Number of ways to choose 2 fruits from {apple, banana, cherry}:

The sets are {apple,banana}, {apple,cherry}, {banana,cherry} → 3 ways.

Formula: \binom{3}{2} = 3.

## Binomial Coefficient Notation and Symmetry

<!-- section: binomial-notation -->

We write \binom{n}{k} for the number above (read "n choose k"). It is zero when k > n or k < 0, and \binom{n}{0} = 1.

A key symmetry: \binom{n}{k} = \binom{n}{n-k}. Choosing who is on the team is the same as choosing who is left out.

**ELI5**

- The number of ways to pick 2 people to go on a trip from 10 is the same as the number of ways to pick 8 people who stay home.
- This identity often lets you compute the smaller of k or n-k.

**Worked Example:**

A class has 12 students. Number of ways to choose a committee of 5: \binom{12}{5} = 792.

By symmetry this equals the number of ways to choose which 7 students are *not* on the committee: \binom{12}{7} = 792.

## Useful Identities and the Subset Sum

<!-- section: identities-and-relations -->

Pascal's identity: \binom{n}{k} = \binom{n-1}{k} + \binom{n-1}{k-1}. It is the recurrence that builds Pascal's triangle.

The total number of subsets of an n-element set is \sum_{k=0}^n \binom{n}{k} = 2^n. Each element can be included or excluded independently.

**ELI5**

- Pascal's triangle: every entry is the sum of the two entries above it from the previous row — exactly the choose-or-not-choose cases.
- 2^n subsets: for a set of 3 elements {1,2,3} the subsets are {}, {1}, {2}, {3}, {1,2}, {1,3}, {2,3}, {1,2,3} — eight of them.

**Worked Example:**

Verify Pascal for n=5, k=2:

\binom{5}{2} = 10.

\binom{4}{2} + \binom{4}{1} = 6 + 4 = 10.

Also, number of subsets of {a,b,c,d}: 16 = 2^4.

## Common Mistakes

- **Forgetting to divide by k! when turning permutations into combinations**: P(n,k)/k! is required; many people stop at the ordered count.
- **Computing \binom{n}{k} when k > n**: It is zero, not a negative or error.
- **Mixing up the two arguments**: \binom{n}{k} has n on top (the total pool), k on bottom (the size chosen).
- **Thinking \binom{n}{k} = \binom{n}{k} × k! always gives permutations of the *same* set**: No — that gives selections of k *distinct* items then arranging them.
- **Ignoring that combinations assume distinct objects**: If items have types or repeats, different formulas (multisets) apply.
