# Special Sequences & Numbers

Catalan, Stirling, and Bell numbers — classical combinatorial sequences with deep structural meaning.

The Catalan numbers form one of the most famous sequences in combinatorics: 1, 1, 2, 5, 14, 42, 132, …

They count an astonishing variety of objects that at first glance seem unrelated, all unified by the same recursive structure or the reflection principle.

## The Formula and Generating Function

<!-- section: definition-formula -->

The nth Catalan number is

$$C_n = \frac{1}{n+1}\binom{2n}{n} = \binom{2n}{n} - \binom{2n}{n-1}.$$

The ordinary generating function C(x) = sum C_n x^n satisfies the quadratic equation C = 1 + x C^2, solved by C(x) = (1 − sqrt(1−4x))/(2x).

**ELI5**

- The 1/(n+1) factor is the "averaging" or reflection correction that removes the bad paths.
- The formula magically turns the binomial that counts all paths into the count of only the good (Dyck) ones.

**Worked Example:**

C_4 = 1/5 * \binom{8}{4} = 1/5 * 70 = 14.

Also 70 − \binom{8}{3} = 70 − 56 = 14.

## Combinatorial Interpretations

<!-- section: combinatorial-interpretations -->

Catalan numbers count:

- Balanced parentheses strings with n pairs (Dyck words)
- Binary trees with n+1 leaves (or n internal nodes, plane binary trees)
- Monotonic lattice paths from (0,0) to (n,n) never rising above the diagonal
- Triangulations of a convex (n+2)-gon
- Non-crossing partitions of n points on a circle
- Stack-sortable permutations of [n]

**ELI5**

- Parentheses: every prefix has at least as many opens as closes, and total equal.
- Trees: every left/right subtree split obeys the same rule recursively.
- Paths: never have more up steps than down steps in any prefix (like balanced parentheses).

**Worked Example:**

Number of ways to correctly match 3 pairs of parentheses: C_3 = 5.

The strings are ((())), (()()), (())(), ()(()), ()()().

## Applications and Proof Techniques

<!-- section: applications -->

The reflection principle (or cycle lemma) proves the formula by counting total paths and subtracting the bad ones that cross the diagonal.

The recursive definition C_0=1, C_n = sum_{i=0}^{n-1} C_i C_{n-1-i} directly encodes the first-return decomposition of a Dyck path or the root-subtree split of a binary tree.

**ELI5**

- To count good paths: total paths to (n,n) minus the bad ones. Any bad path must cross or touch y=x+1; reflect the initial segment over that line to get a bijection with paths from (0,0) to (n,n) that start with an up — actually from (1,-1) or standard (0,2) etc. The count of bad is \binom{2n}{n-1}, hence the subtraction formula.
- Recursion comes from "the first time the path returns to the diagonal".

**Worked Example:**

Number of triangulations of a convex hexagon (n=4, 6-gon): C_4 = 14.

Fix one side as base; the triangle using the base splits the polygon into the triangle plus two smaller polygons whose sizes add to 4; sum over split point gives the Catalan recurrence.

## Common Mistakes

- **Forgetting the 1/(n+1) or using plain binomial**: \binom{2n}{n} counts *all* paths or all sequences; Catalan counts only the balanced/good ones.
- **Off-by-one in indexing**: C_n for n pairs, n internal nodes, (n+2)-gon, semilength n.
- **Thinking all trees are Catalan**: Only *plane binary* or *rooted plane* trees with the ordering; unlabeled unordered trees have different counts (Otter etc.).
- **Missing the base case C_0 = 1**: The empty object is counted; without it the recurrence fails for n=1.
- **Confusing with other famous sequences**: Motzkin, Schröder, Fine numbers are close cousins but count slightly different objects (colored steps, etc.).

Stirling numbers come in two flavors and are the "change of basis" coefficients between the monomial basis and the falling/rising factorial bases. Combinatorially they count set partitions (second kind) and permutations by number of cycles (first kind).

They are fundamental in enumerative combinatorics and appear in many explicit formulas.

## Stirling Numbers of the Second Kind

<!-- section: stirling-second -->

S(n,k) = number of ways to partition an n-element set into exactly k non-empty *unlabeled* subsets.

The total Bell number B_n = sum_k S(n,k) counts all partitions with any number of blocks.

**ELI5**

- S(4,2)=7: the ways to split 4 people into two non-empty teams where the teams themselves are not distinguished ( { {1,2,3},{4} } is one partition).
- If the teams were labeled "team A and team B" you would multiply by k!.

**Worked Example:**

S(3,2) = 3: for {1,2,3} the partitions into 2 blocks are {{1,2}{3}}, {{1,3}{2}}, {{1}{2,3}}.

## Stirling Numbers of the First Kind

<!-- section: stirling-first -->

The unsigned Stirling numbers of the first kind c(n,k) (or |s(n,k)|) count the number of permutations of n elements with exactly k cycles.

The signed versions s(n,k) = (−1)^{n-k} c(n,k) appear in the change-of-basis (x)_n = sum s(n,k) x^k for the falling factorial.

**ELI5**

- A permutation can be decomposed into disjoint cycles. c(n,k) is "how many permutations have exactly k cycles in their cycle decomposition".
- For n=3: identity has 3 cycles (fixed points), the 3 transpositions have 2 cycles each (one 2-cycle + one fixed), the two 3-cycles have 1 cycle.

**Worked Example:**

c(4,2)=11. There are 11 permutations of [4] with exactly 2 cycles.

## Relation to Bell Numbers and Other Objects

<!-- section: bell-relation -->

B_n = sum_k S(n,k) is the nth Bell number.

Also S(n,k) = 1/k! sum_{j=0}^k (−1)^{k-j} \binom{k}{j} j^n  (inclusion-exclusion for surjections divided by k!).

The EGF for the column S(n,k) fixed k is (e^x − 1)^k / k! .

**ELI5**

- Bell numbers are the "total partitions" row sums of the Stirling triangle of the second kind.
- The explicit sum formula comes from inclusion-exclusion on the number of functions onto exactly a labeled k-set, then divide by k! because blocks unlabeled.

**Worked Example:**

B_4 = 15.

From the formula or table: 1 + 7 + 6 + 1 = 15.

## Common Mistakes

- **Confusing first and second kind**: Second kind = set partitions (blocks), first kind = cycle counts in permutations.
- **Forgetting that blocks/subsets are unlabeled**: S(n,k) already treats the k subsets as indistinguishable; k! S(n,k) = number of surjections.
- **Sign errors with signed Stirling first**: The signed ones alternate so that the polynomial identity holds with falling factorials.
- **Using S(n,k) when k > n or k=0 (except n=0)**: S(n,0)=0 for n>0, S(0,0)=1.
- **Mixing Bell with Stirling**: Bell is the sum; individual S(n,k) are the "exactly k blocks" numbers.

The Bell numbers B_n count the partitions of an n-element set — every possible way to group n distinct objects into non-empty unlabeled groups, with no restriction on the number of groups.

They are the row sums of the Stirling numbers of the second kind and have a rich exponential generating function exp(e^x − 1).

## Definition and Basic Values

<!-- section: definition-bell -->

B_0 = 1, B_1 = 1, B_2 = 2, B_3 = 5, B_4 = 15, B_5 = 52, B_6 = 203, …

B_n is the number of equivalence relations on an n-set, or the number of ways to partition [n] into non-empty blocks.

**ELI5**

- For 3 people A,B,C the groupings are: all together; three ways to have one pair and one loner; and all separate. Total 5.
- Each partition corresponds to a way the people can be put into indistinguishable rooms, rooms non-empty.

**Worked Example:**

B_4 = 15 for elements {1,2,3,4}.

1 (all one block) + 7 (one triple + singleton, 4 choices but adjusted) + 6 (two doubles) + 1 (all single) wait standard count 15.

## Set Partitions and the Recurrence

<!-- section: set-partitions -->

The fundamental recurrence is

B_n = \sum_{k=0}^{n-1} \binom{n-1}{k} B_k .

Interpretation: look at the block containing element n. Suppose it also contains exactly k other elements; choose those k from the previous n−1, then partition the remaining n−1−k arbitrarily (B_{n-1-k}).

**ELI5**

- When you add the nth person, you decide who else is in their group (any subset of the previous), and the people not chosen get partitioned however they want among themselves.
- This avoids overcounting because blocks are unlabeled.

**Worked Example:**

B_3 = \binom{2}{0}B_0 + \binom{2}{1}B_1 + \binom{2}{2}B_2 = 1 + 2 + 2 = 5.

## Applications and Growth

<!-- section: applications-growth -->

Bell numbers appear in set theory (equivalence relations), database theory (grouping records), probability (Poisson-Dirichlet process limits), and the enumeration of relational structures.

The EGF is exp(e^x − 1) = sum B_n x^n / n! .

Asymptotically B_n grows roughly like (n / ln n)^n — super-exponential but slower than n!.

**ELI5**

- Every way to cluster n data points with no distinguished clusters is a Bell number.
- The EGF exp(e^x -1) says "a partition is a set of non-empty sets", and e^x-1 is the EGF for a single non-empty set.

**Worked Example:**

The number of equivalence relations on a 5-element set is B_5 = 52.

Each equivalence relation corresponds to one partition of the set into equivalence classes.

## Common Mistakes

- **Thinking partitions are ordered or labeled**: The blocks are unordered; if you label the blocks you count k! S(n,k) instead.
- **Confusing with Stirling second kind**: B_n is the *total* over all k; S(n,k) is "exactly k blocks".
- **Forgetting B_0=1**: The empty partition exists and is needed for recurrences.
- **Using 2^n**: That counts *labeled* or *ordered* something (subsets, or functions to {0,1}), not partitions.
- **Assuming all partitions have the same block size**: Most have mixed sizes; the count sums over all type partitions.
