# Enumerative Combinatorics

Young tableaux, the hook-length formula, Robinson–Schensted correspondence, and systematic enumeration.

Enumerative combinatorics is the part of combinatorics whose central question is "how many are there?" — how many objects of a given type and size exist, often with the goal of finding a closed formula, a generating function, a recurrence, or a bijection with a better-understood set.

It is both an art (finding the right bijection or decomposition) and a science (systematic use of species, GFs, and algebraic combinatorics).

## What Enumerative Combinatorics Studies

<!-- section: what-is-enumerative -->

Typical objects: subsets, permutations, partitions, trees, graphs with given properties, lattice paths, tableaux, matchings, tilings, words avoiding patterns, etc.

The output is usually a formula, a generating function, or an algorithm that computes the count.

**ELI5**

- "How many ways are there to parenthesize a product of n+1 factors?" (Catalan)
- "How many ways can n distinct people sit at a round table?" ((n-1)!)
- The subject turns these questions into precise, often beautiful, answers.

**Worked Example:**

Number of ways to choose any number of items from n types with repetition allowed (multisets): \binom{n + k - 1}{k} for exactly k, or overall 2^n if limited to 0-1.

## The Role of Generating Functions

<!-- section: generating-functions-role -->

Ordinary and exponential generating functions turn combinatorial constructions into algebraic operations.

The exponential formula, the symbolic method (Flajolet & Sedgewick), and species theory provide a dictionary: 'sequence', 'set', 'cycle' constructions have direct GF translations.

**ELI5**

- Instead of counting directly, you write a "recipe" for building the objects and the GF follows mechanically.
- Then you extract coefficients by algebra, partial fractions, or singularity analysis for asymptotics.

**Worked Example:**

The OGF for the number of plane trees (or rooted labeled? ) etc. Many classic counts become routine once the construction is translated.

For integer partitions the product formula directly encodes the additive structure.

## Bijective Proofs and Other Techniques

<!-- section: bijective-and-other -->

A bijective proof establishes an identity a_n = b_n by constructing an explicit one-to-one correspondence between the two families of objects.

Other tools: inclusion-exclusion for overcount correction, the reflection principle for ballot/Catalan-type problems, the cycle lemma, LGV lemma for determinants counting non-intersecting paths, transfer matrices for linear recurrences on paths/tilings, and algebraic combinatorics (symmetric functions, representation theory) for more advanced counts.

**ELI5**

- Instead of computing two ugly sums and showing they equal, you say "here is a perfect matching between the red objects and the blue objects; therefore same number."
- The best proofs explain *why* the numbers are equal at a structural level.

**Worked Example:**

The number of ways to choose a committee with chair from n people is n 2^{n-1} (choose chair, any subset of the rest). Also equal to sum_k k \binom{n}{k} (choose the committee then the chair inside it). The bijection or algebraic identity (k \binom{n}{k} = n \binom{n-1}{k-1}) proves equality.

## Common Mistakes

- **Thinking enumeration is 'just listing'**: For large n the point is closed forms or efficient computation, not enumeration by hand.
- **Confusing OGF and EGF**: Choose the right one for the symmetry (labeled vs unlabeled).
- **Giving only a recurrence or GF without extracting the formula**: Sometimes the GF or recurrence *is* the answer; often one wants the explicit [x^n].
- **Overlooking that bijections are the gold standard**: They give understanding; algebraic proofs may be shorter but less explanatory.
- **Ignoring asymptotics**: Many enumerative results are accompanied by asymptotic analysis (singularity analysis, saddle point) giving the growth rate of the counts.

A Young tableau is a filling of a Young diagram (the shape given by an integer partition) with numbers satisfying increasing conditions in rows and columns. Standard Young tableaux (SYT) use each number from 1 to n exactly once and are strictly increasing in rows and columns.

They are central objects in the representation theory of the symmetric group and in many areas of algebraic and enumerative combinatorics.

## Young Diagrams

<!-- section: young-diagrams -->

A partition λ ⊢ n is written in decreasing order λ_1 ≥ λ_2 ≥ … ≥ λ_ℓ > 0 with sum n.

The Young diagram consists of λ_i left-justified boxes in the ith row.

Conjugate partition λ' is obtained by reading column lengths (transposing the diagram).

**ELI5**

- The diagram is a "Ferrers diagram" of dots or boxes visualizing how the integer n is split into parts.
- (4,2,1) looks like four boxes, then two, then one, like a staircase missing some steps.

**Worked Example:**

Partition (5,3,3,1) of 12. Diagram has rows of those lengths. Its conjugate is (4,3,3,1,1).

## Standard Young Tableaux

<!-- section: standard-tableaux -->

An SYT of shape λ is a bijection from the boxes of the diagram to {1,2,…,n} such that rows and columns are strictly increasing.

The number f^λ of SYT of shape λ is the dimension of the irreducible representation of S_n corresponding to λ.

**ELI5**

- You place the numbers 1 through n into the boxes so that everything gets bigger as you go right or down — like a Young diagram version of a sorted matrix.
- Different shapes correspond to different "types" of permutations or representations.

**Worked Example:**

For shape (2,1): the two SYT are

1 2

3

and

1 3

2

Any other placement violates a row or column increase.

## The Hook-Length Formula and Algorithms

<!-- section: hook-and-other -->

The hook length h(u) of a cell u is 1 + (cells strictly to the right) + (cells strictly below).

The hook-length formula:

$$f^\lambda = \frac{n!}{\prod_{u \in \lambda} h(u)}.$$

There is a beautiful probabilistic "hook-walk" algorithm that samples an SYT uniformly by a sequence of random hook moves; its analysis proves the formula.

**ELI5**

- The denominator "penalizes" each cell by the size of its hook (the L-shape of cells that "see" it).
- The formula magically gives an integer even though it is written as a huge factorial divided by many integers.

**Worked Example:**

Shape (2,2): hooks 3,2 / 2,1. Product 12. 24/12 = 2 SYT, as listed earlier.

The jeu de taquin (sliding) and RSK algorithm (next topic) are algorithmic companions that manipulate tableaux.

## Common Mistakes

- **Confusing standard with semi-standard**: Semi-standard allow repeats (weakly increasing) and use numbers up to k; they count Kostka numbers and Schur functions.
- **Wrong hook definition**: The hook includes the cell itself; arm + leg + 1.
- **Forgetting that f^λ is always an integer**: The formula proves it, but it is not obvious a priori.
- **Thinking only rectangular shapes matter**: Hooks work for arbitrary shapes; the most famous are for hooks, staircases, rectangles.
- **Mixing row and column reading**: Partitions are usually drawn with longest row on top; columns give the conjugate.

The hook-length formula is a strikingly simple and explicit product formula for the number of standard Young tableaux of a given shape. It is one of the most elegant results in enumerative combinatorics and has numerous proofs (combinatorial, probabilistic, algebraic, bijective).

## Hook Lengths

<!-- section: hook-definition -->

For a cell in row i, column j of the diagram of λ, the hook consists of the cell, the arm (cells to the right in row i), and the leg (cells below in column j).

Hook length h(i,j) = λ_i − j + λ'_j − i + 1 = arm + leg + 1.

**ELI5**

- Imagine a hook-shaped polyomino sitting at the cell and reaching right and down to the edge of the diagram.
- The length is how many cells that hook covers, including the corner cell.

**Worked Example:**

In shape (4,2):

Cell (1,1): right 3, below 1 → hook length 5.

Cell (1,4): right 0, below 0 → 1.

Cell (2,2): right 0, below 0 → 1.

## The Formula

<!-- section: the-formula -->

If λ ⊢ n, then the number f^λ of standard Young tableaux of shape λ is

$$f^\lambda = \frac{n!}{\prod_{(i,j) \in \lambda} h(i,j)}.$$

The product runs over all cells of the diagram.

**ELI5**

- Take n!, which would be the number of ways to fill the boxes with 1..n in any order.
- Divide by the product of hook lengths; each hook "accounts for" the overcounting or the constraints along that L-shape.
- The result is exactly the number of the increasing fillings.

**Worked Example:**

Shape (2,2), n=4. Hooks 3,2,2,1. Product 12.

4! / 12 = 2. Indeed there are exactly two SYT.

## Proofs and Algorithms

<!-- section: proofs-and-algos -->

- Original proof (Frame–Robinson–Thrall 1954) by induction or manipulation of factorials.
- Probabilistic hook-walk proof (Greene–Nijenhuis–Wilf 1979): a random process that generates SYT with the exact probability given by the formula, thereby proving it.
- Algebraic proofs via the hook-content formula for semi-standard tableaux or via representation theory (Weyl dimension formula in type A).
- There are also bijective proofs and q-analogues (q-hook formula).

The hook walk also gives an efficient practical way to generate a random SYT of given shape uniformly.

**ELI5**

- The walk: pick a cell at random; while not at a corner, jump to a uniformly random cell in the current hook (including current). When you stop at a corner, remove it and repeat on the smaller diagram, recording the order of removal in reverse to get the filling.
- Analysis shows the probability for any particular standard filling is exactly the reciprocal of the hook product times 1/n! .

**Worked Example:**

For any shape the algorithm produces each of the f^λ tableaux with equal probability 1/f^λ, and the closed-form probability calculation yields the hook formula.

## Common Mistakes

- **Forgetting the +1 for the cell itself**: Hook length is never just arm+leg.
- **Computing hooks on the conjugate instead of the shape**: Use the given diagram.
- **Thinking the formula only works for partitions with at most 2 rows**: It is completely general.
- **Confusing with the content hook formula**: The hook-content formula counts semi-standard tableaux: product over cells of (content + k choices) / hook.
- **Expecting the product to obviously divide n!**: The integrality is a non-trivial consequence proved by the existence of the SYT or by the proof techniques.

The Robinson–Schensted (RS) correspondence is a beautiful bijection between permutations of [n] and pairs of standard Young tableaux of the same shape. It provides a combinatorial explanation for many phenomena in the representation theory of the symmetric group and gives a powerful tool for studying the longest increasing and decreasing subsequences of a permutation.

## The Correspondence via Insertion

<!-- section: the-correspondence -->

Given a permutation π = π_1 … π_n , one builds two tableaux P (insertion) and Q (recording) of the same shape by the following process:

- Start with empty P and Q.
- For i = 1 to n: insert the value π_i into the current P using Schensted row-insertion (bumping procedure): place it in the first row by displacing the smallest larger entry to the right; the displaced entry is bumped to the next row, and so on, possibly creating a new box at the end of some row.
- Add the number i to Q in the position of the newly created box in P.

At the end both P and Q are standard Young tableaux of identical shape λ ⊢ n, and every such pair arises from exactly one permutation.

**ELI5**

- P "sorts" the permutation values according to a bumping rule that maintains the tableau property.
- Q records the "history" of when each new box was added.
- The pair (P,Q) uniquely encodes the permutation.

**Worked Example:**

For π = 2 3 1:

Insert 2: P=[2], Q=[1]

Insert 3: first row becomes 2 3, new box, Q adds 2 at that position: Q has 1 2 in row.

Insert 1: 1 bumps 2 in first row (1<2), 2 bumps nothing in second row? Wait simulation: first row after bump: 1 3 , bumped 2 creates new row 2. Etc. The resulting shape and tableaux can be computed; the important point is the process is deterministic and reversible.

## Connection to Increasing Subsequences

<!-- section: increasing-subseq -->

Schensted's theorem: the length of the longest increasing subsequence of π is equal to the length of the first row λ_1 of the shape (or of P).

Dually, the length of the longest decreasing subsequence is the length of the first column λ'_1.

This gives an O(n log n) algorithm for LIS via patience sorting (which is essentially the insertion process).

**ELI5**

- The first row of P at the end "holds" the smallest possible tail of all increasing subsequences of each length; its length is the longest one you can build.
- The bumping records the "patience" piles in the solitaire game that models the LIS.

**Worked Example:**

Permutation 3 1 4 2. Longest increasing: 1 4 or 1 2 or 3 4, length 2.

The RS shape will have first row of length 2.

By Erdős–Szekeres any sequence longer than rs must have monotone subseq of length r+1 or s+1.

## Further Properties and Generalizations

<!-- section: properties -->

- The correspondence restricts to a bijection between involutions and SYT (when P=Q).
- The number of permutations with given RS shape λ is (f^λ)^2.
- Hence sum_λ (f^λ)^2 = n! (a representation-theoretic identity proved combinatorially).
- RSK generalization to matrices / biwords gives the combinatorial proof of the Cauchy identity for Schur functions s_λ(x) s_λ(y) = product 1/(1-x_i y_j).

The inverse is obtained by reverse bumping (deletion) from the recording tableau.

**ELI5**

- When the permutation is an involution the insertion and recording tableaux coincide.
- The square (f^λ)^2 counts the "fiber" over each shape — the permutations that "grow" that shape.

**Worked Example:**

There are (f^{(n)})^2 = 1 permutations with shape the single row (the increasing permutation).

There are (f^{(1^n)})^2 = 1 with the single column (the decreasing permutation).

For the hook shapes one recovers Catalan numbers etc.

## Common Mistakes

- **Confusing P and Q**: P receives the values of the permutation; Q receives the time steps 1..n.
- **Thinking the shape is always a rectangle**: Most permutations produce irregular shapes.
- **Forgetting that the correspondence is for all permutations, not just involutions**: Involutions are the special case P=Q.
- **Misstating the LIS theorem**: It is the *first row*, not the number of rows (which gives LDS).
- **Believing RSK is only for counting**: It is a fundamental tool that proves deep identities in symmetric function theory via bijective proofs.
