# Pigeonhole principle

The pigeonhole principle is deceptively simple yet extraordinarily powerful: it lets you prove that something *must* happen in any large enough structure, without constructing an example or checking all cases.

It is one of the first "non-constructive" proof techniques most students encounter.

## The Basic Principle

<!-- section: basic-principle -->

If you have more pigeons than pigeonholes, then at least one pigeonhole contains more than one pigeon.

In mathematical language: if n > m, then when distributing n items into m containers, at least one container has at least two items.

**ELI5**

- 13 socks into 12 drawers: even if you try to spread them out, at least one drawer ends up with two socks.
- 13 people, 12 months: at least two people share a birthday month. You don't need their names; pure counting forces the collision.

**Worked Example:**

In a group of 367 people, at least two share the same birthday (ignoring leap years, 365 days).

Worst case: 365 people with distinct birthdays. The 366th person collides with someone. For 367 we are guaranteed even after worst case.

## The Generalized Pigeonhole Principle

<!-- section: generalized -->

To guarantee that at least one container has *at least k* items when there are m containers, you need (k−1)m + 1 items.

Equivalently, when distributing n items into m containers, at least one container has at least ceil(n/m) items.

**ELI5**

- To force 3 in one drawer with 12 drawers: put 2 in each first (24), then the 25th forces a third.
- Average is n/m; the ceiling tells you the guaranteed maximum load.

**Worked Example:**

Distribute 100 identical candies to 7 children. Guarantee that one child gets at least how many?

ceil(100/7) = 15. Or: worst case 14 each for 7 children = 98, the remaining 2 force some to 15 or more. Yes, at least one gets ≥15.

## Applications and Existence Proofs

<!-- section: applications-proofs -->

The principle shines in Ramsey theory, graph theory (e.g. in any 2-coloring of K_6 there is a monochromatic triangle), number theory (Dirichlet's approximation theorem is a continuous analogue), and computer science (hash collisions, birthday paradox bounds).

It proves existence; it does not tell you *which* hole or *how to find* the collision.

**ELI5**

- In a big enough party you are guaranteed either a group of 3 mutual friends or 3 mutual strangers (Ramsey R(3,3)=6). The proof is a clever double application of pigeonhole on a person's friendships.
- It is "I don't know who, but someone must have..."

**Worked Example:**

Prove that in any set of 5 integers, there are two whose difference is divisible by 4.

Consider residues mod 4: 0,1,2,3 — only 4 "pigeonholes". 5 numbers → at least two have the same residue r. Their difference is 0 mod 4.

## Common Mistakes

- **Forgetting the "+1"**: m pigeons into m holes does *not* force a collision; you need m+1.
- **Using the basic version when you need the generalized**: "at least 2" is basic; for "at least 3" or higher you must use (k-1)m+1.
- **Thinking it gives you the exact distribution**: It only lower-bounds the largest bin; the others can be zero or anything.
- **Applying it to continuous quantities without discretization**: The principle is discrete; for reals you need the Dirichlet box principle variant.
- **Confusing guarantee with probability**: The principle is deterministic worst-case, not "likely".
- **Claiming a stronger bound than the principle gives**: It may not be tight if additional structure exists.
