# Binomial coefficient

The binomial coefficient \binom{n}{k} is one of the most ubiquitous objects in mathematics. It counts subsets, appears as coefficients in polynomial expansions, generates Pascal's triangle, and underpins the binomial distribution in probability.

Its algebraic and combinatorial properties make it a Swiss-army knife for counting arguments.

## Pascal's Triangle and the Recursive Structure

<!-- section: pascal-triangle -->

Pascal's triangle is the triangular array where each entry is a binomial coefficient. Row n (starting from 0) contains \binom{n}{0}, \binom{n}{1}, ..., \binom{n}{n}. The beautiful additive property \binom{n}{k} = \binom{n-1}{k-1} + \binom{n-1}{k} lets you build the entire triangle from the top.

**ELI5**

- Each number is the sum of the two numbers "parents" above it. It is like a family tree of choices: you either took the item (add the left parent) or skipped it (add the right parent).
- The triangle encodes every possible "choose k from n" question in a visual pyramid.

**Worked Example:**

Row 5: 1 5 10 10 5 1

These are \binom{5}{0} through \binom{5}{5}.

The 10 in the middle is \binom{5}{2} = \binom{5}{3}.

## The Binomial Theorem

<!-- section: binomial-theorem -->

The binomial theorem states:

$$(x + y)^n = \sum_{k=0}^n \binom{n}{k} x^{n-k} y^k.$$

Each term's coefficient \binom{n}{k} tells exactly how many ways to pick the y factor k times when multiplying n copies of (x+y).

**ELI5**

- When you multiply (x+y)(x+y)... n times, the term x^{n-k} y^k arises every time you pick y from exactly k of the factors. There are exactly \binom{n}{k} ways to choose which k factors contribute the y.
- Setting x=1, y=1 instantly gives the subset count 2^n.

**Worked Example:**

Expand (a + b)^4:

a^4 + 4 a^3 b + 6 a^2 b^2 + 4 a b^3 + b^4.

The middle coefficient 6 is \binom{4}{2}.

## Important Identities and Applications

<!-- section: identities-applications -->

Besides Pascal's identity and the subset sum 2^n, we have the hockey-stick identity \sum_{i=r}^m \binom{i}{r} = \binom{m+1}{r+1}, Vandermonde's identity for convolutions of coefficients, and many others.

Applications: probability (binomial distribution), generating functions, algebraic proofs of combinatorial identities, and counting lattice paths.

**ELI5**

- Hockey-stick: imagine choosing r+1 elements from 1..m+1; let the largest chosen element be i+1, then the first r are chosen from 1..i, counted by \binom{i}{r}. Summing gives the total.
- Lattice paths from (0,0) to (n-k,k) with right and up steps: exactly \binom{n}{k} paths.

**Worked Example:**

Number of ways to choose 2 or 3 items from 6: \binom{6}{2} + \binom{6}{3} = 15 + 20 = 35.

By hockey-stick with r=2, sum from i=2 to 5 \binom{i}{2} = \binom{6}{3} = 20 (partial).

## Common Mistakes

- **Computing the wrong k in the expansion**: Remember the exponent on y (or the second variable) is the index k that multiplies \binom{n}{k}.
- **Forgetting symmetry when k is large**: Always replace k by n-k if it is smaller to compute by hand.
- **Thinking the sum of coefficients is n or something else**: It is always 2^n (set both variables to 1).
- **Confusing binomial coefficients with multinomial coefficients**: For more than two categories the multinomial \frac{n!}{k_1! k_2! \dots} generalizes.
- **Using the recursive definition without base cases**: \binom{n}{0}=1 and \binom{n}{n}=1 are essential to start the recursion or triangle.
