# Lovász Local Lemma

The Lovász Local Lemma (LLL) is a fundamental tool that strengthens the probabilistic method to situations with many bad events that are only locally dependent. It shows that if each bad event is sufficiently unlikely and depends on only a limited number of other bad events, then there is a positive probability that none of them occur.

## The Lemma (Symmetric Version)

<!-- section: the-lemma -->

Let A_1, … , A_m be events in a probability space. Suppose:

- Pr[A_i] ≤ p for each i,
- each A_i is mutually independent of all the A_j except for at most D of them (its "neighbors" in the dependency graph).

If e p (D + 1) ≤ 1, then

Pr[ A_1^c ∩ … ∩ A_m^c ] > 0.

That is, there is a positive probability that none of the bad events happen.

**ELI5**

- Each potential disaster is rare (p small) and only "knows about" a few other disasters (D small).
- Then even if there are tons of potential disasters, the chance that they all get avoided is still positive.

**Worked Example:**

2-coloring the vertices of a k-uniform hypergraph with maximum degree D so that no edge is monochromatic.

Bad event per edge: "this edge is mono". p = 2^{1-k}.

Dependency: an edge depends on edges that share a vertex, at most k(D) or so.

When e * 2^{1-k} * (k D) ≲ 1 the LLL guarantees a good coloring exists.

## Proof Sketch

<!-- section: proof-sketch -->

The proof is by showing a stronger statement on conditional probabilities.

One proves (by induction on the number of events or by careful use of the chain rule) that for any event A_i and any set of events not including its neighbors,

Pr[A_i | none of those occurred] ≤ x_i ,

where the x_i are numbers in (0,1) satisfying the general LLL inequalities Pr[A_i] ≤ x_i ∏_{neighbors j} (1 − x_j).

The symmetric case takes x_i = 1/(D+1) for all i and uses the inequality (1 − 1/(D+1))^D ≥ 1/e (approximately) to obtain the numerical condition.

The product ∏ (1 − x_i) then lower-bounds the probability that all are avoided.

**ELI5**

- You bound the chance that one more disaster happens, given that all the disasters it could possibly influence have already been prevented.
- The limited dependence lets the bound factor nicely; the hypothesis makes that conditional chance <1 in a way that multiplies to something positive overall.

**Worked Example:**

For the calculation with p and D, plugging x = 1/(D+1) yields exactly the condition e p (D+1) ≤ 1 after using the standard inequality for (1-x)^D.

## Applications

<!-- section: applications -->

The LLL is used for:

- Hypergraph 2-colorability (property B) when maximum degree is not too large relative to uniformity.
- List coloring and choice number bounds χ_l(G) = O(Δ).
- Non-repetitive colorings, acyclic colorings, frugal colorings.
- Latin squares, edge colorings, Ramsey numbers for sparse graphs, etc.
- Many results in positional games, random structures, and theoretical CS.

The algorithmic LLL (Moser-Tardos resampling) turns many of these existence proofs into efficient randomized algorithms: while there is a currently bad event, resample all the random variables that affect it. Under the LLL condition the expected number of resamples is linear.

**ELI5**

- "Keep rerolling the dice that affect the currently broken constraints. Because the bad events are locally rare and locally dependent, you won't have to reroll forever; you'll quickly reach a global good configuration."

**Worked Example:**

For list coloring with lists of size roughly 2eΔ: assign each vertex a random color from its list. While there is a monochromatic edge, resample the colors of its two endpoints. The LLL condition guarantees that the process terminates quickly with a proper list coloring.

## Common Mistakes

- **Using the symmetric LLL when probabilities or degrees vary wildly**: The general (asymmetric) version with per-event x_i often saves the day.
- **Forgetting the +1 or the e in the bound**: The precise constant matters for borderline cases; sometimes a slightly better analysis (or the lopsided LLL) works when the basic bound is 1.01 >1.
- **Confusing dependence with correlation**: The LLL requires *mutual independence* from the non-neighbors (in the probability space), not merely uncorrelated.
- **Thinking the LLL gives probability close to 1**: It only gives >0; for high-probability results one needs concentration or the algorithmic version with many independent trials.
- **Applying it without defining the probability space and the exact dependency graph**: The lemma is only as good as the modeling of "bad events" and their dependencies.
