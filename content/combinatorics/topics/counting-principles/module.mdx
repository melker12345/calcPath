# Counting Principles

The pigeonhole principle, inclusion–exclusion, and bijective proofs for existence and exact counting.

The pigeonhole principle is deceptively simple yet extraordinarily powerful: it lets you prove that something *must* happen in any large enough structure, without constructing an example or checking all cases.

It is one of the first "non-constructive" proof techniques most students encounter.

## The Basic Principle

<!-- section: basic-principle -->

If you have more pigeons than pigeonholes, then at least one pigeonhole contains more than one pigeon.

In mathematical language: if n > m, then when distributing n items into m containers, at least one container has at least two items.

**ELI5**

- 13 socks into 12 drawers: even if you try to spread them out, at least one drawer ends up with two socks.
- 13 people, 12 months: at least two people share a birthday month. You don't need their names; pure counting forces the collision.

**Worked Example:**

In a group of 367 people, at least two share the same birthday (ignoring leap years, 365 days).

Worst case: 365 people with distinct birthdays. The 366th person collides with someone. For 367 we are guaranteed even after worst case.

## The Generalized Pigeonhole Principle

<!-- section: generalized -->

To guarantee that at least one container has *at least k* items when there are m containers, you need (k−1)m + 1 items.

Equivalently, when distributing n items into m containers, at least one container has at least ceil(n/m) items.

**ELI5**

- To force 3 in one drawer with 12 drawers: put 2 in each first (24), then the 25th forces a third.
- Average is n/m; the ceiling tells you the guaranteed maximum load.

**Worked Example:**

Distribute 100 identical candies to 7 children. Guarantee that one child gets at least how many?

ceil(100/7) = 15. Or: worst case 14 each for 7 children = 98, the remaining 2 force some to 15 or more. Yes, at least one gets ≥15.

## Applications and Existence Proofs

<!-- section: applications-proofs -->

The principle shines in Ramsey theory, graph theory (e.g. in any 2-coloring of K_6 there is a monochromatic triangle), number theory (Dirichlet's approximation theorem is a continuous analogue), and computer science (hash collisions, birthday paradox bounds).

It proves existence; it does not tell you *which* hole or *how to find* the collision.

**ELI5**

- In a big enough party you are guaranteed either a group of 3 mutual friends or 3 mutual strangers (Ramsey R(3,3)=6). The proof is a clever double application of pigeonhole on a person's friendships.
- It is "I don't know who, but someone must have..."

**Worked Example:**

Prove that in any set of 5 integers, there are two whose difference is divisible by 4.

Consider residues mod 4: 0,1,2,3 — only 4 "pigeonholes". 5 numbers → at least two have the same residue r. Their difference is 0 mod 4.

## Common Mistakes

- **Forgetting the "+1"**: m pigeons into m holes does *not* force a collision; you need m+1.
- **Using the basic version when you need the generalized**: "at least 2" is basic; for "at least 3" or higher you must use (k-1)m+1.
- **Thinking it gives you the exact distribution**: It only lower-bounds the largest bin; the others can be zero or anything.
- **Applying it to continuous quantities without discretization**: The principle is discrete; for reals you need the Dirichlet box principle variant.
- **Confusing guarantee with probability**: The principle is deterministic worst-case, not "likely".
- **Claiming a stronger bound than the principle gives**: It may not be tight if additional structure exists.

When sets overlap, simply adding their sizes overcounts the elements that belong to multiple sets. Inclusion-exclusion corrects these overcounts with a precise alternating sum.

It is the systematic generalization of the two-set union formula to any number of sets and is indispensable for exact counting with overlaps.

## The Two-Set Case

<!-- section: two-sets -->

For any two sets:

$$|A \cup B| = |A| + |B| - |A \cap B|.$$

The intersection term is subtracted because those elements were added twice in the first sum; we want them counted only once.

**ELI5**

- People who like cats + people who like dogs counts the cat-and-dog lovers twice. Subtract them once and every lover is counted exactly once.
- If the sets are disjoint the intersection is empty and the formula collapses to plain addition.

**Worked Example:**

30 students. 18 play soccer, 12 play basketball, 5 play both.

Students playing at least one sport: 18 + 12 − 5 = 25.

Students playing neither: 30 − 25 = 5.

## Three or More Sets

<!-- section: three-or-more -->

For three sets:

$$|A \cup B \cup C| = |A| + |B| + |C| - |A∩B| - |A∩C| - |B∩C| + |A∩B∩C|.$$

In general, for a family of sets the term for the intersection of exactly k of them receives sign (−1)^{k+1}.

**ELI5**

- Triples were added three times then subtracted three times (once for each pair), netting zero — but they belong in the union, so we add them back at the end.
- The signs alternate + − + − … exactly like the principle of inclusion and exclusion.

**Worked Example:**

|A|=20, |B|=15, |C|=10; pairwise 5,4,3; triple 2.

Union = 20+15+10 − (5+4+3) + 2 = 45 − 12 + 2 = 35.

## Applications and Derivation Intuition

<!-- section: applications -->

The principle is derived by considering for each element how many times it is counted in the naive sum of singles, then in the corrections: an element in exactly r sets is overcounted in a way that the alternating sum leaves it with coefficient exactly 1 if r ≥ 1.

Classic uses: counting integers divisible by certain numbers (sieve), derangements, counting surjective functions, probability of unions, graph edge covers, etc.

**ELI5**

- "How many numbers ≤ 100 are divisible by 2, 3 or 5?" You add the three floors, subtract the pairwise lcms, add back the triple.
- It is the exact discrete version of "adding probabilities with overlaps corrected."

**Worked Example:**

Numbers from 1 to 100 divisible by 2, 3 or 5:

|A|=50, |B|=33, |C|=20, |A∩B|=16, |A∩C|=10, |B∩C|=6, |A∩B∩C|=3.

50+33+20 −16−10−6 +3 = 74.

## Common Mistakes

- **Getting the signs wrong**: + singles, − pairs, + triples, − quadruples… The pattern is (-1)^{k+1} for the k-fold intersections.
- **Forgetting to add back the triple (or higher) intersections**: After subtracting pairs the triple people have been added 3× and subtracted 3×, so they disappear unless you add them.
- **Using the formula when sets are already disjoint**: It still works (intersections zero) but you are doing unnecessary work.
- **Miscounting the intersections themselves**: |A ∩ B ∩ C| requires elements in *all three*, not just some pairs.
- **Applying to infinite sets without care**: The principle is for finite cardinalities; infinite cases need different tools.
- **Confusing union count with probability without dividing**: The count is useful, but probability requires dividing by the size of the universe.

A bijective proof establishes that two sets have the same cardinality by exhibiting an explicit, size-preserving, one-to-one correspondence between them. It is often considered the most satisfying kind of proof in combinatorics because it explains *why* the counts are equal at the level of the objects themselves rather than through algebraic manipulation or analysis.

## The Idea of a Bijective Proof

<!-- section: idea-of-bijection -->

To prove |A_n| = |B_n| for each n, construct a map f : A_n → B_n that is invertible (or an involution on the disjoint union) and preserves the size parameter n.

The existence of such an f immediately implies the cardinalities are equal; no further computation is needed.

**ELI5**

- Instead of calculating two big formulas and showing they simplify to the same thing, you say "here is a perfect pairing: every red thing corresponds to exactly one blue thing of the same size."
- The pairing itself is the proof.

**Worked Example:**

To prove \binom{n}{k} = \binom{n}{n-k}, the map "take the complement" sends a k-subset to the (n-k)-subset of elements not chosen. It is clearly an involution and reverses the size parameter.

## Classic Examples

<!-- section: classic-examples -->

- Complement for binomial symmetry.
- Toggle a fixed element to prove even/odd sized subsets are equinumerous (for n>0).
- Many Catalan bijections: binary trees ↔ Dyck paths ↔ non-crossing partitions ↔ triangulations ↔ stack-sortable permutations, etc.
- LGV lemma for counting non-intersecting lattice paths via determinants (sign-reversing involution on intersecting tuples).
- The cycle lemma for Catalan and for counting necklaces/ Lyndon words.

**ELI5**

- The reflection principle for ballot theorem / Catalan numbers: bad paths are paired with paths that end at the wrong place by reflecting the prefix until first touch of the barrier.
- Each such pairing cancels the bad objects, leaving only the good ones counted by the difference of binomials.

**Worked Example:**

The reflection proof that the number of Dyck paths of semilength n is \binom{2n}{n} − \binom{2n}{n-1} (or equivalently 1/(n+1) binom): any path from (0,0) to (2n,0) that touches or crosses +1 is reflected in the initial segment until first +1; this gives a bijection with all paths from (0,2) or standard from (1,1) to (2n,0) that correspond to choosing n-1 up in 2n-1 steps, i.e. \binom{2n}{n-1}.

## Power and Limitations

<!-- section: power-and-limitations -->

Bijective proofs often generalize beautifully, admit q-analogues, or lift to richer categories (species, categorification).

Finding a "nice" bijection can be a deep open problem even when the equality is known by other means (e.g. some Rogers-Ramanujan identities had no bijective proofs for a long time).

They provide combinatorial understanding and can suggest algorithms or probabilistic interpretations.

**ELI5**

- A good bijection is like a Rosetta stone: it translates facts about one family into facts about the other.
- Sometimes the algebra is easier; sometimes the bijection is the only way to see the positivity or the reason.

**Worked Example:**

The famous "bijective proof" of the Rogers-Ramanujan identities (Andrews, Bressoud, Zeilberger et al. in various forms) gives direct correspondences between partitions with certain difference conditions and partitions into parts congruent to ±1 or ±2 mod 5, explaining the equality without analysis.

## Common Mistakes

- **Claiming a bijection exists without describing it**: "There is a bijection" is not a proof; you must exhibit or at least rigorously define the map and its inverse.
- **Forgetting to preserve the parameter**: The map must send size-n objects to size-n objects (or weight-preserving in weighted counts).
- **Thinking algebraic proofs are inferior**: They are often faster and sufficient; bijections are prized for the insight they provide.
- **Assuming every identity has a 'simple' bijective proof**: Some require very intricate involutions or sign-reversing involutions with fixed-point-free parts.
- **Confusing bijection with injection or surjection only**: You need both directions (or an explicit inverse).
