# Pythagorean theorem

The Pythagorean theorem states that in a right-angled triangle, the square of the length of the hypotenuse (the side opposite the right angle) is equal to the sum of the squares of the lengths of the other two sides.

It is one of the most famous and useful theorems in mathematics, with hundreds of proofs and applications in distance, vectors, and physics.

## Statement and Notation

<!-- section: statement -->

For right triangle ABC with right angle at C, AB^{2} = AC^{2} + BC^{2}, where AB is the hypotenuse.

In algebraic form: c^{2} = a^{2} + b^{2} where c is hypotenuse, a and b the legs.

**ELI5**

- If you build squares outward on each side of a right triangle, the two smaller squares together exactly cover the big square on the hypotenuse.
- It is the "3-4-5" rule generalized: any right triangle obeys the same squaring relationship.

**Worked Example:**

A right triangle has legs 5 and 12. Hypotenuse c satisfies c^{2} = 25 + 144 = 169, so c = 13. This is the classic 5-12-13 right triangle.

## Proofs (Euclid and Modern)

<!-- section: proofs -->

Euclid's proof (Prop. I.47) uses congruence of triangles obtained by dropping perpendiculars and rearranging areas. There are algebraic proofs (using similar triangles), vector proofs (dot product), and even proofs by rearrangement (van Schooten, etc.).

One simple modern proof: drop an altitude from the right angle to the hypotenuse, creating two smaller right triangles similar to each other and to the original. Then a/c = c/b or similar proportions lead to c^{2} = a^{2} + b^{2} after cross-multiplying.

**ELI5**

- Euclid's proof is a giant jigsaw: he shows four copies of the triangle plus squares can be rearranged in two ways whose areas must match.
- The similar-triangles proof says "the pieces are scaled versions of the big one, so their sizes multiply to give the squares."

**Worked Example:**

Using similar triangles: the altitude to hypotenuse creates segments p and q on the hypotenuse with p + q = c. Then a^{2} = c·p and b^{2} = c·q. Adding: a^{2} + b^{2} = c(p + q) = c·c = c^{2}.

## Converse and Applications

<!-- section: converse-apps -->

The converse is also true: if in a triangle a^{2} + b^{2} = c^{2} then the angle opposite c is right.

Applications: distance formula in coordinate geometry is just Pythagorean on the differences Δx and Δy. In physics it appears in work, energy, and vector magnitude.

**ELI5**

- If the sides satisfy the square relation, you know for sure there is a right angle even if you cannot see the corner.
- GPS, video games, and construction all secretly use "square the differences and add" to find straight-line distances.

**Worked Example:**

Points (1,2) and (4,6). Δx=3, Δy=4. Distance = √(9+16) = 5. This is Pythagorean applied to the horizontal and vertical legs of the right triangle formed by the points and the axes.

## Common Mistakes

- **Applying it to non-right triangles**: The relation fails for acute or obtuse triangles (use law of cosines instead).
- **Forgetting which side is squared alone**: Only the hypotenuse (longest side, opposite right angle) goes on one side of the equation.
- **Confusing with area formula**: (1/2)ab is area, not related directly to the squares in Pythagoras.
- **Assuming all 3-4-5 scaled triangles are the only right triangles**: Any legs a,b with hypotenuse √(a^{2}+b^{2}) work; 3-4-5 is just the smallest integer example.
