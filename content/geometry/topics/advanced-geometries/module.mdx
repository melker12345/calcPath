# Advanced Geometries

Projective, non-Euclidean, hyperbolic, elliptic, and spherical geometry.

Projective geometry studies properties of figures that are preserved under projections (perspective mappings).

It unifies the treatment of parallel lines (which 'meet at infinity') and is the natural setting for perspective drawing and algebraic geometry.

## Motivation from Perspective

<!-- section: motivation-perspective -->

In a perspective drawing, parallel lines in the real world appear to converge at a vanishing point on the horizon line.

Projective geometry makes this rigorous by adding 'points at infinity' so that every pair of lines meets exactly once.

**ELI5**

- On a flat painting, railroad tracks that are parallel in real life get closer and meet at a point on the canvas.
- Projective geometry says 'in the picture they really do meet; we just have to allow a special 'infinity' line where they meet.'

**Worked Example:**

Two parallel lines in the plane, when projected onto a canvas from a viewpoint, intersect on the canvas at the vanishing point. In the projective plane they intersect at a single point at infinity.

## The Projective Plane

<!-- section: proj-plane-def -->

The real projective plane RP^{2} can be constructed as the ordinary plane plus a line at infinity, or as the set of lines through the origin in 3-space, or as a sphere with antipodal points identified.

In homogeneous coordinates [x : y : z], points are equivalence classes of (x,y,z) not all zero, scaled by nonzero \lambda.

**ELI5**

- Every 'direction' gets its own point at infinity; all parallel lines in that direction share that one point.
- Homogeneous coordinates let you write equations without choosing an origin or worrying about 'infinity' cases separately.

**Worked Example:**

The equation of a line in the projective plane is a homogeneous linear equation a x + b y + c z = 0. Setting z=1 recovers the usual affine line; z=0 is the line at infinity.

## Desargues, Pappus, and Incidence Theorems

<!-- section: incidence-theorems -->

Many beautiful theorems (Desargues, Pappus) hold in projective geometry that have no direct Euclidean analogue or are more uniform.

Cross ratios are preserved by projective transformations.

**ELI5**

- Projective geometry has its own 'Pythagorean theorem' analogues that are simpler because there are no parallel exceptions.
- Incidence (which points lie on which lines) becomes the main relation.

**Worked Example:**

Desargues' theorem: two triangles in perspective from a point are in perspective from a line. The statement is purely about points and lines meeting; it holds in any projective plane over a field.

## Relation to Euclidean and Affine Geometry

<!-- section: relation-eucl-affine -->

Affine geometry is projective geometry minus the line at infinity (parallels distinguished but no metric).

Euclidean geometry adds a metric (circle, angles, distances) on top of affine.

Projective geometry is the most basic incidence geometry.

**ELI5**

- Start with projective (just points and lines meeting). Add 'which lines are parallel' to get affine. Add 'which distances and angles' to get Euclidean.
- Many Euclidean theorems become cleaner when viewed projectively.

**Worked Example:**

The statement 'the three altitudes of a triangle meet at a point' (orthocenter) has a projective proof or generalization; in projective terms the orthocenter, circumcenter, etc., are related by polarity.

## Common Mistakes

- **Thinking projective geometry has distances and angles**: It does not; those are added later in Euclidean or metric geometries.
- **Confusing the line at infinity with 'very far away'**: It is a single line added; all 'directions' live on it.
- **Assuming every projective plane is the real one**: There are many non-Desarguesian planes and planes over finite fields.
- **Forgetting that in projective geometry every two lines meet**: There are no parallels by definition.

A projective plane is a geometric structure consisting of points and lines satisfying: any two distinct points determine a unique line, any two distinct lines intersect in a unique point, and there exist four points no three of which are collinear (non-degeneracy).

The real projective plane RP^{2} is the most familiar example.

## Axioms of a Projective Plane

<!-- section: axioms-proj-plane -->

1. Any two distinct points lie on exactly one line.
2. Any two distinct lines meet in exactly one point.
3. There exist four points, no three collinear.

These are dual: swapping 'point' and 'line' gives the same axioms.

**ELI5**

- Every pair of 'dots' has exactly one 'stick' connecting them.
- Every pair of 'sticks' crosses at exactly one 'dot'.
- The structure is big enough to not be a single line or a triangle (the four-point condition).

**Worked Example:**

The smallest projective plane is the Fano plane (7 points, 7 lines, each line 3 points). It satisfies the axioms but is finite.

## Models

<!-- section: models-proj-plane -->

- RP^{2}: lines through origin in R^{3}, or plane + line at infinity.
- Over finite fields: PG(2,q) has q^{2} + q + 1 points and same number of lines.
- There exist non-Desarguesian planes (not coming from fields) in some orders.

**ELI5**

- The 'usual' model adds one infinite line to the ordinary plane.
- Finite models are like 'finite geometries' used in combinatorics and coding theory.

**Worked Example:**

PG(2,2) is the Fano plane: 7 points, lines of 3, every pair in exactly one line, every pair of lines meet in one point.

## Duality and Self-Dual Nature

<!-- section: duality -->

Because the axioms are symmetric in point and line, every theorem has a dual theorem obtained by swapping the words 'point' and 'line' (and 'lie on' / 'intersect').

This often gives two theorems for the price of one.

**ELI5**

- If a statement about dots and sticks is true, then the 'reversed' statement about sticks and dots is automatically true by the symmetry of the rules.
- Desargues' theorem is self-dual; its dual is itself.

**Worked Example:**

The dual of 'two points determine a line' is 'two lines determine a point.' Both are axioms.

## Embedding Affine and Euclidean Planes

<!-- section: embedding -->

Removing one line (the 'line at infinity') from a projective plane yields an affine plane (parallels appear).

Adding a metric on the affine plane can yield a Euclidean plane.

**ELI5**

- Take the projective plane; declare one line to be 'infinity.' The remaining points and lines behave like an ordinary plane with parallels.
- Then you can add rulers and protractors if you want Euclidean geometry.

**Worked Example:**

In RP^{2} remove the line z=0. The remaining points have homogeneous coordinates with z \neq 0; normalize z=1 to get ordinary (x,y) coordinates. Lines not the infinity line become ordinary lines; parallels meet at infinity.

## Common Mistakes

- **Thinking 'projective plane' always means the real one with pictures**: There are many abstract ones, finite and infinite.
- **Forgetting duality**: Many proofs have a dual proof that is obtained mechanically by swapping words.
- **Assuming all projective planes are Desarguesian**: In higher dimensions or certain finite orders there are exotic ones.
- **Confusing the projective plane with the sphere or disk**: Topologically RP^{2} is a sphere with antipodes identified, or a disk with opposite boundary points glued with a twist (Möbius).

Non-Euclidean geometry is any geometry in which Euclid's parallel postulate does not hold.

The two classical examples are hyperbolic geometry (many parallels) and elliptic geometry (no parallels).

## The Parallel Postulate and Its Independence

<!-- section: parallel-independence -->

Euclid's fifth postulate (or Playfair: exactly one parallel through a point not on a line) is independent of the other axioms.

Assuming its negation produces consistent geometries (hyperbolic or elliptic) that have models inside or on ordinary Euclidean space.

**ELI5**

- The 'exactly one parallel' rule is not forced by the other drawing rules. You can change it and still have a perfectly logical geometry.
- The new geometries look 'curved' when drawn inside flat paper, but they are self-consistent.

**Worked Example:**

On a sphere, 'lines' = great circles. Any two great circles intersect; there are no parallels. This is a model of elliptic geometry (with some antipodal identifications for the strict projective version).

## Hyperbolic Geometry

<!-- section: hyperbolic-geom -->

In hyperbolic geometry, through a point not on a line there are infinitely many lines that do not meet the given line.

The angle sum of every triangle is less than 180°; the defect is proportional to area.

Models: Poincaré disk, upper half-plane, hyperboloid.

**ELI5**

- On a giant saddle or floppy hat, from a point you can draw many 'straight' paths that never cross a given 'straight' path.
- Triangles are 'skinny'; their angles add to less than a straight angle, and the bigger the triangle the 'skinnier' the angles.

**Worked Example:**

In the Poincaré disk model, 'lines' are circular arcs perpendicular to the boundary circle. Many such arcs through an interior point can be drawn that never meet a given arc (they approach the same boundary point asymptotically).

## Elliptic Geometry

<!-- section: elliptic-geom -->

In elliptic geometry there are no parallel lines; every pair of lines intersects.

The angle sum of every triangle is greater than 180°.

The sphere with antipodes identified is a model (projective elliptic plane).

**ELI5**

- On the surface of a ball, any two 'great circle' routes eventually cross.
- A triangle on the globe can have three 90° corners (sum 270°).

**Worked Example:**

On Earth, two meridians and the equator form a triangle with angles 90°+90°+90° = 270° > 180°. This is impossible in Euclidean geometry.

## Comparison Table and Curvature

<!-- section: comparison-curvature -->

Euclidean: curvature 0, angle sum = 180°, exactly one parallel.

Hyperbolic: negative curvature, angle sum < 180°, many parallels.

Elliptic: positive curvature, angle sum > 180°, no parallels.

**ELI5**

- Curvature tells whether the world is flat (Euclidean), saddle-like (hyperbolic), or ball-like (elliptic).
- The parallel behavior and angle sums are symptoms of the curvature.

**Worked Example:**

Gauss-Bonnet theorem (global version) relates total curvature (integral of Gaussian curvature) over a triangle to the angle defect/excess: \int K dA = angle sum - 180° (in appropriate units). This unifies all three geometries.

## Applications and Significance

<!-- section: apps-significance -->

Hyperbolic geometry appears in special relativity (Minkowski space has hyperbolic geometry in velocity space), in the geometry of trees and networks, and in art (Escher's Circle Limit prints).

Elliptic geometry models the real projective plane and appears in some models of the universe (closed universes).

The discovery that the parallel postulate is independent revolutionized the philosophy of mathematics: 'truth' depends on the chosen axioms.

**ELI5**

- When you zoom out far enough or live in a space with different 'shape,' the rules for parallels and triangles change.
- The discovery freed mathematicians to invent new 'worlds' by changing the rules, as long as the new rules are consistent.

**Worked Example:**

In special relativity the velocity vectors of observers live in hyperbolic 3-space; the 'distance' corresponds to relative rapidity. Parallel transport and triangles behave hyperbolically.

## Common Mistakes

- **Thinking non-Euclidean geometry is 'wrong' or only for huge scales**: It is a different but equally valid consistent system; it is useful at any scale once the model is chosen.
- **Confusing the model with the geometry**: The Poincaré disk is a Euclidean picture of hyperbolic geometry; the intrinsic geometry inside the disk (with the right distance) is hyperbolic.
- **Assuming spherical geometry is exactly elliptic**: The sphere has 'antipodal' issues; the true elliptic plane identifies antipodes so that lines have unique intersections without double-counting.
- **Forgetting that Euclid's other postulates still hold in the new geometries**: Only the parallel postulate is changed; the first four are common.

Hyperbolic geometry is the non-Euclidean geometry in which the parallel postulate is replaced by the assumption that through a point not on a given line there pass at least two (in fact infinitely many) lines that do not intersect the given line.

It has constant negative curvature and rich applications in mathematics and physics.

## Axioms and Parallel Behavior

<!-- section: axioms-parallel-hyp -->

The axioms are Euclid's first four plus the hyperbolic parallel axiom: given line L and point P not on L, there exist at least two lines through P that do not meet L.

In fact there is a whole pencil of non-secant lines, bounded by two asymptotic parallels.

**ELI5**

- From P you can 'aim' in a range of directions and the 'straight' path will never hit L; only a limited cone of directions will hit L.
- The two 'limiting' directions give the asymptotic parallels that approach L at infinity but never meet.

**Worked Example:**

In the Poincaré disk, fix L as a diameter arc. From an interior point P draw many arcs orthogonal to the boundary that never cross L inside the disk; they 'escape' to the boundary without crossing.

## Angle Sum, Area, and Defect

<!-- section: angle-sum-area -->

In any hyperbolic triangle the angle sum is < 180°. The defect \delta = 180° − sum is positive and proportional to the area of the triangle: Area = k^{2} \delta (for curvature −1/k^{2}).

There is an absolute unit of length (unlike Euclidean geometry).

**ELI5**

- Hyperbolic triangles are always 'too skinny'; the bigger they are the more their angles fall short of 180°.
- Area is measured by the angle defect; there is a largest possible area for triangles (when angles approach 0).

**Worked Example:**

An 'ideal' triangle with all vertices at infinity has angles 0,0,0 and finite positive area (\pi in curvature −1 normalization). Any ordinary triangle inside it has smaller area equal to its defect.

## Models

<!-- section: models-hyp -->

Poincaré disk: conformal model, lines = arcs ⊥ boundary.

Upper half-plane: similar, lines = semicircles or vertical lines ⊥ real axis.

Hyperboloid / Minkowski model: intrinsic geometry on the hyperboloid in 3D Minkowski space.

Klein-Beltrami: projective model (lines are straight chords, but angles and lengths distorted).

**ELI5**

- The disk model makes angles look correct (conformal) but distances stretch wildly near the edge.
- The hyperboloid model lets you see the geometry as the 'natural' geometry on a surface sitting in a higher-dimensional flat (but with Minkowski metric) space.

**Worked Example:**

In the upper half-plane model y > 0, the 'lines' are vertical rays or semicircles centered on the x-axis. The hyperbolic distance involves log of cross-ratio or integral dy/y along the path.

## Trigonometry and Functions

<!-- section: trig-hyp -->

Hyperbolic trigonometry: cosh c = cosh a cosh b − sinh a sinh b cos C, etc.

The hyperbolic functions sinh, cosh, tanh replace the circular ones.

**ELI5**

- The formulas look like spherical trig but with hyperbolic functions (which grow exponentially).
- For small triangles the formulas reduce to ordinary Euclidean trig (as expected locally).

**Worked Example:**

In a hyperbolic right triangle with right angle at C, cosh c = cosh a cosh b (hypotenuse c). For small a,b this is approximately c^{2} \approx a^{2} + b^{2} (the Euclidean Pythagorean).

## Common Mistakes

- **Thinking the Poincaré disk 'is' hyperbolic geometry with Euclidean measurements**: The disk is only a picture; distances and angles must be measured with the hyperbolic metric (which stretches near the boundary).
- **Confusing asymptotic parallels with ordinary parallels**: Asymptotic parallels approach each other at infinity; ultra-parallels diverge in both directions and have a unique common perpendicular.
- **Assuming there is an absolute 'straightness' visible in the model**: 'Straight' means geodesic in the hyperbolic metric, which looks curved in the Euclidean picture of the model.
- **Forgetting that hyperbolic geometry has an absolute length scale**: The curvature sets a unit; you cannot scale the whole geometry without changing the curvature.

Elliptic geometry is the non-Euclidean geometry in which there are no parallel lines: every pair of lines intersects.

It has constant positive curvature; the angle sum of every triangle is greater than 180°.

The standard model is the real projective plane with a suitable metric (or the sphere with antipodal points identified).

## Axioms and Incidence

<!-- section: axioms-incidence-ell -->

Euclid's first four postulates plus the elliptic parallel axiom: there are no parallels; any two lines meet.

Incidence is like the projective plane: any two points determine a unique line, any two lines meet in a unique point.

**ELI5**

- There are no 'parallel' roads; every road eventually crosses every other road.
- The geometry is 'closed' and finite in a certain sense (though the metric can be scaled).

**Worked Example:**

On a sphere, great circles are the 'lines.' Any two great circles intersect in two antipodal points. Identifying antipodes gives a model where they intersect in exactly one point.

## Angle Sum and Excess

<!-- section: angle-excess-ell -->

Angle sum of a triangle > 180°. The excess \epsilon = sum − 180° is positive and proportional to area: Area = k^{2} \epsilon (curvature +1/k^{2}).

There is no 'ideal triangle' with infinite area; all triangles are 'fat.'

**ELI5**

- Triangles on a ball 'bulge'; their angles add up to more than a straight angle.
- The bigger the triangle (in area), the more the angles exceed 180°.

**Worked Example:**

A spherical triangle with three 90° angles has excess 90° = \pi/2 radians. On the unit sphere its area is exactly the excess (\pi/2).

## Models and Double Covers

<!-- section: models-double-ell -->

The sphere S^{2} with the round metric is a double cover of the elliptic plane: antipodal points are identified.

Lines are great circles (projected).

Distances are the shorter great-circle distance (between 0 and \pi/2 in the elliptic plane).

**ELI5**

- Walking on the sphere, going through the antipode gets you to the 'same' point in the elliptic world.
- This removes the 'two intersection points' problem of the plain sphere.

**Worked Example:**

Two antipodal points on the sphere become one point in elliptic geometry. A great circle becomes a closed 'line' of length \pi (in curvature 1) that has no 'ends.'

## Trigonometry

<!-- section: trig-ell -->

Spherical trigonometry (for the sphere model): cos c = cos a cos b + sin a sin b cos C.

For the elliptic plane the formulas are similar but distances are halved or adjusted for the identification.

**ELI5**

- The formulas are like the hyperbolic ones but with ordinary trig functions and plus signs.
- For small triangles they reduce to Euclidean trig.

**Worked Example:**

On unit sphere, a right triangle with legs a=b=\pi/2 has hypotenuse c=\pi/2 (three right angles). Check: cos(\pi/2) = 0 = cos(\pi/2)cos(\pi/2) + sin(\pi/2)sin(\pi/2) cos(\pi/2) = 0 + 1*0 = 0. Holds.

## Relation to Projective Geometry

<!-- section: relation-proj-ell -->

Elliptic geometry is the real projective plane equipped with a metric of constant positive curvature.

Many incidence theorems of projective geometry hold, and the metric adds the angle and length notions.

**ELI5**

- Start with the 'dots and sticks' rules of the projective plane.
- Then add a way to measure distances and angles consistently with the positive curvature.

**Worked Example:**

In the elliptic plane the sum of angles in a triangle determines its area (excess law), a metric statement on top of the incidence structure.

## Common Mistakes

- **Using the sphere without identifying antipodes**: On the plain sphere lines intersect in two points; the elliptic plane is the quotient so that lines intersect once.
- **Confusing elliptic with spherical geometry in applications**: Many calculations are done on the sphere and then interpreted in the quotient.
- **Thinking there is a 'largest' triangle**: On the sphere there are hemispheres, but in the elliptic plane the whole space is finite.
- **Forgetting that elliptic geometry is non-orientable in the plane version**: The projective plane is non-orientable; there is no consistent global notion of 'clockwise.'

Spherical geometry is the geometry of the surface of a sphere, where 'lines' are great circles (the shortest paths, intersections of the sphere with planes through the center).

It is a model of elliptic geometry (with the double-cover caveat) and has constant positive curvature.

## 'Lines' and Incidence on the Sphere

<!-- section: lines-incidence-sph -->

A great circle is the intersection of the sphere with a plane through its center.

Any two great circles intersect in two antipodal points.

The shortest path between two points is the minor arc of the great circle they determine.

**ELI5**

- 'Straight lines' on a globe are the equator and the meridians (and other great circles); they are the biggest possible circles on the sphere.
- Any two such 'lines' cross (at two opposite points on the globe).

**Worked Example:**

New York and Madrid determine a unique great circle; the shortest flight path follows the minor arc of that circle (not a constant compass heading, except for due north/south).

## Triangles and Polygons on the Sphere

<!-- section: triangles-poly-sph -->

A spherical triangle is bounded by three minor great-circle arcs.

Its area (on unit sphere) equals its angle excess (sum of angles minus 180°), in steradians.

Girard's theorem.

**ELI5**

- A triangle drawn on a ball with three 'straight' sides bulges; the more it bulges the bigger its angles and its area.
- You can have triangles with angle sum 270° or even close to 540° (hemisphere).

**Worked Example:**

The triangle formed by the equator from 0° to 90° longitude and the two meridians has three 90° angles and area \pi/2 steradians on the unit sphere (1/8 of the sphere's 4\pi).

## Distance and Trigonometry

<!-- section: dist-trig-sph -->

Spherical distance between two points with angular separation \theta (at the center) is r \theta (on sphere of radius r).

Spherical law of cosines, law of sines, polar triangle duality, etc.

**ELI5**

- Distances are measured along the great-circle arcs (the 'ruler' is the angle at the center times radius).
- The trig formulas look like the plane ones but with extra terms coming from the curvature.

**Worked Example:**

Two points with colatitude \theta1, \theta2 and longitude difference \Delta\lambda. Cos c = cos \theta1 cos \theta2 + sin \theta1 sin \theta2 cos \Delta\lambda (unit sphere). This is the spherical law of cosines for sides.

## Navigation and Applications

<!-- section: nav-apps-sph -->

Great-circle navigation is the shortest path for ships and planes (when wind/ currents ignored).

Spherical geometry is used in astronomy (celestial sphere), geodesy, and global positioning.

Loxodromes (rhumb lines) are curves of constant bearing; they are not great circles except meridians and equator.

**ELI5**

- To go shortest distance, follow the great circle (the 'straight line' on the sphere).
- To keep a constant compass heading, follow a rhumb line, which spirals toward the poles and is longer.

**Worked Example:**

The shortest path from New York to Tokyo is a great-circle arc that goes north of the Aleutians, even though the constant-heading rhumb line looks straighter on a Mercator map.

## Relation to Elliptic Geometry

<!-- section: relation-ell-sph -->

The sphere is the double cover of the elliptic plane. Many calculations are done on the sphere and then projected.

On the sphere lines intersect twice; in the elliptic plane they intersect once (after identification).

**ELI5**

- If you consider 'opposite' points on the sphere as the same, you get the elliptic world where every pair of lines crosses once.
- For local calculations (small regions) the sphere and elliptic plane look the same.

**Worked Example:**

A hemispherical 'triangle' that covers 1/8 of the sphere corresponds to a triangle in the elliptic plane whose area is half (because of the identification).

## Common Mistakes

- **Using rhumb lines as shortest paths**: Constant bearing is easy to steer but usually longer than the great-circle route.
- **Forgetting the double intersection of great circles**: On the sphere any two lines meet at two antipodal points.
- **Applying plane trigonometry formulas directly to large spherical triangles**: The spherical excess becomes significant; use spherical trig.
- **Confusing spherical area (steradians) with solid angle in 3D**: On the unit sphere they coincide numerically, but conceptually the spherical triangle is a 2D region.
