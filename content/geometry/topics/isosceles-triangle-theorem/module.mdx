# Isosceles triangle theorem

The isosceles triangle theorem (also called the base angles theorem) states that if two sides of a triangle are equal, then the angles opposite those sides are equal.

The converse is also true: if two angles are equal, the sides opposite them are equal.

## Statement and Converse

<!-- section: statement-converse -->

In △ABC, if AB = AC, then ∠B = ∠C (base angles equal).

Converse: if ∠B = ∠C, then AB = AC.

**ELI5**

- If two sides are the same length, the "base" angles at the bottom must be the same; the triangle is "balanced."
- If two angles are the same, the sides facing them must be the same length.

**Worked Example:**

△ABC with AB = AC = 5, base BC = 6. Then base angles at B and C are equal. By law of cosines or dropping altitude, each is arccos((25+ (3)^2 - something wait) but they are equal by the theorem without calculation.

## Proof

<!-- section: proof -->

Euclid proves the theorem (Prop. I.5, pons asinorum) by constructing the angle bisector from the apex, creating two congruent triangles by SAS, then corresponding parts give the base angles equal.

A modern short proof uses SAS on the two triangles formed by the angle bisector or altitude.

**ELI5**

- Draw the "middle line" from the top vertex straight down the middle of the base. This splits the triangle into two mirror-image right triangles that match by SAS or SSS.
- Because the two small triangles are congruent, their bottom angles must be equal.

**Worked Example:**

Let M be the midpoint of base BC in isosceles △ABC with AB=AC. Then AM is also the angle bisector and altitude. △ABM and △ACM have AB=AC (given), BM=CM (midpoint), AM common. By SSS the triangles are congruent, so ∠ABM = ∠ACM.

## Applications and Equilateral Case

<!-- section: applications-equilateral -->

When all three sides equal (equilateral), all angles are 60° (corollary of the theorem applied three times).

The theorem is used constantly in proofs involving symmetry and in constructing equilateral triangles.

**ELI5**

- An equilateral triangle is a special isosceles where every side is a "base," so every angle must be equal — and they turn out to be 60°.
- Many constructions and proofs "assume isosceles to get equal angles for free."

**Worked Example:**

Construct equilateral triangle on base BC: from B draw arc radius BC, from C draw arc radius CB; they intersect at A. Then AB=BC=CA by construction (radii), so all angles 60° by repeated application of the isosceles theorem.

## Common Mistakes

- **Applying only to the base angles**: The equal angles are the ones opposite the equal sides (the base angles if the equal sides are the legs).
- **Forgetting the converse**: Equal angles imply equal opposite sides; this is useful for proving sides equal.
- **Assuming the altitude is always needed**: The theorem holds without constructing anything; the proof may use construction but the statement does not.
- **Confusing with similarity**: Isosceles triangles are not necessarily similar to each other unless angles match.
