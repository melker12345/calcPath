# ASA congruence

ASA (Angle-Side-Angle) congruence: if two angles and the included side of one triangle equal the corresponding two angles and included side of another, the triangles are congruent.

Because the third angles are automatically equal (180° sum), AAS is also a valid criterion, but the classical ASA emphasizes the included side.

## The ASA Criterion

<!-- section: asa-criterion -->

If in △ABC and △DEF, ∠A = ∠D, AB = DE (the side between the angles at A and B? wait, standard: two angles and the side between them), then the triangles are congruent.

More precisely: two angles and the included side.

**ELI5**

- If you know two corners and the exact length of the side between them, the whole triangle is forced. The other sides and angles have to adjust to make the angles fit.
- The included side acts as the "base" that fixes the scale between the two angles.

**Worked Example:**

△ABC has ∠A=50°, side AB=6 (wait standard ASA: ∠A, side AC? Let's correct: suppose ∠BAC=50°, ∠ABC=60°, side AB (included between them? AB is between A and B). Yes. Then any other triangle with same two angles and included side AB=6 is congruent.

## Relation to AAS and Third Angle

<!-- section: third-angle -->

Since the sum of angles is 180°, if two angles are known the third is determined. Therefore giving two angles and a non-included side (AAS) also determines the triangle uniquely up to congruence.

**ELI5**

- Knowing two angles tells you the third for free. So you really only need two angles plus any side to fix the triangle (with proper correspondence).
- The side can be opposite one of the angles; the law of sines then fixes the scale.

**Worked Example:**

△ABC: ∠A=40°, ∠B=70° ⇒ ∠C=70°. Side BC (opposite A) = 5. Then the triangle is determined. Another triangle with same angles and corresponding side 5 is congruent by AAS (or ASA after identifying the included side once the third angle is computed).

## Proof

<!-- section: proof-asa -->

Euclid proves ASA as Prop. I.26 (along with AAS). The proof usually reduces to SAS or uses contradiction on the third side.

One common proof: assume the third sides differ, derive two different triangles on the same base with same vertex angles, leading to contradiction with exterior angle or isosceles properties.

**ELI5**

- Once the base and the two base angles are fixed, the arms of the angles must meet at a unique point (the third vertex).
- If they met at two different points you would have two different third angles or sides, which contradicts the angle sum.

**Worked Example:**

Fix base BC. At B construct the given angle, at C construct the given angle. The two rays intersect at exactly one point A (because the angles determine unique directions). Hence unique triangle.

## Common Mistakes

- **Confusing ASA with AAS**: Both work, but students must be able to identify the included side correctly for ASA.
- **Assuming AAA is congruence**: AAA gives similarity only; size is not fixed (you can scale the triangle and keep all angles).
- **Forgetting that the side must correspond correctly**: The side must lie between the two given angles in the correspondence.
- **Using ASA when only one angle is given with two sides**: That would be SSA or SAS depending on inclusion.
