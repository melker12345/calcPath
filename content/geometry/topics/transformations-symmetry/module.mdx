# Transformations & Symmetry

Isometries, translations, rotations, reflections, symmetry groups, and congruence transformations.

An isometry is a transformation of the plane (or space) that preserves distances between points. It is a 'rigid motion' — no stretching, shrinking, or bending.

The isometries of the plane are translations, rotations, reflections, and glide reflections.

## Definition and Preservation Properties

<!-- section: definition-preservation -->

An isometry f satisfies d(f(P), f(Q)) = d(P, Q) for all points P, Q.

Consequently isometries preserve lengths, angles (up to orientation for reflections), and congruence of figures.

**ELI5**

- Isometries are the ways you can move a shape without distorting it: slide, turn, flip.
- After the move, any two points are still exactly as far apart as before.

**Worked Example:**

Translation by vector (3,4): every point (x,y) maps to (x+3, y+4). Distance between images = distance between originals because the shift is the same for all.

## Classification of Plane Isometries

<!-- section: classification -->

- Translation: slide by a vector (no fixed points unless zero).
- Rotation: turn around a fixed point by an angle.
- Reflection: flip over a line (mirror).
- Glide reflection: reflect over a line then translate parallel to that line.

Orientation-preserving: translations and rotations. Orientation-reversing: reflections and glides.

**ELI5**

- Slide or spin = keeps 'handedness' (left and right stay left and right).
- Flip or glide-flip = reverses handedness (your left hand becomes a right-hand image).

**Worked Example:**

Rotation 90° counterclockwise around origin: (x,y) \to (-y, x). This preserves distances (rotation matrix is orthogonal).

## Isometries and Congruence

<!-- section: congruence-isom -->

Two figures are congruent iff there exists an isometry mapping one onto the other.

The triangle congruence criteria (SSS etc.) are shortcuts that guarantee such an isometry exists.

**ELI5**

- Congruent means you can rigidly move one to exactly cover the other.
- The criteria (SSS, SAS...) are the 'tests' that tell you without actually moving whether such a rigid motion exists.

**Worked Example:**

If two triangles satisfy SSS, then there is a unique isometry (actually several if reflections allowed) taking one to the other. The proof of SSS constructs or proves existence of that isometry.

## Common Mistakes

- **Including scaling or shear as isometries**: Those change distances; they are similarities or affine maps.
- **Forgetting glide reflections**: In the full classification of plane isometries, glides are the fourth type.
- **Assuming isometries always have fixed points**: Translations (non-zero) have none.
- **Confusing 'preserve distance' with 'preserve the figure'**: The transformation preserves distances between any points; the figure is mapped to a congruent copy.

A translation is an isometry that slides every point of the plane the same distance in the same direction.

It is determined by a vector: every point P maps to P + v.

## Definition and Properties

<!-- section: definition-props -->

Translation by vector v: T(P) = P + v.

Preserves orientation and all distances and angles. No fixed points unless v = 0.

The composition of two translations is a translation by the sum of vectors.

**ELI5**

- Imagine the whole plane is a sheet of paper; you slide it without rotating or flipping. Every dot moves exactly the same way.
- Nothing stays in place (unless the slide is zero); the whole figure just relocates.

**Worked Example:**

Translate by (3, -1): (0,0) \to (3,-1), (1,4) \to (4,3), the segment between them moves rigidly to the image segment.

## Vector and Coordinate Form

<!-- section: vector-coord -->

In coordinates: (x, y) \to (x + a, y + b) for vector <a, b>.

In vectors: T(P) = P + v.

The inverse is translation by -v.

**ELI5**

- Just add the same numbers to every x and every y.
- To undo, subtract the same numbers.

**Worked Example:**

Point (2,5) translated by < -1, 3 > becomes (1,8). To go back, add <1, -3>.

## Translations in Congruence and Symmetry

<!-- section: congruence-symmetry -->

Translations are used to prove SSS and other criteria by superposition.

In wallpaper groups and frieze patterns, translations generate periodic symmetry.

**ELI5**

- To show two triangles are congruent by SSS, you can slide one so that one side coincides with the corresponding side of the other; the rest follows.
- Repeating a translation creates an infinite row of identical copies (like a picket fence pattern).

**Worked Example:**

To map △ABC to △DEF with AB=DE etc by SSS, first translate so A goes to D and the direction of AB aligns with DE (possible by choosing the translation vector DE - AB or appropriate). Then the third vertex follows.

## Common Mistakes

- **Thinking translations have a 'center'**: Unlike rotations, translations have no fixed point (unless trivial).
- **Confusing with rotation or glide**: Translation has no turning and no flip.
- **Forgetting that composition of translations adds vectors**: T_v ∘ T_w = T_{v+w}.
- **Assuming the image of a line under translation is the same line**: The image is a parallel line (or the same if v parallel to the line and the line is invariant, but generally parallel).

A rotation is an isometry that turns the plane around a fixed point (the center) by a fixed angle.

It is orientation-preserving and has exactly one fixed point (the center) unless the angle is a multiple of 360° (identity).

## Definition and Coordinate Formula

<!-- section: definition-formula-rot -->

Rotation by angle \theta counterclockwise around origin: (x, y) \to (x cos \theta - y sin \theta, x sin \theta + y cos \theta).

Around arbitrary center (h,k): translate so center to origin, rotate, translate back.

**ELI5**

- Pick a center pin; spin the plane rigidly around that pin by the angle.
- Every point moves along a circle centered at the pin; the angle is the same for all.

**Worked Example:**

90° counterclockwise around (0,0): (1,0) \to (0,1), (0,1) \to (-1,0), etc. The matrix [[0,-1],[1,0]].

## Angle, Direction, and Order

<!-- section: angle-dir-order -->

Positive angles usually counterclockwise. Rotation by \theta + 360° k is the same as by \theta.

Composition of rotations around the same center adds the angles.

Rotation by 180° is central symmetry.

**ELI5**

- Counterclockwise is the 'standard' positive direction (math convention).
- Spinning 370° is the same as spinning 10° the same way.

**Worked Example:**

Rotate 180° around (2,3): (x,y) maps to (4 - x, 6 - y). Every point and its image are symmetric with respect to (2,3); the segment joining them has midpoint (2,3).

## Rotations and Symmetry

<!-- section: symmetry-rot -->

The rotational symmetries of a regular n-gon are rotations by k*360°/n around its center, k=0..n-1.

In the plane, the only finite-order rotations that can be symmetries of a bounded figure are the ones listed in the crystallographic restriction (order 1,2,3,4,6).

**ELI5**

- A square looks the same after 90°, 180°, 270°, 360° spins around center.
- You cannot have a wallpaper pattern with 5-fold rotational symmetry that repeats in a lattice (crystallographic restriction).

**Worked Example:**

Equilateral triangle has rotational symmetry of order 3: 120° and 240° around centroid, plus identity.

## Common Mistakes

- **Using degrees vs radians inconsistently in formulas**: cos and sin in math formulas expect radians in most software; geometry texts often use degrees.
- **Forgetting the center when composing rotations around different points**: Composition of rotations around different centers is generally a translation or another rotation around a third point.
- **Thinking 360° rotation moves points**: It is the identity; every point returns to itself.
- **Confusing rotation with reflection**: Rotation preserves orientation; reflection reverses it.

A reflection is an isometry that flips the plane over a fixed line (the mirror line).

It is orientation-reversing and every point on the mirror line is fixed.

## Definition and Coordinate Formulas

<!-- section: definition-formulas-refl -->

Reflection over the x-axis: (x,y) \to (x, -y).

Over y-axis: (x,y) \to (-x, y).

Over the line y = x: (x,y) \to (y, x).

Over arbitrary line: translate/rotate so the line becomes the x-axis, reflect, undo the rigid motion.

**ELI5**

- The mirror line is the 'fold line'; each point and its image are symmetric with respect to the line (the line is the perpendicular bisector of the segment joining a point to its image).
- Flipping reverses left and right (or clockwise and counterclockwise).

**Worked Example:**

Reflect (3,4) over x-axis: (3,-4). Over y=x: (4,3). Over the line y=2x (more work): first rotate so the line is horizontal, etc.

## Properties

<!-- section: properties-refl -->

Reflection is its own inverse: doing it twice returns to the original.

The composition of two reflections over lines intersecting at angle \theta is a rotation by 2\theta around the intersection point.

Composition of reflections over parallel lines is a translation by twice the distance between them.

**ELI5**

- Flip twice over the same mirror and you are back (like closing a book and opening it again).
- Two mirrors at an angle create a 'double flip' that is a spin by twice the angle (like in a corner mirror you see yourself rotated).

**Worked Example:**

Reflect over x-axis then over y-axis: first (x,y)\to(x,-y), then (x,-y)\to(-x,-y). This is 180° rotation around origin (or central inversion).

## Reflection and Symmetry; Generating Groups

<!-- section: symmetry-groups-refl -->

Reflection symmetries of a figure: the figure looks the same after the flip.

The dihedral group D_n is generated by reflections (or by rotation + one reflection).

**ELI5**

- A rectangle has two reflection symmetries (over its long and short midlines) plus 180° rotation.
- An isosceles triangle has one reflection symmetry (over the altitude to the base).

**Worked Example:**

Regular hexagon has 6 reflection lines (through opposite vertices and through midpoints of opposite sides). Together with rotations they generate the full D6 of order 12.

## Common Mistakes

- **Assuming reflection always reverses the figure**: Only if the figure is not symmetric across that line.
- **Confusing the mirror line with the perpendicular bisector in proofs**: The mirror is the perp bisector of segments from points to images.
- **Forgetting that composition of two reflections is a rotation or translation**: This is the key way to generate all isometries from reflections.
- **Thinking reflection is the same as 'taking absolute value' in coordinates**: That only works for the coordinate axes.

The symmetry group of a geometric object is the set of all isometries that map the object onto itself.

It forms a group under composition: closed, associative, identity, and inverses.

## Definition and Group Structure

<!-- section: definition-group -->

A symmetry of a set S is an isometry f such that f(S) = S.

The set of all such f forms the symmetry group Sym(S), with operation composition.

**ELI5**

- The symmetries are all the ways you can move or flip the object so it looks exactly the same afterward.
- They form a 'club' with rules: doing two symmetries in a row is again a symmetry, there is a 'do nothing' symmetry, and every move has an 'undo' move.

**Worked Example:**

For an equilateral triangle, the symmetries are rotations by 0°,120°,240° and three reflections. There are 6 = 3! = 2*3 elements (D3).

## Finite and Infinite Symmetry Groups

<!-- section: finite-infinite -->

Finite: dihedral groups D_n for regular n-gons, cyclic groups for rotational only.

Infinite discrete: frieze groups (strip patterns), wallpaper groups (plane tilings).

Continuous: the full isometry group of the plane, or the orthogonal group O(2) fixing the origin.

**ELI5**

- A square has only 8 symmetries (finite).
- A repeating brick wall pattern has infinitely many symmetries (you can slide by multiples of the brick spacing).
- A perfect blank plane has every possible rigid motion as a symmetry (continuous infinity).

**Worked Example:**

The symmetry group of the integer lattice ℤ^{2} is the wallpaper group p4m, which includes translations by integer vectors, 90° rotations around lattice points, and reflections over axes and diagonals.

## Generators and Relations

<!-- section: generators-relations -->

Many symmetry groups can be presented by a small set of generators (e.g., a rotation and a reflection) satisfying a few relations (r^n = 1, s^{2} = 1, (r s)^{2} = 1 for dihedral).

**ELI5**

- You can describe the whole club by saying 'here are the basic moves and the rules for how they combine.'
- For the square: r = 90° rotation, s = one reflection; then r^4 = 1, s^{2} = 1, s r s^{-1} = r^{-1}.

**Worked Example:**

D4 (square symmetries) has presentation <r, s | r^4 = s^{2} = 1, s r s = r^{-1}>. All 8 elements can be written as words in r and s.

## Common Mistakes

- **Including transformations that move the object to a different-looking position**: Only those that make it coincide with itself count.
- **Forgetting that the full group includes all compositions**: Sometimes only the 'obvious' reflections are listed; the group is larger.
- **Confusing the symmetry group of the figure with the isometry group of the ambient space**: The ambient has all isometries; the figure only those that preserve it.
- **Assuming all symmetry groups are finite**: Most interesting patterns in the plane have infinite symmetry groups.

A congruence transformation (or congruence mapping) is an isometry of the plane: a transformation that maps any figure to a congruent copy.

The four types (translation, rotation, reflection, glide reflection) generate all congruences.

## Congruence Transformations as Isometries

<!-- section: cong-as-isom -->

By definition, a transformation is a congruence if it preserves distances (is an isometry).

The triangle congruence criteria guarantee that certain correspondences can be realized by a congruence transformation.

**ELI5**

- A congruence transformation is any 'rigid motion' (including flips) that moves a shape onto a perfect copy of itself (same size and shape).
- The tests like SSS tell you 'yes, there exists a rigid motion that makes them coincide' without having to exhibit the motion.

**Worked Example:**

Given two segments of equal length, there is a translation or rotation (or reflection) that maps one onto the other. This is the 'SAS' or 'SSS' for segments (just translation along the vector or rotation around one endpoint).

## Using Congruence Transformations in Proofs

<!-- section: proofs-cong -->

Many proofs proceed by assuming a correspondence of parts, constructing the congruence transformation that realizes it (or proving it exists), then showing other corresponding parts must coincide.

This is the modern rigorous version of Euclid's superposition method.

**ELI5**

- Instead of 'laying one triangle on the other,' we say 'there exists an isometry doing the laying,' and then use the preservation properties.
- It avoids the philosophical worry about physically moving figures.

**Worked Example:**

Proof of SSS: given AB=DE, BC=EF, CA=FD. Translate so A maps to D and AB direction aligns with DE (possible). Then B maps to E. The two possible positions for C (intersection of circles) ; because distances match, the image of C must land on F (the only point at the right distances from D and E). Hence the isometry maps the triangle to the other.

## Direct vs Opposite Congruences

<!-- section: direct-opposite -->

Direct (orientation-preserving) congruences: translations and rotations.

Opposite (orientation-reversing): reflections and glide reflections.

In some contexts (e.g., when figures are 'one-sided' or chiral), only direct congruences are allowed.

**ELI5**

- Direct: you can slide or spin the shape into place without flipping it over.
- Opposite: you need to flip it (like turning a left glove into a right glove by turning it inside out in 3D, but in 2D the flip is in the plane).

**Worked Example:**

Two triangles that are mirror images (enantiomorphs) are congruent via an opposite isometry but not via a direct one if they have 'handedness' (e.g., labeled clockwise vs counterclockwise vertices).

## Common Mistakes

- **Including similarities that change size**: Congruence requires size-preserving (isometry), not just shape-preserving.
- **Forgetting glide reflections**: They are proper congruences (distance-preserving) even though they have no fixed points.
- **Assuming every pair of congruent figures can be superimposed without flip**: Depends on whether opposite congruences are allowed in the context.
- **Confusing the transformation with the correspondence**: The transformation realizes a particular vertex matching.
