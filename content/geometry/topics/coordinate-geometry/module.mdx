# Coordinate Geometry

The coordinate plane, distance and midpoint, slope, lines, circles, parabolas, and ellipses.

The Cartesian coordinate system assigns to each point in the plane an ordered pair of real numbers (x, y) relative to two perpendicular axes intersecting at the origin.

It allows geometry to be translated into algebra and is the foundation of analytic geometry, calculus, and computer graphics.

## Axes, Origin, and Coordinates

<!-- section: axes-origin -->

Two perpendicular lines (x-axis horizontal, y-axis vertical) cross at the origin (0,0). The x-coordinate is the signed distance to the y-axis; y-coordinate to the x-axis.

Quadrants: I (+,+), II (−,+), III (−,−), IV (+,−).

**ELI5**

- The grid is like a city map with numbered streets (x) and avenues (y). The origin is the downtown crossing.
- Positive x is right, positive y is up; signs tell you which "neighborhood" (quadrant) the point is in.

**Worked Example:**

Point (3,−4) is in quadrant IV: 3 units right of y-axis, 4 units below x-axis. Point (−2,5) is in quadrant II.

## Distance, Midpoint, and Slope from Coordinates

<!-- section: distance-mid-slope -->

Distance between (x1,y1) and (x2,y2): \sqrt{(x2-x1)^2 + (y2-y1)^2} (Pythagorean).

Midpoint: ((x1+x2)/2, (y1+y2)/2).

Slope m = (y2-y1)/(x2-x1) (for x2 \neq x1).

**ELI5**

- Distance is the straight-line "as the crow flies" using the grid legs as a right triangle.
- Midpoint is the average address. Slope is how many y-units you rise per x-unit you run (steepness).

**Worked Example:**

Points (1,2) and (4,6). Distance \sqrt{3^2 + 4^2} = 5. Midpoint (2.5, 4). Slope = (6-2)/(4-1) = 4/3.

## Lines and Circles in Coordinates

<!-- section: lines-circles -->

Line: y = mx + b or ax + by + c = 0.

Circle: (x-h)^2 + (y-k)^2 = r^2 (center (h,k), radius r).

These equations turn geometric questions into algebraic equations that can be solved.

**ELI5**

- An equation is a rule that every point on the figure obeys. Plug in the coordinates; if true, the point is on the figure.
- Solving two equations finds intersection points (where two figures cross).

**Worked Example:**

Line y = 2x + 1 and circle x^2 + y^2 = 5. Substitute: x^2 + (2x+1)^2 = 5 \to 5x^2 + 4x -4 =0. Solve quadratic for intersection x-values, then y.

## Common Mistakes

- **Swapping x and y**: (3,4) is different from (4,3); x is always first (horizontal).
- **Forgetting signs when plotting**: Negative coordinates go left or down.
- **Assuming slope is always defined**: Vertical lines have undefined slope (x = constant).
- **Mixing distance formula with midpoint formula**: One has square root and squares; the other is just averages.

The distance formula gives the Euclidean distance between two points in the plane: d = \sqrt{(x_2 - x_1)^2 + (y_2 - y_1)^2}.

It is a direct application of the Pythagorean theorem to the differences in coordinates.

## Derivation

<!-- section: derivation -->

Given points P1(x1,y1), P2(x2,y2), form the right triangle with legs |x2-x1| (horizontal) and |y2-y1| (vertical). By Pythagorean, hypotenuse (straight-line distance) is the square root of sum of squares of legs.

**ELI5**

- Draw a horizontal and vertical line between the two points; they form the legs of a right triangle. The straight line between the points is the hypotenuse.
- Square, add, square-root — the same 3-4-5 idea for any numbers.

**Worked Example:**

(0,0) to (3,4): legs 3 and 4, d = \sqrt{9+16} = 5.

(1,2) to (4,6): \Delta x=3, \Delta y=4, same 5.

## In 3D and Higher

<!-- section: higher-d -->

In 3D: \sqrt{(x2-x1)^2 + (y2-y1)^2 + (z2-z1)^2}.

The formula generalizes to any dimension: the Euclidean norm of the difference vector.

**ELI5**

- In 3D you just add the squared difference in the third direction (up/down) before taking square root.
- It is the "ordinary" straight-line distance we experience in our space.

**Worked Example:**

(0,0,0) to (1,2,2): d = \sqrt{1+4+4} = 3.

## Applications

<!-- section: applications-dist -->

Used everywhere: length of vectors, collision detection, GPS (great-circle is different but Euclidean is local approximation), computer graphics, physics (displacement magnitude).

**ELI5**

- Any time you want "how far apart are these two spots on the map or in the game world," you use the distance formula (or its 3D version).
- It tells you the length of the arrow from one point to the other.

**Worked Example:**

Two game characters at (10,20) and (13,24). Distance = \sqrt{3^2 + 4^2} = 5 units. If attack range is 4, they are just out of range.

## Common Mistakes

- **Forgetting the square root**: The sum of squares is not the distance; you must take the root to get length.
- **Mixing up which is x and y**: Order inside the deltas does not matter (squared), but you must subtract corresponding coordinates.
- **Using Manhattan distance (|dx| + |dy|) by mistake**: That is grid-path distance, not straight-line.
- **Applying in non-Euclidean spaces without adjustment**: On a sphere or in taxicab geometry the formula is different.

The midpoint of a line segment is the point that divides the segment into two equal parts. It is the average of the endpoints.

In coordinate geometry the midpoint of (x1,y1) and (x2,y2) is ((x1+x2)/2, (y1+y2)/2).

## Definition and Coordinate Formula

<!-- section: definition-formula -->

The midpoint M of AB is the unique point on AB such that AM = MB.

In vectors, M = (A + B)/2.

In coordinates, average the corresponding components.

**ELI5**

- The midpoint is the exact middle of the segment; if you fold the segment in half the crease is at the midpoint.
- On a map, the midpoint is the average address of the two ends.

**Worked Example:**

Endpoints (2,3) and (8,11). Midpoint x = (2+8)/2 = 5, y = (3+11)/2 = 7. So (5,7). Distance from each end is the same.

## Properties and Uses

<!-- section: properties-uses -->

The midpoint is the center of symmetry of the segment. The line segment joining midpoints of two sides of a triangle is parallel to the third and half as long (midline theorem).

Midpoints are used in the definition of medians, in vector geometry, and in computer graphics for subdivision.

**ELI5**

- Connecting the midpoints of a triangle gives a smaller triangle similar and parallel-sided (Varignon's for quads too).
- It is the balance point if the segment had uniform mass.

**Worked Example:**

In △ABC the segment joining midpoints of AB and AC is parallel to BC and half length (midline or midpoint theorem). This is a special case of similar triangles or vector average.

## Common Mistakes

- **Using the distance formula instead of averaging**: Midpoint is not the distance; it is the coordinate average.
- **Forgetting to average both coordinates**: You must do x and y separately.
- **Thinking the midpoint is always the origin**: Only if the segment is centered at 0.
- **Confusing with median**: The median is the segment from vertex to midpoint of opposite side; the midpoint is the end point of the median on the side.

The slope of a line is the ratio of the vertical change (rise) to the horizontal change (run) between any two points on the line: m = \Delta y / \Delta x.

It measures the steepness and direction of the line.

## Definition and Interpretation

<!-- section: definition-interp -->

For non-vertical lines, m = (y2 - y1)/(x2 - x1).

Positive slope: line rises left to right. Negative: falls. Zero: horizontal. Undefined: vertical.

The slope is constant for any pair of points on a straight line.

**ELI5**

- Slope is "how many units up or down you go for each unit you go right."
- Positive like climbing a hill to the right; negative like going down; zero flat; undefined a straight up wall.

**Worked Example:**

Points (1,2) and (3,8). \Delta x = 2, \Delta y = 6, m = 3. For every 1 right, the line goes 3 up.

## Slope-Intercept and Point-Slope Forms

<!-- section: forms -->

Line: y = m x + b (slope m, y-intercept b).

Point-slope: y - y0 = m (x - x0).

These make it easy to write the equation from slope and a point or intercept.

**ELI5**

- y = m x + b is the "rule": start at b on the y-axis, then for each x step, add m times that step to y.
- Point-slope says "from this known point, the change in y is m times change in x."

**Worked Example:**

Slope 2, y-intercept 1: y = 2x + 1. At x=0, y=1; at x=3, y=7. Passes through (0,1) and (3,7).

## Parallel and Perpendicular Slopes

<!-- section: parallel-perp-slopes -->

Parallel lines have equal slopes.

Perpendicular lines have slopes whose product is −1 (m1 * m2 = −1), provided neither is vertical.

**ELI5**

- Same slope = same steepness, never meet (parallel).
- Negative reciprocal slopes = "opposite steepness" that make perfect 90° corners.

**Worked Example:**

Line y = (1/3)x + 2 has slope 1/3. Parallel: y = (1/3)x - 4. Perpendicular: y = -3x + 5 (product 1/3 * -3 = -1).

## Common Mistakes

- **Division by zero for vertical**: Vertical lines have undefined slope; do not say "infinite slope" in calculations.
- **Reversing rise and run**: Slope is rise/run (y over x), not run/rise.
- **Assuming slope 0 means no line**: Horizontal line has slope 0.
- **Forgetting that slope is the same everywhere on the line**: Unlike curves, straight lines have constant slope.

The equation of a line is an algebraic relation satisfied by exactly the points on that line.

Common forms: slope-intercept y = mx + b, point-slope y - y0 = m(x - x0), general ax + by + c = 0, and two-point form.

## Forms and Conversions

<!-- section: forms-conversion -->

From two points, compute slope then use point-slope or two-point form (y - y1)/(y2 - y1) = (x - x1)/(x2 - x1).

Vertical: x = k. Horizontal: y = k.

**ELI5**

- The equation is the "membership test": plug in (x,y); if it makes the equation true, the point is on the line.
- Different forms are convenient for different information you are given (slope + intercept, point + slope, two points).

**Worked Example:**

Points (1,3), (4,9). Slope = 2. Point-slope with (1,3): y-3 = 2(x-1) \to y = 2x +1. Check (4,9): 8+1=9 yes.

## Intercepts and Graphing

<!-- section: intercepts-graph -->

x-intercept: set y=0, solve for x.

y-intercept: set x=0, solve for y.

To graph from general form, find intercepts or solve for y.

**ELI5**

- x-intercept is where the line crosses the horizontal axis (y=0 "no height").
- y-intercept is where it crosses the vertical axis (x=0 "no run yet").

**Worked Example:**

2x + 3y = 6. x-intercept: y=0, 2x=6, x=3. y-intercept: x=0, 3y=6, y=2. Plot (3,0) and (0,2), connect.

## Parallel, Perpendicular, and Distance

<!-- section: parallel-perp-dist -->

Parallel: same slope (or both vertical).

Perpendicular: product of slopes -1.

Distance from point (x0,y0) to line ax+by+c=0 is |ax0 + by0 + c| / \sqrt(a^{2} + b^{2}).

**ELI5**

- Parallel lines have identical "tilt."
- Perpendicular lines have "opposite tilts" whose product is -1.
- The distance formula is the length of the perpendicular from the point to the line.

**Worked Example:**

Line 3x + 4y - 12 = 0. Distance from (0,0): | -12 | / 5 = 12/5 = 2.4.

## Common Mistakes

- **Writing the general form with =0 but forgetting the constant term sign**: ax + by + c = 0, c can be positive or negative.
- **Assuming all lines have y = mx + b form**: Vertical lines do not.
- **Reversing the distance formula numerator/denominator**: The denominator normalizes by the length of the normal vector.
- **Forgetting to check if a point satisfies the equation when verifying**: Always plug back in.

The equation of a circle with center (h, k) and radius r is (x - h)^{2} + (y - k)^{2} = r^{2}.

Every point (x, y) on the circle satisfies this; it is the set of points at exact distance r from the center.

## Standard and General Form

<!-- section: standard-general -->

Standard: (x - h)^{2} + (y - k)^{2} = r^{2}.

General: x^{2} + y^{2} + D x + E y + F = 0 (after expanding and completing the square to find center and radius).

**ELI5**

- The standard form says "distance to (h,k) is exactly r" in squared form (no square root needed).
- The general form is the expanded polynomial; completing the square recovers the center and radius.

**Worked Example:**

(x-2)^{2} + (y+1)^{2} = 9 : center (2,-1), radius 3.

x^{2} + y^{2} - 4x + 2y - 4 = 0 : complete square (x-2)^{2} + (y+1)^{2} = 9, same circle.

## Finding Center and Radius from Equation

<!-- section: find-center-radius -->

Given general form, group x and y terms, complete the square for x and for y, read h = -D/2, k = -E/2, r = \sqrt(h^{2} + k^{2} - F).

**ELI5**

- Completing the square is 'fixing' the equation so it looks like distance squared.
- The numbers that get added/subtracted when completing tell you how far the center is shifted from (0,0).

**Worked Example:**

x^{2} + 6x + y^{2} - 4y = 12. (x+3)^{2} -9 + (y-2)^{2} -4 = 12. (x+3) ^{2} + (y-2)^{2} = 25. Center (-3,2), r=5.

## Intersection with Lines and Other Circles

<!-- section: intersections -->

Substitute line into circle equation to get quadratic; discriminant tells number of intersections (0,1,2).

Two circles: subtract equations to get radical axis (line), then solve.

**ELI5**

- A line can miss, touch (tangent), or cut the circle at two points — the quadratic tells which.
- The points in common satisfy both equations simultaneously.

**Worked Example:**

Circle x^{2} + y ^{2} = 25, line y = x. x^{2} + x^{2} = 25, 2x^{2}=25, x = \pm\sqrt(12.5), two intersections.

## Common Mistakes

- **Forgetting to complete the square correctly (sign errors on h,k)**: h = -D/2, easy to drop the minus.
- **Using the equation for a sphere by mistake**: Sphere has three variables.
- **Assuming the general form always represents a circle**: If the radius squared comes out negative, it is an imaginary circle (no real points).
- **Confusing radius with diameter in the equation**: The r in the equation is always the radius.

A parabola is the set of points in the plane equidistant from a fixed point (focus) and a fixed line (directrix).

It is one of the conic sections and the graph of a quadratic function y = a x^{2} + b x + c.

## Definition and Standard Forms

<!-- section: definition-forms -->

Standard vertical: (x - h)^{2} = 4p (y - k), focus (h, k+p), directrix y = k-p.

Standard horizontal: (y - k)^{2} = 4p (x - h).

Vertex at (h,k); p is the focal length.

**ELI5**

- Every point on the parabola is exactly as far from the focus "magnet" as from the directrix "wall."
- The vertex is the "bottom" (or side) where it turns around.

**Worked Example:**

y = (1/4) x^{2} : 4p = 1, p=1/4. Focus (0,1/4), directrix y=-1/4. Point (0,0) on parabola: distance to focus 1/4, to directrix 1/4.

## Reflective Property and Applications

<!-- section: reflective-apps -->

Rays parallel to the axis reflect off the parabola and pass through the focus (and vice versa).

Used in satellite dishes, headlights, telescopes, and the paths of projectiles (under constant gravity, ignoring air resistance).

**ELI5**

- A parabolic mirror sends all parallel incoming rays (from a distant star or satellite) to one point (the focus), where you put your receiver.
- Headlights do the reverse: light at the focus bounces into a parallel beam.

**Worked Example:**

Satellite dish shaped as paraboloid of revolution. Signal from satellite (essentially parallel rays) hits the dish and reflects to the feed horn at the focus. All paths have equal length, signals arrive in phase.

## Vertex Form and Completing the Square

<!-- section: vertex-form -->

y = a (x - h)^{2} + k is vertex form. a determines width and direction (positive up).

Completing the square on y = a x ^{2} + b x + c converts to vertex form.

**ELI5**

- Vertex form shows the 'turning point' (h,k) and how 'wide' (a) the parabola opens directly.
- Completing the square is the algebra trick that reveals the vertex without graphing.

**Worked Example:**

y = 2x^{2} + 8x + 5 = 2(x+2) ^{2} - 3. Vertex (-2, -3), opens upward, narrower than y=x^{2}.

## Common Mistakes

- **Confusing focus/directrix with vertex**: The vertex is midway between focus and directrix.
- **Mixing vertical and horizontal parabolas**: y = x^{2} is vertical (opens up/down); x = y^{2} is horizontal.
- **Forgetting the 4p in the standard form**: The coefficient is 4p, not p.
- **Thinking all quadratic graphs are parabolas in the geometric sense**: The geometric definition (focus/directrix) is equivalent to the algebraic quadratic only after rotation/translation in the plane.

An ellipse is the set of points whose sum of distances to two fixed points (foci) is constant.

It is a conic section (stretched circle) and the shape of planetary orbits (Kepler's first law).

## Definition and Standard Equation

<!-- section: definition-standard -->

For foci at (-c,0) and (c,0), major axis 2a, the equation is x ^{2}/a^{2} + y^{2}/b ^{2} = 1 where b^{2} = a^{2} - c^{2}, 0 < c < a.

The constant sum is 2a.

**ELI5**

- Two pins (foci) and a string of fixed length; the pencil traces the ellipse as it keeps the string taut.
- The sum of distances to the two pins is always the same (the string length).

**Worked Example:**

Foci at (-3,0), (3,0), sum 10 (2a=10, a=5). Then c=3, b^{2}=25-9=16, b=4. Equation x^{2}/25 + y^{2}/16 = 1. Point (0,4): distance to each focus \sqrt(3^{2}+4 ^{2})=5, sum 10.

## Vertices, Co-vertices, Eccentricity

<!-- section: vertices-ecc -->

Vertices (\pm a,0), co-vertices (0,\pm b). Eccentricity e = c/a < 1 (e=0 is circle).

The major axis is the longer one; minor the shorter.

**ELI5**

- Vertices are the 'ends' of the long direction; co-vertices the 'ends' of the short direction.
- Eccentricity tells how 'squashed' the ellipse is: 0 perfect circle, close to 1 very flat.

**Worked Example:**

For the ellipse above, a=5, b=4, c=3, e=3/5=0.6. Fairly round but visibly elongated.

## Reflective Property and Orbits

<!-- section: reflective-orbits -->

Rays from one focus reflect off the ellipse and go to the other focus.

Planets orbit in ellipses with the sun at one focus (Kepler). The other focus is 'empty.'

**ELI5**

- Sound or light from one focus bounces and converges at the other focus (whispering galleries).
- Gravity from the sun at one focus keeps the planet on the ellipse; the math of inverse-square force produces conics.

**Worked Example:**

In an elliptical billiard table, a ball shot from one focus in any direction will pass through the other focus after one bounce.

## Common Mistakes

- **Confusing sum (ellipse) with difference (hyperbola)**: Ellipse: sum constant. Hyperbola: |difference| constant.
- **Swapping a and b**: a is always the semi-major (larger), b semi-minor; c^{2} = a^{2} - b ^{2}.
- **Thinking the center is at a focus**: The center is midway between the foci; foci are offset by c from center.
- **Forgetting e < 1**: Eccentricity of ellipse is between 0 and 1; =1 parabola, >1 hyperbola.
