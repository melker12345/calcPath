# Line (geometry)

A line is a straight one-dimensional figure that extends infinitely in both directions. It is determined by any two distinct points and contains infinitely many points.

Lines are the simplest curves in geometry. In Euclidean geometry they are the shortest paths between points (geodesics). In coordinate geometry a line is the solution set of a linear equation.

## Definition and Postulates

<!-- section: definition-postulates -->

Euclid defines a line as "breadthless length." Postulate 1 says a straight line can be drawn between any two points; Postulate 2 says it can be extended indefinitely.

A line has no thickness and no endpoints. A **line segment** is the part of a line between two points (has endpoints). A **ray** starts at one point and extends infinitely in one direction.

**ELI5**

- Think of a laser beam that goes on forever in both directions and is perfectly straight and infinitely thin.
- A segment is a piece of string with two ends; a ray is a flashlight beam starting at your hand and going forever.

**Worked Example:**

Given points A and B, the line through them is unique. The segment AB is the set of points between A and B inclusive. The ray starting at A through B contains A, B, and all points on the side of B away from A.

## Properties of Lines

<!-- section: properties -->

Key properties:
- Any two distinct points determine exactly one line.
- Two distinct lines intersect in at most one point (unless coincident).
- A line divides the plane into two half-planes.
- Lines are infinite; their length is not a number in the usual sense.

In Euclidean geometry, lines are straight; curvature belongs to other curves.

**ELI5**

- Two different straight highways can cross at most once. If they cross twice they must be the same highway.
- A line has no "ends" so you can never walk off the edge of it.

**Worked Example:**

Suppose line L1 contains points (0,0) and (1,1). Its equation is y = x. Any other point on L1 satisfies the same equation. A second line through (0,0) with different slope, say y = 2x, intersects L1 only at the origin. This illustrates the "at most one intersection" property.

## Lines in Coordinate Geometry

<!-- section: coordinate-geometry -->

In the Cartesian plane a non-vertical line has equation y = mx + b (slope-intercept) or ax + by + c = 0 (general form). The slope m = (y2 − y1)/(x2 − x1) for any two points on the line.

Vertical lines have undefined slope and equation x = k.

**ELI5**

- The equation is a rule: every point on the line obeys the rule, and every point obeying the rule lies on the line.
- Slope tells you how fast y changes when x changes by 1 — like the steepness of a ramp.

**Worked Example:**

The line through (1,3) and (4,9) has slope (9-3)/(4-1) = 6/3 = 2. Using point-slope form with (1,3): y − 3 = 2(x − 1) ⇒ y = 2x + 1. Checking the second point: 2*4 + 1 = 9, yes.

## Parallel and Perpendicular Lines

<!-- section: parallel-perp -->

Two lines are parallel if they have the same slope (or both vertical) and never intersect. They are perpendicular if the product of slopes is −1 (when defined).

Euclid's parallel postulate guarantees that given a line and a point not on it there is exactly one parallel through the point.

**ELI5**

- Parallel lines are like railroad tracks: they stay the same distance apart forever and never meet.
- Perpendicular lines form perfect corners (90°).

**Worked Example:**

Line y = 2x + 1 has slope 2. A parallel line through (0,0) is y = 2x. A perpendicular line through (0,0) has slope −1/2, so y = (−1/2)x. Their intersection is only at origin; the angles at crossing are 90° because 2 * (−1/2) = −1.

## Common Mistakes

- **Drawing finite segments and calling them lines**: A segment has ends; a true line does not. Always extend your mental picture to infinity.
- **Assuming all lines have equations with numbers**: In pure geometry we talk about lines without coordinates. Algebra is a convenient tool, not the definition.
- **Confusing 'parallel' with 'never crossing in the drawing'**: In Euclidean geometry parallels never meet anywhere; in drawings they may appear to converge due to perspective (projective geometry).
- **Vertical lines have 'infinite slope' not 'no slope'**: They simply do not fit the y = mx + b form; their direction is vertical.
