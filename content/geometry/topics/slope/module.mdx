# Slope

The slope of a line is the ratio of the vertical change (rise) to the horizontal change (run) between any two points on the line: m = \Delta y / \Delta x.

It measures the steepness and direction of the line.

## Definition and Interpretation

<!-- section: definition-interp -->

For non-vertical lines, m = (y2 - y1)/(x2 - x1).

Positive slope: line rises left to right. Negative: falls. Zero: horizontal. Undefined: vertical.

The slope is constant for any pair of points on a straight line.

**ELI5**

- Slope is "how many units up or down you go for each unit you go right."
- Positive like climbing a hill to the right; negative like going down; zero flat; undefined a straight up wall.

**Worked Example:**

Points (1,2) and (3,8). \Delta x = 2, \Delta y = 6, m = 3. For every 1 right, the line goes 3 up.

## Slope-Intercept and Point-Slope Forms

<!-- section: forms -->

Line: y = m x + b (slope m, y-intercept b).

Point-slope: y - y0 = m (x - x0).

These make it easy to write the equation from slope and a point or intercept.

**ELI5**

- y = m x + b is the "rule": start at b on the y-axis, then for each x step, add m times that step to y.
- Point-slope says "from this known point, the change in y is m times change in x."

**Worked Example:**

Slope 2, y-intercept 1: y = 2x + 1. At x=0, y=1; at x=3, y=7. Passes through (0,1) and (3,7).

## Parallel and Perpendicular Slopes

<!-- section: parallel-perp-slopes -->

Parallel lines have equal slopes.

Perpendicular lines have slopes whose product is −1 (m1 * m2 = −1), provided neither is vertical.

**ELI5**

- Same slope = same steepness, never meet (parallel).
- Negative reciprocal slopes = "opposite steepness" that make perfect 90° corners.

**Worked Example:**

Line y = (1/3)x + 2 has slope 1/3. Parallel: y = (1/3)x - 4. Perpendicular: y = -3x + 5 (product 1/3 * -3 = -1).

## Common Mistakes

- **Division by zero for vertical**: Vertical lines have undefined slope; do not say "infinite slope" in calculations.
- **Reversing rise and run**: Slope is rise/run (y over x), not run/rise.
- **Assuming slope 0 means no line**: Horizontal line has slope 0.
- **Forgetting that slope is the same everywhere on the line**: Unlike curves, straight lines have constant slope.
