# Equation of a circle

The equation of a circle with center (h, k) and radius r is (x - h)^{2} + (y - k)^{2} = r^{2}.

Every point (x, y) on the circle satisfies this; it is the set of points at exact distance r from the center.

## Standard and General Form

<!-- section: standard-general -->

Standard: (x - h)^{2} + (y - k)^{2} = r^{2}.

General: x^{2} + y^{2} + D x + E y + F = 0 (after expanding and completing the square to find center and radius).

**ELI5**

- The standard form says "distance to (h,k) is exactly r" in squared form (no square root needed).
- The general form is the expanded polynomial; completing the square recovers the center and radius.

**Worked Example:**

(x-2)^{2} + (y+1)^{2} = 9 : center (2,-1), radius 3.

x^{2} + y^{2} - 4x + 2y - 4 = 0 : complete square (x-2)^{2} + (y+1)^{2} = 9, same circle.

## Finding Center and Radius from Equation

<!-- section: find-center-radius -->

Given general form, group x and y terms, complete the square for x and for y, read h = -D/2, k = -E/2, r = \sqrt(h^{2} + k^{2} - F).

**ELI5**

- Completing the square is 'fixing' the equation so it looks like distance squared.
- The numbers that get added/subtracted when completing tell you how far the center is shifted from (0,0).

**Worked Example:**

x^{2} + 6x + y^{2} - 4y = 12. (x+3)^{2} -9 + (y-2)^{2} -4 = 12. (x+3) ^{2} + (y-2)^{2} = 25. Center (-3,2), r=5.

## Intersection with Lines and Other Circles

<!-- section: intersections -->

Substitute line into circle equation to get quadratic; discriminant tells number of intersections (0,1,2).

Two circles: subtract equations to get radical axis (line), then solve.

**ELI5**

- A line can miss, touch (tangent), or cut the circle at two points — the quadratic tells which.
- The points in common satisfy both equations simultaneously.

**Worked Example:**

Circle x^{2} + y ^{2} = 25, line y = x. x^{2} + x^{2} = 25, 2x^{2}=25, x = \pm\sqrt(12.5), two intersections.

## Common Mistakes

- **Forgetting to complete the square correctly (sign errors on h,k)**: h = -D/2, easy to drop the minus.
- **Using the equation for a sphere by mistake**: Sphere has three variables.
- **Assuming the general form always represents a circle**: If the radius squared comes out negative, it is an imaginary circle (no real points).
- **Confusing radius with diameter in the equation**: The r in the equation is always the radius.
