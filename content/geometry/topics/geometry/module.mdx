# Geometry

Geometry is the branch of mathematics concerned with the properties and relations of points, lines, surfaces, and solids in space. From measuring land in ancient times to designing modern architecture and video games, geometry provides the language and tools to describe, analyze, and construct the shapes that make up our world.

This topic introduces the big picture: what geometry studies, its historical roots, core building blocks, and why its methods of proof and visualization remain indispensable today. Later topics will dive deeper into specific figures and theorems.

## What Is Geometry?

<!-- section: what-is-geometry -->

Geometry (from Greek "geo" for earth and "metron" for measure) originally meant measuring the earth. Today it is the study of spatial relationships, figures, and transformations. It asks questions such as: How do shapes fit together? What remains unchanged when we move or resize a figure? How can we prove two figures are the same?

At its core, geometry combines intuition about space with rigorous logical deduction. It bridges the concrete (drawing figures) and the abstract (proving statements that hold for all such figures).

**ELI5**

- Geometry is like learning the rules of a giant connect-the-dots game in space. You start with simple dots (points) and sticks (lines) and discover what pictures you can build and what rules those pictures must obey.
- Just as grammar tells you how words form valid sentences, geometry tells you how points and lines form valid shapes and why some constructions are impossible.

**Worked Example:**

Consider a simple square drawn on paper. Using only a ruler and compass (no measuring numbers), can you construct a square with exactly twice the area?

This is the classical "doubling the square" problem. It turns out to be impossible with those tools alone because the side length of the new square would require constructing the square root of 2, which is not possible in a finite number of ruler-and-compass steps from a unit length. This illustrates how geometry quickly leads to deep questions about what is constructible.

## Core Building Blocks

<!-- section: building-blocks -->

The primitive notions in most geometric systems are **point**, **line**, and **plane**. These are left undefined (or described intuitively) and then related by axioms.

- A point has position but no size.
- A line is the shortest path between two points and extends forever.
- A plane is a flat surface with no thickness that extends forever.

From these we define segments, rays, angles, polygons, circles, and all higher figures. Other primitives sometimes include "betweenness" and "congruence."

**ELI5**

- Points are like tiny invisible specks that only say "I am here." Lines are perfectly straight highways with no beginning or end. Planes are infinite tabletops with no edges or thickness.
- You cannot "see" a mathematical point; any dot you draw has size, but we treat it as if it does not.

**Worked Example:**

Label three points A, B, and C not on a straight line. The figure ABC is a triangle. The three points determine exactly one plane. Any fourth point D in that plane lies inside, on, or outside the triangle. This classification (interior/exterior) is fundamental and can be decided using betweenness and line-crossing tests.

## Axioms, Postulates, and Theorems

<!-- section: axioms-postulates -->

An **axiom** (or postulate) is a statement we accept as true without proof. Euclid listed five postulates plus common notions. From them he deduced hundreds of propositions (theorems).

Examples of postulates:
- A straight line can be drawn between any two points.
- All right angles are equal.
- Through a point not on a given line exactly one parallel can be drawn (the famous parallel postulate).

A **theorem** is a statement proved from the axioms and previously proved theorems. A **proof** is a logical chain showing why the theorem must be true.

**ELI5**

- Axioms are the "rules of the game" you agree to before playing. You do not prove the rules; you use them to prove everything else.
- Once the rules are set, clever reasoning (proofs) lets you discover surprising facts that follow necessarily, like "the angles in any triangle add to 180°."

**Worked Example:**

Euclid's Proposition 1 (Book I): Given a segment AB, construct an equilateral triangle with side AB.

1. Draw circle centered at A with radius AB.
2. Draw circle centered at B with radius BA (same length).
3. The two circles intersect at two points; pick one as C.
4. Triangles ABC, BAC, etc. have all sides equal by construction (radii), so equilateral.

This construction uses only the postulates about circles and lines.

## Why Geometry Matters

<!-- section: importance -->

Geometry trains spatial reasoning, logical proof, and visualization. It underpins:
- Architecture and engineering (stability of structures)
- Computer graphics and game design (transformations, clipping)
- Physics (trajectories, optics, relativity uses geometry)
- Art and design (perspective, symmetry)
- Navigation and surveying

Modern fields such as algebraic geometry, differential geometry, and topology grew from classical roots.

**ELI5**

- Learning geometry is like getting a superpower for seeing hidden patterns in the world. Once you know why triangles are rigid, you understand why bridges use triangular trusses.
- Proofs teach you to argue carefully: "because of this rule, that must follow."

**Worked Example:**

A carpenter wants a tabletop that will not wobble. A three-legged stool is inherently stable because three points determine a unique plane (any three non-collinear points fix a plane). Adding a fourth leg requires the floor and all four feet to be coplanar, which is rarely true in real rooms. Hence the wobble.

## Common Mistakes

- **"Geometry is just drawing pictures"**: Pictures help intuition but never replace proof. A diagram can be misleading; the logic must hold independently of the drawing.
- **Treating undefined terms as having everyday size**: A "point" has no width; a "line" has no thickness. Real drawings are approximations.
- **Assuming the parallel postulate is obvious**: It feels natural on flat paper but fails on curved surfaces; changing it produces entirely different geometries (hyperbolic, elliptic).
- **Confusing "prove" with "check with numbers"**: Plugging in one example shows consistency but never proves a general statement.
- **Ignoring that some things are impossible**: Not every construction or length is achievable with given tools; impossibility proofs are as important as existence proofs.
