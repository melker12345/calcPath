# Congruence transformation

A congruence transformation (or congruence mapping) is an isometry of the plane: a transformation that maps any figure to a congruent copy.

The four types (translation, rotation, reflection, glide reflection) generate all congruences.

## Congruence Transformations as Isometries

<!-- section: cong-as-isom -->

By definition, a transformation is a congruence if it preserves distances (is an isometry).

The triangle congruence criteria guarantee that certain correspondences can be realized by a congruence transformation.

**ELI5**

- A congruence transformation is any 'rigid motion' (including flips) that moves a shape onto a perfect copy of itself (same size and shape).
- The tests like SSS tell you 'yes, there exists a rigid motion that makes them coincide' without having to exhibit the motion.

**Worked Example:**

Given two segments of equal length, there is a translation or rotation (or reflection) that maps one onto the other. This is the 'SAS' or 'SSS' for segments (just translation along the vector or rotation around one endpoint).

## Using Congruence Transformations in Proofs

<!-- section: proofs-cong -->

Many proofs proceed by assuming a correspondence of parts, constructing the congruence transformation that realizes it (or proving it exists), then showing other corresponding parts must coincide.

This is the modern rigorous version of Euclid's superposition method.

**ELI5**

- Instead of 'laying one triangle on the other,' we say 'there exists an isometry doing the laying,' and then use the preservation properties.
- It avoids the philosophical worry about physically moving figures.

**Worked Example:**

Proof of SSS: given AB=DE, BC=EF, CA=FD. Translate so A maps to D and AB direction aligns with DE (possible). Then B maps to E. The two possible positions for C (intersection of circles) ; because distances match, the image of C must land on F (the only point at the right distances from D and E). Hence the isometry maps the triangle to the other.

## Direct vs Opposite Congruences

<!-- section: direct-opposite -->

Direct (orientation-preserving) congruences: translations and rotations.

Opposite (orientation-reversing): reflections and glide reflections.

In some contexts (e.g., when figures are 'one-sided' or chiral), only direct congruences are allowed.

**ELI5**

- Direct: you can slide or spin the shape into place without flipping it over.
- Opposite: you need to flip it (like turning a left glove into a right glove by turning it inside out in 3D, but in 2D the flip is in the plane).

**Worked Example:**

Two triangles that are mirror images (enantiomorphs) are congruent via an opposite isometry but not via a direct one if they have 'handedness' (e.g., labeled clockwise vs counterclockwise vertices).

## Common Mistakes

- **Including similarities that change size**: Congruence requires size-preserving (isometry), not just shape-preserving.
- **Forgetting glide reflections**: They are proper congruences (distance-preserving) even though they have no fixed points.
- **Assuming every pair of congruent figures can be superimposed without flip**: Depends on whether opposite congruences are allowed in the context.
- **Confusing the transformation with the correspondence**: The transformation realizes a particular vertex matching.
