# Circles & Polygons

Circles, radii and diameters, polygons, inscribed angles, and area.

A circle is the set of all points in a plane that are at a fixed distance (the radius) from a given center point.

It is the most symmetric of all plane figures and appears everywhere from wheels to orbits to the definition of π.

## Definition and Basic Terminology

<!-- section: definition-terms -->

Center O, radius r (distance from center to any point on the circle), diameter d = 2r (longest chord, passing through center), circumference C = 2πr, area A = πr^{2}.

A chord is a segment joining two points on the circle. An arc is a portion of the circle between two points.

**ELI5**

- A circle is every point exactly r steps away from the center, like a perfect round fence with the center as the post.
- The diameter is the widest straight cut through the middle; twice as wide as from center to edge.

**Worked Example:**

A circle with center (0,0) and radius 5 consists of all (x,y) satisfying x^{2} + y^{2} = 25. The point (3,4) lies on it because 9+16=25. The diameter along the x-axis runs from (−5,0) to (5,0).

## Chords, Arcs, and Central Angles

<!-- section: chords-arcs -->

A central angle is formed by two radii. The measure of the arc equals the measure of the central angle. Longer chords subtend larger central angles (in the same circle).

Equal chords subtend equal arcs (and equal central angles).

**ELI5**

- The "slice" of pie from the center (central angle) tells you how big the crust arc is.
- A bigger slice has a longer crust piece and a longer straight chord across the top of the slice.

**Worked Example:**

In a circle of radius 10, central angle 60° subtends an arc of 60° (1/6 of the circumference). The chord length is 2 * 10 * sin(30°) = 10 (equilateral triangle formed by two radii and chord).

## Inscribed Angles and Angles in Semicircles

<!-- section: inscribed -->

An inscribed angle has its vertex on the circle and sides through two other points. Its measure is half the measure of the intercepted arc (or half the central angle).

A special case: an angle inscribed in a semicircle is a right angle (Thales' theorem).

**ELI5**

- An angle sitting on the edge of the circle "sees" only half as much of the arc as the center does.
- If the arc is exactly a semicircle (180°), the inscribed angle is 90° — that's why a diameter makes a right triangle with any point on the circle.

**Worked Example:**

Arc AB measures 80° (central). An inscribed angle with vertex on the remaining part of the circle intercepting arc AB measures 40°. If arc AB is 180°, the inscribed angle is 90°.

## Common Mistakes

- **Confusing central and inscribed angles**: Inscribed is half the central for the same arc.
- **Thinking the diameter is any chord through the center**: Only the one passing through center is diameter; others are just long chords.
- **Forgetting that all radii are equal**: This is what makes the circle so symmetric and allows so many isosceles triangles inside it.
- **Mixing arc measure (degrees) with arc length (units of length)**: Arc measure is angle; length is (theta/360)*2πr.

The radius of a circle or sphere is the distance from its center to any point on its circumference (or surface). It is usually denoted r and is the fundamental length parameter of the circle.

All radii of a given circle are equal, and the radius determines every other measurement of the circle.

## Definition and Properties

<!-- section: definition-properties -->

The radius r is half the diameter. The set of points at distance r from center O is the circle. Any segment from center to circumference is a radius and has length r.

Because all radii are equal, triangles formed by two radii and a chord are isosceles.

**ELI5**

- The radius is the "spoke" length from the axle (center) to the rim. Every spoke is the same.
- Once you fix r, the whole circle is fixed; you cannot have a bigger or smaller circle with the same r.

**Worked Example:**

Center at (2,3), radius 4. The point (2+4,3)=(6,3) is on the circle because distance =4. The point (2,3+4)=(2,7) is also on the circle. Any point at distance exactly 4 from (2,3) lies on it.

## Radius in Formulas

<!-- section: formulas -->

Circumference C = 2πr. Area A = πr^{2}. In a unit circle r=1, many trig identities simplify.

The length of a chord subtending central angle 2\theta is 2r sin\theta.

**ELI5**

- Circumference is "unrolled" length around the circle: like the string you wrap exactly once.
- Area is "how much carpet" inside the circle; it grows with the square of the radius (bigger r means much more area).

**Worked Example:**

r = 5. C = 2\pi*5 = 10\pi \approx 31.4. A = \pi*25 \approx 78.5. Chord for 60° central: 2*5*sin(30°) = 5.

## Radius vs Other Lengths

<!-- section: vs-other -->

The radius is the smallest distance from center to boundary. Apothem of a regular polygon is the radius of the inscribed circle. In 3D the radius of a sphere is analogous.

**ELI5**

- The radius is the shortest "ruler" from middle to edge. Any other line from center to edge is also a radius, same length.
- For a square, the radius of the inscribed circle touches the middle of each side (apothem); the circumradius goes to the corners.

**Worked Example:**

For a regular hexagon with side length s, the radius (distance from center to vertex) equals s. This makes every central triangle equilateral.

## Common Mistakes

- **Using diameter when radius is needed in formulas**: Many formulas (area, C=2\pi r) explicitly use r; doubling by mistake is common.
- **Thinking radius can vary around the circle**: In a true circle it cannot; varying distance means it is not a circle (ellipse, etc.).
- **Confusing radius of circle with radius of curvature**: For more complex curves the local radius of curvature can change.
- **Forgetting r=0 is a degenerate point-circle**: A circle of radius 0 is just its center point.

The diameter of a circle is a chord that passes through the center. It is the longest possible chord and its length is twice the radius (d = 2r).

Any angle inscribed in a semicircle (with the diameter as one side) is a right angle.

## Definition and Properties

<!-- section: definition-props -->

Diameter d = 2r. It is the maximum distance between any two points on the circle. The center is the midpoint of every diameter.

A diameter divides the circle into two equal semicircles.

**ELI5**

- The diameter is the straight-line distance from one side of the circle, through the middle, to the opposite side — the widest you can go.
- Cut a round pizza through the center; the cut is a diameter and you get two equal halves.

**Worked Example:**

Circle center (0,0), r=5. One diameter runs from (−5,0) to (5,0), length 10. Another from (0,−5) to (0,5). The center (0,0) is exactly in the middle of each.

## Diameter and Right Angles (Thales)

<!-- section: thales -->

Thales' theorem: the angle subtended by a diameter at any point on the circle is 90°.

This gives a quick way to construct right angles or recognize right triangles inscribed in circles.

**ELI5**

- If you take the diameter as the base of a triangle and put the third corner anywhere on the circle, the corner at the circumference will always be a perfect 90° corner.
- This is why you can draw a right triangle by picking any point on a circle with a given hypotenuse as diameter.

**Worked Example:**

Diameter AB. Point C on circle. Then △ABC has ∠C = 90° by Thales. Proof sketch: central angle at O for arc AB is 180° (semicircle); inscribed angle at C is half, 90°.

## Diameter in Calculations

<!-- section: calculations -->

Area and circumference can be written in terms of d: C = \pi d, A = (\pi/4) d^{2}.

The longest chord is the diameter; any other chord is shorter.

**ELI5**

- Using d instead of r is convenient when you measure across the whole circle (e.g., with a ruler on a physical wheel).
- The formulas are just rewritten with the factor of 2 absorbed into \pi or 1/4.

**Worked Example:**

Circle with d=10 (r=5). C = \pi*10 \approx 31.4. A = \pi*25 \approx 78.5. Same as before.

## Common Mistakes

- **Calling any long chord a diameter**: Only those passing exactly through the center qualify.
- **Forgetting Thales gives a right angle only when the hypotenuse is the diameter**: If the hypotenuse is a shorter chord the inscribed angle is acute or obtuse accordingly.
- **Mixing radius and diameter in formulas**: C = 2\pi r = \pi d; using 2\pi d by mistake doubles the circumference.
- **Thinking the center is not part of the diameter segment**: The diameter segment includes the center as its midpoint.

A polygon is a closed plane figure bounded by a finite chain of straight line segments (sides) meeting at vertices.

Polygons are the basic building blocks for tiling, computer graphics, and much of discrete geometry.

## Definition and Classification

<!-- section: definition-classification -->

A polygon with n sides is an n-gon. Simple polygons do not cross themselves; complex ones may. Convex polygons have all interior angles <180° and no sides bend back.

Regular polygons have all sides and angles equal.

**ELI5**

- A polygon is a closed shape made of straight sides, like a stop sign (octagon) or a yield sign (triangle).
- Convex means you can draw a rubber band around it and it touches all vertices; no dents.

**Worked Example:**

Quadrilateral (4-gon), pentagon (5), hexagon (6), heptagon (7), octagon (8), nonagon (9), decagon (10). A star pentagram is a complex (self-intersecting) polygon.

## Interior and Exterior Angles

<!-- section: angles -->

Sum of interior angles of an n-gon is (n−2) \times 180°.

Each exterior angle of a regular n-gon is 360°/n; the sum of exterior angles (one at each vertex, turning the same way) is always 360°.

**ELI5**

- As you walk around the polygon, the total turn you make is a full circle (360°). That is the sum of the exterior angles.
- Interior angles are what is inside at each corner; for regular they are all equal.

**Worked Example:**

For a regular pentagon, each exterior = 72°. Each interior = 180° − 72° = 108°. Sum of interiors = 5 \times 108 = 540° = (5−2) \times 180°.

## Convexity and Simple vs Complex

<!-- section: convexity-simple -->

In a convex polygon any line segment between two points inside stays inside. For simple polygons the Jordan curve theorem says the boundary divides the plane into an interior and exterior region.

**ELI5**

- Convex = no "caves"; you can see every point from every other point inside without the wall getting in the way.
- A star is simple? No, self-intersecting; a simple dented shape is concave but still simple (no crossings).

**Worked Example:**

A rectangle is convex. An arrowhead shape (chevron) is concave (one interior angle >180°). A pentagram crosses itself and is complex.

## Common Mistakes

- **Counting sides vs vertices**: An n-gon has n sides and n vertices.
- **Assuming all polygons are convex or regular**: Many are irregular or concave.
- **Forgetting the (n-2) formula**: Quadrilateral 360°, pentagon 540°, etc.
- **Including self-intersecting when talking 'interior angle sum'**: The simple formula assumes simple polygons.

A regular polygon is both equilateral (all sides equal) and equiangular (all interior angles equal). It has rotational symmetry of order n and reflection symmetries.

Regular polygons tile the plane in only three cases (triangles, squares, hexagons) and are the faces of the Platonic solids.

## Properties

<!-- section: properties -->

For regular n-gon with side length s and radius r (to vertex):

- Each central angle = 360°/n.
- Each interior angle = ((n-2)/n) * 180°.
- Area = (1/2) n r^{2} sin(360°/n) or (n s^{2})/ (4 tan(180°/n)).

**ELI5**

- A regular polygon is perfectly "fair"; every corner and every side is identical. You can spin it and it looks the same every 360/n degrees.
- The more sides, the closer it looks to a circle.

**Worked Example:**

Regular hexagon: interior angle 120°. Central angle 60°. If radius (to vertex) = s, it is composed of 6 equilateral triangles. Area = (3\sqrt{3}/2) s^{2}.

## Construction and Symmetry

<!-- section: construction-symmetry -->

Regular polygons with 3,4,5,6 sides are classically constructible with ruler and compass. Gauss showed 17-gon is constructible; not all n are.

The symmetry group is the dihedral group D_n with 2n elements.

**ELI5**

- You can draw some regular polygons exactly with just a compass and straightedge (equilateral triangle, square, regular hexagon, and some others like pentagon). Others require more advanced tools or are impossible exactly.
- The symmetries are all the ways you can turn or flip it and have it land on itself.

**Worked Example:**

Regular pentagon construction (Euclid Book IV): involves constructing the golden ratio via isosceles triangles with specific angles, then laying off equal sides on a circle.

## Limits and Approximation to Circle

<!-- section: limit-circle -->

As n \to \infty, a regular n-gon inscribed in a circle of radius r approaches the circle. Its perimeter \to 2\pi r and area \to \pi r^{2}. This is one historical way to define and compute \pi (Archimedes' method).

**ELI5**

- With more and more sides the polygon gets "rounder" until you cannot see the difference from a true circle.
- Archimedes used 96-sided polygons to bound \pi between 3 10/71 and 3 1/7.

**Worked Example:**

For large n the apothem (radius to side midpoint) is almost r, and area formula (1/2) * perimeter * apothem approximates (1/2) * 2\pi r * r = \pi r^{2}.

## Common Mistakes

- **Calling a regular polygon 'equilateral' only**: It must also have equal angles; a rhombus is equilateral but not regular unless it is a square.
- **Confusing radius and apothem**: Radius goes to a vertex; apothem is perpendicular distance to a side (inradius).
- **Assuming all regular polygons tile the plane**: Only 3,4,6 do; others leave gaps or overlap.
- **Forgetting that constructibility is rare**: Most regular n-gons are not ruler-and-compass constructible.

An inscribed angle is an angle whose vertex lies on the circle and whose sides (chords) pass through two other points on the circle.

Its measure is half the measure of the intercepted arc.

## Definition and Measure

<!-- section: definition-measure -->

The inscribed angle intercepts the arc between its sides. Measure of inscribed angle = (1/2) measure of intercepted arc.

This is half the central angle subtending the same arc.

**ELI5**

- The angle sitting on the rim of the circle sees only half the arc that the center sees.
- The farther from the center, the "smaller" the angle looks for the same arc.

**Worked Example:**

Arc AB = 80° (central). Any inscribed angle with sides through A and B (vertex on the major arc) measures 40°.

## Inscribed Angle in Semicircle

<!-- section: semicircle -->

When the intercepted arc is a semicircle (180°), the inscribed angle is 90° (Thales' theorem). This gives a construction for right angles and shows that any triangle with hypotenuse as diameter is right-angled at the circumference.

**ELI5**

- Put the hypotenuse as the diameter; no matter where you put the third corner on the circle, the angle at that corner is a perfect right angle.
- This is a quick way to draw or recognize right triangles.

**Worked Example:**

Diameter AB. C on circle. Then ∠ACB = 90°. Proof: central angle for semicircle 180°, inscribed half = 90°.

## Common Mistakes

- **Using the wrong arc**: The inscribed angle intercepts the arc that lies in its interior (the one "seen" by the angle), not the reflex arc.
- **Confusing with central**: Always half for the same arc.
- **Forgetting the vertex must be on the circle**: If the vertex is inside or outside, the angle/arc relations differ (power of point, etc.).
- **Assuming all inscribed angles on the same side are equal**: They are, for the same arc; different arcs give different measures.

The circumscribed circle (or circumcircle) of a polygon is the unique circle that passes through all the vertices of the polygon.

Its center is the circumcenter; its radius is the circumradius.

## Definition and Existence

<!-- section: definition-existence -->

For a triangle, the circumcircle always exists and is unique. The circumcenter is the intersection point of the perpendicular bisectors of the sides.

For a general polygon, a circumcircle exists only if the polygon is cyclic (vertices lie on a common circle).

**ELI5**

- The circumcircle is the "round fence" that just touches all the corners of your polygon.
- For a triangle you can always find such a circle; for a random quadrilateral the four corners may not lie on one circle.

**Worked Example:**

Any three non-collinear points determine a unique circle. The circumcenter of △ABC is the point equidistant from A, B, and C (intersection of perp bisectors).

## Properties

<!-- section: properties-circum -->

In a right triangle the circumcenter is the midpoint of the hypotenuse (circumradius = half hypotenuse).

In an acute triangle the circumcenter is inside; in obtuse, outside.

The extended law of sines: a / sin A = 2R where R is circumradius.

**ELI5**

- In a right triangle the hypotenuse is the diameter of the circumcircle (Thales again).
- The bigger the triangle relative to its angles, the larger R.

**Worked Example:**

Right △ with legs 3,4 hypotenuse 5. R = 5/2 = 2.5. Center at midpoint of hypotenuse. Any point on the circle is at distance 2.5 from the midpoint.

## For Regular Polygons and Cyclic Quads

<!-- section: regular-cyclic -->

Every regular polygon has a circumcircle (by symmetry). A quadrilateral has a circumcircle iff opposite angles sum to 180° (Ptolemy or cyclic quad condition).

**ELI5**

- Regular polygons are perfectly symmetric so their vertices lie on a circle centered at the polygon's center.
- For a quad to fit on a circle, the opposite corners must "add up" to a straight angle in a certain way.

**Worked Example:**

A square has circumcircle with diagonal as diameter. A rectangle that is not a square also has one (diagonals equal and bisect). A general parallelogram does not (unless rectangle).

## Common Mistakes

- **Confusing circumcircle with incircle**: Circum passes through vertices (outside or through); in touches the sides (inside).
- **Assuming every quadrilateral has a circumcircle**: Only cyclic ones do.
- **Forgetting the circumcenter may lie outside the triangle**: In obtuse triangles it does.
- **Mixing R with inradius r**: R is usually larger.

The area of a circle of radius r is A = \pi r^{2}.

This formula can be derived by dividing the circle into sectors and rearranging them into a rectangle-like shape whose dimensions involve \pi r and r.

## Derivation by Sectors

<!-- section: derivation-sectors -->

Divide the circle into n thin sectors, each with angle 360°/n. Each sector has arc length (2\pi r)/n and 'height' r.

Rearranged, the sectors form a shape that approaches a rectangle of width \pi r and height r as n \to \infty. Area = \pi r \times r = \pi r^{2}.

**ELI5**

- Cut the pizza into many skinny slices and rearrange them into a rectangle that is half the circumference wide and radius tall.
- The wiggly edges become straight in the limit, giving the clean rectangle area.

**Worked Example:**

For r=1, limit area \pi. For r=5, A = 25\pi. Using 8 sectors gives a rough approximation; more sectors get closer to the true area.

## Other Derivations

<!-- section: other-derivations -->

Using integral calculus: area = \int_{-r}^r 2\sqrt{r^2 - x^2} dx = \pi r^{2} (area under semicircle).

Using Green's theorem or polar coordinates also yields the formula.

**ELI5**

- You can add up the areas of infinitely many thin rings or use trig to integrate the circle equation.
- All methods agree on \pi r^{2}.

**Worked Example:**

In polar: area = (1/2) \int_0^{2\pi} r^2 d\theta = (1/2) r^2 * 2\pi = \pi r^{2}.

## Applications and Scaling

<!-- section: applications-scaling -->

Area scales with the square of the linear dimensions: if you double the radius, area quadruples.

Used in physics (pressure on a circular piston, flux through circular aperture), probability (normal distribution in 2D), and design.

**ELI5**

- A circle twice as wide has four times as much 'stuff' inside (area).
- This quadratic scaling is why large pizzas are better value per crust (less crust per area).

**Worked Example:**

Circle r=1, A=\pi. Circle r=2, A=4\pi. Circle r=10, A=100\pi. Scaling factor k in radius gives k^{2} in area.

## Common Mistakes

- **Using 2\pi r for area**: That is circumference. Area is \pi r^{2}.
- **Forgetting the square**: \pi r is not area.
- **Using diameter instead of radius without adjusting**: A = (\pi/4) d^{2} is correct but easy to forget the 1/4.
- **Confusing with sphere surface/volume**: Sphere surface 4\pi r^{2}, volume (4/3)\pi r^{3}.
