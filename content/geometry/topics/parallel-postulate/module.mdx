# Parallel postulate

The parallel postulate is Euclid's fifth postulate. It is the only one of the five that is not local and "obvious," and it is the key assumption that distinguishes Euclidean geometry from hyperbolic and elliptic geometries.

In its original form it is a statement about lines that, when extended, must meet if interior angles sum to less than two right angles.

## Euclid's Original Statement

<!-- section: euclid-statement -->

"If two lines are drawn which intersect a third in such a way that the sum of the inner angles on one side is less than two right angles, then the two lines inevitably must intersect each other on that side if extended far enough."

Playfair's equivalent (more commonly used today): "Through a point not on a given straight line, exactly one line can be drawn that never meets the given line."

**ELI5**

- On a flat piece of paper, if you have one line and a point off to the side, there is exactly one direction you can draw another line that will never cross the first one, no matter how far you go.
- The postulate is a global statement about what happens "at infinity."

**Worked Example:**

Given line L and point P not on L, construct the unique parallel through P: drop a perpendicular from P to L meeting at Q, then erect a perpendicular to PQ at P. The resulting line M is parallel to L (alternate interior angles are both 90°). Any other line through P will tilt and eventually cross L.

## Attempts to Prove It

<!-- section: attempts -->

For over 2000 years, mathematicians tried to prove the parallel postulate from the first four postulates, believing it was a theorem. Many "proofs" contained hidden assumptions equivalent to the postulate itself (e.g., assuming the angle sum of a triangle is 180°).

These failed attempts were valuable: they clarified exactly what the postulate adds.

**ELI5**

- People kept trying to show that "the flat paper rule" must follow from the other drawing rules. They kept sneaking the flat-paper assumption back in without noticing.
- The long struggle showed that the rule is truly independent.

**Worked Example:**

One common circular "proof" assumes that the angle sum in a triangle is 180° (which itself requires the parallel postulate) to "prove" the parallel postulate. The circularity was not obvious until non-Euclidean examples existed.

## Birth of Non-Euclidean Geometry

<!-- section: non-euclidean -->

In the early 19th century, János Bolyai, Nikolai Lobachevsky, and (privately) Carl Friedrich Gauss developed hyperbolic geometry by assuming "through P there are at least two lines parallel to L."

Later, Bernhard Riemann developed elliptic geometry in which there are no parallels at all (every pair of lines meets).

Both systems are consistent if and only if Euclidean geometry is consistent.

**ELI5**

- Imagine living on a giant saddle surface (hyperbolic): many "straight" paths from a point will never meet a given line. Or on a sphere (elliptic): every great-circle "line" eventually meets every other.
- These are not "weird" or broken; they are perfectly logical worlds with their own theorems.

**Worked Example:**

In hyperbolic geometry the angle sum of every triangle is less than 180°. The defect (180° minus sum) is proportional to the area of the triangle. Larger triangles have larger angle defect. This is impossible in Euclidean geometry where the sum is always exactly 180° regardless of size.

## Modern Understanding and Models

<!-- section: models -->

The Poincaré disk and upper half-plane models realize hyperbolic geometry inside ordinary Euclidean space using a different notion of "straight line" (circular arcs orthogonal to the boundary) and a different distance function. In these models Euclid's first four postulates hold, but the parallel postulate fails.

**ELI5**

- You can draw a perfectly good picture of hyperbolic geometry on a flat piece of paper if you agree that "lines" are curved arcs of a special kind and distances get stretched near the edge.
- The model shows consistency: if Euclidean geometry had a contradiction, the model would reveal it.

**Worked Example:**

In the Poincaré disk, two "lines" (arcs) through an interior point P that are asymptotic (approaching the same boundary point) are parallel in the hyperbolic sense: they never meet inside the disk. Many such arcs exist, violating Playfair's form.

## Common Mistakes

- **"The parallel postulate is obviously true"**: It is true in the geometry of flat paper and local experience, but not in the geometry of the sphere or of curved space-time. "Obvious" depends on the space you live in.
- **Thinking non-Euclidean geometry is only for tiny or huge scales**: While relativity uses curved geometry at cosmic scales, the mathematics is useful at any scale once you choose the right model.
- **Confusing 'parallel' in Euclidean sense with 'parallel' in affine geometry**: In some abstract geometries parallels are defined via direction rather than non-intersection.
- **Believing Euclid's original wording is the only form**: Many equivalent statements exist (Playfair, angle sum = 180°, rectangle exists, etc.). Any one can be taken as the axiom.
