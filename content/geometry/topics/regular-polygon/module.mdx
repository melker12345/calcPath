# Regular polygon

A regular polygon is both equilateral (all sides equal) and equiangular (all interior angles equal). It has rotational symmetry of order n and reflection symmetries.

Regular polygons tile the plane in only three cases (triangles, squares, hexagons) and are the faces of the Platonic solids.

## Properties

<!-- section: properties -->

For regular n-gon with side length s and radius r (to vertex):

- Each central angle = 360°/n.
- Each interior angle = ((n-2)/n) * 180°.
- Area = (1/2) n r^{2} sin(360°/n) or (n s^{2})/ (4 tan(180°/n)).

**ELI5**

- A regular polygon is perfectly "fair"; every corner and every side is identical. You can spin it and it looks the same every 360/n degrees.
- The more sides, the closer it looks to a circle.

**Worked Example:**

Regular hexagon: interior angle 120°. Central angle 60°. If radius (to vertex) = s, it is composed of 6 equilateral triangles. Area = (3\sqrt{3}/2) s^{2}.

## Construction and Symmetry

<!-- section: construction-symmetry -->

Regular polygons with 3,4,5,6 sides are classically constructible with ruler and compass. Gauss showed 17-gon is constructible; not all n are.

The symmetry group is the dihedral group D_n with 2n elements.

**ELI5**

- You can draw some regular polygons exactly with just a compass and straightedge (equilateral triangle, square, regular hexagon, and some others like pentagon). Others require more advanced tools or are impossible exactly.
- The symmetries are all the ways you can turn or flip it and have it land on itself.

**Worked Example:**

Regular pentagon construction (Euclid Book IV): involves constructing the golden ratio via isosceles triangles with specific angles, then laying off equal sides on a circle.

## Limits and Approximation to Circle

<!-- section: limit-circle -->

As n \to \infty, a regular n-gon inscribed in a circle of radius r approaches the circle. Its perimeter \to 2\pi r and area \to \pi r^{2}. This is one historical way to define and compute \pi (Archimedes' method).

**ELI5**

- With more and more sides the polygon gets "rounder" until you cannot see the difference from a true circle.
- Archimedes used 96-sided polygons to bound \pi between 3 10/71 and 3 1/7.

**Worked Example:**

For large n the apothem (radius to side midpoint) is almost r, and area formula (1/2) * perimeter * apothem approximates (1/2) * 2\pi r * r = \pi r^{2}.

## Common Mistakes

- **Calling a regular polygon 'equilateral' only**: It must also have equal angles; a rhombus is equilateral but not regular unless it is a square.
- **Confusing radius and apothem**: Radius goes to a vertex; apothem is perpendicular distance to a side (inradius).
- **Assuming all regular polygons tile the plane**: Only 3,4,6 do; others leave gaps or overlap.
- **Forgetting that constructibility is rare**: Most regular n-gons are not ruler-and-compass constructible.
