# Triangles

Triangle properties, congruence and similarity criteria, the Pythagorean theorem, and key theorems.

A triangle is a closed plane figure formed by three line segments connecting three non-collinear points. It is the simplest polygon and the basic building block of much of Euclidean geometry.

Every theorem about polygons, congruence, similarity, and area ultimately rests on properties of triangles.

## Classification of Triangles

<!-- section: classification -->

Triangles are classified by sides (equilateral, isosceles, scalene) or by angles (acute, right, obtuse).

- Equilateral: all sides equal, all angles 60°.
- Isosceles: at least two sides equal.
- Scalene: all sides different.
- Right: one 90° angle.
- Acute: all angles < 90°.
- Obtuse: one angle > 90°.

**ELI5**

- An equilateral triangle is perfectly even, like a slice of pizza with equal sides. A right triangle has one perfect corner like the corner of a book.
- The names tell you the "personality" of the triangle before you even draw it.

**Worked Example:**

A triangle with sides 3, 4, 5 is scalene (all different) and right-angled (because 3^{2}+4^{2}=9+16=25=5^{2}). A triangle with sides 5, 5, 6 is isosceles but not equilateral.

## Basic Properties and Angle Sum

<!-- section: properties -->

The sum of interior angles is always 180°. The longest side is opposite the largest angle. The area can be computed as (1/2)ab sin C or using Heron's formula.

An exterior angle equals the sum of the two remote interior angles.

**ELI5**

- If you walk around the three corners of any triangle, you turn a total of 180° (a half-turn).
- The biggest angle "faces" the longest side, like the biggest person sitting opposite the biggest slice.

**Worked Example:**

In △ABC with ∠A=50°, ∠B=60°, side BC (opposite A) is the shortest side because 50° is the smallest angle. Side AB (opposite C=70°) is the longest.

## Triangle Congruence and Similarity Preview

<!-- section: congruence-preview -->

Two triangles are congruent if corresponding sides and angles match exactly (same size and shape). The main criteria (SSS, SAS, ASA, AAS, HL for right) are proved using axioms and earlier propositions.

Similarity (same shape, possibly different size) is introduced later but relies on angle equality.

**ELI5**

- Congruent triangles are identical twins; you can slide, flip, or turn one onto the other and they match perfectly.
- Similar triangles are like family members: same angles, proportional sides, but one may be bigger.

**Worked Example:**

If △ABC has sides 3,4,5 and △DEF has sides 6,8,10, the triangles are similar (scale factor 2) but not congruent. All angles match because both are right triangles with the same acute angles.

## Common Mistakes

- **Assuming any three lengths form a triangle**: The triangle inequality must hold; 1+2 is not > 4.
- **Mixing up 'opposite' and 'adjacent' sides**: The side opposite an angle does not touch that angle's vertex.
- **Thinking all isosceles triangles are equilateral**: Only when the third side also equals the other two.
- **Forgetting the 180° sum when solving for an angle**: Always subtract the two known angles from 180°.

Two geometric figures are congruent if one can be transformed into the other by a rigid motion (isometry): translation, rotation, or reflection. They have the same size and shape; corresponding parts are equal.

Congruence is the "same" relation for figures and is central to proofs in Euclidean geometry.

## Definition via Isometries

<!-- section: definition-isometries -->

A congruence is a distance-preserving transformation (isometry) of the plane. If there exists an isometry mapping figure F1 onto F2, then F1 ≅ F2.

For triangles, this reduces to the familiar SSS, SAS, ASA, AAS, HL criteria.

**ELI5**

- Congruent figures are perfect copies; you can pick one up (without stretching or flipping in 3D if we disallow reflections sometimes) and lay it exactly on the other.
- "Same shape and same size" is the everyday phrase.

**Worked Example:**

Two triangles with sides 6,8,10 are congruent (both are scaled 3-4-5 right triangles). You can rotate and translate one onto the other; all sides and angles match.

## Corresponding Parts

<!-- section: corresponding-parts -->

When we write △ABC ≅ △DEF we mean angle A corresponds to angle D, side AB to DE, etc. The order of vertices encodes the correspondence.

Corresponding parts (CPCTC: corresponding parts of congruent triangles are congruent) are equal.

**ELI5**

- The labels tell you which corner goes with which corner. If you wrote the vertices in the wrong order, the correspondence would be wrong even if the numbers match.
- Once you know the triangles are congruent, every matching piece (sides, angles) must be equal.

**Worked Example:**

△ABC ≅ △DEF with correspondence A-D, B-E, C-F. Then ∠A = ∠D, AB = DE, BC = EF, etc. If we had written △ABC ≅ △DFE the correspondence would be different and the equalities would not hold.

## Criteria for Triangles

<!-- section: criteria-summary -->

The five standard triangle congruence criteria (SSS, SAS, ASA, AAS, HL) are shortcuts that guarantee an isometry exists without constructing it explicitly.

Each criterion is proved from the axioms (often using superposition or auxiliary constructions).

**ELI5**

- Instead of moving the whole triangle, we have quick "tests" using only 3 pieces of information that are enough to guarantee the triangles are identical twins.
- HL is special for right triangles (hypotenuse-leg).

**Worked Example:**

Given two right triangles with equal hypotenuses and one equal leg, they are congruent by HL. Proof reduces to SSS after constructing the other leg or using coordinates.

## Common Mistakes

- **Writing the correspondence in the wrong vertex order**: △ABC ≅ △DEF is not the same as △ABC ≅ △DFE.
- **Assuming SSS, SAS etc. work for any polygons**: They are triangle-specific (for quadrilaterals you need more information).
- **Confusing congruence with similarity**: Congruence requires equal size; similarity allows scaling.
- **Forgetting reflections**: In some contexts (oriented congruence) reflections are disallowed; usually in school geometry reflections are allowed.

SSS (Side-Side-Side) is one of the fundamental congruence criteria: if three sides of one triangle are equal in length to the three sides of another triangle (corresponding sides), then the two triangles are congruent.

This criterion is powerful because it shows that three lengths completely determine the shape and size of a triangle (up to congruence).

## The SSS Criterion

<!-- section: sss-criterion -->

If in △ABC and △DEF we have AB = DE, BC = EF, and CA = FD, then △ABC ≅ △DEF (corresponding parts equal, including all angles).

The correspondence is determined by the order of the sides listed.

**ELI5**

- If you have three sticks of lengths 3, 4, and 5, there is only one triangle (up to flipping) you can build with them. The angles are forced.
- SSS is like a rigid lock: once the three sides snap together, the whole shape is fixed.

**Worked Example:**

Given △ABC with AB=5, BC=6, CA=7 and △DEF with DE=5, EF=6, FD=7. Then the triangles are congruent by SSS. In particular ∠A = ∠D, ∠B = ∠E, ∠C = ∠F, and the figures can be superimposed by rigid motion.

## Proof Sketch (Euclid style)

<!-- section: proof-sketch -->

Euclid proves SSS (Prop. I.8) by a method of "application" and superposition, essentially showing that if sides match, the triangles can be made to coincide.

A modern proof often uses the law of cosines or constructs an auxiliary diagonal in the quadrilateral formed by the two triangles and shows the resulting triangles are congruent by SAS, then angles match.

**ELI5**

- Imagine two triangles made of rigid rods hinged at the vertices. If all three rods match in length, the two frames must fold into identical shapes.
- The proof shows you can always move one onto the other without stretching or bending.

**Worked Example:**

Place △ABC and △DEF so that side BC coincides with EF (possible because BC=EF). Because AB=DE and AC=DF, the third vertices A and D must land on the same point (intersection of two circles), hence the triangles coincide.

## Why SSS Works and Uniqueness

<!-- section: uniqueness -->

Given three side lengths satisfying the triangle inequality, there exists a unique triangle up to congruence. This is why SSS is a congruence (not merely similarity) criterion: size is fixed by the actual lengths.

**ELI5**

- Three lengths that can form a triangle "know" exactly what angles they must have. There is no freedom left.
- This is different from SSS similarity, which would allow scaling.

**Worked Example:**

The set of all triangles with sides 3,4,5 is exactly the set of all right triangles with those leg/hypotenuse lengths; any two such are congruent to each other. You cannot have a "bigger" 3-4-5 triangle without scaling the sides.

## Common Mistakes

- **Listing sides in wrong order**: The correspondence must match the sides that are claimed equal; SSS requires the sides to correspond in the stated order.
- **Confusing with SSA (which is not always congruence)**: SSA (two sides and a non-included angle) can be ambiguous (the "ambiguous case").
- **Forgetting the triangle inequality check**: 1,2,4 cannot form any triangle, so SSS does not apply.
- **Thinking SSS only works for equilateral**: It works for any scalene triangle as well; the sides just have to match correspondingly.

SAS (Side-Angle-Side) congruence: if two sides and the included angle of one triangle are equal to two sides and the included angle of another, the triangles are congruent.

This is one of the most frequently used criteria because the included angle "locks" the shape between the two sides.

## The SAS Criterion

<!-- section: sas-criterion -->

If in △ABC and △DEF, AB = DE, ∠B = ∠E, and BC = EF, then △ABC ≅ △DEF.

The angle must be the included angle (between the two sides).

**ELI5**

- Think of two rods of fixed length with a hinge between them set at a fixed angle. The distance between the free ends is forced; the whole triangle is rigid.
- The angle is the "glue" that fixes how the sides open.

**Worked Example:**

△ABC has AB=5, ∠ABC=60°, BC=7. △DEF has DE=5, ∠DEF=60°, EF=7. Then △ABC ≅ △DEF by SAS. The third sides AC and DF are equal, and the other angles match.

## Proof Ideas

<!-- section: proof-ideas -->

Euclid proves SAS as Prop. I.4 by superposition: place the equal angle and sides on top of each other; the third vertices must coincide because the sides are equal.

Modern treatments may use coordinates or the law of cosines to show the third side matches, then SSS.

**ELI5**

- Lay one triangle exactly on top of the other so the two sides and the angle between them match perfectly. Because the sides are the same length, the remaining vertex has nowhere else to go.
- The proof shows the figures coincide completely.

**Worked Example:**

Place ∠B on ∠E so the rays coincide. Then point A lands on D because AB=DE. Point C lands on F because BC=EF. Therefore the segments AC and DF coincide and the triangles are identical.

## Included vs Non-included Angle

<!-- section: included-angle -->

The angle must lie between the two given sides. If the angle is not included (SSA), the criterion fails in general and the ambiguous case can occur (zero, one, or two possible triangles).

**ELI5**

- The angle has to be the "middle" one between the two sides you are given. If it is at the end, the side can swing and create different possible positions for the third vertex.
- SSA is the "dangerous" case taught in trigonometry as the ambiguous case.

**Worked Example:**

Given sides 10 and 6 with included 30° angle: unique triangle. Given sides 10 and 6 with the 30° angle opposite the side of length 6: possibly two triangles (one where the 6-side swings acute, one obtuse).

## Common Mistakes

- **Using SSA instead of SAS**: Students often forget to check whether the given angle is included.
- **Assuming SAS works for any two sides and any angle**: The angle must be between the sides.
- **Forgetting to verify correspondence**: The equal angle must be between the pairs of equal sides in the correspondence.
- **Confusing with ASA**: ASA uses two angles and the included side; SAS uses two sides and the included angle.

ASA (Angle-Side-Angle) congruence: if two angles and the included side of one triangle equal the corresponding two angles and included side of another, the triangles are congruent.

Because the third angles are automatically equal (180° sum), AAS is also a valid criterion, but the classical ASA emphasizes the included side.

## The ASA Criterion

<!-- section: asa-criterion -->

If in △ABC and △DEF, ∠A = ∠D, AB = DE (the side between the angles at A and B? wait, standard: two angles and the side between them), then the triangles are congruent.

More precisely: two angles and the included side.

**ELI5**

- If you know two corners and the exact length of the side between them, the whole triangle is forced. The other sides and angles have to adjust to make the angles fit.
- The included side acts as the "base" that fixes the scale between the two angles.

**Worked Example:**

△ABC has ∠A=50°, side AB=6 (wait standard ASA: ∠A, side AC? Let's correct: suppose ∠BAC=50°, ∠ABC=60°, side AB (included between them? AB is between A and B). Yes. Then any other triangle with same two angles and included side AB=6 is congruent.

## Relation to AAS and Third Angle

<!-- section: third-angle -->

Since the sum of angles is 180°, if two angles are known the third is determined. Therefore giving two angles and a non-included side (AAS) also determines the triangle uniquely up to congruence.

**ELI5**

- Knowing two angles tells you the third for free. So you really only need two angles plus any side to fix the triangle (with proper correspondence).
- The side can be opposite one of the angles; the law of sines then fixes the scale.

**Worked Example:**

△ABC: ∠A=40°, ∠B=70° ⇒ ∠C=70°. Side BC (opposite A) = 5. Then the triangle is determined. Another triangle with same angles and corresponding side 5 is congruent by AAS (or ASA after identifying the included side once the third angle is computed).

## Proof

<!-- section: proof-asa -->

Euclid proves ASA as Prop. I.26 (along with AAS). The proof usually reduces to SAS or uses contradiction on the third side.

One common proof: assume the third sides differ, derive two different triangles on the same base with same vertex angles, leading to contradiction with exterior angle or isosceles properties.

**ELI5**

- Once the base and the two base angles are fixed, the arms of the angles must meet at a unique point (the third vertex).
- If they met at two different points you would have two different third angles or sides, which contradicts the angle sum.

**Worked Example:**

Fix base BC. At B construct the given angle, at C construct the given angle. The two rays intersect at exactly one point A (because the angles determine unique directions). Hence unique triangle.

## Common Mistakes

- **Confusing ASA with AAS**: Both work, but students must be able to identify the included side correctly for ASA.
- **Assuming AAA is congruence**: AAA gives similarity only; size is not fixed (you can scale the triangle and keep all angles).
- **Forgetting that the side must correspond correctly**: The side must lie between the two given angles in the correspondence.
- **Using ASA when only one angle is given with two sides**: That would be SSA or SAS depending on inclusion.

The Pythagorean theorem states that in a right-angled triangle, the square of the length of the hypotenuse (the side opposite the right angle) is equal to the sum of the squares of the lengths of the other two sides.

It is one of the most famous and useful theorems in mathematics, with hundreds of proofs and applications in distance, vectors, and physics.

## Statement and Notation

<!-- section: statement -->

For right triangle ABC with right angle at C, AB^{2} = AC^{2} + BC^{2}, where AB is the hypotenuse.

In algebraic form: c^{2} = a^{2} + b^{2} where c is hypotenuse, a and b the legs.

**ELI5**

- If you build squares outward on each side of a right triangle, the two smaller squares together exactly cover the big square on the hypotenuse.
- It is the "3-4-5" rule generalized: any right triangle obeys the same squaring relationship.

**Worked Example:**

A right triangle has legs 5 and 12. Hypotenuse c satisfies c^{2} = 25 + 144 = 169, so c = 13. This is the classic 5-12-13 right triangle.

## Proofs (Euclid and Modern)

<!-- section: proofs -->

Euclid's proof (Prop. I.47) uses congruence of triangles obtained by dropping perpendiculars and rearranging areas. There are algebraic proofs (using similar triangles), vector proofs (dot product), and even proofs by rearrangement (van Schooten, etc.).

One simple modern proof: drop an altitude from the right angle to the hypotenuse, creating two smaller right triangles similar to each other and to the original. Then a/c = c/b or similar proportions lead to c^{2} = a^{2} + b^{2} after cross-multiplying.

**ELI5**

- Euclid's proof is a giant jigsaw: he shows four copies of the triangle plus squares can be rearranged in two ways whose areas must match.
- The similar-triangles proof says "the pieces are scaled versions of the big one, so their sizes multiply to give the squares."

**Worked Example:**

Using similar triangles: the altitude to hypotenuse creates segments p and q on the hypotenuse with p + q = c. Then a^{2} = c·p and b^{2} = c·q. Adding: a^{2} + b^{2} = c(p + q) = c·c = c^{2}.

## Converse and Applications

<!-- section: converse-apps -->

The converse is also true: if in a triangle a^{2} + b^{2} = c^{2} then the angle opposite c is right.

Applications: distance formula in coordinate geometry is just Pythagorean on the differences Δx and Δy. In physics it appears in work, energy, and vector magnitude.

**ELI5**

- If the sides satisfy the square relation, you know for sure there is a right angle even if you cannot see the corner.
- GPS, video games, and construction all secretly use "square the differences and add" to find straight-line distances.

**Worked Example:**

Points (1,2) and (4,6). Δx=3, Δy=4. Distance = √(9+16) = 5. This is Pythagorean applied to the horizontal and vertical legs of the right triangle formed by the points and the axes.

## Common Mistakes

- **Applying it to non-right triangles**: The relation fails for acute or obtuse triangles (use law of cosines instead).
- **Forgetting which side is squared alone**: Only the hypotenuse (longest side, opposite right angle) goes on one side of the equation.
- **Confusing with area formula**: (1/2)ab is area, not related directly to the squares in Pythagoras.
- **Assuming all 3-4-5 scaled triangles are the only right triangles**: Any legs a,b with hypotenuse √(a^{2}+b^{2}) work; 3-4-5 is just the smallest integer example.

Two figures are similar if they have the same shape but not necessarily the same size. For triangles this means corresponding angles are equal and corresponding sides are proportional.

Similarity is the foundation of scale drawings, maps, fractals, and much of trigonometry.

## Definition for Triangles

<!-- section: definition-triangles -->

△ABC ~ △DEF if ∠A = ∠D, ∠B = ∠E, ∠C = ∠F and the ratios AB/DE = BC/EF = CA/FD = k (the scale factor).

The order of vertices indicates the correspondence.

**ELI5**

- Similar triangles are like photographs of the same scene taken from different distances: angles stay the same, but everything gets bigger or smaller together.
- The sides "scale" by the same factor; the shape is identical.

**Worked Example:**

△ABC has angles 40°, 60°, 80° and sides opposite 3, 4.2, 4.9 (approx). △DEF has the same angles and sides opposite 6, 8.4, 9.8. Scale factor k=2; the triangles are similar.

## AA, SAS, and SSS Similarity Criteria

<!-- section: criteria -->

Because of the 180° sum, two equal angles (AA) suffice for similarity of triangles. There are also SAS similarity (two sides proportional with included angle equal) and SSS similarity (all three sides proportional).

**ELI5**

- AA is the easiest: if two angles match, the third must, so shape matches.
- SSS similarity says "all sides in the same proportions" — like a photocopy at a different zoom.

**Worked Example:**

Two triangles have sides 4,5,6 and 8,10,12. All ratios = 2, so SSS similarity; all angles equal even without measuring them.

## Applications

<!-- section: applications -->

Similar triangles let us solve for unknown lengths using proportions. They justify the basic trigonometric ratios (sin, cos, tan are constant for a given angle across all similar right triangles). Used in shadow problems, map scaling, and perspective drawing.

**ELI5**

- If you know a tree casts a 10 ft shadow when a 6 ft person casts a 4 ft shadow, the triangles are similar and you can set up 6/4 = h/10 to find the tree height h=15 ft.
- Ratios replace measuring everything directly.

**Worked Example:**

A 1.7 m person stands 3 m from a lamppost and casts a 0.85 m shadow. The lamppost casts a 4.25 m shadow. Similar triangles: 1.7 / 0.85 = h / 4.25 ⇒ h = 8.5 m.

## Common Mistakes

- **Confusing similarity with congruence**: Similar triangles can be different sizes; congruent ones must be the same size.
- **Wrong correspondence**: The angles that are equal must be matched correctly when writing the proportion.
- **Assuming all triangles with one equal angle are similar**: Need two angles (or sides in proportion with angle).
- **Forgetting that similarity preserves angles but scales areas by k^{2}**: Linear dimensions scale by k, areas by k^{2}.

The AA (Angle-Angle) similarity criterion states that if two angles of one triangle are equal to two angles of another triangle, then the triangles are similar.

Because the third angles are forced to be equal by the 180° sum, AA is the most commonly used similarity test for triangles.

## The AA Criterion

<!-- section: aa-criterion -->

If ∠A = ∠D and ∠B = ∠E in △ABC and △DEF, then △ABC ~ △DEF (third angles also equal, sides proportional).

The correspondence is A↔D, B↔E, C↔F.

**ELI5**

- If two corners of one triangle match two corners of another, the third corners must also match (they have to add up to 180°). The shapes are the same; only the sizes may differ.
- You do not even need to look at the sides to know the angles are all the same.

**Worked Example:**

△ABC has ∠A=35°, ∠B=65° (so ∠C=80°). △DEF has ∠D=35°, ∠E=65°. Then the triangles are similar by AA; the third angles are both 80° and all corresponding sides will be in the same (unknown) ratio.

## Proof Using Parallel Lines or Basic Proportionality

<!-- section: proof-aa -->

One proof constructs a parallel to one side creating a smaller similar triangle, then uses corresponding angles from parallel lines (transversal) to show angles equal. Another uses the law of sines: a/sin A is the same diameter of the circumcircle when angles match.

**ELI5**

- Draw a line inside the bigger triangle parallel to the base; the small triangle on top has the same angles by alternate interior angles with the transversals.
- The parallel forces the shapes to match.

**Worked Example:**

To prove AA: assume ∠A=∠D, ∠B=∠E. Construct on the larger triangle a line parallel to the base creating a small triangle with the vertex angle. The small triangle has the same angles as the given small one by parallels, hence is similar to it, and the original is similar to the small one by the same angles.

## Consequences and Basic Proportionality Theorem

<!-- section: proportionality -->

From AA similarity follows the basic proportionality theorem (Thales' theorem): a line parallel to one side of a triangle divides the other two sides proportionally.

Conversely, if a line divides two sides proportionally, it is parallel to the third.

**ELI5**

- A line parallel to the base "cuts the sides in the same ratio." If it cuts one side in half, it cuts the other in half too.
- This is why similar triangles give you easy proportion equations for unknown lengths.

**Worked Example:**

In △ABC, line DE || BC with D on AB, E on AC. Then AD/AB = AE/AC. If AD=3, AB=12, AE=4, then AC must be 16 to maintain the ratio 1/4.

## Common Mistakes

- **Thinking you need all three angles given**: Two suffice; the third is automatic.
- **Reversing the correspondence**: Make sure the equal angles are matched to the correct vertices when writing the similarity statement.
- **Confusing AA similarity with AA congruence**: There is no AA congruence; equal angles alone do not fix size.
- **Forgetting that sides must be corresponding**: When setting up proportions, the sides opposite corresponding angles go together.

The isosceles triangle theorem (also called the base angles theorem) states that if two sides of a triangle are equal, then the angles opposite those sides are equal.

The converse is also true: if two angles are equal, the sides opposite them are equal.

## Statement and Converse

<!-- section: statement-converse -->

In △ABC, if AB = AC, then ∠B = ∠C (base angles equal).

Converse: if ∠B = ∠C, then AB = AC.

**ELI5**

- If two sides are the same length, the "base" angles at the bottom must be the same; the triangle is "balanced."
- If two angles are the same, the sides facing them must be the same length.

**Worked Example:**

△ABC with AB = AC = 5, base BC = 6. Then base angles at B and C are equal. By law of cosines or dropping altitude, each is arccos((25+ (3)^2 - something wait) but they are equal by the theorem without calculation.

## Proof

<!-- section: proof -->

Euclid proves the theorem (Prop. I.5, pons asinorum) by constructing the angle bisector from the apex, creating two congruent triangles by SAS, then corresponding parts give the base angles equal.

A modern short proof uses SAS on the two triangles formed by the angle bisector or altitude.

**ELI5**

- Draw the "middle line" from the top vertex straight down the middle of the base. This splits the triangle into two mirror-image right triangles that match by SAS or SSS.
- Because the two small triangles are congruent, their bottom angles must be equal.

**Worked Example:**

Let M be the midpoint of base BC in isosceles △ABC with AB=AC. Then AM is also the angle bisector and altitude. △ABM and △ACM have AB=AC (given), BM=CM (midpoint), AM common. By SSS the triangles are congruent, so ∠ABM = ∠ACM.

## Applications and Equilateral Case

<!-- section: applications-equilateral -->

When all three sides equal (equilateral), all angles are 60° (corollary of the theorem applied three times).

The theorem is used constantly in proofs involving symmetry and in constructing equilateral triangles.

**ELI5**

- An equilateral triangle is a special isosceles where every side is a "base," so every angle must be equal — and they turn out to be 60°.
- Many constructions and proofs "assume isosceles to get equal angles for free."

**Worked Example:**

Construct equilateral triangle on base BC: from B draw arc radius BC, from C draw arc radius CB; they intersect at A. Then AB=BC=CA by construction (radii), so all angles 60° by repeated application of the isosceles theorem.

## Common Mistakes

- **Applying only to the base angles**: The equal angles are the ones opposite the equal sides (the base angles if the equal sides are the legs).
- **Forgetting the converse**: Equal angles imply equal opposite sides; this is useful for proving sides equal.
- **Assuming the altitude is always needed**: The theorem holds without constructing anything; the proof may use construction but the statement does not.
- **Confusing with similarity**: Isosceles triangles are not necessarily similar to each other unless angles match.

The triangle inequality states that the sum of any two sides of a triangle must be greater than the third side.

It is both a necessary and sufficient condition (with positivity) for three lengths to form a triangle, and it generalizes to the shortest-path property of straight lines in metric spaces.

## The Three Inequalities

<!-- section: three-inequalities -->

For sides a, b, c of a triangle: a + b > c, a + c > b, b + c > a.

Equality holds only in the degenerate case when the "triangle" is flat (points collinear).

**ELI5**

- You cannot have two short sides that together are shorter than or equal to the third; the ends would not meet to close the shape.
- "The shortest distance between two points is a straight line" is the geometric reason: going via the third vertex would be longer.

**Worked Example:**

Sides 3, 4, 5: 3+4=7 >5, 3+5=8>4, 4+5=9>3. Valid right triangle.

Sides 2, 3, 6: 2+3=5 <6. Cannot form a triangle; the sides collapse flat.

## Proof and Strict Inequality

<!-- section: proof-strict -->

Proof: the straight line is the shortest path. The path a + b is a broken line from one end of c to the other; it is longer than the direct segment c unless the break point is on the segment (degenerate).

Using triangle inequality in vector terms or coordinates: |u + v| ≤ |u| + |v|, with equality iff u and v parallel and same direction.

**ELI5**

- Walking two sides of the triangle takes you from start to end, but the direct side is shorter or equal.
- Only when you walk in a straight line (no bend) do the lengths add exactly.

**Worked Example:**

In the plane, place side c on the x-axis from (0,0) to (c,0). The third vertex at (x,y). Then distance to (0,0) is a, to (c,0) is b. By straight-line distance, a + b > c always when y \neq 0.

## Generalizations and Applications

<!-- section: generalizations -->

In any metric space the distance satisfies d(x,z) \le d(x,y) + d(y,z). In Euclidean geometry the inequality is strict unless points are collinear.

Used to prove the triangle inequality for vectors, norms, and in analysis (Minkowski inequality etc.).

In geometry it implies that the perimeter is greater than any side, useful in optimization and isoperimetric problems.

**ELI5**

- "Going via a detour point is never shorter than going direct." This is true on maps, in graphs, and in curved spaces (with care).
- GPS route planners use generalized versions when adding "via" points.

**Worked Example:**

In vector terms, |A + B| \le |A| + |B|. For A = (3,0), B = (0,4), |A+B| = 5, |A|+|B|=7 >5. Equality only if A and B point the same way, e.g. B=2A then |A+B|=3|A| = |A| + |B|.

## Common Mistakes

- **Using \ge instead of > for actual triangles**: Strict inequality for non-degenerate (positive area) triangles.
- **Checking only one of the three inequalities**: All three must hold.
- **Confusing with the Pythagorean relation**: Pythagorean is equality for right triangles after squaring; triangle inequality is the linear version for any triangle.
- **Thinking it only applies to sides**: The inequality applies to any three points in the plane: the distances between them must satisfy it.
