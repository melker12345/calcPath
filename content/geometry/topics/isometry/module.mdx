# Isometry

An isometry is a transformation of the plane (or space) that preserves distances between points. It is a 'rigid motion' — no stretching, shrinking, or bending.

The isometries of the plane are translations, rotations, reflections, and glide reflections.

## Definition and Preservation Properties

<!-- section: definition-preservation -->

An isometry f satisfies d(f(P), f(Q)) = d(P, Q) for all points P, Q.

Consequently isometries preserve lengths, angles (up to orientation for reflections), and congruence of figures.

**ELI5**

- Isometries are the ways you can move a shape without distorting it: slide, turn, flip.
- After the move, any two points are still exactly as far apart as before.

**Worked Example:**

Translation by vector (3,4): every point (x,y) maps to (x+3, y+4). Distance between images = distance between originals because the shift is the same for all.

## Classification of Plane Isometries

<!-- section: classification -->

- Translation: slide by a vector (no fixed points unless zero).
- Rotation: turn around a fixed point by an angle.
- Reflection: flip over a line (mirror).
- Glide reflection: reflect over a line then translate parallel to that line.

Orientation-preserving: translations and rotations. Orientation-reversing: reflections and glides.

**ELI5**

- Slide or spin = keeps 'handedness' (left and right stay left and right).
- Flip or glide-flip = reverses handedness (your left hand becomes a right-hand image).

**Worked Example:**

Rotation 90° counterclockwise around origin: (x,y) \to (-y, x). This preserves distances (rotation matrix is orthogonal).

## Isometries and Congruence

<!-- section: congruence-isom -->

Two figures are congruent iff there exists an isometry mapping one onto the other.

The triangle congruence criteria (SSS etc.) are shortcuts that guarantee such an isometry exists.

**ELI5**

- Congruent means you can rigidly move one to exactly cover the other.
- The criteria (SSS, SAS...) are the 'tests' that tell you without actually moving whether such a rigid motion exists.

**Worked Example:**

If two triangles satisfy SSS, then there is a unique isometry (actually several if reflections allowed) taking one to the other. The proof of SSS constructs or proves existence of that isometry.

## Common Mistakes

- **Including scaling or shear as isometries**: Those change distances; they are similarities or affine maps.
- **Forgetting glide reflections**: In the full classification of plane isometries, glides are the fourth type.
- **Assuming isometries always have fixed points**: Translations (non-zero) have none.
- **Confusing 'preserve distance' with 'preserve the figure'**: The transformation preserves distances between any points; the figure is mapped to a congruent copy.
