# SAS congruence

SAS (Side-Angle-Side) congruence: if two sides and the included angle of one triangle are equal to two sides and the included angle of another, the triangles are congruent.

This is one of the most frequently used criteria because the included angle "locks" the shape between the two sides.

## The SAS Criterion

<!-- section: sas-criterion -->

If in △ABC and △DEF, AB = DE, ∠B = ∠E, and BC = EF, then △ABC ≅ △DEF.

The angle must be the included angle (between the two sides).

**ELI5**

- Think of two rods of fixed length with a hinge between them set at a fixed angle. The distance between the free ends is forced; the whole triangle is rigid.
- The angle is the "glue" that fixes how the sides open.

**Worked Example:**

△ABC has AB=5, ∠ABC=60°, BC=7. △DEF has DE=5, ∠DEF=60°, EF=7. Then △ABC ≅ △DEF by SAS. The third sides AC and DF are equal, and the other angles match.

## Proof Ideas

<!-- section: proof-ideas -->

Euclid proves SAS as Prop. I.4 by superposition: place the equal angle and sides on top of each other; the third vertices must coincide because the sides are equal.

Modern treatments may use coordinates or the law of cosines to show the third side matches, then SSS.

**ELI5**

- Lay one triangle exactly on top of the other so the two sides and the angle between them match perfectly. Because the sides are the same length, the remaining vertex has nowhere else to go.
- The proof shows the figures coincide completely.

**Worked Example:**

Place ∠B on ∠E so the rays coincide. Then point A lands on D because AB=DE. Point C lands on F because BC=EF. Therefore the segments AC and DF coincide and the triangles are identical.

## Included vs Non-included Angle

<!-- section: included-angle -->

The angle must lie between the two given sides. If the angle is not included (SSA), the criterion fails in general and the ambiguous case can occur (zero, one, or two possible triangles).

**ELI5**

- The angle has to be the "middle" one between the two sides you are given. If it is at the end, the side can swing and create different possible positions for the third vertex.
- SSA is the "dangerous" case taught in trigonometry as the ambiguous case.

**Worked Example:**

Given sides 10 and 6 with included 30° angle: unique triangle. Given sides 10 and 6 with the 30° angle opposite the side of length 6: possibly two triangles (one where the 6-side swings acute, one obtuse).

## Common Mistakes

- **Using SSA instead of SAS**: Students often forget to check whether the given angle is included.
- **Assuming SAS works for any two sides and any angle**: The angle must be between the sides.
- **Forgetting to verify correspondence**: The equal angle must be between the pairs of equal sides in the correspondence.
- **Confusing with ASA**: ASA uses two angles and the included side; SAS uses two sides and the included angle.
