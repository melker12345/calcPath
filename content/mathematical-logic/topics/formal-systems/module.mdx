# Formal Systems and Proofs

A formal system gives a completely mechanical way to generate theorems from axioms using fixed rules of inference. The goal is to capture the notion of "provable" purely syntactically, without appeal to meaning, so that correctness of a proof can be checked by a machine (or a very careful human following rules blindly).

Classical examples: Hilbert-style axiomatic systems, natural deduction (Gentzen), sequent calculus. All of them aim at the same theorems when they are sound and complete for the same semantics.

## Formal Deduction and Inference Rules

<!-- section: deduction -->

Formal systems turn the intuitive idea of "proof" into a purely mechanical, syntax-driven process that a computer (or a meticulous clerk) can verify without ever understanding what the symbols mean. This separation of syntax from semantics is what makes logic foundational for computer science, automated theorem proving, and the study of what can and cannot be proved.

Every formal system has three essential ingredients: a precisely defined language of symbols and formulas, a collection of axioms (or axiom schemas) that are accepted without proof, and inference rules that generate new formulas from old ones. Different styles—Hilbert systems, natural deduction, sequent calculus—package these ingredients differently, yet they are inter-translatable when they are sound and complete for the same semantics.

In this section you will learn the common skeleton, see a concrete example of a formal proof, understand the crucial distinction between syntactic provability and semantic truth (soundness and completeness), and why these notions matter for the later incompleteness results.

In a formal system we have:

- A language (symbols, well-formed formulas).
- A set of axioms (or axiom schemas).
- Rules of inference (e.g., modus ponens: from $\phi$ and $\phi\to\psi$ infer $\psi$).

A **proof** of $\psi$ from assumptions $\Gamma$ is a finite sequence of formulas ending in $\psi$ where each line is an axiom, an assumption, or follows from earlier lines by a rule.

Natural deduction uses introduction and elimination rules for each connective and quantifier, producing tree-shaped or linear proofs that mirror intuitive reasoning.

**ELI5**

- Think of it as a board game with strict legal moves. Axioms are starting positions you may place for free. Rules tell you exactly how to add the next piece. If you can legally reach a position that spells your target formula, you have a proof.
- No "intuition" or "it feels right" is allowed; every step must cite a rule and prior lines.

**Worked Example:**

Using only implication rules and axioms of propositional logic, a proof of $p \to p$ (identity):

1. $p \to (p \to p)$          (axiom instance)
2. $(p \to (p \to p)) \to ((p \to p) \to (p \to p))$   (another)
3. $(p \to p) \to (p \to p)$  (MP on 1,2)
4. $p \to p$                 (MP on 3 and a copy of 3? — classic short proof exists via deduction theorem too)

In natural deduction the shortest is: assume $p$, discharge to get $p\to p$.

## Soundness

<!-- section: soundness -->

**Soundness** (or consistency of the proof system): If $\Gamma \vdash \phi$ (there is a formal proof of $\phi$ from $\Gamma$), then $\Gamma \models \phi$ (every model of $\Gamma$ is a model of $\phi$).

In plain words: you cannot prove false things from true premises using the rules. The syntactic consequence relation is contained in the semantic one.

Soundness is usually proved by induction on the length of proofs: axioms are valid, rules preserve validity.

**ELI5**

- Soundness is the "safety guarantee": the proof machine never lies. If it outputs "yes, this follows", then in reality (in every possible world consistent with your starting facts) the conclusion really does hold.
- If a system were unsound you could "prove" $2+2=5$ from true arithmetic axioms — a disaster for reliability.

**Worked Example:**

Modus ponens is sound: suppose $\mathcal{M} \models p$ and $\mathcal{M} \models p\to q$. By definition of $\to$, the only way the implication holds when antecedent true is if consequent true. Hence $\mathcal{M} \models q$.

## Completeness and Compactness

<!-- section: completeness -->

**Completeness** (Gödel 1929 for FOL): If $\Gamma \models \phi$ (semantic consequence), then $\Gamma \vdash \phi$ (there exists a formal proof).

Together with soundness we have the perfect match: syntactic provability = semantic entailment.

**Compactness theorem**: If every finite subset of $\Gamma$ is satisfiable, then the whole (possibly infinite) $\Gamma$ is satisfiable.

Compactness is equivalent to completeness in many settings and has powerful consequences (e.g., existence of non-standard models of arithmetic).

**ELI5**

- Completeness: if something is *true in all models* of your premises, then there is a finite formal proof of it that the rule-following machine can find.
- Compactness: if no finite piece of your theory leads to contradiction, the whole infinite theory is still consistent. You never need infinitely many reasons at once to get a contradiction.

**Worked Example:**

By compactness one can prove there exist models of the theory of dense linear orders without endpoints that are not isomorphic to the rationals (add constants for "infinitely many distinct elements between 0 and 1" etc.).

## Decidability

<!-- section: decidability -->

A theory (or logic) is **decidable** if there is an algorithm that, given any sentence, halts and correctly answers whether it is a theorem (or valid).

Propositional logic is decidable (truth tables or resolution).

First-order logic is **undecidable** (Church-Turing): no algorithm decides validity for arbitrary FOL sentences.

Many important fragments and theories are decidable (e.g., Presburger arithmetic, theory of real closed fields).

**ELI5**

- Decidable = a computer program can in principle answer "is this always true?" for any question you feed it, and it will finish with the right yes/no.
- FOL as a whole is undecidable: there will always be some formulas for which the program either loops forever or gives wrong answers on some inputs. Yet for any *particular* valid formula a proof exists and can be found by a (different) search procedure.

**Worked Example:**

Propositional SAT is decidable but NP-complete. You can solve small instances by truth table or DPLL; huge industrial instances require clever heuristics.

## Common Mistakes

- **Provability versus mathematical truth:** Conflating "provable in this system" with "true in mathematics" is wrong. A system can be sound for its intended semantics yet incomplete for all mathematical truth (Gödel).

- **Completeness is the deeper result:** Thinking soundness is the hard direction misses the point. Completeness is the deeper result (you can always find a proof when one "should" exist semantically).

- **Compactness applies to infinite theories:** Misapplying compactness only to finite theories is an error. Its power is precisely for infinite axiom sets (e.g., "there are infinitely many primes" expressed via infinitely many sentences "there is a prime > n" for each n).

- **Undecidability does not eliminate proofs:** Believing that because a logic is undecidable there are no proofs for anything is mistaken. On the contrary: every valid sentence still has a finite proof; we just cannot algorithmically decide in advance which sentences will have them.

- **Consistency versus soundness:** Confusing consistency (no proof of $\bot$) with soundness is common. A consistent but unsound system can still prove false statements about the world.
