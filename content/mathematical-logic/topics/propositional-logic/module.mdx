# Propositional Logic

Propositional logic (also called sentential logic or propositional calculus) is the simplest layer of formal logic. It studies how we combine whole statements ("propositions") using connectives such as "and", "or", "not", "implies", and "if and only if". Unlike predicate logic, it does not look inside the statements at objects or quantifiers; a proposition is treated as an indivisible atom that is either true or false.

This system gives us precise tools to analyze arguments, build truth tables, detect tautologies, and decide satisfiability. Every proof in mathematics ultimately rests on valid propositional reasoning, and computer science uses it for circuit design, automated reasoning, and SAT solvers.

## Propositions and Connectives

<!-- section: connectives -->

A **proposition** is a declarative sentence that is either true or false, but not both. Examples: "2 + 2 = 4", "It is raining", "Every even integer greater than 2 is the sum of two primes". We denote atomic propositions by lowercase letters: $p$, $q$, $r$, ...

The five standard connectives are:

- Negation: $\neg p$ ("not p") — true when $p$ is false.
- Conjunction: $p \land q$ ("p and q") — holds only when both are true.
- Disjunction: $p \lor q$ ("p or q", inclusive) — true when at least one true.
- Implication: $p \to q$ ("if p then q") — false only when $p$ true and $q$ false.
- Biconditional: $p \leftrightarrow q$ ("p if and only if q") — true when both same truth value.

Each has a precise truth table. The implication $p \to q$ is material implication; it does not require causation in natural language.

**ELI5**

- Think of propositions as light switches: on (true) or off (false). Connectives are wiring diagrams: AND requires both switches on, OR needs at least one, NOT flips the switch, IMPLIES is a one-way safety: if the first is on, the second must be too (otherwise the circuit "fails").
- Everyday: "If it rains, the ground is wet" is an implication. It is false only on the day it rains but the ground stays dry.

**Worked Example:**

Build the truth table for $p \to q$ (implication).

Columns for each subexpression, rows for all combinations:

| $p$ | $q$ | $p \to q$ |
|-----|-----|-----------|
| T   | T   | T         |
| T   | F   | F         |
| F   | T   | T         |
| F   | F   | T         |

Only one falsifying case (where the implication is false): when the antecedent $p$ is true but the consequent $q$ is false. All other rows are true. This shows why "false implies anything" is vacuously true.

## Truth Tables and Tautologies

<!-- section: tautologies -->

A **truth table** enumerates all $2^n$ possible assignments of truth values to $n$ atomic propositions and computes the value of the compound formula under each.

A formula is a **tautology** (or logically valid) if it evaluates to true no matter the assignment of truth values. A **contradiction** is false under every assignment. A **contingent** formula is true under some and false under others.

To check: build the full table or use shortcuts (e.g., assume false and derive contradiction for validity proofs).

**ELI5**

- A tautology is a statement that is "always safe", no matter what the facts are. "It is raining or it is not raining" is true even if you have no weather report. Tautologies are the logical skeleton that every correct argument must preserve.
- Building a truth table is like testing every possible combination of light-switch positions to see if the final bulb always lights.

**Worked Example:**

Prove that $p \lor \neg p$ is a tautology (law of excluded middle).

Build the table for the two possible assignments:

| $p$ | $\neg p$ | $p \lor \neg p$ |
|-----|----------|-----------------|
| T   | F        | T               |
| F   | T        | T               |

The formula evaluates to T in both rows, hence it is a tautology (always true, no matter the truth value of p). This is a foundational law in classical logic.

## Satisfiability and Validity

<!-- section: satisfiability -->

A formula (or set of formulas) is **satisfiable** if there is some truth assignment making the whole thing true. It is **valid** (a tautology) if it comes out true for every possible assignment. Unsatisfiable formulas are contradictions.

The **satisfiability problem** (SAT) asks whether a given formula in conjunctive normal form is satisfiable. It is NP-complete, yet central to verification, AI planning, and hardware design.

Two formulas are **logically equivalent** if they have identical truth tables (same value under every assignment). We write $p \equiv q$.

**ELI5**

- Satisfiable = there is some way the world could be that makes your claim true. "It is Tuesday and raining" is satisfiable (just wait for a rainy Tuesday).
- Valid = true no matter how the world is. "It is Tuesday or it is not Tuesday" cannot be false.
- A detective's hypothesis is satisfiable if consistent with evidence; a valid deduction is forced by the evidence alone.

**Worked Example:**

Is $(p \to q) \land (q \to r) \land p \land \neg r$ satisfiable?

Assume it is true. Then $p$ true, $r$ false. From $p \to q$ we get $q$ true. From $q \to r$ we get $r$ true. Contradiction with $\neg r$. Hence unsatisfiable.

## Logical Equivalence and Normal Forms

<!-- section: normal-forms -->

Every propositional formula is logically equivalent to one in **disjunctive normal form** (DNF: OR of ANDs) and one in **conjunctive normal form** (CNF: AND of ORs).

Useful equivalences (replace subformulas):

- Double negation: $\neg\neg p \equiv p$
- De Morgan: $\neg(p \land q) \equiv \neg p \lor \neg q$; $\neg(p \lor q) \equiv \neg p \land \neg q$
- Implication elimination: $p \to q \equiv \neg p \lor q$
- Distribution, commutativity, associativity, idempotence, etc.

**ELI5**

- Normal forms are "standardized recipes". CNF is like a checklist of requirements that must all hold ("AND of rules"); each rule is an "OR of allowed options". SAT solvers love CNF because it is easy to plug in values and see if any clause goes false.
- Converting is like algebraic simplification but for logic: you expand and cancel until only the two allowed connectives remain in the right pattern.

**Worked Example:**

Convert $p \to (q \land r)$ to CNF.

$p \to (q \land r) \equiv \neg p \lor (q \land r) \equiv (\neg p \lor q) \land (\neg p \lor r)$ (by distribution).

CNF with two clauses.

## Common Mistakes

- **Misreading implication:** The formula $p \to q$ is true whenever $p$ is false, regardless of $q$. This is the source of many vacuous-truth confusions; it does not mean "causes" or English "only if".

- **Affirming the consequent:** From $p \to q$ and $q$, one cannot conclude the antecedent. This inference is invalid (see truth table: when the antecedent is false and consequent true, the implication holds).

- **Denying the antecedent:** From $p \to q$ and $\neg p$, one cannot conclude $\neg q$. This inference is also invalid.

- **Confusing conjunction and disjunction:** "and/or" ambiguity in English often leads to mistakes when confusing $\land$ with $\lor$.

- **Exclusive versus inclusive or:** Most math uses inclusive $\lor$ (true if at least one is true).

- **Partial checks for tautologies:** You must check all $2^n$ rows; partial checks are insufficient.

- **Not provable versus false:** In pure propositional logic (no extra axioms), many contingent statements are neither provable nor disprovable.
