# Model Theory

Model theory studies the relationship between formal languages (syntax) and the mathematical structures that interpret them (semantics). It answers questions such as: when do two structures satisfy exactly the same sentences? How can we build new models from old ones? What properties are preserved under various constructions?

It is the branch of logic that most directly connects syntax to "what the world could be like."

## Structures and Satisfaction

<!-- section: structures -->

Model theory lives at the interface between the purely syntactic world of formal languages and the semantic world of concrete mathematical structures that "make the sentences true." A structure (or model) supplies a universe of objects together with interpretations of the symbols in the language; satisfaction tells you whether a given sentence comes out true in that universe.

The satisfaction relation is defined recursively, mirroring the inductive construction of formulas: atomic formulas are checked directly against the interpretations, Boolean combinations follow truth tables, and quantifiers range over the domain of the structure. Two structures are elementarily equivalent when they satisfy exactly the same first-order sentences, even if they are not isomorphic.

In this section you will learn the precise definition of a structure, how satisfaction works, the distinction between theories and models, and the basic constructions (elementary submodels, ultrapowers) that let you build new models with prescribed properties.

A **structure** (or model) $\mathcal{M}$ for a first-order language consists of a domain plus interpretations for all the constant, function, and relation symbols.

The **satisfaction relation** $\mathcal{M} \models \phi[\mathbf{a}]$ (read: $\mathcal{M}$ satisfies formula $\phi$ under assignment $\mathbf{a}$) is defined recursively on formula complexity:

- Atomic: by the interpretation of the predicate/relation.
- Boolean connectives: classical truth tables.
- Quantifiers: $\mathcal{M} \models \forall x \phi[x,\mathbf{a}]$ iff for every $b$ in the domain, $\mathcal{M} \models \phi[b,\mathbf{a}]$.

For sentences (no free variables) we simply write $\mathcal{M} \models \phi$.

**ELI5**

- A structure is a concrete "world" together with names for its important objects, functions, and relations. The natural numbers with + and < is one structure for the language of arithmetic.
- Satisfaction is "the sentence comes out true when you plug in the real meanings in this world."

**Worked Example:**

Structure: domain $\{0,1,2\}$, constant $0$ interpreted as 0, unary function $S$ as successor mod 3, relation $=$ usual.

Then this model satisfies $\forall x (S(S(S(x))) = x)$ but does *not* satisfy the induction schema for all formulas (it is not the standard naturals).

## Elementary Equivalence and Elementary Substructures

<!-- section: elementary -->

Two structures $\mathcal{M}$ and $\mathcal{N}$ are **elementarily equivalent** ($\mathcal{M} \equiv \mathcal{N}$) if they satisfy exactly the same first-order sentences: for every sentence $\phi$, $\mathcal{M} \models \phi \iff \mathcal{N} \models \phi$.

They may be non-isomorphic yet elementarily equivalent (e.g., $(\mathbb{Q},<)$ and $(\mathbb{R},<)$ are both dense linear orders without endpoints, hence elementarily equivalent by quantifier elimination).

A substructure $\mathcal{N} \subseteq \mathcal{M}$ is an **elementary substructure** ($\mathcal{N} \preceq \mathcal{M}$) if for every formula $\phi(\mathbf{x})$ and tuple from $\mathcal{N}$, $\mathcal{N} \models \phi[\mathbf{a}] \iff \mathcal{M} \models \phi[\mathbf{a}]$.

**ELI5**

- Elementary equivalence: the two worlds answer "yes" or "no" to exactly the same first-order questions, even if one is "bigger" or has extra hidden structure that first-order logic cannot see.
- Elementary submodel: when you zoom in on a sub-universe, every first-order fact that was true there remains true in the big universe (and vice versa for the sub-universe).

**Worked Example:**

By the Tarski-Vaught test (or back-and-forth), the rationals are an elementary submodel of the reals for the language of ordered fields? Actually for dense orders without endpoints they are elementarily equivalent, but the substructure relation holds for the order language.

## Ultraproducts and Łoś's Theorem

<!-- section: ultraproducts -->

Given a family of structures $\{\mathcal{M}_i\}_{i\in I}$ and an ultrafilter $U$ on $I$, the **ultraproduct** $\prod_U \mathcal{M}_i$ is the quotient of the direct product by the equivalence "equal on a large set (in $U$)".

**Łoś's theorem**: A first-order sentence holds in the ultraproduct if and only if it holds in "almost all" (i.e., $U$-large set of) the factors.

This is a powerful model-building tool: you can take ultraproducts of finite structures to obtain infinite ones that still satisfy the same first-order sentences.

**ELI5**

- Ultraproduct is a "majority vote" construction across many worlds. A property holds in the big new world precisely when it holds in a "large" (according to the ultrafilter) number of the original worlds.
- It lets logicians manufacture exotic models while preserving all first-order truths.

**Worked Example:**

Take countably many copies of the standard naturals $\mathbb{N}$. Let $U$ be a non-principal ultrafilter on $\mathbb{N}$. The ultraproduct is a non-standard model of arithmetic: it contains "infinite" numbers (equivalence classes of sequences that go to infinity on a large set) yet satisfies exactly the same first-order sentences as $\mathbb{N}$.

## The Completeness Theorem Revisited

<!-- section: completeness -->

Gödel's completeness theorem can be stated model-theoretically: a set of sentences $\Gamma$ is consistent (no proof of contradiction) if and only if it has a model.

The proof usually proceeds by building a term model (Henkin construction) or via compactness + ultraproducts.

This gives a tight link: syntactic consistency ⇔ semantic satisfiability.

**ELI5**

- If your theory never lets the formal proof machine derive a contradiction, then there really exists some mathematical structure (possibly huge and weird) in which all your axioms come out true.
- This is why we can confidently study "the theory of groups" — consistency (easy to see) guarantees models exist.

**Worked Example:**

The theory of algebraically closed fields of characteristic zero is complete (by quantifier elimination + axioms) and has many models (algebraic closure of $\mathbb{Q}$, of $\mathbb{C}(t)$, etc.), all elementarily equivalent.

## Common Mistakes

- **Elementary equivalence is not isomorphism:** Equating elementary equivalence with isomorphism is a mistake. First-order logic cannot distinguish all structures; cardinality, higher-order properties, and "standardness" often require second-order or infinitary logic.

- **Same sentences does not mean identical:** Thinking that if two structures satisfy the same sentences they are "the same" is incorrect. They can differ wildly (different cardinalities, non-isomorphic).

- **Domains are non-empty:** Forgetting that the domain of a structure must be non-empty leads to errors. Empty structures are usually disallowed in classical FOL.

- **Substructure versus elementary substructure:** Confusing substructure with elementary substructure is common. A subring need not be an elementary subring.

- **Principal ultrafilters give ordinary products:** Believing ultraproducts always produce "non-standard" models is wrong. If the ultrafilter is principal the ultraproduct is just one of the factors.

- **Categoricity is rare:** Assuming every complete theory has a unique model up to isomorphism (categoricity) is false for most. $\aleph_0$-categoricity is rare and characterized by the Ryll-Nardzewski theorem.
