# Incompleteness and Independence

Gödel's incompleteness theorems (1931) are among the most profound results in the foundations of mathematics. They show that any sufficiently strong consistent formal system for arithmetic is necessarily incomplete: there are true statements about the natural numbers that the system can never prove.

Later work (Cohen's forcing, 1963) showed that many important questions in set theory (most famously the Continuum Hypothesis) are independent of ZFC — neither provable nor refutable from the standard axioms.

## Gödel's First Incompleteness Theorem

<!-- section: godel1 -->

Gödel's first incompleteness theorem is one of the deepest results in the foundations of mathematics: it shows that no sufficiently powerful consistent formal system for arithmetic can be complete. There will always be true arithmetic statements that the system can neither prove nor disprove.

The hypotheses are crucial: the system must be consistent (otherwise it proves everything), it must be recursively axiomatizable (its axioms must be effectively listable), and it must be strong enough to express basic arithmetic (able to represent the recursive functions or at least Robinson arithmetic Q). Under those conditions one can construct, inside the system itself, a sentence that says "I am not provable in this system."

In this section you will learn the precise statement, the role of the diagonal lemma that produces self-referential sentences, why the Gödel sentence is true but unprovable, and how the second incompleteness theorem and forcing extend the story to consistency and independence.

**First Incompleteness Theorem**: Let $T$ be a consistent, recursively axiomatizable formal system that is strong enough to represent basic arithmetic (e.g., can interpret Robinson arithmetic Q or Peano arithmetic). Then there exists a sentence $\phi$ (the "Gödel sentence") such that:

- $T \nvdash \phi$
- $T \nvdash \neg\phi$

Moreover, if $T$ is sound (proves only true sentences), then $\phi$ is actually true (in the standard model $\mathbb{N}$).

The proof uses the **diagonal lemma** (fixed-point theorem): for any formula $\psi(x)$ there is a sentence $\phi$ such that $T \vdash \phi \leftrightarrow \psi(\ulcorner\phi\urcorner)$, where $\ulcorner\phi\urcorner$ is the Gödel number (code) of $\phi$.

**ELI5**

- Imagine a powerful but consistent book of rules for proving facts about numbers. Gödel shows you can construct a sentence that asserts its own unprovability within the system.
- If the book could prove it, it would be proving something false (that it is provable when it isn't). If it could prove the negation, it would be inconsistent. Hence the sentence is true but unprovable inside the system.

**Worked Example:**

The Gödel sentence for Peano arithmetic PA can be read as "There is no natural number that codes a PA-proof of this very sentence." Its existence follows from the diagonal lemma applied to the provability predicate $\text{Prov}_{PA}(x)$.

## Gödel's Second Incompleteness Theorem

<!-- section: godel2 -->

**Second Incompleteness Theorem**: Under the same assumptions on $T$, if $T$ is consistent then $T$ does not prove $\text{Con}(T)$ (its own consistency statement).

Here $\text{Con}(T)$ is the arithmetic sentence asserting that there is no number coding a proof of $0=1$ from the axioms of $T$.

**ELI5**

- Not only are there true-but-unprovable sentences; even the statement "this system will never prove something contradictory" cannot be proved inside the system itself (assuming it is consistent).
- This dooms Hilbert's program of finding a finitary consistency proof for all of mathematics inside a weak system.

**Worked Example:**

PA does not prove its own consistency. Gentzen gave a consistency proof for PA, but it uses transfinite induction up to a certain ordinal $\varepsilon_0$ (beyond what PA can handle internally).

## Proof Theory and Ordinal Analysis

<!-- section: proof-theory -->

**Proof theory** studies the strength of formal systems by measuring the "proof-theoretic ordinals" needed to prove their consistency.

- Gentzen: $\text{Con}(\text{PA})$ is equivalent (over a weak base) to transfinite induction up to $\varepsilon_0$.
- Stronger systems (second-order arithmetic, set theory) have larger proof-theoretic ordinals.
- **Ordinal analysis** assigns to each theory a recursive ordinal that measures its consistency strength.

**ELI5**

- Instead of just saying "this theory is consistent," proof theorists give a precise "how strong" measure in terms of how far you have to go into the transfinite ordinals before you can prove no contradiction appears.
- This gives a hierarchy of consistency strengths and lets us compare theories that cannot interpret each other.

**Worked Example:**

The proof-theoretic ordinal of PA is a certain countable ordinal $\varepsilon_0$ (the limit of the sequence omega, omega^omega, ...). Gentzen's consistency proof uses induction along this ordinal.

## Forcing and Independence Results

<!-- section: forcing -->

**Forcing** (Cohen 1963) is a technique for building new models of set theory from old ones by "adding generic sets" while preserving the truth of the axioms of ZFC.

Using forcing one can prove the independence of the **Continuum Hypothesis** (CH): there exist models of ZFC where CH holds and models where it does not.

Many other statements are independent: the Axiom of Choice (relative to ZF), existence of measurable cardinals, etc.

**Inner models** (Gödel's constructible universe $L$) give models where CH and AC hold.

**ELI5**

- Forcing is like carefully "adding new real numbers" (or other sets) to a model of set theory in a controlled way that doesn't break the ZFC axioms. Different choices of what you add can make the continuum large or small.
- It shows that ZFC by itself does not decide many natural questions about sets; you need additional axioms (large cardinals, determinacy, forcing axioms, etc.) to settle them.

**Worked Example:**

Cohen's original forcing added $\aleph_2$ many Cohen reals, making $2^{\aleph_0} = \aleph_2 > \aleph_1$ in the extension, while the ground model could satisfy GCH. Hence CH is independent.

## Common Mistakes

- **Crucial hypotheses for incompleteness:** Misstating the first incompleteness theorem as "there are true statements that cannot be proved" omits the requirements. The system must be consistent, recursively axiomatizable, and able to express enough arithmetic.

- **Self-reference is not essential:** Thinking incompleteness applies only to "weird" self-referential sentences is mistaken. While the Gödel sentence is self-referential, incompleteness propagates to many "ordinary" mathematical statements via interpretability (e.g., consistency statements, combinatorial principles).

- **Knowing consistency is still possible:** Believing that the second incompleteness theorem says "we can never know if mathematics is consistent" is wrong. We have good evidence (from stronger theories or from believing in the standard model) even if the weak theory cannot prove its own consistency.

- **Independence is not absolute:** Confusing "independent of ZFC" with "undecidable in principle" or "meaningless" is an error. Many independent statements become decided once we adopt additional natural axioms.

- **Research on CH continues:** Assuming that because CH is independent, set theorists have "given up" on the continuum problem is incorrect. Active research continues on which additional axioms best capture "the true" universe of sets (large cardinals, forcing axioms, etc.).

- **Many unprovable truths exist:** Thinking Gödel sentences are the only unprovable truths is false. There are infinitely many, and the set of theorems is a proper subset of the truths in the standard model.
