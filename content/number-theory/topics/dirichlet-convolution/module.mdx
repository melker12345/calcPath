# Dirichlet Convolution

Dirichlet convolution is the binary operation on arithmetic functions that makes the set of arithmetic functions into a ring, with the constant function 1 as the multiplicative identity and the Möbius function as its inverse. It is the natural "multiplication" when studying sums over divisors.

If f and g are arithmetic functions (defined on positive integers), their Dirichlet convolution (f * g)(n) = sum_{d|n} f(d) g(n/d).

This operation is associative, commutative, distributive over pointwise addition, and turns many number-theoretic identities into simple algebraic statements (e.g. φ * 1 = id, μ * 1 = ε).

Understanding convolution is essential for Möbius inversion, the theory of multiplicative functions, and generating functions (Dirichlet series multiply under convolution).

## Definition and Basic Examples

<!-- section: convolution-definition -->

The Dirichlet convolution of two arithmetic functions f,g is the function h = f * g defined by
\[
h(n) = \sum_{d \mid n} f(d) \, g\left(\frac{n}{d}\right).
\]

It is the sum of products over complementary divisor pairs.

Examples:

- (1 * 1)(n) = sum_{d|n} 1 = d(n) (number of divisors).
- (id * 1)(n) = sum d |n d *1 ? Wait id(n)=n, so sum_{d|n} d * 1(n/d) = sum d where d|n d = σ(n) the sum-of-divisors.
- φ * 1 = id (the identity function id(n)=n).

**ELI5**

- Convolution "multiplies" two functions by looking at all ways to split n = d * (n/d) and adding the product f(d) g(n/d). It's like the Cauchy product but for the divisor lattice instead of addition.
- It turns "cumulative sums over divisors" into multiplication in the ring of functions.

**Worked Example:**

**Compute (1 * μ)(n)**

(1 * μ)(n) = sum_{d|n} 1(d) μ(n/d) = sum_{d|n} μ(d)  (reindex).

We know this is ε(n): 1 if n=1, 0 else. This is the statement that μ is the inverse of 1 under * .

## The Ring of Arithmetic Functions

<!-- section: convolution-ring -->

With pointwise addition (f+g)(n)=f(n)+g(n) and Dirichlet convolution *, the set of all arithmetic functions forms a commutative ring. The multiplicative identity (unit) for the convolution operation * is the function ε defined by ε(1) = 1 and ε(n) = 0 for all n > 1.

Then for any f we have (f * ε)(n) = f(n), because only the term d = n (where n/d = 1) survives in the sum.

The constant function u(n) = 1 for all n (often called the unit function u or 1) is invertible; its inverse is precisely the Möbius function μ, since u * μ = ε. This algebraic statement is equivalent to the classical Möbius inversion formula.

The units (invertible elements) of the ring are the arithmetic functions f with f(1) ≠ 0.

**ELI5**

- You can add functions pointwise (like vectors) and "multiply" them via convolution (like polynomials whose degrees multiply under the divisor splitting).
- The true "number 1" (multiplicative identity) in this algebra is the function ε that is 1 only at 1 and 0 elsewhere. The constant-1 function u has "reciprocal" μ (u * μ = ε).

**Worked Example:**

**Associativity check (small n)**

The operation is associative because both (f*g)*h and f*(g*h) expand to triple sums over d1 d2 d3 with d1 d2 d3 |n of f(d1)g(d2)h(d3), just grouped differently. The divisor lattice is associative.

## Multiplicative Functions and Convolution

<!-- section: multiplicative-convolution -->

A function f is multiplicative if f(1)=1 and f(m n)=f(m)f(n) whenever gcd(m,n)=1.

If f and g are multiplicative, so is f*g.

The set of multiplicative functions is closed under convolution and pointwise operations in nice ways.

Completely multiplicative functions (f(mn)=f(m)f(n) always) are also closed under * in some cases.

The Dirichlet series of a convolution is the product of the series: if F(s)=sum f(n)n^{-s}, G for g, then F G = sum (f*g)(n) n^{-s}.

**ELI5**

- Multiplicative functions are determined by their values at prime powers, and convolution respects the independence of distinct primes (by CRT or unique factorization).
- This is why the Euler product for zeta multiplies nicely, and inverses like 1/zeta have Euler product too.

**Worked Example:**

**φ is multiplicative, 1 is, their convolution id(n)=n is multiplicative.**

Yes, known.

## Common Mistakes

- **Index off-by-one in sum**: Always d | n, f(d) g(n/d); swapping is ok because commutative, but forgetting the /d or using sum f(d) g(d) is wrong.
- **Confusing with Cauchy product of series**: Dirichlet convolution corresponds to product of Dirichlet series, not ordinary generating functions.
- **Forgetting that the identity is the constant 1, not the unit ε**: u=1 is the identity for *: f * 1 = f. ε is the identity for ordinary multiplication of values at 1 or for other structures.
- **Applying inversion without the sum form**: Always verify g = f * 1 before writing f = g * μ.
- **Multiplicativity vs complete multiplicativity**: Many functions like φ, μ, d(n) are multiplicative but not completely (values at p^k not simply powers of value at p).
