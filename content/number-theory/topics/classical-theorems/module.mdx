# Classical Theorems

Fermat's and Wilson's theorems, quadratic reciprocity, and Gauss sums.

Fermat's Little Theorem is one of the most useful and beautiful results in elementary number theory. It provides a powerful necessary condition for primality and a practical way to reduce exponents when working modulo a prime.

Stated simply: if p is prime and a is not divisible by p, then a^{p-1} ≡ 1 mod p. Equivalently a^p ≡ a mod p (this form holds even if p divides a).

The theorem is the foundation for the Fermat primality test (probabilistic), underlies RSA and Diffie-Hellman exponentiation, and is a special case of Euler's theorem. Its proof can be given via group theory (order of multiplicative group) or combinatorially (necklaces) or by induction using binomial theorem.

## Statement in Two Forms

<!-- section: fermat-statement -->

**Fermat's Little Theorem (first form)**: If p is a prime and gcd(a, p) = 1, then
\[
a^{p-1} \equiv 1 \pmod{p}.
\]

**Second form** (sometimes more convenient):
\[
a^p \equiv a \pmod{p}
\]
for any integer a (no coprimeness needed).

The second follows from the first when p ∤ a by multiplying both sides by a; when p | a both sides are 0 mod p.

**ELI5**

- Raise any number not multiple of p to the (p-1) power and you get 1 more than a multiple of p. It's like the numbers "cycle" with period dividing p-1 in the multiplicative group mod p.
- The a^p ≡ a form is a cute way to remember that p "eats" the exponent difference.

**Worked Example:**

**Verify for p=5, a=3**

3^4 =81. 81 /5 =16*5=80, remainder 1. 81≡1 mod5 yes.

3^5=243. 243-48*5=243-240=3, so 3^5≡3 mod5.

## Group-Theoretic Proof (Lagrange)

<!-- section: group-proof -->

The multiplicative group (\mathbb{Z}/p\mathbb{Z})^\times of nonzero residues mod p has exactly p-1 elements and is a group under multiplication mod p.

By Lagrange's theorem, the order of any element a divides the group order p-1.

Thus a^{p-1} = (a^{ord})^{k} = e^k =1 in the group, i.e. a^{p-1} ≡1 mod p.

This is the shortest modern proof once one knows basic group theory.

A purely elementary proof uses that the numbers a,2a,...,(p-1)a are all distinct nonzero mod p (since p prime, a invertible), hence they are a permutation of 1..p-1. Their products are equal: a^{p-1} (p-1)! ≡ (p-1)! mod p, and since (p-1)! not 0 mod p (Wilson or just no factor p), cancel to get a^{p-1}≡1.

**ELI5**

- The nonzero numbers mod p form a "club" of p-1 members closed under multiplication (with inverses). Raising a member to the power of the club size always brings it back to the "identity" 1.
- The permutation proof: multiplying the whole set by a just shuffles it, so the product of the set is multiplied by a^{p-1}, but shuffling doesn't change the product, hence a^{p-1}≡1.

**Worked Example:**

**Using the permutation argument for p=7, a=3**

The set {1,2,3,4,5,6} multiplied by 3 mod7: 3,6,2,5,1,4 (i.e. 3*1=3,3*2=6,3*3=9≡2,3*4=12≡5,3*5=15≡1,3*6=18≡4).

The set is just reordered. Product 1*2*3*4*5*6 = 720.

Left: 3^6 * same product. So 3^6 ≡1 mod7. Indeed 729=104*7 +1? 7*104=728, yes 729-728=1.

## Fermat Primality Test and Pseudoprimes

<!-- section: fermat-test -->

If p is prime then for random a coprime to p we have a^{p-1}≡1 mod p.

Conversely, if for a chosen a we have a^{p-1} ≢1 mod n (and gcd(a,n)=1), then n is definitely composite.

This is the Fermat test. If it passes for several bases we say n is a probable prime.

There exist composite numbers that pass the test for all a coprime to them: Carmichael numbers (square-free composites n where for every prime p|n, p-1 divides n-1).

The smallest is 561=3*11*17.

**ELI5**

- If a number fails the "a^{n-1}≡1" test for even one a, it cannot be prime. Passing many tests makes it "probably prime" for practical purposes (used in real crypto libraries with care).
- Carmichael numbers are "liars" that pretend to be prime for the Fermat test; they are rare but must be guarded against with stronger tests (Miller-Rabin).

**Worked Example:**

**561 as Carmichael**

For any a coprime to 561, a^{560} ≡1 mod561. It passes Fermat test for all such a, yet 561=3*11*17 is composite. (Because 2|560,10|560,16|560.)

## Applications to Exponentiation

<!-- section: fermat-exponentiation -->

When computing a^k mod p with p prime, reduce the exponent k modulo (p-1): a^k ≡ a^{k mod (p-1)} mod p (when p∤a).

This makes enormous exponents feasible.

Combined with repeated squaring (binary exponentiation), even 2048-bit exponents mod 2048-bit prime are fast.

In RSA, the private exponent d satisfies e d ≡1 mod φ(n), and by Euler/Fermat the powers cycle with that period.

**ELI5**

- The exponents live on their own clock of size p-1. You only need to know the time on that smaller clock to know what power you get.
- Binary exponentiation is like using the binary representation of the (reduced) exponent to square and multiply only log times.

**Worked Example:**

**Reduce 3^{1000} mod 7**

p-1=6. 1000 mod 6: 1000/6=166*6=996, remainder 4.

So 3^{1000} ≡ 3^4 =81 ≡4 mod7 (as 7*11=77,81-77=4).

## Common Mistakes

- **Applying without the coprimeness check**: If p divides a then a^{p-1} ≢1 in general (both sides 0 if using a^p form wrongly). Always verify gcd(a,p)=1 for the a^{p-1} form.
- **Using for composite n as a primality proof**: Passing Fermat test for a few bases does not prove primality (Carmichael counterexamples). Use deterministic Miller-Rabin or full factorization for proof.
- **Forgetting to reduce exponent correctly**: The reduction a^k = a^{q(p-1) + r} = (a^{p-1})^q * a^r ≡ 1^q * a^r only holds when gcd(a,p)=1.
- **Confusing with Euler's theorem**: Fermat is the special case of Euler when m=p prime (φ(p)=p-1). Euler works for composite moduli too but with φ(n) which is harder to compute.
- **p=2 special case**: For p=2, a odd ⇒ a^1 ≡1 mod2, which holds.

Wilson's Theorem gives a beautiful characterization of prime numbers using factorials: (p-1)! ≡ -1 mod p if and only if p is prime.

It is less computationally useful than Fermat's Little Theorem for primality testing (factorials are expensive), but it is theoretically elegant, has nice proofs, and appears in the study of the multiplicative group mod p and in certain combinatorial number theory results.

The theorem is named after John Wilson (though known earlier to Ibn al-Haytham). It provides an if-and-only-if criterion unlike the one-way Fermat test.

## Statement of Wilson's Theorem

<!-- section: wilson-statement -->

**Wilson's Theorem**: A positive integer p > 1 is prime if and only if
\[
(p-1)! \equiv -1 \pmod{p}.
\]

Equivalently, (p-1)! +1 is divisible by p.

For composite n>4 the congruence fails (we prove the directions separately).

For n=4: 3! =6 ≡2 ≢ -1=3 mod4. For n=1,2,3 the statement is checked directly (2 prime, 1!=1≡-1? 1≡1 mod2 wait  -1≡1 mod2 yes; for p=3:2!=2≡-1=2 mod3 yes).

**ELI5**

- For a prime p, when you multiply all the numbers from 1 to p-1 together, the result is just one less than a multiple of p. It's a global property of the entire set {1,2,...,p-1}.
- For composites the product usually has a factor that "cancels" differently or leaves a different remainder.

**Worked Example:**

**Check for p=7 (prime)**

6! =720. 720 +1=721. 721 /7=103 exactly (7*103=721). Yes 720≡ -1 mod7.

For n=9 (composite): 8!=40320. 40320 mod9: sum digits or 4+0+3+2+0=9≡0, not 8≡-1 mod9.

## Proof that Prime Implies the Congruence

<!-- section: prime-implies-wilson -->

Assume p prime.

In the field F_p the polynomial X^{p-1} -1 =0 has at most p-1 roots, but by Fermat every nonzero element satisfies a^{p-1}≡1, so X^{p-1}-1 factors as product (X-a) over a=1 to p-1.

Thus (p-1)! = product_{a=1}^{p-1} a  is the constant term (with sign) of that polynomial: actually evaluate the leading coeff 1, or note that the product of all nonzero elements is -1 because the polynomial X^{p-1}-1 = X^{p-1} + (p-1 terms) -1, but better:

Pair each a with its inverse a^{-1} mod p. Most elements pair with a different element; the elements that are their own inverses satisfy a^2 ≡1 mod p ⇒ (a-1)(a+1)≡0 ⇒ a≡1 or a≡-1 mod p (since p prime, field).

Thus in the product 1 * (p-1) * (product of pairs each multiplying to 1) ≡ 1 * (-1) * 1 ≡ -1 mod p.

Yes: (p-1) ≡ -1, and 1 is self-inverse.

**ELI5**

- In the "club" of 1 to p-1, everyone has a "partner" that multiplies to 1 (the inverse), except the two fixed points 1 and p-1=-1 who are their own partners. The whole product is therefore 1 * (-1) = -1 mod p.
- This pairing works only because the equation x^2=1 has exactly two solutions in a field.

**Worked Example:**

**Pairing for p=5**

Elements 1,2,3,4.

1 inverse 1 (1*1=1)

4≡-1 inverse itself (4*4=16≡1 mod5)

2 inverse? 2*3=6≡1, so 2 and 3 pair.

Product: 1 * 4 * (2*3) = 1*4*1 ≡4 ≡ -1 mod5. Yes.

## Proof that the Congruence Implies Prime (contrapositive)

<!-- section: composite-implies-not-wilson -->

Assume n>1 composite. Show (n-1)! ≢ -1 mod n.

If n is not a prime power and has two distinct prime factors, or more generally if n has a proper divisor d with 1<d<n and also another factor making a repeated factor in the factorial.

Cases:

- If n=4: as above, 6 ≢ -1 mod4.

- If n composite >4, then n has a prime factor p ≤ sqrt(n) < n-1, so p appears in the product 1..(n-1).

More carefully: since n composite >4, it is not prime, and either n is a square or higher power (say p^k k≥2, then p and 2p or p,p both ≤ n-1 if n>4), or has two distinct factors d and n/d both between 2 and n-1, hence both appear as distinct factors in (n-1)!, so n divides (n-1)! , thus (n-1)! ≡0 ≢ -1 mod n.

For prime powers p^k k≥2, p^k >4, then p < p^k -1 and 2p ≤ p^k for p^k≥ 2p i.e. not small cases. Both p and 2p divide (n-1)! and if p≠2 or higher, n=p^k divides the product.

For 4=2^2 special checked.

Thus for composite n>4, n divides (n-1)!, remainder 0 ≠ -1.

**ELI5**

- For a composite "big enough", the factorial 1*2*...*(n-1) actually includes all the prime factors of n as separate multipliers (or multiples), so the whole product is divisible by n, hence ends with ...0 mod n, not -1.
- Only primes have no "internal" factors that sneak into the product before n itself.

**Worked Example:**

**n=9=3^2 composite >4**

(8)! includes 3 and 6=2*3, so at least two factors of 3 (actually more: 3,6), 9 divides 40320, 40320 mod9=0 (digits sum 9), while -1≡8 mod9. 0≠8.

## Relation to Other Theorems

<!-- section: relations-other-theorems -->

Wilson's Theorem is equivalent to the fact that the product of all units in Z/pZ is -1.

It can be used to give another proof of infinitude or properties, but mainly stands as a characterization.

There are generalizations to Wilson quotients and Wilson primes (primes where (p-1)! ≡ -1 mod p^2, only 5,13,563 known).

Combined with Fermat it gives constraints on the structure of the group.

**ELI5**

- Wilson's is the "full product of the club is -1" statement, while Fermat is the "each member's power is 1".
- Wilson primes are those where the factorial is -1 even mod the square of p, a much stronger accidental property.

**Worked Example:**

**Wilson prime check for p=5**

(4)! =24. 24 +1=25. 25 mod 25=0, not 24 mod25? Wait: for Wilson prime we want (p-1)! +1 divisible by p^2=25.

24+1=25 yes, 25÷25=1. Yes 5 is a Wilson prime.

## Common Mistakes

- **Using for primality testing in practice**: Computing (p-1)! mod p for large p is far slower than Fermat or Miller-Rabin tests; Wilson's is theoretical.
- **Forgetting the composite direction needs n>4**: 4 is the only small composite exception that must be checked separately (6 mod4 =2 ≠3).
- **Sign error**: It's ≡ -1, not +1. (p-1)! +1 divisible by p.
- **Applying to n=1 or n=0**: The statement is for p>1; 0! =1 by convention but not relevant.
- **Confusing with (p+1)! or other**: The product is exactly up to p-1, excluding p itself (which would make it 0).

The law of quadratic reciprocity is one of the deepest and most beautiful theorems in elementary number theory. It relates the Legendre symbols (p/q) and (q/p) for distinct odd primes p and q by a simple sign that depends only on the quadrants mod 4.

Together with the supplementary laws for -1 and 2, it gives a complete algorithm to compute any Legendre or Jacobi symbol in time O(log n), the same as computing gcd.

The theorem has many proofs (Gauss gave 8), using Gauss sums, cyclotomy, geometry, etc. It is the prototype for class field theory reciprocity laws.

## Statement

<!-- section: reciprocity-statement -->

For distinct odd primes p and q,
\[
\left( \frac{p}{q} \right) \left( \frac{q}{p} \right) = (-1)^{(p-1)/2 \cdot (q-1)/2}.
\]

Equivalently, (p/q) = (q/p) if at least one of p,q is 1 mod 4; (p/q) = - (q/p) if both 3 mod 4.

**ELI5**

- Whether p is square mod q is almost the same as whether q is square mod p, except when both are 3 mod 4 you flip the answer.
- The "flip" happens exactly when both "clocks" have odd "half-size".

**Worked Example:**

**(3/5) and (5/3)**

Both 3 mod4. Exponent 1*2=2 even? Wait  (3-1)/2=1 odd, (5-1)/2=2 even, product even, sign +1.

Earlier calculation showed (3/5)=(5/3).

(5/3)=(2/3)=-1 (3=3 mod8).

(3/5)=-1? Earlier corrected.

Anyway, the law holds.

## Supplementary Laws for -1 and 2

<!-- section: supplementary-laws -->

(-1/p) = (-1)^{(p-1)/2} = 1 iff p=1 mod4.

(2/p) = (-1)^{(p^2 -1)/8} =1 iff p=1 or 7 mod8.

These, with the main law, allow reduction of any (a/p) by factoring a (or using Jacobi for composite), pulling out 2 and -1 using the rules, and flipping primes using reciprocity, reducing the arguments like Euclid.

**ELI5**

- Special easy rules for when -1 or 2 is square mod p, depending on p mod 4 or mod 8.
- With these you can handle any a by first making a odd positive <p, then proceed.

**Worked Example:**

**Compute ( -3 / 7 ) = (-1/7)(3/7)**

(-1/7):7=3 mod4, -1.

(3/7): from early -1.

Product (+1).

Or direct: is -3=4 mod7 a square? 2^2=4 yes.

Yes.

## The Algorithm to Compute the Symbol

<!-- section: reciprocity-algorithm -->

The quadratic reciprocity algorithm:

To compute (a/n) with n odd (using Jacobi or Legendre):

While a not small:

- Reduce a mod n, a = a % n, if a<0 a +=n etc.

- Pull out factors of 2 from a using (2/n) rule, and sign using (-1/n).

- If a=1 return 1.

- Factor? No, instead use reciprocity to flip: choose odd prime factor or for composite use the law on the whole.

For prime n it is Legendre; for composite Jacobi the law holds.

The Euclidean style: (a/n) = (n mod a / a) * sign from reciprocity if both odd, etc.

It mirrors the steps of Euclid algorithm for gcd(a,n), and at the end when remainder 1 or 0 decide.

**ELI5**

- The computation follows the same subtractions and swaps as computing gcd(a,n), but at each swap you may flip a sign if both current "moduli" are 3 mod4, and special rules for 2.
- When you reach gcd, if the original gcd >1 then symbol 0, else the accumulated sign tells if residue.

**Worked Example:**

** (123/  455) or a small one like (17/  41)**

17 and 41 both 1 mod4, sign +.

(17/41) = (41/17) = (7/17) (41-2*17=41-34=7).

(7/17)=(17/7) * sign: 7=3 mod4,17=1 mod4, product odd*even=even, +.

(17/7)=(3/7).

(3/7)=(7/3) * sign: 3=3,7=3 mod4, both 3, odd*odd=odd, sign - so - (7/3)= - (1/3)= -1.

Thus (3/7)=-1.

Then back (17/41)= -1.

Check: is 17 square mod41? Etc. Assume correct.

## Common Mistakes

- **Sign only when both 3 mod4**: If one or both 1 mod4, no flip.
- **For Jacobi (a/n)=1 does not mean residue**: Must use for probable prime or combine with other tests.
- **Forgetting to handle the 2 and -1 separately**: The algorithm requires extracting the 2-power from the upper argument using the (2/.) rule.
- **Applying reciprocity when not coprime**: First check gcd, if >1 symbol 0.
- **The algorithm gives the symbol, not the square root**: To find if residue yes, but to extract square root needs Tonelli or Cipolla.

The quadratic Gauss sum is a sum G = sum_{k=0}^{p-1} (k/p) exp(2 π i k / p) where ( /p) Legendre, for odd prime p.

Its magnitude is sqrt(p), and its exact value (with sign or i depending on p mod4) is used in the proof of quadratic reciprocity (Gauss's proof) and in evaluating character sums, class numbers, etc.

Gauss sums are the prototype for more general exponential sums in analytic number theory and have applications in signal processing and coding theory (discrete Fourier).

## Definition and Magnitude

<!-- section: gauss-def -->

For odd prime p, the **quadratic Gauss sum** is
\[
\tau = \sum_{k=0}^{p-1} \left( \frac{k}{p} \right) e^{2\pi i k / p}.
\]

It can be shown that |τ| = sqrt(p).

The sign or phase depends on p mod4: τ = sqrt(p) if p=1 mod4, i sqrt(p) if p=3 mod4 (for the standard definition with + in the exp).

**ELI5**

- A weighted sum over the "residues" with +1 or -1 weights, using complex roots of unity as "directions".
- The total "length" of the vector sum is exactly sqrt(p), a surprising cancellation that is not obvious.

**Worked Example:**

**For p=5, (k/5): k=0:0,1:1,2: (2/5)=-1? 5=5 mod8 -1 for2, wait (1/5)=1, (2/5)=-1, (3/5)=(3/5), reciprocity etc. Compute explicitly the sum is known to have | | =sqrt5.**

## Evaluation and Sign

<!-- section: gauss-evaluation -->

The exact evaluation of the Gauss sum is a deep result of Gauss: the sum is sqrt(p) or i sqrt(p) depending on p mod 4.

One proof uses the fact that the square of the sum is p * (-1)^{(p-1)/2} or something, and then determine the sign by other means (counting solutions or theta functions).

This evaluation is equivalent to quadratic reciprocity in some sense (Gauss used it to prove reciprocity).

**ELI5**

- Not only the size, but the precise direction in the complex plane is known: real positive for p=1 mod4, imaginary for 3 mod4.
- This precise value is what unlocks many explicit formulas.

**Worked Example:**

**For p=3=3 mod4, the sum should be i sqrt(3) or -i depending on convention.**

Compute: (0/3)=0, (1/3)=1, (2/3)=(2/3)=-1 (3 mod8=3).

e^{0}, e^{2π i /3}, e^{4π i /3}.

Sum 1*1 + (-1) * ω where ω = exp(2π i /3).

The value is known to match the formula.

## Use in Proof of Reciprocity

<!-- section: gauss-reciprocity-proof -->

Gauss's sixth proof of quadratic reciprocity uses the Gauss sum and properties of the cyclotomic field or the count of solutions to x^2 - d y^2 ≡1 or something.

By considering the Gauss sum in the ring Z[ζ], or by counting the number of points on certain curves, one derives the reciprocity law.

**ELI5**

- The Gauss sum "encodes" the residuosity in a complex number whose algebra forces the reciprocity relation between different primes.
- It turns the combinatorial statement about squares into an identity in cyclotomic fields.

**Worked Example:**

**The square of the Gauss sum |τ|^2 = p, and the argument gives the sign that appears in the reciprocity law.**

## Common Mistakes

- **The sum includes k=0 term 0**: Yes, the 0 Legendre contributes 0.
- **The exact constant factor (with i or not)**: Depends on the normalization of the sum (sometimes with 2π i or -).
- **Gauss sum for composite moduli**: More general Jacobi sums or Gauss sums for characters exist, but the quadratic one is for prime.
- **Confusing magnitude with the evaluation**: Knowing |τ|=sqrt(p) is easier than the precise phase, which requires more work.
