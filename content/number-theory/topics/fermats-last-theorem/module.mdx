# Fermat's Last Theorem

Fermat's Last Theorem (FLT) states that there are no positive integers a,b,c,n with n>2 such that a^n + b^n = c^n.

Stated by Pierre de Fermat in 1637 in the margin of a book, with the claim "I have a truly marvelous proof which this margin is too narrow to contain."

It was proved by Andrew Wiles in 1994-95 (with a correction in 1995 with Taylor), using elliptic curves, modular forms, and the modularity theorem for semistable elliptic curves (the Taniyama-Shimura conjecture in the needed case).

The proof is one of the highest achievements of 20th century mathematics, connecting Diophantine equations to the Langlands program.

## Statement and Small Cases

<!-- section: flt-statement -->

For n>2, the equation a^n + b^n = c^n has no positive integer solutions a,b,c.

For n=1 trivial (many solutions).

For n=2, infinitely many Pythagorean triples.

Fermat proved the case n=4 using infinite descent (if a solution, then a smaller one, contradiction).

Euler proved n=3 (with a gap later filled).

Dirichlet and Legendre n=5, etc. Up to n=4 the cases were known before Wiles; the general case was open for large n.

**ELI5**

- You cannot have two perfect powers with exponent >2 that add to another perfect power with the same exponent.
- For squares it works (Pythagoras), for cubes and higher, no.

**Worked Example:**

**No solutions for n=3 small check**

1^3+2^3=9 not cube. 3^3+4^3+5^3=6^3 famous but sum of three. For two: 3^3+6^3=243= ? not  6. something. Known no small, and proved none at all.

## Fermat's Proof for n=4 by Descent

<!-- section: flt-n4-descent -->

Assume a^4 + b^4 = c^2 (since (c')^4 = c^2 if c = (c')^2, wait the equation a^4 + b^4 = c^4 = (c^2)^2, so a Pythagorean with legs a^2 b^2 hypotenuse c^2.

Then using the param for the triple (a^2, b^2, c^2), one gets smaller solution, infinite descent contradiction.

**ELI5**

- Assume a solution for exponent 4. Then it gives a Pythagorean triple where the legs are themselves squares. The param for that triple forces even smaller solution of the same form, and so on forever, impossible in positive integers.

**Worked Example:**

**The descent step produces another solution a',b',c' with c' < c, contradicting minimality.**

## The Modularity Approach for General n

<!-- section: flt-wiles-proof -->

Wiles' proof: assume a solution to a^p + b^p = c^p for odd prime p (the hard case; p=2 done).

Associate the elliptic curve y^2 = x (x - a^p) (x + b^p) (the Frey curve).

This curve is semistable, and if the solution exists it would be a counterexample to the modularity theorem (every elliptic curve over Q is modular, i.e. comes from a modular form).

Wiles (with Taylor) proved the modularity for semistable curves, hence no such Frey curve, hence no solution.

The proof involved deformation theory of Galois representations, Hecke rings, etc.

**ELI5**

- A hypothetical solution for large exponent would create a very special elliptic curve (Frey) that cannot exist because all elliptic curves are "modular" (have a corresponding modular form or Galois rep coming from automorphic), but this one wouldn't.
- The contradiction proves no solution.

**Worked Example:**

**For the famous  a=3 b=  4? No for higher. The proof shows that if such a,b,c,p existed the associated Frey curve would have conductor etc that contradicts the properties forced by modularity.**

## Common Mistakes

- **FLT for n=2**: There are solutions, the Pythagorean triples.
- **Fermat had a simple proof**: Unlikely; the full proof is hundreds of pages of advanced machinery developed over centuries.
- **Checked for small a b c**: Yes, but the theorem is for all, and the proof is global.
- **Counterexamples in other rings**: In some number rings FLT fails, but over Z for n>2 no.
- **"Last" theorem**: It was the last of Fermat's claims to be proved; he proved or claimed several others.
