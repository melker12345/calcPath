# Dedekind Domains

A Dedekind domain is an integral domain that is Noetherian, integrally closed, and of Krull dimension 1 (every nonzero prime ideal is maximal).

The key property: in a Dedekind domain, every nonzero ideal factors uniquely as a product of prime ideals.

This makes them the "correct" generalization of Z or PID where unique factorization holds at the ideal level.

Rings of integers O_K of number fields are the main examples (and motivation).

Fractional ideals form a group, and the class group is the quotient by principals.

## Definition and Equivalent Characterizations

<!-- section: dedekind-def -->

A domain R is Dedekind if:

1. Noetherian (every ideal f.g.)

2. Integrally closed in its frac field (if element in frac satisfies monic over R, it is in R)

3. Every nonzero prime ideal is maximal (dim 1).

Equivalent: every nonzero ideal is invertible (as fractional ideal), or unique factorization of ideals into primes, or localization at every max ideal is DVR (discrete valuation ring).

**ELI5**

- Noetherian: "finitely generated ideals", like Z.
- Integrally closed: "no extra integers nearby", like Z.
- Dim 1: "primes are as big as possible", no chain of primes.
- Together, they force ideal unique factorization.

**Worked Example:**

**Z is Dedekind: Noetherian, integrally closed, primes (p) maximal.**

Z[x] is not (dim 2, (x) < (2,x) primes).

## Unique Ideal Factorization

<!-- section: dedekind-uf-ideal -->

In Dedekind R, every nonzero ideal I = P1^{e1} ... Pk^{ek} unique product of prime ideals (up to order).

Proof sketch: first every ideal is invertible (using dim1 + Noeth + closed to show max ideals invertible, then all), then the monoid of ideals is cancellative etc, and one shows primes are the atoms, and ACC or something gives factorization, uniqueness from cancellation.

**ELI5**

- Every "ideal number" factors into "prime ideals" uniquely, like numbers into primes in Z.
- This holds even if the elements themselves don't factor uniquely.

**Worked Example:**

**In O of Q(sqrt(-5)), (6) = (2)(3) = P^2 Q R as in earlier example, and this factorization is unique among ideals.**

## Localization at Prime Ideals are DVRs

<!-- section: dedekind-local-dvr -->

For P prime ideal in Dedekind R, the localization R_P is a DVR: local PID with max ideal P R_P principal, generated by a uniformizer π, every nonzero ideal (π^k).

The discrete valuation v_P (I) = the exponent of P in the factorization of I.

v_P(α) for elements α.

**ELI5**

- Locally at each prime ideal, the ring looks like a power series or p-adic integers: everything is π^k * unit.
- The exponent k is the "order of vanishing" or the power of that prime dividing the ideal.

**Worked Example:**

**In Z, at P=(p), Z_{(p)} is the locals with denom not p, and it is DVR with uniformizer p, v_p the p-adic val.**

## Fractional Ideals and the Ideal Group

<!-- section: dedekind-fractional -->

A fractional ideal is a nonzero R-submodule I of the frac field K such that d I ⊆ R for some d in R nonzero ( "denominator").

The set of fractional ideals forms a group under multiplication, with inverse I^{-1} = {x in K | x I ⊆ R}.

Principal fractional ideals (α) = α R for α in K^* form a subgroup.

The ideal class group is the quotient.

In Dedekind, every fractional ideal factors uniquely into primes (negative exponents allowed).

**ELI5**

- Fractional ideals are like "ideals with denominator", e.g. (1/2) in Z is fractional.
- They form a group, and the class group quotients out the "obvious" principal ones.

**Worked Example:**

**In Z, fractional ideals are of form (a/b) with a,b Z, but up to principals all are the same as Z itself (class number 1).**

## O_K are Dedekind

<!-- section: dedekind-ok-dedekind -->

For number field K, O_K is Noetherian (as Z-module f.g., ideals are Z-submodules too), integrally closed by definition (integral closure), and dim 1 because if Q prime ideal nonzero in O_K, then Q ∩ Z = (p) nonzero prime in Z, and O_K /Q is finite integral over Z/p, hence field (artinian integral domain finite over field is field), so Q maximal.

Thus Dedekind, hence unique ideal factorization.

**ELI5**

- All the properties hold for rings of integers in number fields, so they enjoy unique ideal factorization.
- This is the starting point of algebraic number theory.

**Worked Example:**

**In any O_K, (p) factors into prime ideals P_i^{e_i} with sum e_i f_i =n, the decomposition of p.**

## Common Mistakes

- **Dedekind domains are always PID or UFD**: No; they have unique ideal fact, but elements may not (class number >1).
- **Z[x] is Dedekind**: No, not dim 1.
- **Every Noetherian domain is Dedekind**: No, needs the other two properties.
- **Fractional ideals are ideals**: No, they live in the frac field, may have "denom".
- **In Dedekind every ideal is principal**: Only if class number 1.
