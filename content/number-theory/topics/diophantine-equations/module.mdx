# Diophantine Equations

Linear and classical Diophantine equations: Pythagorean triples, Pell's equation, Hasse principle, Thue's theorem, and Fermat's Last Theorem.

A Diophantine equation is a polynomial equation with integer coefficients for which one seeks integer solutions (or rational, or in other rings).

The theory studies existence, finiteness, parametrization, or effective bounds for solutions in various cases.

Linear: solved by extended Euclid.

Quadratic: Hasse principle, Pell, etc.

Higher degree: Fermat, Thue, Mordell, elliptic curves (finitely many integral points by Siegel), and in general undecidable by Matiyasevich (Hilbert 10th negative).

This topic introduces the general notion and basic examples before the specialized chapters.

## General Notions

<!-- section: diophantine-general -->

A Diophantine equation is f(x1,...,xk)=0 with f polynomial in Z[x's], seek x in Z^k.

Or inhomogeneous f= h.

Rational solutions (clear denom) or integer.

Local-global principles, descent, heights, etc are tools.

**ELI5**

- Polynomial eq with integer coeff, want integer (or rational) answers for the variables.
- Some have none, some infinitely many (parametrized), some finite but hard to find all.

**Worked Example:**

**x^2 + y^2 = 3 has no integer solutions (mod 4 squares 0 or1, sum 0,1,2 not 3 mod4). Has rational? No by descent or Hasse (no real? Wait real yes, but mod some p no).**

## Hilbert Tenth Problem

<!-- section: diophantine-hilbert10 -->

Hilbert's 10th problem (1900): is there an algorithm that, given a Diophantine eq, decides if it has integer solutions?

Answered negatively by Matiyasevich (1970, building on Davis-Putnam-Robinson): the r.e. sets are exactly the Diophantine sets. Since halting problem undecidable, no algorithm for Dioph eq.

**ELI5**

- There is no general computer program that can look at any polynomial eq with integer coeffs and always correctly say "yes there are integer solutions" or "no".
- Some families are decidable (linear, quadratic in some cases, elliptic curves under conjectures), but not uniformly for all.

**Worked Example:**

**The negative solution uses encoding of Turing machines or exponential Dioph eq into polynomials via clever number theory (Fibonacci, binomial coeffs etc).**

## Finiteness Results

<!-- section: diophantine-finiteness -->

By Faltings (Mordell conjecture proved 1983): a curve of genus ≥2 over Q has only finitely many rational points.

Siegel: integral points on elliptic curves (genus 1 with rank) finite? For affine, Siegel theorem finite integral points on elliptic.

Thue, Mordell etc for specific.

But effective bounds are hard; Baker etc give some.

**ELI5**

- High genus curves (complicated shapes) can't have too many rational points; only finite.
- This solves many Dioph eq by viewing as points on curves.

**Worked Example:**

**The curve y^2 = x^3 + k (Mordell) has finitely many integral (x,y) for each k; tabulated for small k.**

## Common Mistakes

- **All Dioph eq are decidable**: No, Hilbert 10 negative.
- **Rational solutions always exist if real do**: No, Hasse fails for higher deg.
- **Finiteness means we can find them all**: Finiteness yes, but finding may be hard without effective proofs.
- **Linear always solvable if gcd divides**: Yes for two vars; for more vars similar but with more params.

A linear Diophantine equation is ax + by = c with a,b,c integers, seeking integer solutions x,y.

It has solutions iff gcd(a,b) divides c.

The general solution can be found from one particular solution using the extended Euclidean algorithm, then adding the homogeneous solutions (multiples of b/d and -a/d).

This is the most basic Diophantine equation and the starting point for the theory of linear equations in more variables and for the geometry of numbers.

## Solvability Condition

<!-- section: linear-dioph-solvability -->

The equation a x + b y = c has integer solutions x,y if and only if d = gcd(a,b) divides c.

Proof: if solution, then d divides left hence right.

Conversely, if d|c, use extended Euclid to find x0,y0 with a x0 + b y0 = d, then multiply by c/d: a (x0 c/d) + b (y0 c/d) = c.

**ELI5**

- The left side a x + b y is always a multiple of the greatest common "step" d=gcd(a,b). So c must be reachable, i.e. multiple of d.
- If yes, then you can reach it.

**Worked Example:**

**Solve 6x + 9y = 3**

d=3 divides 3.

Extended: 6*(-1) + 9*1 =3?  -6+9=3 yes.

So x0=-1, y0=1 for =3.

Particular for =3: same (c/d=1).

General: x = -1 + (9/3) t = -1 +3 t

y = 1 - (6/3) t =1 -2 t.

Check: 6(-1+3t) +9(1-2t)= -6+18t +9 -18t =3 yes.

## Finding All Solutions

<!-- section: linear-dioph-general -->

Once you have one particular solution (x0,y0) to a x + b y = c, the general is

x = x0 + (b/d) t

y = y0 - (a/d) t

for t integer.

This parametrizes all solutions; the coefficients come from the kernel of the map (x,y) |→ a x + b y, which is generated by (b/d, -a/d).

**ELI5**

- From one solution, you can add "full cycles" of the smaller coefficient and subtract from the other to stay at the same total c.
- The step size is b/d and a/d because dividing by d gives the primitive equation.

**Worked Example:**

**From the particular (-1,1) for 6x+9y=3, the general is x=-1+3t , y=1-2t as above.**

For t=0: -1,1

t=1: 2, -1 : 6*2 +9*(-1)=12-9=3 yes.

## Applications to Frobenius and Chicken Nugget

<!-- section: linear-dioph-applications -->

For two coprime a,b the largest number not representable as nonnegative ax+by is a b -a -b (Frobenius coin problem).

For more than two variables it is hard (NP-hard in general).

In programming and contest math, the linear Diophantine is used to solve "buy exactly c items with packs of a and b".

**ELI5**

- With coins of value a and b coprime, the amounts you cannot make are finite; the biggest is ab-a-b.
- Once you can make d consecutive amounts (d=max(a,b)), you can make all larger by adding the small coin.

**Worked Example:**

**Frobenius for 4 and 7: 4*7-4-7=17. Indeed 17 cannot, 18= 1*4 +2*7? Wait 4+14=18, 19=3*4 +1*7=12+7=19, 20=5*4, 21=3*7, etc. 17 no combination of nonneg.**

## Common Mistakes

- **Forgetting the d | c check**: Many solutions claimed when impossible.
- **Negative solutions allowed**: Diophantine usually allows negative x,y unless specified nonnegative (Frobenius is nonnegative).
- **Unique solution**: There are infinitely many if any, parametrized by t.
- **d= gcd(a,b) in the steps**: Always divide the coefficients by d when writing the general solution.

A Pythagorean triple is a triple of positive integers a,b,c with a^2 + b^2 = c^2 (sides of a right triangle with integer lengths).

Primitive triples (gcd(a,b,c)=1) are parametrized by two integers m>n>0 of opposite parity, gcd(m,n)=1: a=m^2-n^2, b=2 m n, c=m^2 + n^2 (or a,b swapped).

All triples are multiples of primitive ones.

This is one of the oldest Diophantine problems, solved by Euclid or earlier, with connections to Gaussian integers (unique factorization in Z[i] gives the parametrization).

## Definition and Examples

<!-- section: pythagorean-def -->

Positive integers a ≤ b < c with a^2 + b^2 = c^2.

Examples: 3,4,5 ; 5,12,13 ; 8,15,17 ; 7,24,25 ; 20,21,29 etc.

Multiples: 6,8,10 =2*(3,4,5).

**ELI5**

- Integer sided right triangles. The sides satisfy the Pythagorean theorem exactly.
- The primitive ones are the "basic" shapes; others are scaled versions.

**Worked Example:**

**Generate 5,12,13**

m=3,n=2 (opposite parity, coprime).

a=9-4=5, b=2*3*2=12, c=9+4=13. Yes.

## The Parametrization Theorem

<!-- section: pythagorean-parametrization -->

Every primitive Pythagorean triple is of the form m^2 - n^2, 2mn, m^2 + n^2 for m > n >0, m n coprime, not both odd.

All triples are k times a primitive one for k≥1.

Proof: from a^2 + b^2 = c^2, assume a odd b even (or swap), then (c-b)(c+b) = a^2, and the factors are coprime etc, leading to the m,n.

In Z[i]: (a + b i)(a - b i) = c^2, and since primitive the factors are coprime, hence each is a square up to units, giving the param.

**ELI5**

- All primitive triples come from picking two "parameters" m>n with the conditions, and the formulas spit out the sides.
- The even leg is always the 2mn one.

**Worked Example:**

**m=4,n=1: a=16-1=15, b=8, c=17. 8^2+15^2=64+225=289=17^2. gcd=1, yes primitive (m n coprime, opposite parity).**

## Proof via Gaussian Integers (optional advanced)

<!-- section: pythagorean-gaussian -->

In Z[i] which is UFD, norm(a+bi)=a^2+b^2.

If a^2 + b^2 = c^2 and gcd(a,b,c)=1, not both a b odd, then a+bi and a-bi are coprime in Z[i] (their gcd would divide 2a,2b,2c but primitive), and their product is c^2 (or (c)^2 up unit).

Hence each is unit times a square: a+bi = unit (m + n i)^2, expanding gives the formulas.

**ELI5**

- The equation says the norm of a+bi is a square (c^2).
- In a UFD with the right units, this forces a+bi itself to be (up unit) a square in the ring, which expands to the param.

**Worked Example:**

**For 3+4i, norm 25=5^2. 3+4i = (2+i)^2 ? (2+i)^2=4+4i +i^2=4+4i-1=3+4i yes. m=2 n=1.**

## Common Mistakes

- **Both m n odd gives non-primitive or not triple**: Both odd makes a,b,c all odd, but then a^2+b^2 ≡1+1=2 mod4, not a square (squares 0 or1 mod4).
- **Forgetting the k multiple for non-primitive**: Many triples are multiples.
- **a and b order**: The formula gives one even leg; swap if needed for a odd.
- **n=0 or m=n**: Gives degenerate  m,0,m not positive triple.

Pell's equation is the Diophantine equation x^2 - d y^2 = 1 where d >0 square-free integer.

It always has infinitely many solutions, generated from the fundamental solution (the smallest nontrivial x1,y1 >0) by (x + y sqrt(d))^k for k=1,2,...

The solutions are given by the continued fraction convergents to sqrt(d); the fundamental solution corresponds to the period or a multiple.

It is the key to solving the negative Pell x^2 - d y^2 = -1 (sometimes solvable), units in real quadratic fields, and approximation of sqrt(d) by rationals.

Named after John Pell, though studied by Fermat, Euler, etc.

## The Equation and Trivial Solutions

<!-- section: pell-def -->

x^2 - d y^2 =1, d>0 not square.

Trivial solutions: (x,y)=(±1,0).

Nontrivial solutions exist infinitely many.

For d=2: 3^2 -2*2^2=9-8=1. Fundamental (3,2).

For d=3: 2^2 -3*1^2=1. (2,1).

**ELI5**

- Find integers x,y so that x/y is a very good approximation to sqrt(d), with error 1/(y^2 something).
- The equation says (x - y sqrt(d))(x + y sqrt(d)) =1, so units of norm 1 in Z[sqrt(d)].

**Worked Example:**

**For d=61 the fundamental solution is huge: x=1766319049 , y=226153980. But exists.**

## Solutions from Continued Fractions

<!-- section: pell-continued-fractions -->

The continued fraction expansion of sqrt(d) is periodic [a0; \overline{a1...a_l} ].

The convergents h_k / k_k satisfy | h_k^2 - d k_k^2 | small.

The solutions to the Pell equation (and = -1) occur at the convergents where the period divides or at the end of the period.

If the period length l is odd, the negative Pell has solution at l-1, the +1 at 2l-1 or something.

The fundamental unit is the convergent at the period.

**ELI5**

- sqrt(d) has a repeating "decimal" in continued fraction. The best approximations (convergents) give the solutions to x^2 - d y^2 = ±1 alternately or at the repeat.
- The smallest solution comes from the first repeat of the pattern.

**Worked Example:**

**sqrt(2)= [1; \overline{2} ], period 1 odd. Convergents 1/1 (1-2*1=-1), 3/2 (9-2*4=1). Yes, negative at first convergent after a0, +1 at next.**

## The Group of Solutions and Units

<!-- section: pell-units -->

The solutions (x_k , y_k) satisfy x_k + y_k sqrt(d) = (x1 + y1 sqrt(d))^k for the fundamental unit ε = x1 + y1 sqrt(d) >1.

The unit group of the real quadratic field Q(sqrt(d)) is {± ε^k | k in Z} (Dirichlet unit theorem, rank 1).

This gives all units of norm 1 (the Pell) and norm -1 if the fundamental has norm -1.

**ELI5**

- The solutions "multiply" using the ring multiplication: if two solutions, their "product" (x1 x2 + d y1 y2 , x1 y2 + y1 x2) is another solution.
- Powers of the smallest nontrivial give all others.

**Worked Example:**

**For d=2, ε=1+sqrt(2)? Wait 3+2 sqrt(2). (3+2sqrt2)^2 =9 +12 sqrt2 +8=17+12 sqrt2, 17^2 -2*12^2=289-288=1 yes. Next larger.**

## Common Mistakes

- **All d have solutions to x^2 - d y^2 = -1**: No, only when the fundamental unit has norm -1 (period odd).
- **The fundamental solution is always small**: For some d like 61 it is huge (10 digits).
- **Pell equation always has only trivial**: No, infinitely many for every square-free d>0.
- **Confusing with x^2 + d y^2 =1**: That has only ±1,0 for d>0.
- **Solutions in rationals**: Trivial by clearing, the interest is integers.

The Hasse (or local-global) principle for quadratic forms says that a quadratic form over the rationals represents 0 non-trivially if and only if it represents 0 non-trivially over the reals and over all p-adic fields Q_p.

In other words, a quadratic equation has a rational solution if and only if it has real solution and solutions in every p-adic completion.

For quadratic forms this holds (Hasse-Minkowski theorem), but for higher degree it fails (counterexamples by Selmer etc).

It is a fundamental principle in arithmetic geometry: local solubility everywhere implies global.

## The Local-Global Principle for Quadratics

<!-- section: hasse-local-global -->

A homogeneous quadratic equation in several variables has a nontrivial rational solution iff it has one over R and over every Q_p.

Equiv, the projective quadric has a rational point iff it has real and p-adic points for all p.

The "only if" is obvious (embed Q in R and Q_p).

The "if" is the deep Hasse-Minkowski theorem, proved using the theory of quadratic forms over local fields, Hilbert symbol, and class field or genus theory.

**ELI5**

- To decide if a quadratic thing has a rational solution, you can check "locally" in every completion (real numbers and p-adics, which are like numbers with p-adic "decimal" expansion).
- For quadratics, if it works everywhere locally, it works globally over Q.

**Worked Example:**

**The form x^2 - 2 y^2 - 3 z^2 or simple isotropic.**

Classic counterexample for higher: 3x^3 +4 y^3 +5 z^3 =0 has local solutions everywhere but no rational (Selmer).

For quadratic, no counterexample.

## Hilbert Symbol and Local Invariants

<!-- section: hasse-hilbert -->

The Hilbert symbol (a,b)_p =1 or -1 tells if a x^2 + b y^2 =1 has solution in Q_p, or equivalently if a is norm from Q_p(sqrt(b)) etc.

The product over all places of the Hilbert symbols is 1 (product formula).

This is the key reciprocity that makes the local conditions compatible for the global existence.

**ELI5**

- The Hilbert symbol is a local "does this pair of numbers behave like a norm or square" yes/no at each p and real place.
- The product of all these local yes/no is always +1; this is the only global obstruction for quadratic forms.

**Worked Example:**

**For the equation a x^2 + b y^2 = z^2, it has rational solution iff the Hilbert symbols satisfy the product 1 condition, which reduces to Legendre symbols via the explicit formula for (a,b)_p.**

## Failure for Higher Degree and Brauer-Manin

<!-- section: hasse-failure -->

For cubic and higher, the Hasse principle fails: there are varieties with points everywhere locally but no global point.

The Brauer-Manin obstruction (from the Brauer group of the variety) explains many (but not all) failures.

For quadratics the Brauer group is trivial in the relevant sense, so Hasse holds.

**ELI5**

- For more complicated equations, being solvable "in every p-adic world and the reals" is not enough; there can be a global "entanglement" obstruction.
- For squares (quadratic), no such extra obstruction.

**Worked Example:**

**The Selmer curve 3X^3 + 4Y^3 +5Z^3 =0 has solutions in R and all Q_p but no rational solution. The obstruction is in the Sha of the elliptic curve or Brauer.**

## Common Mistakes

- **Hasse holds for all varieties**: No, only for quadratics (and some other classes like Severi-Brauer).
- **Local always implies global for integers**: No, even for linear sometimes (but for linear it does by Hasse for degree 1).
- **p-adics are hard**: In practice for quadratic forms there are explicit criteria using Hilbert symbols that reduce to Legendre/Jacobi at the primes dividing the discriminant.
- **The real place is important**: Sometimes the only obstruction is the real place (signature of the form).

Thue's theorem (1909) says that if f(x,y) is an irreducible binary form of degree d≥3 with integer coefficients, then the equation f(x,y)= h has only finitely many integer solutions (x,y) for any fixed nonzero h.

In particular, for the Thue equation f(x,y)=1 with deg≥3 irreducible, only finitely many solutions.

This is a far-reaching generalization of FLT for fixed exponent (the homogeneous form x^n + y^n - z^n but projective), and is proved using Diophantine approximation (how well algebraic numbers can be approximated by rationals, Thue's improvement on Liouville).

It is one of the first major results in the theory of Diophantine equations using approximation.

## Statement

<!-- section: thue-statement -->

Let f(X,Y) ∈ Z[X,Y] be a binary form of degree d ≥ 3 that is irreducible over Q (as a polynomial in two variables, or the homogeneous polynomial is irreducible).

Then for any fixed nonzero integer h, the equation f(x,y) = h has only finitely many solutions in integers x,y.

**ELI5**

- A "curvy" homogeneous polynomial equation of degree 3 or higher (irreducible) can only equal a fixed h for finitely many integer points (x,y).
- Unlike linear or quadratic (Pell infinite), higher degree "curves" have finite integer points for fixed height h.

**Worked Example:**

**f(x,y)= x^3 + y^3 - 3 x y (x+y) or simpler x^3 + y^3 =1 has only the obvious (1,0),(0,1),(-1,-1) etc by Thue or descent.**

## Proof Idea via Approximation

<!-- section: thue-approximation -->

Thue improved Liouville's theorem on approximation of algebraic numbers by rationals: if α algebraic of degree d, then |α - p/q| > c / q^{d/2 + eps} or better exponents.

Then, if f(x,y)=h, assume y≠0, let α be a root of the polynomial f(X,1) - h/y^d or something? If f( x/y ,1 ) = h / y^d .

If (x,y) solution with y large, then x/y is very close to a root α of the irreducible f(T,1)=0 (degree d≥3).

Then |α - x/y | = |h| / |y|^d  / |leading * product (α - other roots or denom)| small like 1/ y^d .

But Thue's approximation theorem says it cannot be too small (better than Liouville's 1/y^d ), contradiction for large y.

Hence only finitely many.

**ELI5**

- A rational x/y that makes f(x/y,1) very small (1/y^d) would be an extremely good approximation to the algebraic root α of f(T,1)=0.
- Thue proved that algebraic numbers cannot be approximated that well by rationals (the exponent is too good), hence only small y possible.

**Worked Example:**

**For the form x^3 - 2 y^3 =1, the approximations would give | (x/y) - 2^{1/3} | ~ 1/y^3 , but Thue says for degree 3 the bound is roughly >1/y^{1.5 +} , and 1/y^3 is smaller for large y, contradiction.**

## Effective Versions and Baker's Method

<!-- section: thue-effective -->

Thue's proof is ineffective (does not give bound on the size of solutions).

Effective bounds came later with Baker's theory of linear forms in logarithms, which give explicit (huge) bounds on solutions to Thue equations.

Today Thue equations can be solved completely for reasonable coefficients using computational methods combining Baker, lattice reduction (LLL), and enumeration.

**ELI5**

- Thue proves "only finitely many" but the proof doesn't tell you how large to search.
- Baker's logs give "the solutions are smaller than 10^{10^{10}} " or something; still huge but finite, and with computers one can find all.

**Worked Example:**

**Many Thue equations like x^3 + y^3 + z^3 -3 x y z =1 or norm equations from cubic fields are solved completely this way, giving all units or S-unit solutions.**

## Connection to FLT and Other Equations

<!-- section: thue-flt-connection -->

FLT for fixed n is a special case of Thue (the form x^n + y^n , but it is reducible over Q(ζ), but one can reduce to irreducible factors or use in the cyclotomic).

Thue's work was motivated by trying to prove FLT.

More generally, superelliptic equations, generalized Fermat equations are attacked with similar approximation or modular methods.

**ELI5**

- Proving no solutions to x^n + y^n =1 for n>2 is a Thue-type statement (after homogenizing or projectivizing).
- Thue gave the first general finiteness for such "higher degree" Diophantine.

**Worked Example:**

**The equation x^3 + y^3 = 1 has only the trivial solutions by Thue or by descent; fits the theorem.**

## Common Mistakes

- **Thue gives effective bounds**: The original proof does not; effective versions require Baker theory.
- **Applies to all forms**: The form must be irreducible (or one reduces to the irreducible factors).
- **Degree 2**: For degree 2 it can have infinitely many (Pell-like).
- **h=0**: For h=0 it is about rational points on the curve, which may be infinite if genus 0 or 1 with rank.
- **Binary form only**: The theorem is for two variables; more variables need other tools (Schmidt subspace theorem etc).

Fermat's Last Theorem (FLT) states that there are no positive integers a,b,c,n with n>2 such that a^n + b^n = c^n.

Stated by Pierre de Fermat in 1637 in the margin of a book, with the claim "I have a truly marvelous proof which this margin is too narrow to contain."

It was proved by Andrew Wiles in 1994-95 (with a correction in 1995 with Taylor), using elliptic curves, modular forms, and the modularity theorem for semistable elliptic curves (the Taniyama-Shimura conjecture in the needed case).

The proof is one of the highest achievements of 20th century mathematics, connecting Diophantine equations to the Langlands program.

## Statement and Small Cases

<!-- section: flt-statement -->

For n>2, the equation a^n + b^n = c^n has no positive integer solutions a,b,c.

For n=1 trivial (many solutions).

For n=2, infinitely many Pythagorean triples.

Fermat proved the case n=4 using infinite descent (if a solution, then a smaller one, contradiction).

Euler proved n=3 (with a gap later filled).

Dirichlet and Legendre n=5, etc. Up to n=4 the cases were known before Wiles; the general case was open for large n.

**ELI5**

- You cannot have two perfect powers with exponent >2 that add to another perfect power with the same exponent.
- For squares it works (Pythagoras), for cubes and higher, no.

**Worked Example:**

**No solutions for n=3 small check**

1^3+2^3=9 not cube. 3^3+4^3+5^3=6^3 famous but sum of three. For two: 3^3+6^3=243= ? not  6. something. Known no small, and proved none at all.

## Fermat's Proof for n=4 by Descent

<!-- section: flt-n4-descent -->

Assume a^4 + b^4 = c^2 (since (c')^4 = c^2 if c = (c')^2, wait the equation a^4 + b^4 = c^4 = (c^2)^2, so a Pythagorean with legs a^2 b^2 hypotenuse c^2.

Then using the param for the triple (a^2, b^2, c^2), one gets smaller solution, infinite descent contradiction.

**ELI5**

- Assume a solution for exponent 4. Then it gives a Pythagorean triple where the legs are themselves squares. The param for that triple forces even smaller solution of the same form, and so on forever, impossible in positive integers.

**Worked Example:**

**The descent step produces another solution a',b',c' with c' < c, contradicting minimality.**

## The Modularity Approach for General n

<!-- section: flt-wiles-proof -->

Wiles' proof: assume a solution to a^p + b^p = c^p for odd prime p (the hard case; p=2 done).

Associate the elliptic curve y^2 = x (x - a^p) (x + b^p) (the Frey curve).

This curve is semistable, and if the solution exists it would be a counterexample to the modularity theorem (every elliptic curve over Q is modular, i.e. comes from a modular form).

Wiles (with Taylor) proved the modularity for semistable curves, hence no such Frey curve, hence no solution.

The proof involved deformation theory of Galois representations, Hecke rings, etc.

**ELI5**

- A hypothetical solution for large exponent would create a very special elliptic curve (Frey) that cannot exist because all elliptic curves are "modular" (have a corresponding modular form or Galois rep coming from automorphic), but this one wouldn't.
- The contradiction proves no solution.

**Worked Example:**

**For the famous  a=3 b=  4? No for higher. The proof shows that if such a,b,c,p existed the associated Frey curve would have conductor etc that contradicts the properties forced by modularity.**

## Common Mistakes

- **FLT for n=2**: There are solutions, the Pythagorean triples.
- **Fermat had a simple proof**: Unlikely; the full proof is hundreds of pages of advanced machinery developed over centuries.
- **Checked for small a b c**: Yes, but the theorem is for all, and the proof is global.
- **Counterexamples in other rings**: In some number rings FLT fails, but over Z for n>2 no.
- **"Last" theorem**: It was the last of Fermat's claims to be proved; he proved or claimed several others.
