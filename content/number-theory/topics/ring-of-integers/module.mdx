# Ring of Integers

The ring of integers O_K of a number field K is the integral closure of Z in K: the algebraic integers lying in K.

It is a Dedekind domain of rank n=[K:Q] as Z-module, with Z-basis an integral basis.

Computing an integral basis for a given K=Q(α) given by a min poly is a standard (but nontrivial) task in computational algebraic number theory; there are algorithms using discriminant, p-adic completions, or round 2/4 methods.

O_K is the natural place to do "integer arithmetic" in K: factorization of ideals, units, class group all live here.

## Definition and Module Structure

<!-- section: ring-int-def-module -->

O_K = { β ∈ K | β is algebraic integer } = integral closure of Z in K.

It is a subring of K.

There exists a Z-basis ω1,...,ωn of O_K (integral basis), so O_K = Z ω1 + ... + Z ωn as abelian group.

The discriminant of the basis (det (Tr(ωi ωj)) )^2 or the field disc is independent of basis up to sign, and measures the "volume" and ramification.

**ELI5**

- O_K is the "full set of integers" inside K.
- Like Z has basis 1, O_K has n "independent" generators over Z that span all the integers in K.

**Worked Example:**

**For K=Q(sqrt(-5)), integral basis 1, sqrt(-5), disc= -20.**

## Power Basis vs Integral Basis

<!-- section: ring-int-power-vs-integral -->

If K=Q(α) with α alg int, min poly f monic Z, then Z[α] ⊆ O_K, but may be proper.

The index [O_K : Z[α]] is finite, and divides the index related to disc(f)/disc(K).

To find if Z[α]=O_K, compute the disc of f and see if square-free or use criteria (e.g. for quadratic as above).

**ELI5**

- Z[α] is the "obvious" integers polynomials in α with Z coeff.
- Sometimes there are "extra" integers in K not in Z[α], like halves when d=1 mod4.
- The full O_K may be bigger, with a different basis.

**Worked Example:**

**Q(sqrt(5)), α=sqrt5, Z[sqrt5] has disc 20, but full O has disc 5, index 2, basis 1, (1+sqrt5)/2 not in Z[sqrt5].**

## Discriminant and Ramification

<!-- section: ring-int-disc-ram -->

The discriminant disc(K) of the field is disc of any integral basis (up to sign? the det squared or standard def).

It is an integer, and a prime p ramifies in K (some e_i>1 in p O_K) iff p divides disc(K).

For quadratic d, disc = d or 4d, ramified primes divide it.

**ELI5**

- The discriminant is an invariant "size" or "complexity" of the ring O_K, like the volume of the fundamental parallelepiped in the log or Minkowski embedding.
- It tells exactly which rational primes "ramify" (have repeated factors in the ideal factorization) in O_K.

**Worked Example:**

**Q(sqrt(-3)), disc=-3 or -12? d=-3=1 mod4, disc=-3? Standard disc(Q(sqrt d))= d if 1 mod4, 4d else. For -3, -3. 3 ramifies: 3 O = (sqrt(-3))^2 / something? Yes (3, sqrt(-3))^2 / (3) or known.**

## Computing Integral Basis (sketch)

<!-- section: ring-int-computing-basis -->

Given min poly f of α, to find O_K:

- Factor disc(f), for each prime p dividing, use local methods (p-adic) to see if the basis can be improved at p (dedekind criterion or round 2: look for elements (x + α y)/p integral but not in the current order).
- Or use the theorem: an element (a0 + a1 α +... )/d is integral iff it satisfies monic, or trace conditions, but practical algorithms exist in Sage/Pari/Magma.

**ELI5**

- Start with Z[α], compute its disc, for bad primes p dividing the index or disc, solve locally mod p^k to see if there are extra elements with denominator p that are still integral.
- Glue the local improvements to get the global basis.

**Worked Example:**

**For cubic fields there are tables or algorithms that output the integral basis for small disc.**

## Common Mistakes

- **O_K = Z[α] always for primitive element α**: No, counterexamples when p^2 divides the index for some p.
- **Discriminant is always square-free**: No, can have squares (ramified).
- **The basis is unique**: No, any Z-basis, but the disc is invariant.
- **All elements of K are algebraic integers**: No, only those in O_K; K has elements like 1/2, α/2 that may not be integral.
- **Computing O_K is trivial**: For high degree or large coeffs it requires serious computation (factoring disc, local lifts).
