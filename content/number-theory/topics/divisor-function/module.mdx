# Sum of Divisors Function

The sum of divisors function σ(n) (or σ_1(n)) is the sum of all positive divisors of n. It is a classic multiplicative arithmetic function that appears in the study of perfect numbers, abundant and deficient numbers, and the Dirichlet series ζ(s) ζ(s-1) = sum σ(n) n^{-s}.

σ(n) = sum_{d|n} d.

For prime power p^k , σ(p^k) = 1 + p + ... + p^k = (p^{k+1}-1)/(p-1).

Because multiplicative, for general n the value is the product of the geometric sums over its prime powers.

## Definition and Multiplicativity

<!-- section: sigma-definition -->

σ(n) = sum of positive divisors of n.

It is multiplicative: if gcd(a,b)=1 then σ(a b) = σ(a) σ(b).

This follows because the divisors of ab are products of divisors of a and of b when coprime.

The abundance σ(n) - n is the sum of proper divisors.

**ELI5**

- σ(n) adds up all the "parts" of n, including 1 and n itself. For primes it is 1+p.
- For numbers with many factors it grows; perfect numbers have σ(n) - n = n i.e. σ(n)=2n.

**Worked Example:**

**σ(12)**

Divisors 1,2,3,4,6,12 sum 28. σ(12)=28. 12 is abundant (proper sum 16>12, or σ=28>24).

## Common Mistakes

- **Including or excluding n in σ**: σ includes n; the proper divisor sum excludes n (sometimes excludes 1 too in aliquot parts).
- **σ(n)/n is the abundance ratio**: >2 abundant, =2 perfect, <2 deficient.
- **Odd perfect unknown**: No proof they don't exist, none known; search bound is huge.
- **σ multiplicative only for coprime**: For non-coprime σ(4)=1+2+4=7, σ(2)σ(2)=3*3=9 ≠7.
