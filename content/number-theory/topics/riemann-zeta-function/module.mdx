# Riemann Zeta Function

The Riemann zeta function ζ(s) = sum_{n=1}^∞ n^{-s} for Re(s)>1, with analytic continuation to the complex plane except a simple pole at s=1.

It encodes the distribution of primes via its Euler product ζ(s) = ∏_p (1 - p^{-s})^{-1}.

The Riemann hypothesis (all nontrivial zeros have Re=1/2) is one of the most famous open problems, with consequences for the error term in the prime number theorem.

Zeta is the prototype L-function, and its study is central to analytic number theory.

## Definition and Euler Product

<!-- section: zeta-definition -->

For Re(s) >1,
\[
ζ(s) = \sum_{n=1}^\infty n^{-s} = \prod_p (1 - p^{-s})^{-1}.
\]

The product is over primes; the equality is unique factorization (every n has unique prime factors, the geometric series multiply).

The harmonic series ζ(1) diverges (prime harmonic also diverges, Euclid).

**ELI5**

- Zeta(s) is the "sum of 1 over n to the s", like a continuous version of the harmonic series for s=1.
- The product over primes says "every number is built from primes independently", turning the sum into a product.

**Worked Example:**

**ζ(2) = π^2 /6 (Basel problem, solved by Euler).**

The product ∏ (1-1/p^2)^{-1} = π^2/6.

## Analytic Continuation and Functional Equation

<!-- section: zeta-continuation -->

ζ(s) extends to a meromorphic function on all C, with only pole at s=1 (simple, residue 1).

The functional equation relates ζ(s) and ζ(1-s):
\[
π^{-s/2} Γ(s/2) ζ(s) = π^{-(1-s)/2} Γ((1-s)/2) ζ(1-s).
\]

Or in xi form ξ(s) = ξ(1-s) where ξ is entire.

This comes from the theta function Poisson summation or contour integration.

**ELI5**

- Although the sum only converges for Re s>1, there is a unique "nice" way to extend the function to the left half-plane, with a symmetry relating s and 1-s.
- The gamma factors account for the "archimedean place" contribution.

**Worked Example:**

**At negative even integers, ζ(-2k)=0 (trivial zeros), from the functional equation or Bernoulli.**

ζ(-1) = -1/12 (in the extended sense, used in physics).

## The Riemann Hypothesis and Zeros

<!-- section: zeta-rh -->

The nontrivial zeros of ζ(s) lie in the critical strip 0 < Re s <1.

The Riemann hypothesis (RH): all nontrivial zeros have Re(s)=1/2.

There are infinitely many on the line (Hardy), and a positive proportion (Levinson, Conrey ~40%).

Trivial zeros at negative even integers from the gamma poles in the functional eq.

The prime number theorem is equivalent to no zeros on Re s=1.

**ELI5**

- The zeros of zeta control the "wiggles" in the distribution of primes. RH says they are all on the midline of the strip, giving the best possible error in π(x) ~ li(x).
- It is the most important unsolved problem in pure math, with million dollar prize.

**Worked Example:**

**The first nontrivial zero is at 1/2 + 14.1347 i (approx), and its conjugate, and more.**

The product formula over zeros gives explicit formulas for ψ(x) the Chebyshev function.

## Euler Product and Primes

<!-- section: zeta-primes -->

Taking log of the Euler product: -log(1-p^{-s}) = sum p^{-k s}/k , so log ζ(s) = sum_p sum_k p^{-k s}/k .

The von Mangoldt function appears in - ζ'/ζ (s) = sum Λ(n) n^{-s}.

This links the zeros (via contour integral or explicit formula) to the primes.

**ELI5**

- The primes are the "atoms" in the Euler product for zeta. The behavior of zeta (growth, zeros) tells how the primes are distributed.
- If zeta has no zeros near s=1, primes are dense enough (no large gaps in certain sense).

**Worked Example:**

**The prime number theorem π(x) ~ x / log x is equivalent to ζ(s) ≠0 for Re s=1 (s≠1).**

Deeper zero free regions give better errors.

## Common Mistakes

- **ζ(s) converges for all s**: No, only Re>1; continuation is the extension.
- **RH is proved**: No, it is open (though checked for first 10^13 zeros or so).
- **Trivial zeros are the only ones**: No, there are infinitely many nontrivial in the strip.
- **Zeta at s=1 is infinite**: Yes, harmonic series, pole, related to infinitude of primes (Euler).
- **The functional equation is simple**: It involves the gamma function which has its own poles and growth.
