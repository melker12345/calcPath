# Thue's Theorem

Thue's theorem (1909) says that if f(x,y) is an irreducible binary form of degree d≥3 with integer coefficients, then the equation f(x,y)= h has only finitely many integer solutions (x,y) for any fixed nonzero h.

In particular, for the Thue equation f(x,y)=1 with deg≥3 irreducible, only finitely many solutions.

This is a far-reaching generalization of FLT for fixed exponent (the homogeneous form x^n + y^n - z^n but projective), and is proved using Diophantine approximation (how well algebraic numbers can be approximated by rationals, Thue's improvement on Liouville).

It is one of the first major results in the theory of Diophantine equations using approximation.

## Statement

<!-- section: thue-statement -->

Let f(X,Y) ∈ Z[X,Y] be a binary form of degree d ≥ 3 that is irreducible over Q (as a polynomial in two variables, or the homogeneous polynomial is irreducible).

Then for any fixed nonzero integer h, the equation f(x,y) = h has only finitely many solutions in integers x,y.

**ELI5**

- A "curvy" homogeneous polynomial equation of degree 3 or higher (irreducible) can only equal a fixed h for finitely many integer points (x,y).
- Unlike linear or quadratic (Pell infinite), higher degree "curves" have finite integer points for fixed height h.

**Worked Example:**

**f(x,y)= x^3 + y^3 - 3 x y (x+y) or simpler x^3 + y^3 =1 has only the obvious (1,0),(0,1),(-1,-1) etc by Thue or descent.**

## Proof Idea via Approximation

<!-- section: thue-approximation -->

Thue improved Liouville's theorem on approximation of algebraic numbers by rationals: if α algebraic of degree d, then |α - p/q| > c / q^{d/2 + eps} or better exponents.

Then, if f(x,y)=h, assume y≠0, let α be a root of the polynomial f(X,1) - h/y^d or something? If f( x/y ,1 ) = h / y^d .

If (x,y) solution with y large, then x/y is very close to a root α of the irreducible f(T,1)=0 (degree d≥3).

Then |α - x/y | = |h| / |y|^d  / |leading * product (α - other roots or denom)| small like 1/ y^d .

But Thue's approximation theorem says it cannot be too small (better than Liouville's 1/y^d ), contradiction for large y.

Hence only finitely many.

**ELI5**

- A rational x/y that makes f(x/y,1) very small (1/y^d) would be an extremely good approximation to the algebraic root α of f(T,1)=0.
- Thue proved that algebraic numbers cannot be approximated that well by rationals (the exponent is too good), hence only small y possible.

**Worked Example:**

**For the form x^3 - 2 y^3 =1, the approximations would give | (x/y) - 2^{1/3} | ~ 1/y^3 , but Thue says for degree 3 the bound is roughly >1/y^{1.5 +} , and 1/y^3 is smaller for large y, contradiction.**

## Effective Versions and Baker's Method

<!-- section: thue-effective -->

Thue's proof is ineffective (does not give bound on the size of solutions).

Effective bounds came later with Baker's theory of linear forms in logarithms, which give explicit (huge) bounds on solutions to Thue equations.

Today Thue equations can be solved completely for reasonable coefficients using computational methods combining Baker, lattice reduction (LLL), and enumeration.

**ELI5**

- Thue proves "only finitely many" but the proof doesn't tell you how large to search.
- Baker's logs give "the solutions are smaller than 10^{10^{10}} " or something; still huge but finite, and with computers one can find all.

**Worked Example:**

**Many Thue equations like x^3 + y^3 + z^3 -3 x y z =1 or norm equations from cubic fields are solved completely this way, giving all units or S-unit solutions.**

## Connection to FLT and Other Equations

<!-- section: thue-flt-connection -->

FLT for fixed n is a special case of Thue (the form x^n + y^n , but it is reducible over Q(ζ), but one can reduce to irreducible factors or use in the cyclotomic).

Thue's work was motivated by trying to prove FLT.

More generally, superelliptic equations, generalized Fermat equations are attacked with similar approximation or modular methods.

**ELI5**

- Proving no solutions to x^n + y^n =1 for n>2 is a Thue-type statement (after homogenizing or projectivizing).
- Thue gave the first general finiteness for such "higher degree" Diophantine.

**Worked Example:**

**The equation x^3 + y^3 = 1 has only the trivial solutions by Thue or by descent; fits the theorem.**

## Common Mistakes

- **Thue gives effective bounds**: The original proof does not; effective versions require Baker theory.
- **Applies to all forms**: The form must be irreducible (or one reduces to the irreducible factors).
- **Degree 2**: For degree 2 it can have infinitely many (Pell-like).
- **h=0**: For h=0 it is about rational points on the curve, which may be infinite if genus 0 or 1 with rank.
- **Binary form only**: The theorem is for two variables; more variables need other tools (Schmidt subspace theorem etc).
