# Multiplicative Functions

A function f is multiplicative if f(1)=1 and f(mn)=f(m)f(n) whenever gcd(m,n)=1. This class includes almost all the important arithmetic functions we study: φ, μ, σ, d(n), id, the constant 1, etc.

Multiplicative functions are completely determined by their values at prime powers, and their Dirichlet series have Euler products.

The convolution of two multiplicative functions is multiplicative. This makes the ring structure respect the multiplicative property.

## Definition and Examples

<!-- section: multiplicative-def -->

f multiplicative: f(1)=1, and for coprime m,n f(mn)=f(m)f(n).

Examples: φ, μ, d(n)=number of divisors, σ(n), the identity id(n)=n, u(n)=1, |μ(n)| (square-free indicator), etc.

Not multiplicative in general: Ω(n) is additive not multiplicative.

**ELI5**

- For coprime "parts", the function value on the whole is the product of values on the parts. Like independent choices for each prime's contribution.
- This lets us compute f(n) from the prime factorization by multiplying the local f(p^e).

**Worked Example:**

**φ(12)=φ(4*3)=φ(4)φ(3)=2*2=4, since gcd(4,3)=1.**

Yes.

## Preservation under Convolution

<!-- section: multiplicative-conv-preserve -->

If f and g multiplicative then f*g is multiplicative.

Proof: when m,n coprime the divisors factor, the double sum factors into product of two convolutions.

This is why if you have two multiplicative, their "combined" via divisor sums is again multiplicative.

**ELI5**

- The algebra of multiplicative functions is closed under the convolution "multiplication".
- Hence identities like φ * u = id preserve the property.

**Worked Example:**

**d(n) = 1 * 1, both multiplicative, hence d multiplicative.**

Yes, known (d(mn)=d(m)d(n) when coprime).

## Euler Products and Dirichlet Series

<!-- section: multiplicative-euler-products -->

For multiplicative f, sum f(n) n^{-s} = ∏_p (sum_{k=0}^∞ f(p^k) p^{-k s}).

This reduces global sums to local computations at primes.

For completely multiplicative, the local factor is 1 / (1 - f(p) p^{-s}).

**ELI5**

- Because the numbers factor uniquely into primes, the generating function factors into independent prime contributions.
- This is the reason zeta has Euler product, and why 1/zeta has the μ product.

**Worked Example:**

**For f=μ, the product ∏_p (1 - p^{-s}) = 1/ζ(s).**

Yes.

## Common Mistakes

- **Multiplicative does not imply completely multiplicative**: φ( p^2 ) = p^2-p ≠ (p-1)^2 = φ(p)^2.
- **f(1) must be 1**: Some texts require it for the definition; if f(1)≠1 it cannot be multiplicative in the strong sense used for inversion etc.
- **Convolution of multiplicative is multiplicative**: True, but the pointwise product is also.
- **All arithmetic functions are multiplicative**: No, only special ones; random f will not factor over coprimes.
