# Carmichael Function

The Carmichael function λ(n) is the smallest exponent m such that a^m ≡ 1 mod n for all a coprime to n. It is the exponent of the multiplicative group of units (Z/nZ)* , i.e. the lcm of the orders of all elements, or the Carmichael lambda.

It is the maximal order of elements in some cases, but more importantly it is the universal exponent.

λ is used in RSA (the exponent d is mod λ(n) rather than φ(n), since it divides φ(n) and is sometimes smaller), and in the definition of Carmichael numbers (composites n where λ(n) divides n-1).

It refines Euler's theorem: a^λ(n) ≡1 mod n for gcd(a,n)=1, and λ(n) | φ(n).

## Definition and Values for Prime Powers

<!-- section: carmichael-definition -->

λ(1)=1, λ(2)=1, λ(4)=2.

For p odd prime, λ(p^k) = φ(p^k) = p^{k-1}(p-1).

For 2^k , k≥3, λ(2^k) = φ(2^k)/2 = 2^{k-2}.

For general n = 2^{k} * odd, λ(n) = lcm[ λ(2^k), λ(odd part) ].

It is the lcm of the λ of the prime power factors.

**ELI5**

- λ(n) is the "master period" that works for every invertible residue mod n: no matter which a you pick (coprime), raising to λ(n) brings it back to 1.
- It is often smaller than φ(n) when there is a power of 2, making exponents in crypto a bit smaller.

**Worked Example:**

**λ(8), λ(15), λ(105)**

8=2^3, λ(8)=2^{3-2}=2.

15=3*5, λ(15)=lcm(λ(3),λ(5))=lcm(2,4)=4.

105=3*5*7, λ(105)=lcm(2,4,6)=12.

Note φ(105)=105*(2/3*4/5*6/7)=48, and 12|48.

## Relation to Euler and Group Exponent

<!-- section: carmichael-euler-relation -->

By definition, since the group order is φ(n), the exponent λ(n) divides φ(n).

Euler's theorem says the exponent divides φ(n); Carmichael gives the minimal such universal exponent (or at least a universal one that is the actual exponent of the group).

For RSA modulus n=pq distinct odd primes, λ(n)=lcm(p-1,q-1), which divides φ(n)=(p-1)(q-1), and often used for the private exponent to make d smaller.

**ELI5**

- φ(n) is the size of the club of invertible numbers. λ(n) is the smallest number that is a multiple of every member's "order" (the steps to return to 1).
- Using λ instead of φ in exponents still guarantees a^{k} ≡1 when k multiple of λ, but the numbers are smaller.

**Worked Example:**

**In RSA, d = e^{-1} mod λ(n) still works**

Because a^{φ} ≡1, and since λ | φ, a^λ ≡1, so a^{e d} = a^{1 + m λ} = a * (a^λ)^m ≡ a *1 ≡ a mod n, for a coprime (which the message representative is after padding).

## Carmichael Numbers

<!-- section: carmichael-numbers -->

A composite n is Carmichael if it is square-free and for every prime p dividing n, p-1 divides n-1. Equiv, λ(n) divides n-1.

Then for all a coprime to n, a^{n-1} ≡1 mod n, so they pass the Fermat test for all bases.

The condition ensures λ(n) | (n-1).

**ELI5**

- Carmichael numbers are composite "Fermat liars" that satisfy the conclusion of Fermat's Little Theorem for every base coprime to them.
- They are the reason we cannot use plain Fermat test for deterministic primality without checking many bases or using stronger tests.

**Worked Example:**

**561=3*11*17 is Carmichael**

λ(561)=lcm(λ(3),λ(11),λ(17))=lcm(2,10,8)=40.

n-1=560, 40 divides 560? 560/40=14 yes.

Hence for gcd(a,561)=1, a^{560}≡1 mod561.

## Common Mistakes

- **λ(n) always equals φ(n)**: No, for powers of 2 ≥8 it is half, and for products it is lcm which can be smaller than the product.
- **Using λ for non-coprime a**: The power a^λ may not be 1 if gcd(a,n)>1.
- **Carmichael numbers are prime**: No, they are the canonical composites that fool Fermat.
- **λ is always even for n>2**: Yes in practice, but the definition doesn't force it for small.
