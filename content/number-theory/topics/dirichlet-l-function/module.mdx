# Dirichlet L-functions

Dirichlet L-functions generalize the zeta function: for a Dirichlet character χ mod q,
L(s,χ) = sum_{n=1}^∞ χ(n) n^{-s} = ∏_p (1 - χ(p) p^{-s})^{-1} (Re s >1, with care for the principal).

They are the "zeta functions" attached to abelian characters of Gal(Qbar/Q) or of class groups, and their non-vanishing and zero-free regions are central to primes in arithmetic progressions, class number problems, and the distribution of primes in number fields.

The functional equation and analytic continuation exist for all, and the generalized Riemann hypothesis is the RH for all these L-functions.

## Definition and Euler Product

<!-- section: dirichlet-l-def -->

For a character χ mod q,
\[
L(s, \chi) = \sum_{n=1}^\infty \chi(n) n^{-s}.
\]

If χ is principal χ0, L(s,χ0) = ζ(s) ∏_{p|q} (1 - p^{-s}).

For nontrivial χ the product converges for Re s>1 and L is entire (after continuation).

χ is primitive if not induced from a smaller modulus.

**ELI5**

- Like zeta but each n is weighted +1, -1, or complex phase according to its residue class mod q (via the character).
- The Euler product still holds, "twisted" by the character values on primes.

**Worked Example:**

**The non-principal mod4: L(s,χ) = sum_{k=0}^∞ [ 1/(4k+1)^s - 1/(4k+3)^s ], at s=1 = π/4.**

## Analytic Continuation and Functional Equation

<!-- section: dirichlet-l-continuation -->

Every L(s,χ) has analytic continuation to entire function if χ nontrivial, or meromorphic with pole at s=1 if principal.

There is a functional equation relating L(s,χ) to L(1-s, \bar χ) with gamma factors and a root number (complex number of abs 1) depending on the Gauss sum of χ.

The completed Lambda(s,χ) = (q/π)^{ (s+a)/2 } Γ( (s+a)/2 ) L(s,χ) satisfies Lambda(s,χ) = ε Lambda(1-s, \bar χ) with |ε|=1.

**ELI5**

- The twisted sum can be continued leftward by the same methods as zeta (contour, theta, Poisson), but with an extra "conductor" q and a character twist in the gamma.
- The root number ε encodes the "sign" or the Gauss sum phase.

**Worked Example:**

**For the mod4 character, the functional eq relates the L to itself (real), and gives the value at negative etc.**

## Non-vanishing and Zero Free Regions

<!-- section: dirichlet-l-nonvanish -->

L(1,χ) ≠0 for nontrivial χ (Dirichlet, key for his theorem).

For real primitive χ (quadratic), L(1,χ) = π h / sqrt(d) or 2 h log ε / sqrt(d) (class number formula), hence >0.

Zero free regions similar to zeta but with possible Siegel zeros (real zeros close to 1 for real characters), whose existence would be bad for AP but are hard to rule out effectively (Siegel theorem ineffective).

**ELI5**

- No "bad" cancellation at s=1 for the twisted sums; this forces the primes to be positive density in each class.
- Real characters correspond to quadratic fields, and L(1,χ) measures the class number (size of ideal class group).

**Worked Example:**

**For χ the character for Q(sqrt(-d)), L(1,χ) proportional to the class number h(-d). The formula proves h>0 obviously, and gives lower bounds.**

## Applications

<!-- section: dirichlet-l-applications -->

- Dirichlet theorem on primes in AP (from L(1,χ)≠0).

- Prime number theorem in AP (from zero free regions).

- Class number formula and lower bounds for class numbers.

- Artin conjecture on primitive roots (under GRH).

- Distribution of quadratic non-residues, etc.

The GRH for all Dirichlet L-functions implies strong error terms in AP and many other problems.

**ELI5**

- The L-functions "detect" the primes in each "color" (residue class via characters).
- Their zeros control the fluctuations; non-vanishing at 1 gives infinitude, zero free gives asymptotics.

**Worked Example:**

**The Bombieri-Vinogradov theorem (average PNT in AP for d up to x^{1/2-}) is proved using zero density estimates for families of L-functions.**

## Common Mistakes

- **L(s,χ) always entire**: No, principal has pole like zeta.
- **All characters are real ±1**: No, for modulus with φ(d)>2 there are complex characters (values roots of unity).
- **The conductor is always the modulus**: For imprimitive, the L is the same as for the primitive inducing character (with same L).
- **Siegel zeros are known not to exist**: They may exist but any exceptional one has ineffective bound on how close; no known examples.
- **GRH for Dirichlet L is proved**: No, open (like RH for zeta).
