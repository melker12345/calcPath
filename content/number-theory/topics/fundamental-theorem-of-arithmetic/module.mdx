# Fundamental Theorem of Arithmetic

The Fundamental Theorem of Arithmetic (FTA) states that every integer greater than 1 can be expressed as a product of primes in exactly one way, up to the order of the factors. This unique factorization is what makes the integers a unique factorization domain (UFD) and is the single most important structural fact about the multiplicative semigroup of positive integers.

Without FTA, many results about divisors, gcd, lcm, and arithmetic functions would collapse or require much more complicated statements. It justifies the prime factorization "canonical form" used everywhere in number theory, algebra, and cryptography.

The theorem has two parts: existence of a factorization into primes, and uniqueness of that factorization (up to ordering).

## Statement of the Theorem

<!-- section: statement-of-fta -->

**Fundamental Theorem of Arithmetic**: Every integer $n > 1$ can be written as a product of primes
\[
n = p_1^{e_1} p_2^{e_2} \cdots p_k^{e_k}
\]
where the $p_i$ are distinct primes and the exponents $e_i \geq 1$ are positive integers. This representation is unique up to the ordering of the prime factors.

We call this the **prime factorization** or **canonical factorization** of $n$. By convention we write primes in increasing order.

The theorem does not apply directly to 1 (empty product) or negatives (handled by units).

**ELI5**

- Every number >1 has a unique "ingredient list" of prime atoms with quantities (exponents). No matter how you discover the factors, you always get the same list.
- It is like saying every molecule has a unique atomic composition; you cannot rearrange atoms to get a different valid formula for the same molecule.

**Worked Example:**

**Prime factorization of 360**

We already saw 360 = 2^3 × 3^2 × 5^1.

Any other way of factoring (e.g. grouping 8*45 then factoring further) yields exactly the same primes with the same powers.

## Existence of Factorization

<!-- section: existence -->

Existence is proved by strong induction on n.

Base: n=2 is prime, done.

Assume every integer from 2 to n-1 has a prime factorization.

If n is prime, done.

If n composite, then n = a b with 1 < a,b < n. By induction both a and b have prime factorizations; concatenate them to get one for n.

This works because the process terminates (factors strictly decrease).

**ELI5**

- If the number is already prime, stop. If not, split it into two smaller pieces, each of which (by assumption for smaller) can be fully broken down, then combine the pieces.
- Like recursively dismantling a machine into subassemblies until only indivisible parts remain.

**Worked Example:**

**Existence for 30 by recursive splitting**

30 = 5 * 6

6 = 2 * 3

5 is prime; 2,3 prime. So 30 = 2 * 3 * 5. All pieces <30, induction applies.

## Uniqueness of Factorization

<!-- section: uniqueness -->

Uniqueness is deeper and relies on Euclid's lemma (or Bézout's identity): if a prime p divides a product a b, then p divides a or p divides b. More generally for multiple factors.

Proof sketch of uniqueness: suppose two factorizations
n = p1...pr = q1...qs (primes, not necessarily distinct).

Then p1 divides the right side, so by repeated Euclid's lemma p1 equals some qi. Cancel and induct on the number of prime factors.

The key lemma itself is proved using the Euclidean algorithm / gcd: if p | a b but p does not divide a, then gcd(p,a)=1 so there are x,y with p x + a y =1; multiply by b: p (x b) + (a b) y = b. Since p divides a b and p divides p (x b), p divides b.

**ELI5**

- Suppose two different recipes give the same cake. Then the first ingredient of the first recipe must appear in the second recipe (otherwise it couldn't divide the product). Cancel it and repeat; the recipes must match.
- Euclid's lemma is the "prime ingredient cannot be split across the other factors" rule.

**Worked Example:**

**Why 12 cannot have two different factorizations**

Suppose 12 = 2*2*3 = 3*4 but 4=2*2 so same. Suppose wrongly 12=2*6 and claim 6 prime — contradiction because 6=2*3 not prime. The lemma forces any prime dividing a product to "belong" to one side.

## Consequences for Divisibility, GCD, and LCM

<!-- section: consequences-gcd-lcm -->

From unique factorization one immediately obtains:

- $a \mid b$ iff every exponent in a's factorization is ≤ the corresponding in b.
- $\gcd(a,b)$ is obtained by taking min exponents for each prime.
- $\operatorname{lcm}(a,b)$ by taking max exponents.
- $\gcd(a,b) \cdot \operatorname{lcm}(a,b) = |a b|$

These give algorithmic ways to compute gcd/lcm once factorizations are known, and explain why they are multiplicative.

**ELI5**

- To see if one number divides another, just check that it doesn't "use more" of any prime than the bigger one has.
- GCD is the "shared ingredients" at the lowest quantities; LCM is "all ingredients needed to cover both" at highest quantities.

**Worked Example:**

**GCD and LCM of 12 and 18 via exponents**

12 = 2^2 * 3^1

18 = 2^1 * 3^2

gcd: 2^min(2,1) * 3^min(1,2) = 2 * 3 = 6

lcm: 2^max * 3^max = 2^2 * 3^2 = 4*9=36

Check: 6*36=216=12*18 yes.

## Common Mistakes

- **Forgetting the empty product for 1**: 1 has no prime factors; its factorization is the empty product (by convention). It is the multiplicative identity.
- **Treating order or grouping as different factorizations**: 12=2*(2*3) is the same as (2*2)*3 = 2^2 * 3; uniqueness is up to order and association.
- **Assuming FTA holds in other rings**: In $\mathbb{Z}[\sqrt{-5}]$ we have 6=2*3=(1+\sqrt{-5})(1-\sqrt{-5})$ with irreducibles that are not associates and not prime in the ring sense; unique factorization fails.
- **Exponents must be positive**: Writing p^0 is just omitting the prime; zero exponents are not part of the factorization.
- **Uniqueness requires primality**: If you allow composites in the "factors", uniqueness obviously fails (12=3*4=2*6).
