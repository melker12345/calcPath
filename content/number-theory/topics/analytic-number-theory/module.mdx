# Analytic Number Theory

The Riemann zeta function, the prime number theorem, Dirichlet L-functions, and Dirichlet's theorem on arithmetic progressions.

The Riemann zeta function ζ(s) = sum_{n=1}^∞ n^{-s} for Re(s)>1, with analytic continuation to the complex plane except a simple pole at s=1.

It encodes the distribution of primes via its Euler product ζ(s) = ∏_p (1 - p^{-s})^{-1}.

The Riemann hypothesis (all nontrivial zeros have Re=1/2) is one of the most famous open problems, with consequences for the error term in the prime number theorem.

Zeta is the prototype L-function, and its study is central to analytic number theory.

## Definition and Euler Product

<!-- section: zeta-definition -->

For Re(s) >1,
\[
ζ(s) = \sum_{n=1}^\infty n^{-s} = \prod_p (1 - p^{-s})^{-1}.
\]

The product is over primes; the equality is unique factorization (every n has unique prime factors, the geometric series multiply).

The harmonic series ζ(1) diverges (prime harmonic also diverges, Euclid).

**ELI5**

- Zeta(s) is the "sum of 1 over n to the s", like a continuous version of the harmonic series for s=1.
- The product over primes says "every number is built from primes independently", turning the sum into a product.

**Worked Example:**

**ζ(2) = π^2 /6 (Basel problem, solved by Euler).**

The product ∏ (1-1/p^2)^{-1} = π^2/6.

## Analytic Continuation and Functional Equation

<!-- section: zeta-continuation -->

ζ(s) extends to a meromorphic function on all C, with only pole at s=1 (simple, residue 1).

The functional equation relates ζ(s) and ζ(1-s):
\[
π^{-s/2} Γ(s/2) ζ(s) = π^{-(1-s)/2} Γ((1-s)/2) ζ(1-s).
\]

Or in xi form ξ(s) = ξ(1-s) where ξ is entire.

This comes from the theta function Poisson summation or contour integration.

**ELI5**

- Although the sum only converges for Re s>1, there is a unique "nice" way to extend the function to the left half-plane, with a symmetry relating s and 1-s.
- The gamma factors account for the "archimedean place" contribution.

**Worked Example:**

**At negative even integers, ζ(-2k)=0 (trivial zeros), from the functional equation or Bernoulli.**

ζ(-1) = -1/12 (in the extended sense, used in physics).

## The Riemann Hypothesis and Zeros

<!-- section: zeta-rh -->

The nontrivial zeros of ζ(s) lie in the critical strip 0 < Re s <1.

The Riemann hypothesis (RH): all nontrivial zeros have Re(s)=1/2.

There are infinitely many on the line (Hardy), and a positive proportion (Levinson, Conrey ~40%).

Trivial zeros at negative even integers from the gamma poles in the functional eq.

The prime number theorem is equivalent to no zeros on Re s=1.

**ELI5**

- The zeros of zeta control the "wiggles" in the distribution of primes. RH says they are all on the midline of the strip, giving the best possible error in π(x) ~ li(x).
- It is the most important unsolved problem in pure math, with million dollar prize.

**Worked Example:**

**The first nontrivial zero is at 1/2 + 14.1347 i (approx), and its conjugate, and more.**

The product formula over zeros gives explicit formulas for ψ(x) the Chebyshev function.

## Euler Product and Primes

<!-- section: zeta-primes -->

Taking log of the Euler product: -log(1-p^{-s}) = sum p^{-k s}/k , so log ζ(s) = sum_p sum_k p^{-k s}/k .

The von Mangoldt function appears in - ζ'/ζ (s) = sum Λ(n) n^{-s}.

This links the zeros (via contour integral or explicit formula) to the primes.

**ELI5**

- The primes are the "atoms" in the Euler product for zeta. The behavior of zeta (growth, zeros) tells how the primes are distributed.
- If zeta has no zeros near s=1, primes are dense enough (no large gaps in certain sense).

**Worked Example:**

**The prime number theorem π(x) ~ x / log x is equivalent to ζ(s) ≠0 for Re s=1 (s≠1).**

Deeper zero free regions give better errors.

## Common Mistakes

- **ζ(s) converges for all s**: No, only Re>1; continuation is the extension.
- **RH is proved**: No, it is open (though checked for first 10^13 zeros or so).
- **Trivial zeros are the only ones**: No, there are infinitely many nontrivial in the strip.
- **Zeta at s=1 is infinite**: Yes, harmonic series, pole, related to infinitude of primes (Euler).
- **The functional equation is simple**: It involves the gamma function which has its own poles and growth.

The prime number theorem (PNT) states that the number of primes ≤ x, π(x), is asymptotically ~ x / log x (or li(x) the logarithmic integral).

Proved independently by Hadamard and de la Vallée Poussin in 1896, using the fact that ζ(s) ≠0 on Re s=1.

It is the fundamental theorem on the distribution of primes, with many refinements (zero free regions, explicit formulas from the zeros of zeta).

Equivalent forms involve the Chebyshev function ψ(x) ~ x, or the nth prime p_n ~ n log n.

## Statement and Equivalent Forms

<!-- section: pnt-statement -->

π(x) ~ li(x) ~ x / log x as x→∞.

Equiv:

- sum_{p≤x} log p ~ x  (Chebyshev ψ(x) ~ x)

- The nth prime p_n ~ n log n.

The ~ means the ratio →1.

**ELI5**

- Primes are roughly as dense as 1/log x; the "probability" a random number near x is prime is 1/log x.
- There are about x/log x primes up to x; the gaps average log x.

**Worked Example:**

**π(10)=4 (2,3,5,7), 10/log10≈4.34, close.**

π(100)=25, 100/log100≈21.7.

π(10^6)=78498, 10^6 / log(10^6)≈72382, ratio ~1.08, approaching 1.

## Proof Ideas (Zero Free Region)

<!-- section: pnt-proof-ideas -->

Hadamard/de la Vallée Poussin: ζ(s) has no zeros on Re s=1, s≠1.

Then -ζ'/ζ (s) = sum Λ(n) n^{-s} has no pole on the line except from the pole of zeta at 1.

By Tauberian theorems or contour integration (Perron's formula) one deduces ψ(x) ~ x.

The classical proof uses a zero-free region to the left of Re=1, giving some error term π(x) = li(x) + O( x exp(-c sqrt{log x}) ).

**ELI5**

- If there were a zero on the line Re=1, it would "cancel" the pole and make the primes thinner (or create large gaps), but there isn't.
- Moving the contour past the line picks up the main term from the pole at 1, and the zeros contribute the error.

**Worked Example:**

**The classical zero free region is σ > 1 - c / log(|t|+2), giving the exp(-c sqrt log x) error.**

## Explicit Formula and Zeros

<!-- section: pnt-explicit-formula -->

The explicit formula (von Mangoldt, Riemann): ψ(x) = x - sum_ρ x^ρ /ρ - log(2π) -1/2 log(1-x^{-2}) ... where ρ run over nontrivial zeros.

The primes (via ψ) are the "main term x" minus "oscillations from the zeros".

RH is equivalent to ψ(x) = x + O( sqrt(x) log^2 x ).

**ELI5**

- The primes are not smoothly distributed; they have "waves" caused by the zeros of zeta. Each zero ρ contributes a wiggly term x^Reρ / |ρ| oscillating with frequency Im ρ.
- If all Reρ=1/2 the wiggles are no larger than sqrt(x), the best possible on average.

**Worked Example:**

**The bias in Chebyshev function (more primes in certain arithmetic progressions or the skew in π(x) - li(x)) is explained by the low zeros.**

## History and Equivalent Statements

<!-- section: pnt-history-equivalents -->

Chebyshev proved bounds c1 x/log x < π(x) < c2 x/log x (elementary).

Riemann's 1859 paper gave the explicit formula and conjectured RH.

Hadamard and Poussin 1896: PNT.

Erdős-Selberg 1949: elementary proof of PNT (no complex analysis).

Many equivalents: no zeros on Re=1, M(x)=sum_{n≤x} μ(n) = o(x), etc.

**ELI5**

- Before the proof there were only bounds with unknown constants c1,c2 <1< c2.
- The full asymptotic ~ required the zeta function and its zeros not on the edge.

**Worked Example:**

**The elementary proof by Selberg uses the formula involving sum Λ(d) log d + ... ~ 2 x log x or something, and Selberg symmetry formula, to deduce the PNT without zeta.**

## Common Mistakes

- **PNT says primes are random with prob 1/log x**: Good heuristic, but the proof is deterministic via analysis.
- **RH is needed for PNT**: No, PNT is proved; RH gives much stronger error O(sqrt x log x).
- **π(x) ~ x / log x exactly counts**: The ~ is asymptotic; for small x there are biases (li(x) > π(x) for known range, perhaps always).
- **Elementary proof is simple**: The Erdős-Selberg proof is elementary (no complex) but quite intricate.
- **PNT for arithmetic progressions**: Dirichlet theorem is infinitude; the prime number theorem in AP (Siegel-Walfisz or Bombieri-Vinogradov) requires zero free regions for L-functions.

Dirichlet L-functions generalize the zeta function: for a Dirichlet character χ mod q,
L(s,χ) = sum_{n=1}^∞ χ(n) n^{-s} = ∏_p (1 - χ(p) p^{-s})^{-1} (Re s >1, with care for the principal).

They are the "zeta functions" attached to abelian characters of Gal(Qbar/Q) or of class groups, and their non-vanishing and zero-free regions are central to primes in arithmetic progressions, class number problems, and the distribution of primes in number fields.

The functional equation and analytic continuation exist for all, and the generalized Riemann hypothesis is the RH for all these L-functions.

## Definition and Euler Product

<!-- section: dirichlet-l-def -->

For a character χ mod q,
\[
L(s, \chi) = \sum_{n=1}^\infty \chi(n) n^{-s}.
\]

If χ is principal χ0, L(s,χ0) = ζ(s) ∏_{p|q} (1 - p^{-s}).

For nontrivial χ the product converges for Re s>1 and L is entire (after continuation).

χ is primitive if not induced from a smaller modulus.

**ELI5**

- Like zeta but each n is weighted +1, -1, or complex phase according to its residue class mod q (via the character).
- The Euler product still holds, "twisted" by the character values on primes.

**Worked Example:**

**The non-principal mod4: L(s,χ) = sum_{k=0}^∞ [ 1/(4k+1)^s - 1/(4k+3)^s ], at s=1 = π/4.**

## Analytic Continuation and Functional Equation

<!-- section: dirichlet-l-continuation -->

Every L(s,χ) has analytic continuation to entire function if χ nontrivial, or meromorphic with pole at s=1 if principal.

There is a functional equation relating L(s,χ) to L(1-s, \bar χ) with gamma factors and a root number (complex number of abs 1) depending on the Gauss sum of χ.

The completed Lambda(s,χ) = (q/π)^{ (s+a)/2 } Γ( (s+a)/2 ) L(s,χ) satisfies Lambda(s,χ) = ε Lambda(1-s, \bar χ) with |ε|=1.

**ELI5**

- The twisted sum can be continued leftward by the same methods as zeta (contour, theta, Poisson), but with an extra "conductor" q and a character twist in the gamma.
- The root number ε encodes the "sign" or the Gauss sum phase.

**Worked Example:**

**For the mod4 character, the functional eq relates the L to itself (real), and gives the value at negative etc.**

## Non-vanishing and Zero Free Regions

<!-- section: dirichlet-l-nonvanish -->

L(1,χ) ≠0 for nontrivial χ (Dirichlet, key for his theorem).

For real primitive χ (quadratic), L(1,χ) = π h / sqrt(d) or 2 h log ε / sqrt(d) (class number formula), hence >0.

Zero free regions similar to zeta but with possible Siegel zeros (real zeros close to 1 for real characters), whose existence would be bad for AP but are hard to rule out effectively (Siegel theorem ineffective).

**ELI5**

- No "bad" cancellation at s=1 for the twisted sums; this forces the primes to be positive density in each class.
- Real characters correspond to quadratic fields, and L(1,χ) measures the class number (size of ideal class group).

**Worked Example:**

**For χ the character for Q(sqrt(-d)), L(1,χ) proportional to the class number h(-d). The formula proves h>0 obviously, and gives lower bounds.**

## Applications

<!-- section: dirichlet-l-applications -->

- Dirichlet theorem on primes in AP (from L(1,χ)≠0).

- Prime number theorem in AP (from zero free regions).

- Class number formula and lower bounds for class numbers.

- Artin conjecture on primitive roots (under GRH).

- Distribution of quadratic non-residues, etc.

The GRH for all Dirichlet L-functions implies strong error terms in AP and many other problems.

**ELI5**

- The L-functions "detect" the primes in each "color" (residue class via characters).
- Their zeros control the fluctuations; non-vanishing at 1 gives infinitude, zero free gives asymptotics.

**Worked Example:**

**The Bombieri-Vinogradov theorem (average PNT in AP for d up to x^{1/2-}) is proved using zero density estimates for families of L-functions.**

## Common Mistakes

- **L(s,χ) always entire**: No, principal has pole like zeta.
- **All characters are real ±1**: No, for modulus with φ(d)>2 there are complex characters (values roots of unity).
- **The conductor is always the modulus**: For imprimitive, the L is the same as for the primitive inducing character (with same L).
- **Siegel zeros are known not to exist**: They may exist but any exceptional one has ineffective bound on how close; no known examples.
- **GRH for Dirichlet L is proved**: No, open (like RH for zeta).

Dirichlet's theorem (1837): if a and d are coprime positive integers (gcd(a,d)=1), then there are infinitely many primes congruent to a mod d.

This generalizes Euclid's proof of infinitude of primes (the case d=1 or a=1).

The proof introduces L-functions L(s,χ) for Dirichlet characters χ mod d, shows that L(1,χ) ≠0 for nontrivial χ, and deduces that the primes are equidistributed in the coprime residue classes (the prime number theorem for AP is the quantitative version).

It is the first major result in analytic number theory and the beginning of class field theory (abelian extensions correspond to ray class groups, here mod d the classes mod d).

## Statement

<!-- section: dirichlet-ap-statement -->

If gcd(a,d)=1, then there are infinitely many primes p ≡ a mod d.

The density is 1/φ(d) among all primes (by the prime number theorem in arithmetic progressions).

**ELI5**

- In any arithmetic progression a, a+d, a+2d, ... where the common difference and the starting share no common factor, there are infinitely many primes.
- You cannot "avoid" primes by fixing a remainder mod d if it is possible (coprime).

**Worked Example:**

**Primes ≡1 mod 4: 5,13,17,29,37,... infinite by Dirichlet (a=1,d=4 gcd1,4=1).**

Primes ≡3 mod 4: 3,7,11,19,... also infinite.

(If gcd>1, e.g. even numbers >2, only possibly one prime.)

## Dirichlet Characters and L-functions

<!-- section: dirichlet-l-functions -->

A Dirichlet character χ mod d is a homomorphism from (Z/dZ)* to C^*, extended to all Z by 0 when gcd(k,d)>1, and periodic mod d.

There are φ(d) characters mod d.

The principal character χ0 is 1 when coprime, 0 else.

The L-function L(s,χ) = sum χ(n) n^{-s} = ∏ (1 - χ(p) p^{-s})^{-1} (for Re s>1, with modification if χ not primitive).

For nontrivial χ, L(s,χ) extends to entire function.

**ELI5**

- Characters are "signs" or "colors" assigned to the coprime residues mod d that multiply nicely (homomorphism).
- The L-function is like zeta but with the numbers "signed" by the character; its value at s=1 tells if there are "enough" primes in the "colored" classes.

**Worked Example:**

**Mod 4: characters χ0 (principal), and χ(odd)= (-1)^{(k-1)/2} or the non-principal: χ(1)=1, χ(3)=-1 mod4.**

L(s,χ) = sum (-1)^{(n-1)/2} /n^s for odd n = 1 -1/3^s +1/5^s -1/7^s ... = π/4 at s=1 (Leibniz).

## Non-vanishing at s=1

<!-- section: dirichlet-nonvanishing -->

The key analytic step: for nontrivial character χ mod d, L(1,χ) ≠0.

Proof for real (quadratic) characters uses the class number formula or positivity of a certain combination with ζ(s) L(s,χ) which has a pole only from zeta, but if L(1,χ)=0 the order would contradict.

For complex, similar or using the fact that if L(1,χ)=0 then L(1, \bar χ)=0 too, and the product has a zero that cancels the pole in a way that contradicts the non-negative coefficients or Dirichlet's class number formula generalization.

Once L(1,χ)≠0 for χ nontrivial, the log of the product over characters gives that sum_{p≡a mod d} 1/p diverges (like (1/φ(d)) log log x), hence infinitely many such p.

**ELI5**

- If L(1,χ) were 0 for some nontrivial χ, it would "cancel" the divergence from the principal L~ zeta, and some arithmetic progression would have only finitely many primes, contradicting the pole of zeta.
- The non-vanishing is proved by showing that a certain combination of L(1,χ) is positive or related to class numbers >0.

**Worked Example:**

**For d=4, the non-principal L(1,χ)= π/4 ≠0, hence infinitely many p=1 mod4 and p=3 mod4 (the - gives the split).**

## Quantitative Version (PNT in AP)

<!-- section: dirichlet-pnt-ap -->

The prime number theorem in arithmetic progressions: for gcd(a,d)=1,
π(x; d,a) ~ (1/φ(d)) li(x) .

Proved by de la Vallée Poussin with zero free regions for the L-functions (no Siegel zeros in the classical proof, but Siegel theorem gives ineffective constants for exceptional zeros).

Bombieri-Vinogradov theorem gives average over d up to x^{1/2 - eps} the PNT in AP holds.

**ELI5**

- Not only infinite, but the primes are equally distributed among the φ(d) possible remainders coprime to d, with density 1/φ(d).
- The proof requires showing the L-functions have no zeros too close to s=1.

**Worked Example:**

**Among primes, half are 1 mod4, half 3 mod4 (in the limit).**

## Common Mistakes

- **The theorem requires gcd(a,d)=1**: If gcd>1, at most one prime (the prime itself if a prime =a).
- **Dirichlet characters are only the Legendre**: There are φ(d) of them, including complex for d with more classes.
- **L(1,χ)≠0 proved by class number**: For real characters it relates to class number of quadratic fields >0.
- **Effective constants in Siegel theorem**: The constant in the exceptional zero bound is ineffective, so some results using it are ineffective.
- **PNT in AP for all d**: For d growing with x, one needs Bombieri-Vinogradov or GRH for full strength.
