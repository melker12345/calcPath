# Diophantine Equations

A Diophantine equation is a polynomial equation with integer coefficients for which one seeks integer solutions (or rational, or in other rings).

The theory studies existence, finiteness, parametrization, or effective bounds for solutions in various cases.

Linear: solved by extended Euclid.

Quadratic: Hasse principle, Pell, etc.

Higher degree: Fermat, Thue, Mordell, elliptic curves (finitely many integral points by Siegel), and in general undecidable by Matiyasevich (Hilbert 10th negative).

This topic introduces the general notion and basic examples before the specialized chapters.

## General Notions

<!-- section: diophantine-general -->

A Diophantine equation is f(x1,...,xk)=0 with f polynomial in Z[x's], seek x in Z^k.

Or inhomogeneous f= h.

Rational solutions (clear denom) or integer.

Local-global principles, descent, heights, etc are tools.

**ELI5**

- Polynomial eq with integer coeff, want integer (or rational) answers for the variables.
- Some have none, some infinitely many (parametrized), some finite but hard to find all.

**Worked Example:**

**x^2 + y^2 = 3 has no integer solutions (mod 4 squares 0 or1, sum 0,1,2 not 3 mod4). Has rational? No by descent or Hasse (no real? Wait real yes, but mod some p no).**

## Hilbert Tenth Problem

<!-- section: diophantine-hilbert10 -->

Hilbert's 10th problem (1900): is there an algorithm that, given a Diophantine eq, decides if it has integer solutions?

Answered negatively by Matiyasevich (1970, building on Davis-Putnam-Robinson): the r.e. sets are exactly the Diophantine sets. Since halting problem undecidable, no algorithm for Dioph eq.

**ELI5**

- There is no general computer program that can look at any polynomial eq with integer coeffs and always correctly say "yes there are integer solutions" or "no".
- Some families are decidable (linear, quadratic in some cases, elliptic curves under conjectures), but not uniformly for all.

**Worked Example:**

**The negative solution uses encoding of Turing machines or exponential Dioph eq into polynomials via clever number theory (Fibonacci, binomial coeffs etc).**

## Finiteness Results

<!-- section: diophantine-finiteness -->

By Faltings (Mordell conjecture proved 1983): a curve of genus ≥2 over Q has only finitely many rational points.

Siegel: integral points on elliptic curves (genus 1 with rank) finite? For affine, Siegel theorem finite integral points on elliptic.

Thue, Mordell etc for specific.

But effective bounds are hard; Baker etc give some.

**ELI5**

- High genus curves (complicated shapes) can't have too many rational points; only finite.
- This solves many Dioph eq by viewing as points on curves.

**Worked Example:**

**The curve y^2 = x^3 + k (Mordell) has finitely many integral (x,y) for each k; tabulated for small k.**

## Common Mistakes

- **All Dioph eq are decidable**: No, Hilbert 10 negative.
- **Rational solutions always exist if real do**: No, Hasse fails for higher deg.
- **Finiteness means we can find them all**: Finiteness yes, but finding may be hard without effective proofs.
- **Linear always solvable if gcd divides**: Yes for two vars; for more vars similar but with more params.
