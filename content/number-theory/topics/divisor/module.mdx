# Divisors

Divisibility is the most basic relation in number theory. When we say one integer "divides" another, we are expressing that one is a building block of the other through multiplication. This simple idea underlies primes, factorization, gcd computations, and almost every advanced theorem in the subject.

Understanding divisors lets us answer questions like "does 7 divide 1001?", "how many divisors does 60 have?", and "what makes a number perfect or abundant?" These concepts appear in cryptography, computer science (hashing, divisibility checks), and pure mathematics.

## Definition of Divisor and Divisibility

<!-- section: definition-of-divisor -->

An integer $d$ **divides** an integer $n$, written $d \mid n$, if there exists an integer $k$ such that $n = d \cdot k$. Equivalently, $n$ is a multiple of $d$, or the remainder when $n$ is divided by $d$ is zero.

We say $d$ is a **divisor** (or factor) of $n$. Every integer $n$ has at least the trivial divisors $\pm 1$ and $\pm n$. If $d \mid n$ and $d > 0$, we often focus on positive divisors.

Note that divisibility is defined for all integers, including negatives and zero: $0 \mid 0$ is sometimes left undefined or treated specially, but $d \mid 0$ for any $d \neq 0$ since $0 = d \cdot 0$.

**ELI5**

- Think of division as "does this number fit evenly into that number with nothing left over?" Like cookies: 3 divides 12 because you can give exactly 4 cookies to each of 3 people with none left.
- The symbol $\mid$ is like a "goes into" arrow that only points one way when it works perfectly.

**Worked Example:**

**Checking divisibility**

Does 6 divide 42? Yes, because $42 = 6 \times 7$, so $6 \mid 42$.

Does 5 divide 42? $42 / 5 = 8.4$, not integer; 42 = 5*8 + 2, remainder 2, so no.

## Proper Divisors and Classification of Numbers

<!-- section: proper-divisors -->

A **proper divisor** of $n$ is a positive divisor of $n$ other than $n$ itself. Some definitions exclude 1 as well (aliquot parts), but the common modern usage for "proper divisors" includes 1 but excludes $n$.

The **sum of proper divisors** function is the basis for classifying numbers:

- **Perfect**: sum of proper divisors equals $n$ (e.g., 6: 1+2+3=6)
- **Abundant**: sum > $n$ (e.g., 12: 1+2+3+4+6=16 >12)
- **Deficient**: sum < $n$ (most numbers)

These ideas go back to Pythagoras and the ancient Greeks.

**ELI5**

- Proper divisors are the "friends" or "parts" of the number excluding the number itself. For 6 they are 1,2,3 and they add back exactly to 6 — a perfect number, rare and "balanced."
- Abundant numbers have "too many" parts; deficient have "too few."

**Worked Example:**

**Classify 28**

Divisors of 28: 1,2,4,7,14,28.

Proper: 1+2+4+7+14=28. Perfect!

## Basic Properties of Divisibility

<!-- section: divisibility-properties -->

Divisibility satisfies several important properties that behave like a partial order (when restricted to positives):

- Reflexive: $n \mid n$ for all $n$.
- Antisymmetric (on positives): if $a \mid b$ and $b \mid a$ then $a = b$ (up to units $\pm1$).
- Transitive: if $a \mid b$ and $b \mid c$ then $a \mid c$.
- If $a \mid b$ and $a \mid c$ then $a \mid (b+c)$ and $a \mid (b-c)$.
- If $a \mid b$ then $a \mid (b \cdot k)$ for any integer $k$.

Also, 1 divides everything, and everything divides 0 (nonzero).

These rules let us manipulate divisibility statements algebraically without always going back to multiplication.

**ELI5**

- Transitivity is the "chain rule" of divisibility: if A builds B and B builds C, then A builds C.
- If a number divides two things, it divides any integer linear combination of them (key to gcd proofs).

**Worked Example:**

**Using properties**

Given 3 divides 12 and 3 divides 9, then 3 divides 12+9=21 (true) and 12-9=3 (true).

If 4 | 20 and we know 20 | 60? Wait no, from trans: since 4|20 and 20|60? 60/20=3 yes, then 4|60.

## Counting the Divisors

<!-- section: counting-divisors -->

If $n = p_1^{e_1} p_2^{e_2} \cdots p_k^{e_k}$ is the prime factorization, then the total number of positive divisors is $(e_1+1)(e_2+1)\cdots(e_k+1)$.

This follows because any divisor is of form $p_1^{a_1} \cdots p_k^{a_k}$ with $0 \leq a_i \leq e_i$, giving $e_i + 1$ choices per prime.

This formula is extremely useful for quickly determining how many divisors a number has without listing them.

**ELI5**

- Each prime power in the factorization is like a "slot" with a certain number of choices (exponent 0 up to the max). Multiply the options together.
- For 12=2^2 * 3^1 the choices are (2+1)*(1+1)=6 divisors: 1,2,4,3,6,12.

**Worked Example:**

**Number of divisors of 360**

360 = 2^3 * 3^2 * 5^1

Number of divisors: (3+1)(2+1)(1+1) = 4*3*2 = 24.

The divisors range from 1 up to 360 in a symmetric list.

## Common Mistakes

- **Forgetting negatives and 1**: Every nonzero integer has positive and negative divisors; 1 and -1 always divide. Do not list only positives when the definition allows all.
- **1 is not prime and not composite**: 1 has only one positive divisor (itself), so by definition it is excluded from both primes and composites.
- **Divides vs is divided by**: $a \mid b$ means $a$ divides $b$ (b is multiple of a). Confusing the direction is common in word problems.
- **Zero issues**: Nonzero d always divides 0, but 0 never divides nonzero n. Division by zero is undefined in this context too.
- **Assuming "divisor" implies positive**: In many problems we restrict to positive divisors for counting, but the relation $d \mid n$ holds for negatives as well.
