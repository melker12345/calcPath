# Quadratic Gauss Sums

The quadratic Gauss sum is a sum G = sum_{k=0}^{p-1} (k/p) exp(2 π i k / p) where ( /p) Legendre, for odd prime p.

Its magnitude is sqrt(p), and its exact value (with sign or i depending on p mod4) is used in the proof of quadratic reciprocity (Gauss's proof) and in evaluating character sums, class numbers, etc.

Gauss sums are the prototype for more general exponential sums in analytic number theory and have applications in signal processing and coding theory (discrete Fourier).

## Definition and Magnitude

<!-- section: gauss-def -->

For odd prime p, the **quadratic Gauss sum** is
\[
\tau = \sum_{k=0}^{p-1} \left( \frac{k}{p} \right) e^{2\pi i k / p}.
\]

It can be shown that |τ| = sqrt(p).

The sign or phase depends on p mod4: τ = sqrt(p) if p=1 mod4, i sqrt(p) if p=3 mod4 (for the standard definition with + in the exp).

**ELI5**

- A weighted sum over the "residues" with +1 or -1 weights, using complex roots of unity as "directions".
- The total "length" of the vector sum is exactly sqrt(p), a surprising cancellation that is not obvious.

**Worked Example:**

## Evaluation and Sign

<!-- section: gauss-evaluation -->

The exact evaluation of the Gauss sum is a deep result of Gauss: the sum is sqrt(p) or i sqrt(p) depending on p mod 4.

One proof uses the fact that the square of the sum is p * (-1)^{(p-1)/2} or something, and then determine the sign by other means (counting solutions or theta functions).

This evaluation is equivalent to quadratic reciprocity in some sense (Gauss used it to prove reciprocity).

**ELI5**

- Not only the size, but the precise direction in the complex plane is known: real positive for p=1 mod4, imaginary for 3 mod4.
- This precise value is what unlocks many explicit formulas.

**Worked Example:**

**For p=3=3 mod4, the sum should be i sqrt(3) or -i depending on convention.**

Compute: (0/3)=0, (1/3)=1, (2/3)=(2/3)=-1 (3 mod8=3).

e^{0}, e^{2π i /3}, e^{4π i /3}.

Sum 1*1 + (-1) * ω where ω = exp(2π i /3).

The value is known to match the formula.

## Use in Proof of Reciprocity

<!-- section: gauss-reciprocity-proof -->

Gauss's sixth proof of quadratic reciprocity uses the Gauss sum and properties of the cyclotomic field or the count of solutions to x^2 - d y^2 ≡1 or something.

By considering the Gauss sum in the ring Z[ζ], or by counting the number of points on certain curves, one derives the reciprocity law.

**ELI5**

- The Gauss sum "encodes" the residuosity in a complex number whose algebra forces the reciprocity relation between different primes.
- It turns the combinatorial statement about squares into an identity in cyclotomic fields.

**Worked Example:**

**The square of the Gauss sum |τ|^2 = p, and the argument gives the sign that appears in the reciprocity law.**

## Common Mistakes

- **The sum includes k=0 term 0**: Yes, the 0 Legendre contributes 0.
- **The exact constant factor (with i or not)**: Depends on the normalization of the sum (sometimes with 2π i or -).
- **Gauss sum for composite moduli**: More general Jacobi sums or Gauss sums for characters exist, but the quadratic one is for prime.
- **Confusing magnitude with the evaluation**: Knowing |τ|=sqrt(p) is easier than the precise phase, which requires more work.
