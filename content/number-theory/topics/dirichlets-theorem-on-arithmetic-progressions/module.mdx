# Dirichlet's Theorem on Arithmetic Progressions

Dirichlet's theorem (1837): if a and d are coprime positive integers (gcd(a,d)=1), then there are infinitely many primes congruent to a mod d.

This generalizes Euclid's proof of infinitude of primes (the case d=1 or a=1).

The proof introduces L-functions L(s,χ) for Dirichlet characters χ mod d, shows that L(1,χ) ≠0 for nontrivial χ, and deduces that the primes are equidistributed in the coprime residue classes (the prime number theorem for AP is the quantitative version).

It is the first major result in analytic number theory and the beginning of class field theory (abelian extensions correspond to ray class groups, here mod d the classes mod d).

## Statement

<!-- section: dirichlet-ap-statement -->

If gcd(a,d)=1, then there are infinitely many primes p ≡ a mod d.

The density is 1/φ(d) among all primes (by the prime number theorem in arithmetic progressions).

**ELI5**

- In any arithmetic progression a, a+d, a+2d, ... where the common difference and the starting share no common factor, there are infinitely many primes.
- You cannot "avoid" primes by fixing a remainder mod d if it is possible (coprime).

**Worked Example:**

**Primes ≡1 mod 4: 5,13,17,29,37,... infinite by Dirichlet (a=1,d=4 gcd1,4=1).**

Primes ≡3 mod 4: 3,7,11,19,... also infinite.

(If gcd>1, e.g. even numbers >2, only possibly one prime.)

## Dirichlet Characters and L-functions

<!-- section: dirichlet-l-functions -->

A Dirichlet character χ mod d is a homomorphism from (Z/dZ)* to C^*, extended to all Z by 0 when gcd(k,d)>1, and periodic mod d.

There are φ(d) characters mod d.

The principal character χ0 is 1 when coprime, 0 else.

The L-function L(s,χ) = sum χ(n) n^{-s} = ∏ (1 - χ(p) p^{-s})^{-1} (for Re s>1, with modification if χ not primitive).

For nontrivial χ, L(s,χ) extends to entire function.

**ELI5**

- Characters are "signs" or "colors" assigned to the coprime residues mod d that multiply nicely (homomorphism).
- The L-function is like zeta but with the numbers "signed" by the character; its value at s=1 tells if there are "enough" primes in the "colored" classes.

**Worked Example:**

**Mod 4: characters χ0 (principal), and χ(odd)= (-1)^{(k-1)/2} or the non-principal: χ(1)=1, χ(3)=-1 mod4.**

L(s,χ) = sum (-1)^{(n-1)/2} /n^s for odd n = 1 -1/3^s +1/5^s -1/7^s ... = π/4 at s=1 (Leibniz).

## Non-vanishing at s=1

<!-- section: dirichlet-nonvanishing -->

The key analytic step: for nontrivial character χ mod d, L(1,χ) ≠0.

Proof for real (quadratic) characters uses the class number formula or positivity of a certain combination with ζ(s) L(s,χ) which has a pole only from zeta, but if L(1,χ)=0 the order would contradict.

For complex, similar or using the fact that if L(1,χ)=0 then L(1, \bar χ)=0 too, and the product has a zero that cancels the pole in a way that contradicts the non-negative coefficients or Dirichlet's class number formula generalization.

Once L(1,χ)≠0 for χ nontrivial, the log of the product over characters gives that sum_{p≡a mod d} 1/p diverges (like (1/φ(d)) log log x), hence infinitely many such p.

**ELI5**

- If L(1,χ) were 0 for some nontrivial χ, it would "cancel" the divergence from the principal L~ zeta, and some arithmetic progression would have only finitely many primes, contradicting the pole of zeta.
- The non-vanishing is proved by showing that a certain combination of L(1,χ) is positive or related to class numbers >0.

**Worked Example:**

**For d=4, the non-principal L(1,χ)= π/4 ≠0, hence infinitely many p=1 mod4 and p=3 mod4 (the - gives the split).**

## Quantitative Version (PNT in AP)

<!-- section: dirichlet-pnt-ap -->

The prime number theorem in arithmetic progressions: for gcd(a,d)=1,
π(x; d,a) ~ (1/φ(d)) li(x) .

Proved by de la Vallée Poussin with zero free regions for the L-functions (no Siegel zeros in the classical proof, but Siegel theorem gives ineffective constants for exceptional zeros).

Bombieri-Vinogradov theorem gives average over d up to x^{1/2 - eps} the PNT in AP holds.

**ELI5**

- Not only infinite, but the primes are equally distributed among the φ(d) possible remainders coprime to d, with density 1/φ(d).
- The proof requires showing the L-functions have no zeros too close to s=1.

**Worked Example:**

**Among primes, half are 1 mod4, half 3 mod4 (in the limit).**

## Common Mistakes

- **The theorem requires gcd(a,d)=1**: If gcd>1, at most one prime (the prime itself if a prime =a).
- **Dirichlet characters are only the Legendre**: There are φ(d) of them, including complex for d with more classes.
- **L(1,χ)≠0 proved by class number**: For real characters it relates to class number of quadratic fields >0.
- **Effective constants in Siegel theorem**: The constant in the exceptional zero bound is ineffective, so some results using it are ineffective.
- **PNT in AP for all d**: For d growing with x, one needs Bombieri-Vinogradov or GRH for full strength.
