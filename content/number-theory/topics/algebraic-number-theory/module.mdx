# Algebraic Number Theory

Algebraic integers, rings of integers, Dedekind domains, ideal class groups, and Dirichlet's unit theorem.

An algebraic integer is a complex number that is a root of a monic polynomial with integer coefficients.

Examples: all ordinary integers Z, sqrt(2), (1+sqrt(-3))/2, roots of x^2 - x -1=0 (golden ratio), etc.

The algebraic integers form a ring (sum and product of algebraic integers are algebraic integers).

In a number field, the algebraic integers inside it form the ring O_K.

They generalize the notion of "integer" beyond Z, and their arithmetic is richer (and harder).

## Definition and Basic Properties

<!-- section: alg-int-def -->

α algebraic integer if there is monic f(x) = x^n + a_{n-1} x^{n-1} + ... + a0 ∈ Z[x] with f(α)=0.

Equiv, the minimal polynomial over Q is monic with Z coeffs.

The set of all algebraic integers is a ring: if α,β alg int, then Z[α,β] is finitely generated as Z-module (basis products of powers), hence α+β, αβ integral over Z.

**ELI5**

- Like integers, but allowed to satisfy higher degree monic eqs like x^2=2 or x^2 +x +1=0.
- Closed under + and *, so you can add and multiply them and stay "integral".

**Worked Example:**

**Golden ratio φ=(1+sqrt5)/2 satisfies x^2 -x -1=0 monic Z coeffs, hence alg integer. φ+1 = (3+sqrt5)/2 also, etc.**

## Algebraic Numbers vs Integers

<!-- section: alg-int-vs-numbers -->

An algebraic number is root of any nonzero poly with Q coeffs (or Z).

The algebraic numbers form a field (the algebraic closure of Q in C).

Algebraic integers are the "integers" among them: those with monic min poly over Z.

The quotient field of the alg integers is the field of alg numbers.

**ELI5**

- Algebraic numbers are "fractions" of alg integers in some sense (every alg number is alg int divided by ordinary int).
- Z is to Q as alg integers to alg numbers.

**Worked Example:**

**1/2 is algebraic number (root of 2x-1=0), but not alg integer (min poly 2x-1 not monic? Min poly x-1/2, leading 1 but coeffs not all Z; or clear: suppose monic Z poly, then by rational root or Gauss would imply 1/2 integer, contradiction).**

## Rings of Algebraic Integers in Extensions

<!-- section: alg-int-rings -->

If K/Q finite, the alg integers in K are exactly O_K the integral closure.

O_K is Z-free of rank [K:Q], and is the "right" ring to do arithmetic in K.

For quadratic fields explicit as above.

**ELI5**

- When you adjoin an algebraic number to Q, the "integers" in the new field are all the alg integers that live there (not just polynomials in the adjoined element).
- It may require a bigger Z-basis than the obvious powers.

**Worked Example:**

**Q(sqrt(1/2)) = Q(sqrt2), but  sqrt(1/2)= sqrt2 /2 is alg number but the integers are still those of Q(sqrt2), which include sqrt2 (alg int) but not necessarily halves unless in the basis.**

## Minimal Polynomial and Characteristic Polynomial

<!-- section: alg-int-minpoly-charpoly -->

If α alg int of degree d, its min poly m(x) monic in Z[x].

If K=Q(α), the char poly of multiplication-by-α on K as Q-vector space is m(x)^ {[K:Q(α)] ? No for power basis.

In general for element in O_K, its char poly over Q is monic in Z[x], and the constant term ± norm, trace etc are in Z.

**ELI5**

- The min poly tells the "equation" α satisfies, with integer coeffs monic.
- The char poly of the linear map "multiply by α" on the vector space K gives the norm (det) and trace (sum eigenvalues) which are integers for alg int α.

**Worked Example:**

**For α=sqrt2 in Q(sqrt2), char poly of mult by α is x^2 -2 (the min poly). Norm of α is - const = -(-2)? Char poly x^2 - tr x + norm = x^2 -0x -2? Norm of sqrt2 is -2? N( a +b sqrt2)=a^2 -2 b^2, N(sqrt2)=-2. Yes, and -(-2) or sign convention in char poly det(xI - m_α).**

## Common Mistakes

- **Every algebraic number is an algebraic integer**: No; 1/2 is alg number not integer.
- **The ring of all alg integers is a UFD or PID**: No, it is not even Noetherian; it is a Bezout domain or something but factorization is wild (infinitely many primes etc).
- **Z[sqrt(-3)] is the full ring of integers of Q(sqrt(-3))**: No, for d=-3=1 mod4, O_K = Z[ω] the Eisenstein, larger, includes ( -1 + sqrt(-3))/2.
- **Min poly always has integer coeffs for alg numbers**: For alg numbers min poly has rational coeffs; for alg int it can be taken monic Z.
- **α alg int implies 1/α alg int**: No (unless unit).

The ring of integers O_K of a number field K is the integral closure of Z in K: the algebraic integers lying in K.

It is a Dedekind domain of rank n=[K:Q] as Z-module, with Z-basis an integral basis.

Computing an integral basis for a given K=Q(α) given by a min poly is a standard (but nontrivial) task in computational algebraic number theory; there are algorithms using discriminant, p-adic completions, or round 2/4 methods.

O_K is the natural place to do "integer arithmetic" in K: factorization of ideals, units, class group all live here.

## Definition and Module Structure

<!-- section: ring-int-def-module -->

O_K = { β ∈ K | β is algebraic integer } = integral closure of Z in K.

It is a subring of K.

There exists a Z-basis ω1,...,ωn of O_K (integral basis), so O_K = Z ω1 + ... + Z ωn as abelian group.

The discriminant of the basis (det (Tr(ωi ωj)) )^2 or the field disc is independent of basis up to sign, and measures the "volume" and ramification.

**ELI5**

- O_K is the "full set of integers" inside K.
- Like Z has basis 1, O_K has n "independent" generators over Z that span all the integers in K.

**Worked Example:**

**For K=Q(sqrt(-5)), integral basis 1, sqrt(-5), disc= -20.**

## Power Basis vs Integral Basis

<!-- section: ring-int-power-vs-integral -->

If K=Q(α) with α alg int, min poly f monic Z, then Z[α] ⊆ O_K, but may be proper.

The index [O_K : Z[α]] is finite, and divides the index related to disc(f)/disc(K).

To find if Z[α]=O_K, compute the disc of f and see if square-free or use criteria (e.g. for quadratic as above).

**ELI5**

- Z[α] is the "obvious" integers polynomials in α with Z coeff.
- Sometimes there are "extra" integers in K not in Z[α], like halves when d=1 mod4.
- The full O_K may be bigger, with a different basis.

**Worked Example:**

**Q(sqrt(5)), α=sqrt5, Z[sqrt5] has disc 20, but full O has disc 5, index 2, basis 1, (1+sqrt5)/2 not in Z[sqrt5].**

## Discriminant and Ramification

<!-- section: ring-int-disc-ram -->

The discriminant disc(K) of the field is disc of any integral basis (up to sign? the det squared or standard def).

It is an integer, and a prime p ramifies in K (some e_i>1 in p O_K) iff p divides disc(K).

For quadratic d, disc = d or 4d, ramified primes divide it.

**ELI5**

- The discriminant is an invariant "size" or "complexity" of the ring O_K, like the volume of the fundamental parallelepiped in the log or Minkowski embedding.
- It tells exactly which rational primes "ramify" (have repeated factors in the ideal factorization) in O_K.

**Worked Example:**

**Q(sqrt(-3)), disc=-3 or -12? d=-3=1 mod4, disc=-3? Standard disc(Q(sqrt d))= d if 1 mod4, 4d else. For -3, -3. 3 ramifies: 3 O = (sqrt(-3))^2 / something? Yes (3, sqrt(-3))^2 / (3) or known.**

## Computing Integral Basis (sketch)

<!-- section: ring-int-computing-basis -->

Given min poly f of α, to find O_K:

- Factor disc(f), for each prime p dividing, use local methods (p-adic) to see if the basis can be improved at p (dedekind criterion or round 2: look for elements (x + α y)/p integral but not in the current order).
- Or use the theorem: an element (a0 + a1 α +... )/d is integral iff it satisfies monic, or trace conditions, but practical algorithms exist in Sage/Pari/Magma.

**ELI5**

- Start with Z[α], compute its disc, for bad primes p dividing the index or disc, solve locally mod p^k to see if there are extra elements with denominator p that are still integral.
- Glue the local improvements to get the global basis.

**Worked Example:**

**For cubic fields there are tables or algorithms that output the integral basis for small disc.**

## Common Mistakes

- **O_K = Z[α] always for primitive element α**: No, counterexamples when p^2 divides the index for some p.
- **Discriminant is always square-free**: No, can have squares (ramified).
- **The basis is unique**: No, any Z-basis, but the disc is invariant.
- **All elements of K are algebraic integers**: No, only those in O_K; K has elements like 1/2, α/2 that may not be integral.
- **Computing O_K is trivial**: For high degree or large coeffs it requires serious computation (factoring disc, local lifts).

A Dedekind domain is an integral domain that is Noetherian, integrally closed, and of Krull dimension 1 (every nonzero prime ideal is maximal).

The key property: in a Dedekind domain, every nonzero ideal factors uniquely as a product of prime ideals.

This makes them the "correct" generalization of Z or PID where unique factorization holds at the ideal level.

Rings of integers O_K of number fields are the main examples (and motivation).

Fractional ideals form a group, and the class group is the quotient by principals.

## Definition and Equivalent Characterizations

<!-- section: dedekind-def -->

A domain R is Dedekind if:

1. Noetherian (every ideal f.g.)

2. Integrally closed in its frac field (if element in frac satisfies monic over R, it is in R)

3. Every nonzero prime ideal is maximal (dim 1).

Equivalent: every nonzero ideal is invertible (as fractional ideal), or unique factorization of ideals into primes, or localization at every max ideal is DVR (discrete valuation ring).

**ELI5**

- Noetherian: "finitely generated ideals", like Z.
- Integrally closed: "no extra integers nearby", like Z.
- Dim 1: "primes are as big as possible", no chain of primes.
- Together, they force ideal unique factorization.

**Worked Example:**

**Z is Dedekind: Noetherian, integrally closed, primes (p) maximal.**

Z[x] is not (dim 2, (x) < (2,x) primes).

## Unique Ideal Factorization

<!-- section: dedekind-uf-ideal -->

In Dedekind R, every nonzero ideal I = P1^{e1} ... Pk^{ek} unique product of prime ideals (up to order).

Proof sketch: first every ideal is invertible (using dim1 + Noeth + closed to show max ideals invertible, then all), then the monoid of ideals is cancellative etc, and one shows primes are the atoms, and ACC or something gives factorization, uniqueness from cancellation.

**ELI5**

- Every "ideal number" factors into "prime ideals" uniquely, like numbers into primes in Z.
- This holds even if the elements themselves don't factor uniquely.

**Worked Example:**

**In O of Q(sqrt(-5)), (6) = (2)(3) = P^2 Q R as in earlier example, and this factorization is unique among ideals.**

## Localization at Prime Ideals are DVRs

<!-- section: dedekind-local-dvr -->

For P prime ideal in Dedekind R, the localization R_P is a DVR: local PID with max ideal P R_P principal, generated by a uniformizer π, every nonzero ideal (π^k).

The discrete valuation v_P (I) = the exponent of P in the factorization of I.

v_P(α) for elements α.

**ELI5**

- Locally at each prime ideal, the ring looks like a power series or p-adic integers: everything is π^k * unit.
- The exponent k is the "order of vanishing" or the power of that prime dividing the ideal.

**Worked Example:**

**In Z, at P=(p), Z_{(p)} is the locals with denom not p, and it is DVR with uniformizer p, v_p the p-adic val.**

## Fractional Ideals and the Ideal Group

<!-- section: dedekind-fractional -->

A fractional ideal is a nonzero R-submodule I of the frac field K such that d I ⊆ R for some d in R nonzero ( "denominator").

The set of fractional ideals forms a group under multiplication, with inverse I^{-1} = {x in K | x I ⊆ R}.

Principal fractional ideals (α) = α R for α in K^* form a subgroup.

The ideal class group is the quotient.

In Dedekind, every fractional ideal factors uniquely into primes (negative exponents allowed).

**ELI5**

- Fractional ideals are like "ideals with denominator", e.g. (1/2) in Z is fractional.
- They form a group, and the class group quotients out the "obvious" principal ones.

**Worked Example:**

**In Z, fractional ideals are of form (a/b) with a,b Z, but up to principals all are the same as Z itself (class number 1).**

## O_K are Dedekind

<!-- section: dedekind-ok-dedekind -->

For number field K, O_K is Noetherian (as Z-module f.g., ideals are Z-submodules too), integrally closed by definition (integral closure), and dim 1 because if Q prime ideal nonzero in O_K, then Q ∩ Z = (p) nonzero prime in Z, and O_K /Q is finite integral over Z/p, hence field (artinian integral domain finite over field is field), so Q maximal.

Thus Dedekind, hence unique ideal factorization.

**ELI5**

- All the properties hold for rings of integers in number fields, so they enjoy unique ideal factorization.
- This is the starting point of algebraic number theory.

**Worked Example:**

**In any O_K, (p) factors into prime ideals P_i^{e_i} with sum e_i f_i =n, the decomposition of p.**

## Common Mistakes

- **Dedekind domains are always PID or UFD**: No; they have unique ideal fact, but elements may not (class number >1).
- **Z[x] is Dedekind**: No, not dim 1.
- **Every Noetherian domain is Dedekind**: No, needs the other two properties.
- **Fractional ideals are ideals**: No, they live in the frac field, may have "denom".
- **In Dedekind every ideal is principal**: Only if class number 1.

The ideal class group Cl_K of a number field K is the group of fractional ideals of O_K modulo the subgroup of principal fractional ideals.

It is finite (class number h_K <∞), and its order h_K measures the failure of O_K to be a PID.

The class group is a fundamental invariant of K; computing it (or even deciding if h=1) is a central problem in computational number theory, with connections to the regulator, L-functions (class number formula), and cryptography (class group based crypto in some old systems, or ideal lattices).

Dirichlet's class number formula relates h R (regulator) to the residue of zeta_K at s=1, involving sqrt|disc| and L(1,χ) or the product.

## Definition

<!-- section: class-group-def -->

The group of fractional ideals I_K is the free abelian group generated by the prime ideals of O_K (with Z coeffs, negative ok).

The principal fractional ideals P_K = { (α) = α O_K | α ∈ K^* } form a subgroup.

Cl_K = I_K / P_K .

Two ideals I ~ J if I = α J for some α in K^* (equivalent).

The class [I] of an integral ideal I is its class in Cl_K.

**ELI5**

- Ideals are like "generalized numbers". Two are "the same up to units and elements" if one is the other scaled by a field element.
- The classes are the distinct "types" of ideals; the group law is multiplying them.

**Worked Example:**

**In Q(sqrt(-5)), [P] where P=(2,1+sqrt(-5)) has order 2: [P]^2 = [(2)] =1 the principal class.**

## Finiteness (Minkowski)

<!-- section: class-group-finiteness -->

Every ideal class contains an integral ideal I with Norm(I) ≤ M_K , the Minkowski constant ( (4/π)^{r2} n! / n^n sqrt|disc(K)| or similar).

Since only finitely many ideals of bounded norm (the ideals of norm m correspond to factorizations of m in the possible ways), only finitely many classes.

Hence Cl_K finite.

**ELI5**

- In each class, there is a "small" representative ideal (bounded norm, like smallest vector in lattice coset).
- Only finitely many small ideals, hence finite classes.

**Worked Example:**

**For quadratic fields the Minkowski bound is O(sqrt|d|), so one lists all ideals up to that norm and finds the relations to compute the group.**

## The Class Number Formula

<!-- section: class-group-formula -->

The Dedekind zeta function ζ_K(s) = sum ideals 1/N(I)^s = ∏ (1 - N(P)^{-s})^{-1} has a simple pole at s=1 with residue 
κ = 2^{r1} (2π)^{r2} h_K R_K / (w sqrt|disc K| )   where R regulator, w #roots of unity.

This is the class number formula.

It relates the arithmetic (h,R,w,disc) to the analytic (residue of zeta_K).

**ELI5**

- The "density" of ideals (how zeta blows up at s=1) is proportional to the class number times the regulator (size of units) divided by the discriminant "volume".
- It generalizes the fact that for Q, residue of zeta is 1, h=1,R=1 (trivial units), w=2, disc=1.

**Worked Example:**

**For imaginary quadratic, r1=0 r2=1, formula gives h = w sqrt|d| / (2π) * Res , and Res = L(1,χ) for the character, giving explicit h in terms of L(1,χ).**

## Computation and Structure

<!-- section: class-group-computation -->

To compute Cl_K: find all prime ideals of norm ≤ Minkowski bound (factor small primes in O_K), then find relations among them by finding elements of small norm (short vectors in lattices) or using the unit group and reductions, build the relation matrix, smith normal form gives the group structure (usually cyclic or product of cyclics for small fields).

Under GRH there are subexp algorithms etc.

**ELI5**

- List "small prime ideals", then find "equations" like product of some = principal (by finding an element with that factorization), get a matrix of exponents, the cokernel is the class group.
- In practice for small disc one can tabulate all class groups.

**Worked Example:**

**Many quadratic fields have class number 1 (PID), like the 9 imaginary ones with d=-1,-2,-3,-7,-11,-19,-43,-67,-163 (Heegner-Stark-Baker). For real quadratic, many small class number but conjecturally infinite with h=1?**

## Common Mistakes

- **Class group trivial means UFD**: Yes (in Dedekind = PID = UFD).
- **h=1 for all K**: No, most fields have h>1; only finitely many imag quad with h=1.
- **The class group is the unit group**: No, units are in the formula with regulator, class group is about ideals.
- **Computing class group is easy**: For large disc or degree it is hard (subexp but practical only up to certain size).
- **Class number formula is effective**: The residue involves L(1,χ) which may be hard, but gives lower bounds etc.

Algebraic number theory studies algebraic integers (roots of monic polynomials with integer coeffs) and the rings they generate in finite extensions of Q (number fields).

The fundamental phenomenon is that unique factorization into primes often fails in these rings (e.g. in Z[sqrt(-5)], 6=2*3=(1+sqrt(-5))(1-sqrt(-5)), and the factors are irreducible but not prime).

To restore unique factorization one passes to ideals: every nonzero ideal factors uniquely into prime ideals (Dedekind domains).

The ideal class group measures the failure of principal ideals, and its finiteness (class number finite) is a basic theorem.

Dirichlet's unit theorem describes the unit group.

This is the foundation for modern algebraic number theory, class field theory, arithmetic geometry, and applications to cryptography (elliptic curves over number fields, etc).

## Number Fields and Rings of Integers

<!-- section: alg-nt-fields-integers -->

A number field K = Q(α) where α algebraic integer of degree n=[K:Q].

The ring of integers O_K is the integral closure of Z in K: all elements of K that are roots of monic polys with Z coeffs.

O_K is a free Z-module of rank n (has Z-basis).

For K=Q(sqrt(d)) d square-free, O_K = Z[sqrt(d)] if d=2,3 mod4, Z[(1+sqrt(d))/2] if d=1 mod4.

**ELI5**

- Instead of just integers Z, we adjoin roots like sqrt(2) or cube root of 2, and take all "polynomial expressions" that happen to satisfy monic integer equations.
- The ring O_K is the "integers" in this larger world.

**Worked Example:**

**For Q(sqrt(5)), d=5=1 mod4, O= Z[(1+sqrt5)/2] the golden integers, basis 1, (1+sqrt5)/2.**

## Failure of Unique Factorization

<!-- section: alg-nt-failure-ufd -->

In Z[sqrt(-5)], the element 6 factors as 2*3 and as (1+sqrt(-5))(1-sqrt(-5)).

These are irreducible (norm argument: norm 2=4, norm(1+sqrt-5)=6, etc, cannot factor further non-units).

But 2 does not divide 1+sqrt(-5) (otherwise norm 2 divides norm 6? But in the ring 2 does not divide the element).

Hence not UFD, not PID.

**ELI5**

- In this "weird integers" 2 and 3 "split" into other "primes" in two different ways; the atoms are not unique.
- The norm N(a+b sqrt(-5))=(a^2 +5 b^2) is multiplicative and helps prove irreducibility (if norm prime in Z then irreducible).

**Worked Example:**

**Norm of 1+sqrt(-5)=1+5=6. If it factored nontrivially norm would factor as 2*3, but leads to units or contradiction in the ring.**

## Dedekind Domains and Ideal Factorization

<!-- section: alg-nt-dedekind -->

O_K is a Dedekind domain: Noetherian, integrally closed, dimension 1 (every nonzero prime ideal maximal).

In Dedekind domains, every nonzero ideal factors uniquely as product of prime ideals (not necessarily principal).

The prime ideals lying over a rational prime p (p O_K = product P_i ^{e_i}) have ramification e_i, residue degree f_i with sum e f =n.

**ELI5**

- Although elements may not factor uniquely, the "ideal numbers" (ideals) do: every "number" ideal factors into "prime ideals" uniquely.
- This restores the fundamental theorem in the ideal world.

**Worked Example:**

**In Z[sqrt(-5)], the ideal (2) = P^2 where P=(2,1+sqrt(-5)) is prime ideal (norm 2, O/P ≅ F2). Similarly (3)=Q R or something. Then (6)=(2)(3)= P^2 Q R, and the element factorizations correspond to grouping the ideal factors differently.**

## Ideal Class Group and Class Number

<!-- section: alg-nt-class-group -->

Two ideals I,J are equivalent if I = α J for some α in K^* (fractional principal).

The ideal class group Cl_K = fractional ideals / principal fractional ideals (or ideal classes of integral ideals).

It is finite (Minkowski bound: every class has ideal of norm ≤ constant depending on disc and degree).

The class number h_K = |Cl_K|.

h=1 iff O_K is PID (hence UFD).

**ELI5**

- The class group measures how many "different kinds" of ideals there are up to "scaling by elements".
- Finite means only finitely many "shapes"; h=1 means every ideal is principal (like in Z, all ideals (n) principal).

**Worked Example:**

**For Q(sqrt(-5)), class number 2. The ideal P=(2,1+sqrt(-5)) is not principal, but P^2=(2) is. So classes {1, [P]}, order 2.**

## Dirichlet Unit Theorem

<!-- section: alg-nt-units -->

The unit group O_K ^* is finitely generated: O_K ^* ≅ μ × Z^{r} where μ roots of unity in K (finite cyclic), r = r1 + r2 -1 where r1 real embeddings, r2 pairs of complex.

The regulator is the covolume of the log embedding of the free part.

For real quadratic, r=1, units ± ε^k , ε fundamental unit >1, solving Pell-like.

**ELI5**

- Besides the obvious ±1 (or more roots of unity), there are "infinite order" units that you can raise to powers, coming from solutions to norm ±1 equations.
- The number of "independent" infinite units is r1+r2-1 (one less than the number of "directions" or embeddings, because of the product of norms=±1 constraint).

**Worked Example:**

**For Q(sqrt(2)), units ± (1+sqrt2)^k , fundamental 1+sqrt2 (norm -1).**

## Common Mistakes

- **O_K always Z[α] for the defining α**: Not always; the power basis may not be integral basis (e.g. Q(sqrt(5)) needs (1+sqrt5)/2).
- **Unique factorization of elements always fails in extensions**: Sometimes holds (e.g. Z[i] UFD for Gaussian).
- **Class number 1 means UFD**: Yes for Dedekind domains, PID iff UFD for these.
- **Units are only ±1**: In most fields there are more; only for Q and imaginary quadratic with small disc.
- **Ideal factorization is into principal ideals**: No, the prime ideals in the factorization may not be principal; that's measured by class group.

Dirichlet's unit theorem describes the structure of the unit group of the ring of integers O_K of a number field K: it is finite torsion (the roots of unity in K) times a free abelian group of rank r = r1 + r2 - 1, where r1 is the number of real embeddings K→R, r2 the number of pairs of complex conjugate embeddings.

The free generators are called fundamental units.

The proof uses the logarithmic embedding of the units into a hyperplane in R^{r1+r2} (logs of the absolute values under the embeddings), and shows that the image is a lattice of full rank in the trace-zero hyperplane (from product of norms = ±1).

The covolume of the lattice is the regulator R_K, which appears in the class number formula.

This is one of the first finiteness + structure theorems in algebraic number theory (Dirichlet 1846).

## Embeddings and Logarithmic Map

<!-- section: unit-embeddings-log -->

Let σ1,...,σ_{r1} the real embeddings K→R, τ1, \bar τ1 , ... τ_{r2} the complex pairs.

For a unit ε in O_K ^*, N(ε)= ±1, so product over all |σ(ε)| =1.

Define the log map l : K^* → R^{r1 + r2} by l(α)_i = log |σ_i (α)| for real, and log |τ_j (α)|^2 for the complex (to make the product correspond to sum of coords = log |N(α)| ).

Then for units, sum of the coordinates of l(ε) = log |N(ε)| =0, so image in the hyperplane sum coords =0.

**ELI5**

- Each embedding gives a "size" |σ(ε)| of the unit; take log to turn mult into add.
- For units the product of all these sizes (with multiplicity for complex) is 1, so the logs sum to 0; they live in a hyperplane.

**Worked Example:**

**For real quadratic, r1=2 r2=0, l(ε)=(log|σ1(ε)| , log|σ2(ε)| ), and log|σ1| + log|σ2| = log |N|=0, so on the line x+y=0.**

## The Image is a Lattice

<!-- section: unit-lattice -->

The image l(O_K ^*) is a discrete subgroup (lattice) in the hyperplane sum=0.

It is of full rank r1+r2-1 (Dirichlet's theorem: there exist r independent units whose logs form a basis of the hyperplane).

The kernel of l on the units is the finite group of roots of unity in K (elements with all |σ(ε)|=1, hence on unit circle, algebraic integers ⇒ roots of unity).

Thus O_K ^* ≅ μ(K) × Z^r with r=r1+r2-1.

**ELI5**

- The logs of units form a grid (lattice) that spans the full "sum zero" space; you can't have more independent directions than the dimension.
- The only units with log=0 (all embeddings size 1) are the roots of unity.

**Worked Example:**

**For Q(sqrt(2)), units ±(1+sqrt2)^k , l(1+sqrt2) = (log(1+sqrt2), log|1-sqrt2|) = (log(1+sqrt2), log(sqrt2-1)) = (c, -c) since product (1+sqrt2)(sqrt2-1)=1? Norm -1, abs product 1. Yes spans the line.**

## Regulator

<!-- section: unit-regulator -->

Choose a basis ε1,...,εr of the free part.

The regulator R_K is the absolute value of the determinant of the r x r matrix obtained by taking the logs of the r+1 embeddings, omitting one coordinate (any row, the covolume is independent).

It is independent of the choice of fundamental units (up to sign, the abs).

R_K >0, and appears in class number formula.

For real quadratic, R = |log ε| where ε>1 fundamental unit (the log of the size).

**ELI5**

- The regulator is the "volume" of the fundamental parallelepiped spanned by the logs of the fundamental units.
- It measures "how large" the fundamental units are; small R means small units, good approximations.

**Worked Example:**

**For d=2, ε=1+sqrt2≈2.414, log ε ≈0.881, R≈0.881 (since the matrix is just that value up to the omitted coord -log).**

## Proof Sketch (Minkowski + Dirichlet)

<!-- section: unit-proof-sketch -->

Consider the log map on all of K^* or on elements of bounded norm or the units directly.

To show the image of units is lattice of full rank: use Minkowski geometry on the embeddings to find units (or elements of norm ±1) in "boxes" that force the logs to be linearly independent or generate dense or full rank.

More precisely: the units are the kernel of the norm map on the S-units or directly: consider the embedding of O_K ^* into the hyperplane, show discrete (obvious, since alg integers bounded in all embeddings are finite), and that the quotient of the hyperplane by the image has finite volume or use pigeonhole on the torus or Minkowski on the lattice of all integers to produce units with logs in certain regions that generate.

Standard proof uses that there are infinitely many units or produces r independent by considering the successive minima or pigeon on the embeddings.

**ELI5**

- Using the geometry of numbers (Minkowski theorem on convex bodies in the embedding space R^n), one finds nonzero integers in K with all embeddings small in certain ways, then their ratios or associates give units with controlled logs.
- Repeating in different "directions" gives the independent units.

**Worked Example:**

**For quadratic real, consider the convex body |x| |y| ≤1 , |x| + |y| ≤ C large in the embedding (x,y) of a+b sqrt d. Minkowski gives short vector, norm bounded, then divide by content or use that there are units from associates.**

## Common Mistakes

- **The rank is r1 + r2**: No, r1+r2 -1, because of the norm=±1 constraint (sum logs=0).
- **All units are ±1**: Only for fields with r=0 (Q and imaginary quadratic with finite units).
- **The regulator is an integer**: No, usually transcendental or irrational, like log(1+sqrt2).
- **Unit theorem gives all solutions to norm equations**: It gives the units (norm ±1), but for norm = other fixed, there may be finitely many or none (S-unit equations etc).
- **Computing fundamental units is easy**: For large degree or disc it is hard (like class group, requires LLL or enumeration in the unit lattice).
