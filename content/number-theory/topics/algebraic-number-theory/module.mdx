# Algebraic Number Theory

Algebraic number theory studies algebraic integers (roots of monic polynomials with integer coeffs) and the rings they generate in finite extensions of Q (number fields).

The fundamental phenomenon is that unique factorization into primes often fails in these rings (e.g. in Z[sqrt(-5)], 6=2*3=(1+sqrt(-5))(1-sqrt(-5)), and the factors are irreducible but not prime).

To restore unique factorization one passes to ideals: every nonzero ideal factors uniquely into prime ideals (Dedekind domains).

The ideal class group measures the failure of principal ideals, and its finiteness (class number finite) is a basic theorem.

Dirichlet's unit theorem describes the unit group.

This is the foundation for modern algebraic number theory, class field theory, arithmetic geometry, and applications to cryptography (elliptic curves over number fields, etc).

## Number Fields and Rings of Integers

<!-- section: alg-nt-fields-integers -->

A number field K = Q(α) where α algebraic integer of degree n=[K:Q].

The ring of integers O_K is the integral closure of Z in K: all elements of K that are roots of monic polys with Z coeffs.

O_K is a free Z-module of rank n (has Z-basis).

For K=Q(sqrt(d)) d square-free, O_K = Z[sqrt(d)] if d=2,3 mod4, Z[(1+sqrt(d))/2] if d=1 mod4.

**ELI5**

- Instead of just integers Z, we adjoin roots like sqrt(2) or cube root of 2, and take all "polynomial expressions" that happen to satisfy monic integer equations.
- The ring O_K is the "integers" in this larger world.

**Worked Example:**

**For Q(sqrt(5)), d=5=1 mod4, O= Z[(1+sqrt5)/2] the golden integers, basis 1, (1+sqrt5)/2.**

## Failure of Unique Factorization

<!-- section: alg-nt-failure-ufd -->

In Z[sqrt(-5)], the element 6 factors as 2*3 and as (1+sqrt(-5))(1-sqrt(-5)).

These are irreducible (norm argument: norm 2=4, norm(1+sqrt-5)=6, etc, cannot factor further non-units).

But 2 does not divide 1+sqrt(-5) (otherwise norm 2 divides norm 6? But in the ring 2 does not divide the element).

Hence not UFD, not PID.

**ELI5**

- In this "weird integers" 2 and 3 "split" into other "primes" in two different ways; the atoms are not unique.
- The norm N(a+b sqrt(-5))=(a^2 +5 b^2) is multiplicative and helps prove irreducibility (if norm prime in Z then irreducible).

**Worked Example:**

**Norm of 1+sqrt(-5)=1+5=6. If it factored nontrivially norm would factor as 2*3, but leads to units or contradiction in the ring.**

## Dedekind Domains and Ideal Factorization

<!-- section: alg-nt-dedekind -->

O_K is a Dedekind domain: Noetherian, integrally closed, dimension 1 (every nonzero prime ideal maximal).

In Dedekind domains, every nonzero ideal factors uniquely as product of prime ideals (not necessarily principal).

The prime ideals lying over a rational prime p (p O_K = product P_i ^{e_i}) have ramification e_i, residue degree f_i with sum e f =n.

**ELI5**

- Although elements may not factor uniquely, the "ideal numbers" (ideals) do: every "number" ideal factors into "prime ideals" uniquely.
- This restores the fundamental theorem in the ideal world.

**Worked Example:**

**In Z[sqrt(-5)], the ideal (2) = P^2 where P=(2,1+sqrt(-5)) is prime ideal (norm 2, O/P ≅ F2). Similarly (3)=Q R or something. Then (6)=(2)(3)= P^2 Q R, and the element factorizations correspond to grouping the ideal factors differently.**

## Ideal Class Group and Class Number

<!-- section: alg-nt-class-group -->

Two ideals I,J are equivalent if I = α J for some α in K^* (fractional principal).

The ideal class group Cl_K = fractional ideals / principal fractional ideals (or ideal classes of integral ideals).

It is finite (Minkowski bound: every class has ideal of norm ≤ constant depending on disc and degree).

The class number h_K = |Cl_K|.

h=1 iff O_K is PID (hence UFD).

**ELI5**

- The class group measures how many "different kinds" of ideals there are up to "scaling by elements".
- Finite means only finitely many "shapes"; h=1 means every ideal is principal (like in Z, all ideals (n) principal).

**Worked Example:**

**For Q(sqrt(-5)), class number 2. The ideal P=(2,1+sqrt(-5)) is not principal, but P^2=(2) is. So classes {1, [P]}, order 2.**

## Dirichlet Unit Theorem

<!-- section: alg-nt-units -->

The unit group O_K ^* is finitely generated: O_K ^* ≅ μ × Z^{r} where μ roots of unity in K (finite cyclic), r = r1 + r2 -1 where r1 real embeddings, r2 pairs of complex.

The regulator is the covolume of the log embedding of the free part.

For real quadratic, r=1, units ± ε^k , ε fundamental unit >1, solving Pell-like.

**ELI5**

- Besides the obvious ±1 (or more roots of unity), there are "infinite order" units that you can raise to powers, coming from solutions to norm ±1 equations.
- The number of "independent" infinite units is r1+r2-1 (one less than the number of "directions" or embeddings, because of the product of norms=±1 constraint).

**Worked Example:**

**For Q(sqrt(2)), units ± (1+sqrt2)^k , fundamental 1+sqrt2 (norm -1).**

## Common Mistakes

- **O_K always Z[α] for the defining α**: Not always; the power basis may not be integral basis (e.g. Q(sqrt(5)) needs (1+sqrt5)/2).
- **Unique factorization of elements always fails in extensions**: Sometimes holds (e.g. Z[i] UFD for Gaussian).
- **Class number 1 means UFD**: Yes for Dedekind domains, PID iff UFD for these.
- **Units are only ±1**: In most fields there are more; only for Q and imaginary quadratic with small disc.
- **Ideal factorization is into principal ideals**: No, the prime ideals in the factorization may not be principal; that's measured by class group.
