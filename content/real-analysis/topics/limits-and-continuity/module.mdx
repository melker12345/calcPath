# Limits & Continuity

ε-δ limits of functions, continuity, uniform continuity, and the intermediate and extreme value theorems.

The limit of f(x) as x approaches a is the value L that f(x) approaches when x gets close to a (but not necessarily equal to a). This idea is the heart of calculus: derivatives are limits of difference quotients, integrals are limits of Riemann sums, continuity is defined using limits.

The rigorous definition uses ε and δ in a way completely analogous to the sequence case, but now the "index" is a real variable approaching a.

## Intuitive Idea and One-Sided Limits

<!-- section: intuitive-one-sided -->

We write lim_{x→a} f(x) = L when f(x) can be made arbitrarily close to L by taking x sufficiently close to a (x ≠ a).

Left-hand limit lim_{x→a^−} uses only x < a; right-hand uses x > a. The two-sided limit exists only when both one-sided limits exist and are equal.

Vertical asymptotes, removable discontinuities, and jump discontinuities are all described with limits.

**ELI5**

- Imagine walking toward a point a on the x-axis; look at the height of the graph. If the heights settle toward a single number L no matter how you approach (from left or right), that L is the limit.
- The function value exactly at a may be different or even undefined; the limit only cares about nearby points.

**Worked Example:**

For f(x) = (x² − 1)/(x − 1) when x ≠ 1, the limit as x→1 is 2, even though f is undefined at 1. Simplifying gives f(x) = x + 1 for x ≠ 1, whose value at 1 would be 2.

## The Precise ε-δ Definition

<!-- section: epsilon-delta-limit -->

lim_{x→a} f(x) = L means:

For every ε > 0 there exists δ > 0 such that if 0 < |x − a| < δ then |f(x) − L| < ε.

The δ is allowed to depend on ε (and on a and f), but not on the particular x inside the punctured δ-neighborhood.

This is the direct translation of "no matter how small a tolerance ε around L you demand, I can find a small enough neighborhood around a (excluding a itself) inside which f stays within ε of L."

**ELI5**

- ε is your vertical tolerance on the y-values. δ is the horizontal tolerance you are willing to allow around a.
- You get to choose δ after seeing ε; smaller ε usually forces you to pick a smaller δ.

**Worked Example:**

Prove lim_{x→3} (2x + 1) = 7.

Let ε > 0. Choose δ = ε/2. If 0 < |x−3| < δ then | (2x+1) − 7 | = |2x − 6| = 2|x−3| < 2δ = ε.

## Infinite Limits, Limits at Infinity, and Limit Laws

<!-- section: infinite-limits-laws -->

We say lim_{x→a} f(x) = +∞ if for every M > 0 there exists δ > 0 such that 0 < |x−a| < δ implies f(x) > M.

Similar for −∞.

lim_{x→∞} f(x) = L means for every ε>0 there exists N such that x > N implies |f(x)−L|<ε.

Standard limit laws (sum, product, quotient, composition, squeeze) hold for function limits exactly as for sequences, provided the individual limits exist (with the usual caveats).

**ELI5**

- f(x) → +∞ as x→a means the graph shoots upward without bound as you approach a (vertical asymptote).
- f(x) → L as x→∞ means that far to the right the graph settles near height L (horizontal asymptote).

**Worked Example:**

lim_{x→0^+} 1/x = +∞. Given M>0 choose δ = 1/M. Then 0 < x < δ implies 1/x > M.

## Common Mistakes

- **Requiring f(a) to equal the limit**: The definition explicitly excludes x=a; the limit can exist even when f is undefined or has a different value at a (removable discontinuity).
- **Reversing quantifiers in the definition**: 'There exists ε such that for all δ …' is wrong; it must be for all ε there exists δ.
- **Assuming one-sided limits automatically agree**: A jump discontinuity has different left and right limits.
- **Forgetting that limit laws require the component limits to exist**: You cannot conclude lim (f+g) exists just because you know something about lim f and lim g separately if one fails to exist.
- **Confusing 'limit is infinity' with 'the limit does not exist'**: In extended reals we write =+∞; in strict real analysis we say the limit does not exist (but diverges to +∞).

The ε-δ definition is the precise, quantifier-based formulation that replaced the intuitive but vague language of "approaches" and "arbitrarily close." It is the definition that makes all subsequent rigorous proofs in calculus and analysis possible.

Mastering the ε-δ definition is the single biggest conceptual hurdle in a first real analysis course; once it is internalized, most other definitions (continuity, uniform continuity, derivative, integral) become natural variations on the same theme.

## The Full Formal Statement

<!-- section: the-definition -->

Let f : D ⊆ ℝ → ℝ and let a be a limit point of D. We say

lim_{x→a} f(x) = L

if and only if

∀ ε > 0 ∃ δ > 0 such that ∀ x ∈ D,   0 < |x − a| < δ   ⇒   |f(x) − L| < ε .

The phrase "a is a limit point of D" means every punctured neighborhood of a contains points of D (so there are points x ≠ a arbitrarily close to a at which f is defined).

**ELI5**

- "No matter which positive tolerance ε (vertical) I hand you, you must be able to hand me back a positive tolerance δ (horizontal) that forces the function values to stay inside the ε-band whenever x is inside the δ-band around a (but not at a itself)."
- The order of the quantifiers is crucial: ε is chosen first (adversarially), δ responds to it.

**Worked Example:**

f(x) = x², a=3, L=9.

Given ε>0 we need |x² − 9| < ε whenever 0<|x−3|<δ.

Factor: |x−3| |x+3| < ε.

If we restrict δ ≤ 1, then |x+3| ≤ 7 when |x−3|<1, so |x²−9| ≤ 7 |x−3|.

Choose δ = min(1, ε/7). Then the product is < ε.

## Proving Limits from the Definition (Typical Strategies)

<!-- section: proofs-using-def -->

Common techniques:
- Factor or bound |f(x) − L| by a constant times |x − a| (linear case, polynomials).
- Restrict δ to a convenient value (e.g. ≤1) to control other factors that depend on x.
- Use the triangle inequality or reverse triangle inequality.
- For rational functions, bound the denominator away from zero in a small neighborhood.

The δ you produce is allowed to be any positive number that works; there is no "best" or "largest" δ required.

**ELI5**

- You are allowed to make the neighborhood smaller than you think you need in order to simplify the algebra (the "min(1, …)" trick).
- Once you have an expression |f(x)−L| ≤ K |x−a|, you simply solve for |x−a| < ε/K.

**Worked Example:**

lim_{x→0} x sin(1/x) = 0 (classic "oscillating but squeezed").

| x sin(1/x) | ≤ |x| · 1 = |x|.

Given ε>0 choose δ = ε. Then 0<|x|<δ ⇒ |x sin(1/x)| < ε. (Squeeze theorem in disguise, but here proved directly.)

## Limit Laws and Their ε-δ Proofs

<!-- section: limit-laws-epsdelta -->

To prove the sum rule from the definition:

Assume lim f = L, lim g = M.

Given ε>0, obtain δ1 for f with ε/2, δ2 for g with ε/2.

Let δ = min(δ1,δ2).

Then 0<|x−a|<δ implies both |f−L|<ε/2 and |g−M|<ε/2, hence |(f+g)−(L+M)| < ε.

Similar (but more bookkeeping) proofs exist for product, quotient, and composition.

**ELI5**

- You split your total error budget ε into pieces (ε/2 + ε/2) and ask each function to stay within its piece.
- The min of the two δ's makes both promises hold simultaneously.

**Worked Example:**

Prove lim (f g) = L M (assuming the limits exist).

| f g − L M | = |f(g−M) + M(f−L)| ≤ |f| |g−M| + |M| |f−L|.

First bound |f| by |L| + 1 in a small neighborhood (using that f→L), then allocate error budgets accordingly and take min of several δ candidates.

## Common Mistakes

- **Writing 'let ε = …' before 'given ε>0'**: The proof must begin with an arbitrary ε handed to you; you may not choose ε.
- **Forgetting the punctured neighborhood**: The implication is only required when 0 < |x−a|, so the value f(a) is irrelevant.
- **Choosing δ that depends on x**: δ must be a fixed positive number once ε is fixed; it cannot vary with the x you later pick.
- **Assuming a largest possible δ exists and must be found**: Any positive δ that works is acceptable; often a very small one is easiest to write down.
- **Neglecting to handle the case when the expression for δ would be negative or zero**: Always take min( some convenient number, ε/K ) with K>0.

A function f is continuous at a point a if lim_{x→a} f(x) = f(a). In other words, small changes in the input produce small changes in the output, and the function value at the point itself matches the limiting value.

Continuity is the most basic regularity condition in analysis; it is preserved by sums, products, quotients (denom ≠0), and compositions.

## The ε-δ Definition of Continuity at a Point

<!-- section: epsilon-delta-continuity -->

f is continuous at a (where a is in the domain) if:

For every ε > 0 there exists δ > 0 such that if |x − a| < δ (now including x = a) then |f(x) − f(a)| < ε.

Compare with the limit definition: the only difference is that we no longer exclude x = a, and we require that the limit equals the actual function value.

**ELI5**

- At a continuous point, when you zoom in on the graph around (a, f(a)) it looks like a single unbroken curve without jumps or holes; the height at the exact point matches the heights nearby.
- The δ-ε promise now includes the center point itself, which is automatically satisfied when x=a (both sides zero).

**Worked Example:**

f(x) = x² is continuous at every a. |x² − a²| = |x−a| |x+a|. Given ε, restrict δ ≤ 1 so |x+a| ≤ 2|a| + 1, then choose δ = min(1, ε / (2|a|+1)). Then |x−a| < δ implies the product < ε.

## Continuity on a Set and Global Properties

<!-- section: continuity-on-sets -->

f is continuous on a set S if it is continuous at every point of S.

A function continuous on a closed bounded interval [a,b] is bounded, attains its max and min (extreme value theorem), and has the intermediate value property.

Uniform continuity on S is a stronger uniform version of the δ-ε (δ independent of the point a ∈ S); see next lesson.

**ELI5**

- On a closed interval a continuous function cannot "run away to infinity" or have jumps; it must reach actual highest and lowest points.
- The graph can be drawn without lifting the pencil on a connected domain.

**Worked Example:**

Any polynomial is continuous on all of ℝ (by algebra of limits + continuity of identity and constants). Rational functions are continuous on their natural domains (where denominator ≠ 0).

## Sequential Characterization and Discontinuities

<!-- section: sequential-discontinuities -->

f is continuous at a iff for every sequence x_n → a (with x_n in domain) we have f(x_n) → f(a).

Discontinuities are classified as removable (limit exists but ≠ f(a) or undefined), jump (left and right limits exist but differ), or essential (oscillatory or infinite).

**ELI5**

- Sequential continuity: if you approach a along any path of points, the function values must approach f(a). If some sneaky sequence approaching a makes f(x_n) misbehave, then f is discontinuous at a.
- Thomae's function is continuous at irrationals and discontinuous at rationals — a subtle example.

**Worked Example:**

Define f(x) = 0 for x irrational, f(p/q) = 1/q in lowest terms. At an irrational a, any sequence x_n → a must eventually have denominators → ∞ (otherwise it would be eventually rational with bounded denom, hence isolated), so f(x_n) → 0 = f(a). At a rational the constant sequence a,a,a,… gives f(x_n)= positive number not → 0.

## Common Mistakes

- **Thinking continuity at a point implies continuity in a neighborhood**: A function can be continuous only at a single point (or on ℚ).
- **Forgetting that the δ now includes the point a**: But because both sides become zero it does not change the algebra much.
- **Confusing uniform continuity with ordinary continuity**: On unbounded sets or sets with holes the two differ (e.g. f(x)=x² on ℝ is continuous but not uniformly continuous).
- **Assuming every discontinuity is a jump**: Essential discontinuities (sin(1/x) at 0) are worse.
- **Believing the sequential characterization only works for metric spaces**: It characterizes continuity in metric spaces; in general topological spaces one needs nets or filters.

A function f is uniformly continuous on a set S if for every ε > 0 there exists δ > 0 (depending only on ε and f and S, not on any particular point) such that for all x, y ∈ S with |x − y| < δ we have |f(x) − f(y)| < ε.

The key word is "uniform": the same δ works everywhere in S simultaneously.

On compact sets (closed bounded intervals in ℝ) ordinary continuity implies uniform continuity (Heine-Cantor theorem). On unbounded domains this can fail.

## Definition and Comparison with Pointwise Continuity

<!-- section: definition-comparison -->

Pointwise continuity at each a ∈ S: for every a and every ε there is a δ_a (that may shrink as a moves or as ε shrinks).

Uniform continuity: one δ(ε) works for the whole set at once — for any pair of points in S that are < δ apart, their images are < ε apart.

If f is uniformly continuous on S then it is (pointwise) continuous on S, but the converse requires extra assumptions on S or on f.

**ELI5**

- Uniform continuity means your "zoom level" or tolerance δ is the same no matter where you are on the domain; you do not have to zoom in more carefully near some dangerous points.
- On a wiggly function on the whole real line you may need finer and finer control as |x| gets large.

**Worked Example:**

f(x) = x is uniformly continuous on ℝ: |f(x)−f(y)| = |x−y|, so δ = ε works everywhere.

f(x) = x² is continuous on ℝ but not uniformly: |x² − y²| = |x−y| |x+y|; when |x| and |y| are large, even a tiny |x−y| can make the product large. For ε=1 no single δ works for all pairs (take x large, y = x + δ/2).

## Heine-Cantor Theorem: Continuous on Compact ⇒ Uniformly Continuous

<!-- section: heine-cantor -->

Theorem: If f is continuous on a compact set K (in ℝ, a closed bounded interval), then f is uniformly continuous on K.

Proof sketch (one common version): suppose not. Then there is ε>0 such that for every n there are points x_n, y_n in K with |x_n − y_n| < 1/n but |f(x_n)−f(y_n)| ≥ ε. By Bolzano-Weierstrass extract convergent subsequences; both subsequences converge to the same limit point c ∈ K (because |x_n−y_n|→0). By continuity f(x_{n_k}) → f(c) and f(y_{n_k}) → f(c), contradicting the separation by ε.

**ELI5**

- On a closed bounded interval the function cannot "get steeper and steeper forever" because it has to attain its max slope somewhere or the steepness would be controlled by compactness.
- The proof uses that any sequence of pairs that get closer must have a limit pair where continuity forces the images to get close.

**Worked Example:**

Any continuous function on [0,1] is uniformly continuous. In particular sin(x)/x extended by 1 at 0 is uniformly continuous on [0,1] even though its derivative blows up? No, on [0,1] derivative of sin x /x is bounded.

## Criterion via Cauchy Sequences; Extensions

<!-- section: cauchy-criterion-extension -->

A function f : S → ℝ (S metric) is uniformly continuous iff it maps Cauchy sequences in S to Cauchy sequences in ℝ.

This criterion is useful for showing that a uniformly continuous f on a dense subset (e.g. ℚ) extends uniquely to a continuous function on the closure (e.g. ℝ).

**ELI5**

- Uniform continuity preserves "terms getting close to each other"; ordinary continuity only preserves "terms approaching a fixed point."
- This is exactly why the completion process works nicely for uniformly continuous maps.

**Worked Example:**

The function f(q) = q² on ℚ is uniformly continuous on any bounded subset of ℚ (because it extends to the continuous x² on ℝ, or by direct estimate). It therefore extends continuously to all reals.

## Common Mistakes

- **Assuming continuity on ℝ implies uniform continuity**: x² and x sin x are counter-examples.
- **Forgetting that the δ must be independent of location**: Many students produce a δ that secretly depends on the point a when trying to prove uniform continuity.
- **Thinking uniform continuity implies bounded derivative**: A function can be uniformly continuous with unbounded derivative (e.g. √x on [0,1]).
- **Confusing with Lipschitz continuity**: Lipschitz ( |f(x)−f(y)| ≤ K |x−y| ) implies uniform continuity with δ = ε/K, but the converse is false (√x is uniformly continuous but not Lipschitz at 0).
- **Neglecting the domain**: A function may be uniformly continuous on [0,1] but its natural extension to ℝ may not be.

The intermediate value theorem (IVT) says that a continuous function on an interval takes on every value between f(a) and f(b). It is the reason we know that continuous curves cannot "jump over" horizontal lines without crossing them.

IVT is the theoretical foundation for the bisection method for root finding and for the existence of solutions to many equations.

## Statement and Proof Idea

<!-- section: statement-proof -->

Let f be continuous on the closed interval [a,b]. Let k be any number strictly between f(a) and f(b). Then there exists c ∈ (a,b) such that f(c) = k.

Without loss of generality assume f(a) < k < f(b). Let S = {x ∈ [a,b] | f(x) ≤ k}. S is nonempty (contains a) and bounded above (by b), so let c = sup S (exists by LUB property of ℝ). One then shows by continuity and the definition of sup that f(c) = k and c ∈ (a,b).

**ELI5**

- Imagine the graph of a continuous curve from (a, f(a)) to (b, f(b)). If you draw any horizontal line y = k between the starting and ending heights, the curve must cross that line at least once; it cannot teleport from below k to above k without passing through height k.
- The proof uses the "last point where the function is still ≤ k"; continuity forces it to be exactly k.

**Worked Example:**

f(x) = x² − 2 on [1,2]. f(1) = −1 < 0 < 2 = f(2). By IVT there is c ∈ (1,2) with c² = 2, i.e. a positive square root of 2 exists in the reals.

## Consequences and Applications

<!-- section: consequences-applications -->

- Every odd-degree polynomial with real coefficients has at least one real root (because it goes to +∞ in one direction and −∞ in the other, so crosses zero).
- The bisection method: repeatedly halve an interval where the function changes sign; the nested intervals converge to a root (when the function is continuous).
- Fixed-point theorems in one dimension: if f : [a,b] → [a,b] is continuous then f has a fixed point (apply IVT to g(x) = f(x) − x).

**ELI5**

- A polynomial like x^5 − x + 1 must cross the x-axis because as x → +∞ it is positive and as x → −∞ it is negative.
- Bisection is a practical algorithm that only needs sign changes and continuity; it is guaranteed to work.

**Worked Example:**

To solve x^3 + x − 1 = 0. f(0) = −1, f(1) = 1. Bisection: midpoint 0.5, f(0.5)≈ −0.375 <0, so root in (0.5,1). Continue; the intervals shrink to the unique real root ≈ 0.6823.

## Why Continuity Is Essential (Counter-examples)

<!-- section: necessity-continuity -->

If continuity is dropped, IVT can fail dramatically. Example: f(x) = 0 for x < 0, f(x) = 1 for x ≥ 0 on [−1,1]. f(−1)=0, f(1)=1, but no c with f(c) = 1/2.

Another classic: the function that is 0 on rationals and 1 on irrationals takes all values between 0 and 1? No, it only takes 0 and 1; it is discontinuous everywhere.

**ELI5**

- A jump discontinuity lets the graph leap from below k to above k without ever being exactly k.
- A function that is wildly discontinuous can miss values even if it starts below and ends above.

**Worked Example:**

Define f(x) = x if x rational, f(x) = x + 1 if x irrational on [0,1]. Then f(0)=0, f(1)=2 (rational) or 1+1=2 (irrational? 1 rational so f(1)=1). Anyway, the image is complicated and misses many values; the function is discontinuous everywhere.

## Common Mistakes

- **Stating IVT without the continuity hypothesis**: The theorem is false without continuity (step functions).
- **Thinking the c is unique**: There may be many crossings (oscillating functions).
- **Applying IVT on open intervals or to discontinuous functions**: The closed interval and continuity are both essential for the standard statement.
- **Confusing IVT with the mean value theorem**: IVT is about values of the function; MVT is about values of the derivative.
- **Believing IVT gives a constructive method**: The pure existence proof via sup does not tell you how to compute c; bisection does, but slowly.

The extreme value theorem (EVT) asserts that a continuous real-valued function on a closed bounded interval attains both a maximum and a minimum value on that interval.

Together with the intermediate value theorem, EVT is one of the two pillars that make continuous functions on compact intervals behave like "nice" functions from calculus.

## Statement

<!-- section: statement -->

If f is continuous on the closed bounded interval [a,b], then there exist points c, d ∈ [a,b] such that f(c) ≤ f(x) ≤ f(d) for all x ∈ [a,b].

In other words, the image f([a,b]) is a closed bounded interval [m, M] where m = min f, M = max f.

**ELI5**

- On a closed interval a continuous function cannot "escape to infinity" at the ends or have jumps; it must actually reach a highest point and a lowest point somewhere on the interval (possibly at the endpoints).
- Think of the graph as a continuous curve from x=a to x=b; it must have a highest and lowest y-value attained.

**Worked Example:**

f(x) = x² on [−1,1] attains its minimum 0 at x=0 and its maximum 1 at both endpoints x=±1.

## Proof Using Completeness

<!-- section: proof-completeness -->

One proof: the image f([a,b]) is bounded (otherwise by IVT or directly one constructs a sequence x_n where |f(x_n)| > n, which by Bolzano-Weierstrass has a convergent subsequence; continuity would imply f bounded near the limit point, contradiction).

Let M = sup f([a,b]) (exists by LUB). There is a sequence y_n in the image with y_n → M. Write y_n = f(x_n). The x_n have a convergent subsequence x_{n_k} → c ∈ [a,b] by compactness. By continuity f(x_{n_k}) → f(c), hence f(c) = M, so the sup is attained.

The argument for the inf is identical (or apply to −f).

**ELI5**

- Boundedness comes from compactness preventing sequences from running away while f grows.
- The sup of the values is approached by some sequence of values f(x_n); the x_n must cluster somewhere c; continuity carries the values f(x_n) to f(c), forcing f(c) to be the sup.

**Worked Example:**

To see why closed is necessary: f(x) = 1/x on (0,1] is continuous but has no maximum (approaches +∞ as x→0+) and no minimum on the open end (inf=0 not attained).

## Why Both Hypotheses Are Necessary

<!-- section: necessity-hypotheses -->

- Without continuity: the step function on [0,1] that is 0 on [0,1/2) and 1 on [1/2,1] is bounded but does not attain all values between 0 and 1? Wait, it attains 0 and 1, but for a function that is 0 on rationals in [0,1] and 1 on irrationals, the image is {0,1}, so min and max exist but the function is discontinuous everywhere.
- A better counter-example for continuity: f(x) = x on [0,1), f(1)=0. This is discontinuous at 1, bounded, but does not attain its sup 1 (the values approach 1 but f never equals 1 on the domain? f(x)=x <1 for x<1, f(1)=0, so sup=1 not attained.
- Without closed/bounded: f(x)=x on [0,∞) has no maximum; f(x)=1/x on (0,1] has no minimum (inf=0 not attained) and no maximum? Wait it has max at 1 but inf not attained.

**ELI5**

- A jump lets the function miss values or fail to reach a supposed max in the middle.
- On an open or unbounded interval the function can "run away" toward the missing end or to infinity without ever reaching a max or min.

**Worked Example:**

f(x) = tan x on (−π/2, π/2) is continuous but unbounded (no max or min).

## Relation to Compactness

<!-- section: compactness-relation -->

In general metric spaces (or topological spaces) the extreme value theorem generalizes to: a continuous real-valued function on a compact set attains its max and min.

In ℝ^n this is equivalent (Heine-Borel) to closed and bounded.

This is one of the reasons compact sets are so important in analysis.

**ELI5**

- Compact = "small and complete" in a precise sense: every sequence has a convergent subsequence (Bolzano-Weierstrass) and the set contains its limit points.
- Continuous images of compact sets are compact; for subsets of ℝ compact = closed bounded interval, whose only compact subsets are themselves closed bounded.

**Worked Example:**

The unit circle is compact in ℝ²; any continuous function (e.g. height) on it attains max and min.

## Common Mistakes

- **Stating EVT on open intervals**: Counter-examples are easy (x on (0,1) has no max).
- **Forgetting that the max/min may be attained at endpoints**: Students sometimes look only for critical points inside (a,b).
- **Confusing attainment of extrema with existence of critical points**: EVT guarantees attainment; Fermat's theorem (if interior extremum and differentiable then f'=0) gives a way to locate them, but requires differentiability.
- **Thinking bounded continuous functions on ℝ attain max/min**: x² is bounded below but has no minimum? Wait it does at 0; sin(x) + x is unbounded. A bounded example without max: sin(x) on ℝ is bounded but never attains a maximum (it gets arbitrarily close to 1 infinitely often but if we modify slightly... actually sin attains ±1. Better: f(x) = x/(1+|x|) on ℝ is bounded by 1 but never reaches ±1.
- **Applying EVT to discontinuous functions on compact sets**: The image need not be closed or bounded in the way expected (though boundedness may still hold or not).
