# The Real Number System

Construction and properties of the real numbers: completeness, order, density, Dedekind cuts, and cardinality.

The real numbers are the numbers we use for measurement, geometry, and all of calculus. They include all the rationals together with irrationals such as $\sqrt{2}$, $\pi$, and $e$. Intuitively they are "all possible lengths" or "all possible points on the number line."

The decisive property that distinguishes the reals from the rationals is completeness: there are no "gaps."

## Definition and Basic Examples

<!-- section: definition -->

A real number can be thought of as any number that can be written as an infinite decimal $3.14159\ldots$, or as the limit of a sequence of rationals, or via a Dedekind cut. The set of all real numbers is denoted $\mathbb{R}$.

Examples: all integers, all fractions, $\sqrt{2}\approx 1.414213\ldots$ (which cannot be written as a ratio of integers), and every number whose decimal expansion eventually repeats or not.

**ELI5**

- Imagine the number line: rationals are points you can reach with a ruler marked in fractions; reals fill every possible point, even those you can never name exactly with a simple fraction.
- $\sqrt{2}$ is real because you can approximate it arbitrarily closely by decimals or fractions (1.4, 1.41, 1.414, …) and the "target" position is a definite place on the line.

**Worked Example:**

Is $0.999\ldots$ (infinite 9s) equal to 1? Yes, they are the same real number. The difference would be $0.000\ldots1$ with the 1 infinitely far to the right, which is zero. Thus $1 - 0.999\ldots = 0$, so they name the same point.

## The Reals as an Ordered Field

<!-- section: ordered-field -->

The real numbers form a field: you can add, subtract, multiply, and divide (except by zero) and the usual algebraic rules hold. They are also totally ordered: for any two reals exactly one of $x < y$, $x = y$, $x > y$ is true, and the order respects the operations (if $x < y$ then $x + z < y + z$, and if $0 < z$ then $xz < yz$).

These properties make $\mathbb{R}$ an ordered field. The rationals $\mathbb{Q}$ are also an ordered field, but they are not complete.

**ELI5**

- Field rules are the "calculator laws": $(x+y)+z = x+(y+z)$, $x\cdot(y+z)=xy+xz$, etc. Order rules say bigger plus anything stays bigger, and multiplying by a positive number preserves inequality.
- You can do all ordinary arithmetic on reals exactly as you do on fractions or decimals.

**Worked Example:**

Prove that if $x > 0$ then $1/x > 0$. Suppose not. If $1/x \le 0$, multiply both sides by $x$ (positive) to obtain $1 \le 0$, contradiction. Hence $1/x > 0$.

## Completeness of the Reals

<!-- section: completeness -->

The reals are complete: every nonempty subset that is bounded above has a least upper bound (supremum) that is itself a real number. Equivalently, every Cauchy sequence of reals converges to a real limit.

This is the property that "fills the gaps" left by the rationals. For example, the set $\{x \in \mathbb{Q} \mid x^2 < 2\}$ is bounded above in $\mathbb{Q}$ but has no least upper bound inside $\mathbb{Q}$; that least upper bound is the real number $\sqrt{2}$.

**ELI5**

- Completeness means you cannot fall through a crack in the number line; if a bunch of numbers get closer and closer together (Cauchy), they must be approaching some definite real number that is already there.
- The rationals have holes: you can have a sequence of rationals approaching $\sqrt{2}$ but $\sqrt{2}$ itself is not rational, so the "limit address" is missing until you enlarge the number system.

**Worked Example:**

Let $A = \{ x \in \mathbb{R} \mid x^2 < 2 \}$. This set is nonempty (contains 1) and bounded above (by 2). Its least upper bound is $\sqrt{2}$, which is real, and $\sqrt{2}$ is not in $A$ (since $(\sqrt{2})^2 = 2$ is not $<2$). Every rational upper bound can be improved.

## Common Mistakes

- **Thinking every real has a 'simple' expression**: Most reals are transcendental or at least irrational and have no finite description; we only ever work with approximations or symbolic names.
- **Confusing $\mathbb{Q}$ and $\mathbb{R}$ completeness**: The rationals are not complete; the set of rationals with square less than 2 has no rational least upper bound.
- **Assuming decimals are the definition**: Infinite decimals are one convenient representation, but they are not the only or the most rigorous one (0.999… = 1 shows the care needed).
- **Forgetting that reals are uncountable**: There are 'more' reals than rationals or naturals; you cannot list them all in a sequence.
- **Mixing up supremum and maximum**: A set may have a least upper bound that is not attained (not a maximum element of the set).

The rational numbers have gaps. To do rigorous calculus we must fill them in a precise, axiomatically controlled way. Two classical constructions achieve this: Cauchy sequences of rationals (with a suitable equivalence) and Dedekind cuts. Both produce a complete ordered field that we call the reals, and they are isomorphic.

Understanding the constructions shows that the reals are not mysterious; they can be manufactured from simpler objects we already trust.

## Cauchy Sequence Construction

<!-- section: cauchy-construction -->

A sequence $(q_n)$ of rationals is Cauchy if for every rational $\varepsilon > 0$ there exists $N$ such that for all $m, n > N$, $|q_m - q_n| < \varepsilon$.

Two Cauchy sequences $(q_n)$ and $(r_n)$ are equivalent if $\lim (q_n - r_n) = 0$ (i.e., for every $\varepsilon > 0$ eventually $|q_n - r_n| < \varepsilon$).

A real number is defined to be an equivalence class of Cauchy sequences of rationals. The rational $q$ is identified with the constant sequence $q, q, q, \dots$

Addition and multiplication are defined termwise on representatives; the order is defined by eventual positivity of differences.

**ELI5**

- Think of each real as a "target" that a sequence of better and better rational approximations is chasing. Two sequences that chase the exact same target are declared the same real.
- The constant sequences give you all the old rationals back; the new numbers come from sequences that are Cauchy but do not converge inside $\mathbb{Q}$.

**Worked Example:**

The sequence defined by $q_1 = 1$, $q_{n+1} = \frac12 (q_n + 2/q_n)$ is Cauchy (it is the Babylonian method for $\sqrt{2}$) and does not converge to any rational. Its equivalence class is the real number $\sqrt{2}$.

## Dedekind Cut Construction

<!-- section: dedekind-construction -->

A Dedekind cut is a pair $(A, B)$ where $A \cup B = \mathbb{Q}$, $A \cap B = \emptyset$, every element of $A$ is less than every element of $B$, and $A$ has no greatest element.

The cut defines a real number: intuitively the "gap" between $A$ and $B$. The rational $r$ corresponds to the cut $A = \{q \in \mathbb{Q} \mid q < r\}$, $B = \{q \mid q \ge r\}$.

Order is by inclusion of the lower sets: cut $(A,B) \le (C,D)$ when $A \subseteq C$.

Addition: lower set of sum is $\{a + c \mid a \in A, c \in C\}$.

**ELI5**

- A cut is literally a "scissors cut" through the rationals: everything to the left of the cut is A, everything to the right is B. The place you cut is the real number, even if no rational sits exactly there.
- For $\sqrt{2}$ the lower set A is all rationals whose square is less than 2; there is no largest such rational.

**Worked Example:**

The cut for $\sqrt{2}$ has lower set $A = \{ q \in \mathbb{Q} \mid q \le 0 \text{ or } q^2 < 2 \}$. This A has no largest element (if $q^2 < 2$ you can find a slightly larger rational still with square <2). The corresponding real is positive and squares to 2.

## Equivalence of the Two Constructions

<!-- section: equivalence -->

It can be shown that the two constructions yield isomorphic ordered fields: every Cauchy equivalence class corresponds to a unique Dedekind cut (the cut consisting of all rationals strictly less than any term of a representative sequence, eventually), and vice versa.

Both satisfy the least-upper-bound property and contain a copy of $\mathbb{Q}$ as a dense subfield. Once we have either model we usually stop worrying about the particular construction and work with the axiomatic properties of $\mathbb{R}$.

**ELI5**

- Different recipes (sequences vs cuts) produce "the same cake": the resulting number systems behave identically, so mathematicians are free to use whichever mental picture is convenient.
- In practice we rarely unwind the definitions; we use the abstract properties (completeness, ordered field) that both constructions guarantee.

**Worked Example:**

Given a Cauchy sequence of rationals $(q_n)$ with limit candidate L (the real it defines), the corresponding Dedekind lower set is all rationals strictly smaller than all but finitely many $q_n$. One proves this set is indeed a cut and that the operations match.

## Common Mistakes

- **Believing the constructions are needed for everyday calculus**: In most analysis courses we take the reals as given by axioms; constructions are only shown once to prove consistency and existence of a complete ordered field.
- **Forgetting equivalence relation**: Not every sequence of rationals defines a new real; only the Cauchy ones, and only up to equivalence.
- **Confusing a sequence with its limit**: The sequence is not the real; the equivalence class is. Different sequences can define the same real.
- **Thinking Dedekind cuts are 'sets of reals'**: Cuts are partitions of the *rationals*; the reals are the cuts themselves.
- **Neglecting to verify the field axioms on the new objects**: Termwise operations on Cauchy sequences must be shown to preserve the Cauchy property and respect equivalence.

The least-upper-bound property (also called the supremum property or completeness axiom) is the single most important distinguishing feature of the real numbers. It says that every nonempty set of reals that is bounded above possesses a least upper bound that is itself a real number.

Without this property, many of the fundamental theorems of calculus (intermediate value theorem, extreme value theorem, existence of maxima, convergence of bounded monotonic sequences, etc.) would fail.

## Statement of the Property

<!-- section: statement -->

Let $S \subseteq \mathbb{R}$ be nonempty and bounded above. Then there exists a real number $u$ such that:

- $u$ is an upper bound: $x \le u$ for all $x \in S$;
- $u$ is the least such: if $v$ is any upper bound then $u \le v$.

We write $u = \sup S$ (or $\operatorname{lub} S$).

If the set also attains its upper bound (i.e., $u \in S$), then $u = \max S$.

**ELI5**

- Imagine a crowd of people on a beach all shorter than some height; there is a shortest possible "tallest person allowed" height that still keeps everyone below it. That height is the least upper bound of their heights.
- The property guarantees that this "tightest ceiling" is a real number you can talk about, even if nobody in the crowd is exactly that tall.

**Worked Example:**

Let $S = \{ x \in \mathbb{R} \mid x < \sqrt{2} \}$. $S$ is nonempty and bounded above (by 2, for instance). Its least upper bound is exactly $\sqrt{2}$, which is not in $S$. Every number smaller than $\sqrt{2}$ fails to be an upper bound.

## Examples and Immediate Consequences

<!-- section: examples-and-consequences -->

- Any finite nonempty set of reals has a maximum, which is also its sup.
- The set of rationals with square less than 2 (inside $\mathbb{R}$) has sup $\sqrt{2}$.
- Every nonempty set bounded below has a greatest lower bound (infimum), obtained by applying sup to the negated set: $\inf S = -\sup (-S)$.
- A bounded monotonic sequence converges (to its sup or inf).

**ELI5**

- Finite sets are easy: just look at the biggest number written down. The power of the axiom appears with infinite sets that have no obvious largest member.
- Monotonic bounded sequences cannot "run away"; they must settle at their least upper bound.

**Worked Example:**

Let $a_1 = 1$, $a_{n+1} = (a_n + 2/a_n)/2$ (Newton for $\sqrt{2}$). The sequence is increasing and bounded above by 2, hence converges to some $L \le 2$. Taking limits gives $L = (L + 2/L)/2$, so $L^2 = 2$, $L = \sqrt{2}$ (positive). The sup of the set of terms is the limit.

## Relation to Other Forms of Completeness

<!-- section: relation-to-completeness -->

The least-upper-bound property is equivalent (over the ordered-field axioms) to:

- Every Cauchy sequence converges.
- The nested interval property (Cantor).
- Bolzano-Weierstrass: every bounded sequence has a convergent subsequence.

In any course one usually takes one of these as the axiom and proves the others. The LUB form is especially convenient for proving existence of suprema used in definitions of integral, derivative, etc.

**ELI5**

- All the different ways people say "the reals have no holes" are logically the same once you have the field and order rules.
- You can start with LUB and derive that Cauchy sequences land inside the system; or start with Cauchy convergence and prove every bounded set has a sup.

**Worked Example:**

To show LUB implies nested interval property: suppose $I_n = [a_n, b_n]$ with $I_{n+1} \subset I_n$ and length $\to 0$. Let $S = \{a_n \mid n \in \mathbb{N}\}$. $S$ is bounded above by any $b_m$. Let $c = \sup S$. Then $c$ lies in every $I_n$ (because $a_n \le c \le b_n$ by construction of sup and nestedness), and by length $\to 0$ the intersection is exactly $\{c\}$.

## Common Mistakes

- **Confusing sup with max**: The supremum need not belong to the set (open intervals, strict inequalities).
- **Assuming every set has a sup**: Only sets that are bounded above are guaranteed one; unbounded sets have sup = +∞ by convention in extended reals.
- **Thinking the property holds for the rationals**: The classic counter-example set $\{q \in \mathbb{Q} : q^2 < 2\}$ is bounded above in $\mathbb{Q}$ yet has no least upper bound inside $\mathbb{Q}$.
- **Forgetting that inf exists too**: The axiom is usually stated for upper bounds; lower bounds follow by symmetry (negation).
- **Using sup when the set is empty**: The empty set is bounded above by everything, so has no least upper bound (or every number is an upper bound).

The Archimedean property expresses that the natural numbers are not bounded above in the real numbers: no matter how large a positive real number you pick, some natural number is even larger. Equivalently, the reciprocals of the naturals get arbitrarily close to zero.

This property is true in $\mathbb{R}$ but can fail in certain non-Archimedean ordered fields (which contain "infinite" and "infinitesimal" elements). It is a direct consequence of the least-upper-bound property.

## Statement and Equivalent Forms

<!-- section: statement -->

The following are equivalent in an ordered field:

1. (Archimedean) For every real $x > 0$ there exists a natural number $n$ such that $n > x$.
2. For every $\varepsilon > 0$ there exists $n \in \mathbb{N}$ such that $0 < 1/n < \varepsilon$.
3. $\mathbb{N}$ is not bounded above in the field.
4. For any two positive reals $x, y$ there exists $n$ with $nx > y$.

**ELI5**

- No real length is "infinitely long" compared with the unit length; you can always lay enough unit segments end-to-end to exceed it.
- Infinitesimals (positive numbers smaller than every 1/n) cannot exist in the reals.

**Worked Example:**

Given $x = 10^{100}$, the number $n = 10^{100} + 1$ is a natural number larger than $x$. In general take any integer strictly larger than $x$ (exists by definition of reals via decimals or LUB applied to the set of naturals).

## Proof from the Least-Upper-Bound Property

<!-- section: proof-lub -->

Suppose for contradiction that $\mathbb{N}$ is bounded above by some real $M$. Let $s = \sup \mathbb{N}$. Then $s - 1$ is not an upper bound, so there exists a natural $k$ with $k > s - 1$. But then $k + 1 > s$, contradicting that $s$ is an upper bound. Hence $\mathbb{N}$ is unbounded.

The other forms follow quickly by taking reciprocals or scaling.

**ELI5**

- If the naturals had a "highest floor" s, then s-1 would still leave room for a larger natural (k), and k+1 would be bigger than s — impossible. So no ceiling exists.
- This is one of the first places we see LUB doing real work.

**Worked Example:**

To prove form 2 from form 1: let $\varepsilon > 0$. By form 1 applied to $x = 1/\varepsilon$ there is n > 1/ε, hence 1/n < ε.

## Consequences and Applications

<!-- section: consequences -->

- Between any two reals there is a rational (actually follows from Archimedean + density of ℤ in a coarser sense, but often proved together).
- The set {1/n | n ∈ ℕ} has infimum 0.
- For any real x the integer part ⌊x⌋ exists: the greatest integer ≤ x (apply Archimedean to find candidates, then LUB on the finite set below x).
- Limits of the form lim n→∞ 1/n = 0, lim n→∞ n = ∞ are justified.

**ELI5**

- You can always find a big enough denominator to make a unit fraction as small as you like.
- Every real has a "whole number part" because naturals go far enough.

**Worked Example:**

Existence of floor: let S = {k ∈ ℤ | k ≤ x}. By Archimedean, S is nonempty (some negative large enough) and bounded above by x, hence has a max m = sup S (finite set of integers). Then m ≤ x < m+1.

## Common Mistakes

- **Thinking Archimedean is part of the field axioms**: It is independent; there exist ordered fields that are non-Archimedean.
- **Confusing with well-ordering of naturals**: Well-ordering says every nonempty subset of ℕ has a least element; Archimedean is about ℕ being unbounded in ℝ.
- **Believing it prevents all 'infinities'**: Extended reals allow ±∞ as symbols, but they are not real numbers; inside ℝ everything is finite.
- **Misapplying to sequences**: A sequence a_n can still tend to infinity even though each term is finite; Archimedean only says no single term is infinite.
- **Forgetting the converse implication**: In the presence of LUB, Archimedean holds; without LUB one can have non-Archimedean complete fields (but those are exotic).

The rational numbers are dense in the reals: between any two distinct real numbers, no matter how close, there lies at least one (in fact infinitely many) rational number. The irrationals are also dense.

This fact lets us approximate any real by rationals to any desired accuracy and is used constantly when passing from theoretical statements about reals to concrete decimal or fractional computations.

## Statement

<!-- section: statement -->

For any reals $a < b$ there exists a rational $q$ such that $a < q < b$.

Equivalently, the set $\mathbb{Q}$ is dense in $\mathbb{R}$ with respect to the standard topology (every nonempty open interval contains a rational).

**ELI5**

- No matter how tiny an interval you draw on the number line, it always contains a point whose coordinate is a fraction p/q.
- You can never draw two reals with "only irrationals between them"; rationals are everywhere.

**Worked Example:**

Take a = 0.1, b = 0.1001. Then 1001/10000 = 0.1001 is too big? Wait, 1001/10000 = 0.1001 exactly at b? Choose 10001/100000 = 0.10001 which is still < b? Simpler: 1/10 = 0.1 = a not strictly >. Take q = 1001/10000 = 0.1001 but if b=0.1001 may equal. Better: for a=√2 ≈1.414, b=1.415 choose the rational 707/500 = 1.414 which lies between them after checking squares or decimals.

## Proof Using Archimedean Property

<!-- section: proof-archimedean -->

Let a < b. Choose a positive integer n large enough that 1/n < b − a (possible by Archimedean property).

Consider the set of integers m such that m > n a. This set is nonempty (Archimedean again) and has a least element m₀ (well-ordering of ℕ or integers).

Then m₀ − 1 ≤ n a, so m₀ / n > a.

Also m₀ ≤ n a + 1, hence m₀ / n ≤ a + 1/n < a + (b − a) = b.

Thus q = m₀ / n satisfies a < q < b and q is rational.

**ELI5**

- Pick a fine enough grid with spacing 1/n smaller than the gap b−a.
- There must be a grid line (multiple of 1/n) that falls strictly inside the open interval; the first one after a does the job.

**Worked Example:**

a = 0, b = 1/√2 ≈ 0.7071. Choose n=2 > 1/(b−a). Then m₀ = 2 (smallest integer > 0). q=2/2=1 > b? Adjust: n=3, smallest m>0 is m=1, 1/3≈0.333 which is between 0 and 0.707. Yes.

## Corollaries and Density of the Irrationals

<!-- section: corollaries -->

- Between any two reals there are infinitely many rationals (repeat the argument on subintervals).
- The irrationals are also dense: if a < b, pick rational q in (a, (a+b)/2) and irrational r = q + (√2)/n for large n so that r still < b.
- Any real is the limit of a sequence of rationals (and of a sequence of irrationals).
- Continuous functions are determined by their values on ℚ (if two continuous f,g agree on ℚ then f=g everywhere).

**ELI5**

- Rationals and irrationals are both "dense dust" filling the line; removing one still leaves the other everywhere.
- This is why we can study a continuous curve by only looking at rational points and taking limits.

**Worked Example:**

To approximate π by rationals: 22/7 ≈ 3.1428 > π, 355/113 ≈ 3.14159292… is a famous good rational approximation lying within 10^{-7} of π.

## Common Mistakes

- **Thinking density means the reals are just the rationals**: Density says rationals are close to everything, not that they exhaust the reals.
- **Confusing with discreteness of integers**: ℤ is not dense; large gaps exist between consecutive integers.
- **Believing 'between a and b there is exactly one rational'**: There are always infinitely many.
- **Forgetting that the rational produced depends on a and b**: The proof is existential; it does not give a canonical 'simplest' fraction.
- **Misstating for open vs closed intervals**: The statement is for open intervals (a,b); on a closed interval the endpoints may be irrational and no rational equals them.

A Dedekind cut is a way to define a real number directly as a "cut" in the ordered set of rationals. It was Richard Dedekind's elegant solution to the problem of filling the gaps in $\mathbb{Q}$ without appealing to decimal expansions or limits.

Cuts give an explicit construction of $\mathbb{R}$ that makes the order and the least-upper-bound property almost immediate from the definition.

## Definition of a Cut

<!-- section: definition -->

A Dedekind cut is a subset $A \subset \mathbb{Q}$ such that:

- $A$ is nonempty and not all of $\mathbb{Q}$;
- if $p \in A$ and $q < p$ (q rational) then $q \in A$ (downward closed);
- $A$ contains no greatest element.

The real number corresponding to the cut is the "position" of the cut. We often write the cut as the pair $(A, \mathbb{Q}\setminus A)$.

**ELI5**

- Picture all rationals lined up. You take a pair of scissors and cut the line into everything on the left (A) and everything on the right. The cut location is the real, whether or not a rational sits exactly at the blades.
- Downward closed + no max means the scissors never land on a rational "last leftmost" point; the cut can sit in a gap.

**Worked Example:**

The cut for zero: $A = \{ q \in \mathbb{Q} \mid q < 0 \}$. It is downward closed, nonempty, proper, and has no largest element (if q<0 then (q+0)/2 <0 and larger).

## Arithmetic Operations on Cuts

<!-- section: operations -->

For cuts $A$ and $B$:

- Sum: $A + B = \{ a + b \mid a \in A, b \in B \}$ (the lower set of the sum).
- For positive cuts, product can be defined via positives; general product uses signs.
- Order: $A \le B$ iff $A \subseteq B$.

These operations turn the set of all cuts into an ordered field. The embedding of a rational r is the cut of all q < r.

**ELI5**

- Adding cuts: slide the two cuts by each other's amounts; the combined left-set is everything reachable by adding a left from each.
- The inclusion order matches "to the left of".

**Worked Example:**

The cut for 1 is all q <1. The cut for √2 (positive) has lower set all q with q<0 or q²<2. Their sum cut corresponds to 1+√2; one verifies the resulting lower set satisfies the cut axioms.

## Why Cuts Give a Complete Ordered Field

<!-- section: why-cuts-work -->

Given any nonempty collection of cuts that is bounded above (by another cut), the union of all the lower sets in the collection is again a cut, and it is the least upper bound of the collection.

This immediately yields the LUB property inside the cuts. One then proves the other field axioms hold, that the cuts contain an isomorphic copy of $\mathbb{Q}$, and that the resulting object is unique up to isomorphism (any two complete ordered fields are isomorphic).

**ELI5**

- The sup of a bunch of cuts is simply "the rightmost cut you can form by taking the union of everything that was on the left for at least one of them."
- Because union preserves the downward-closed and no-max properties, the sup is again a legitimate cut.

**Worked Example:**

Let C be the collection of all cuts A such that A is the cut for some rational q with q² < 2. Then ∪_{A in C} A is exactly the Dedekind cut for √2, which is the sup of the collection inside the reals constructed from cuts.

## Common Mistakes

- **Forgetting the 'no greatest element' clause**: Without it every rational would correspond to two cuts (one including the rational on the left, one on the right), breaking the bijection with reals.
- **Thinking cuts are subsets of reals**: Cuts partition the rationals; the reals are the cuts.
- **Confusing a cut with its 'value'**: Until the isomorphism is proved one must manipulate sets of rationals, not assume the cut 'is' a number you already know.
- **Neglecting to check that sum/product of cuts is again a cut**: The verification that A+B is downward closed and has no max is part of the construction.
- **Assuming the construction is the only possible one**: Cauchy sequences give an equivalent but different-looking model.

The set of real numbers has strictly larger cardinality than the set of natural numbers or the rationals. Its cardinality is called the cardinality of the continuum and is denoted $2^{\aleph_0}$ or $\mathfrak{c}$.

Cantor's diagonal argument shows that no list can contain every real; there are "more" reals than there are countable slots to put them in.

## Countable and Uncountable Sets

<!-- section: countable-vs-uncountable -->

A set is countable if it is finite or can be put into bijection with the natural numbers (i.e., its elements can be listed without repetition or omission as a sequence indexed by ℕ).

The naturals, integers, and rationals are all countable. The reals are not.

Two sets have the same cardinality if there exists a bijection between them.

**ELI5**

- Countable = you can make a (possibly infinite) shopping list that eventually includes every item exactly once.
- The rationals, though infinite, can be listed by traversing diagonals in the grid of fractions; reals cannot.

**Worked Example:**

The set of even naturals is countable via the bijection n ↦ 2n. The set of all integers is countable by zig-zag: 0,1,−1,2,−2,3,−3,…

## Cantor's Diagonal Argument

<!-- section: diagonal-argument -->

Suppose for contradiction that the reals in (0,1) are listed as an infinite sequence r₁, r₂, r₃, … where each r_k has decimal expansion 0.d_{k1} d_{k2} d_{k3}….

Construct a new real s = 0.s₁ s₂ s₃ … by setting s_n = 4 if d_{nn} ≠ 4 and s_n = 5 if d_{nn} = 4.

Then s differs from r_n in the n-th decimal place, so s is not equal to any r_n on the list. Contradiction.

Hence (0,1) (and therefore ℝ) is uncountable.

**ELI5**

- Imagine an infinite hotel with rooms numbered by every possible listing of reals. You build a new guest whose room number differs from the guest in room n in the n-th digit; that guest cannot be on any existing list.
- Changing the diagonal entry guarantees the constructed number is "missed" by the supposed enumeration.

**Worked Example:**

Suppose someone claims to list all reals in (0,1):
r1 = 0.12345…
r2 = 0.31415…
r3 = 0.27182…
…
Then s begins 0.4 (diff from r1's 1), then 4 (diff from r2's 1), then 5 (diff from r3's 8 if we chose 4/5 rule), etc. s ∉ the list.

## Cardinality of the Continuum

<!-- section: cardinality-2aleph0 -->

It is easy to show |ℝ| ≤ |P(ℕ)| = 2^{ℵ₀} (each real corresponds to the set of rationals less than it, or via binary expansion).

Combined with the diagonal argument showing |ℝ| > ℵ₀ we obtain ℵ₀ < |ℝ| = 2^{ℵ₀} = 𝔠.

The famous continuum hypothesis asks whether there is a cardinal strictly between ℵ₀ and 𝔠; it is independent of the usual axioms of set theory.

**ELI5**

- The power set of the naturals (all possible infinite subsets) has the same "size" as the reals.
- There are more subsets of naturals than there are naturals themselves, and exactly as many reals as such subsets.

**Worked Example:**

Inject (0,1) into P(ℕ) by sending x to the set of positions where the binary digits of x are 1. The map is not surjective (some subsets correspond to two expansions) but shows |ℝ| ≤ 2^{ℵ₀}. The other inequality follows because there is an injection of P(ℕ) into ℝ (characteristic functions give binary decimals).

## Common Mistakes

- **Thinking 'uncountable' means 'cannot be listed in any order'**: It means cannot be listed in a sequence indexed exactly by the naturals; well-orderings of the reals exist but require the axiom of choice and are highly pathological.
- **Confusing cardinality with measure or length**: The interval (0,1) and the whole line ℝ have the same cardinality even though one is 'shorter'.
- **Believing the diagonal argument only works for decimals**: It works for any representation that has countably many 'digits'; binary or continued fractions also work with minor adjustments.
- **Assuming all infinities are the same size**: Hilbert's hotel shows countable + countable = countable; diagonal shows some infinities are larger.
- **Claiming the continuum hypothesis is settled**: It is independent of ZFC; one can consistently assume 2^{ℵ₀} = ℵ₁ or much larger values.
