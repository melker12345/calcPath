# Sequences & Series

Convergence of sequences, Cauchy sequences, infinite series, absolute convergence, and power series.

A sequence is simply an infinite ordered list of numbers: a₁, a₂, a₃, … . In real analysis sequences are the basic objects whose limiting behavior encodes continuity, derivatives, integrals, and completeness.

We write (a_n)_{n=1}^∞ or simply (a_n). The index n runs through the positive integers.

## Definition and Notation

<!-- section: definition -->

A sequence in ℝ is a function a : ℕ → ℝ. We usually denote the value at n by a_n rather than a(n).

The sequence is determined by the rule that produces the n-th term. Examples:
- a_n = 1/n
- a_n = (−1)^n
- a_n = n² / (n + 1)

The range of the sequence is the set of values that appear; the sequence itself keeps track of order and repetitions.

**ELI5**

- A sequence is like a film strip: each frame n shows the number a_n. Even if the picture repeats, the position in time (the index) matters.
- Constant sequence a_n = 5 for all n is a perfectly valid (boring) sequence.

**Worked Example:**

The sequence a_n = n / (n+1) has first few terms 1/2, 2/3, 3/4, 4/5, … . Its range is {k/(k+1) | k ∈ ℕ}, which approaches but never reaches 1.

## Common Types of Sequences

<!-- section: types-examples -->

- Arithmetic: a_n = a + (n−1)d (constant difference).
- Geometric: a_n = a r^{n−1} (constant ratio).
- Monotonic: either non-decreasing (a_n ≤ a_{n+1} for all n) or non-increasing.
- Bounded: there exists M such that |a_n| ≤ M for all n.
- Eventually constant or periodic.

Many important sequences are defined recursively (Fibonacci, Newton’s method) rather than by a closed formula.

**ELI5**

- Arithmetic = evenly spaced steps on the number line (like 2,5,8,11,…).
- Geometric = each term multiplies the previous by a fixed factor (1, 1/2, 1/4, 1/8,…).
- Monotonic sequences only move one way: always up or always down (or stay flat).

**Worked Example:**

The geometric sequence with first term 3 and ratio 1/2: 3, 1.5, 0.75, 0.375, … . It is decreasing and bounded below by 0. The partial sums form another important sequence (approaching 6).

## Convergence and Divergence — First Look

<!-- section: convergence-first-look -->

We say the sequence (a_n) converges to L (written a_n → L or lim a_n = L) when the terms eventually get and stay arbitrarily close to L.

If no such L exists the sequence diverges. Classic divergent examples: a_n = n (goes to +∞), a_n = (−1)^n (oscillates), a_n = n sin n (unbounded oscillation).

A sequence can diverge even if it is bounded (oscillation).

**ELI5**

- Convergence = the numbers on the film strip eventually settle down near one fixed target and never wander far again.
- Divergence by oscillation = the film keeps flipping between two distant pictures forever.

**Worked Example:**

a_n = 1/n → 0: given any tolerance ε>0 (say 0.001), after N = ceil(1/ε) all later frames have |a_n| < ε. The terms get visibly smaller on the number line.

## Common Mistakes

- **Confusing the sequence with its range set**: The set {1, 1/2, 1/3, …} is the same for a_n = 1/n and for a rearranged listing; the sequence remembers the order of appearance.
- **Thinking every sequence has a limit**: Bounded sequences need not converge (oscillating ones); unbounded ones usually diverge to ±∞.
- **Ignoring the 'eventually' quantifier**: Early wild terms do not prevent convergence; only the tail matters.
- **Writing lim a_n = ∞ as if ∞ is a real number**: In real analysis we say the sequence diverges to +∞; the limit does not exist in ℝ.
- **Forgetting that constant sequences converge**: They converge (trivially) to their constant value.

The notion of the limit of a sequence is the precise mathematical embodiment of the idea that the terms "eventually get and stay close" to a number L. It is the foundation on which continuity, derivatives, integrals, and infinite series are built.

The ε-N definition replaces vague phrases such as "approaches" or "gets closer" with a clear, quantifier-driven statement that can be used in proofs.

## The ε-N Definition

<!-- section: epsilon-definition -->

We say that the sequence (a_n) converges to L (lim_{n→∞} a_n = L) if:

For every ε > 0 there exists a natural number N such that for all n > N we have |a_n − L| < ε.

L is called the limit. If no such L exists the sequence diverges.

The definition only cares about the tail of the sequence; any finite number of initial terms can be ignored.

**ELI5**

- ε is your "tolerance" or "how close is close enough". N tells you "after this frame in the movie, every subsequent frame is inside your tolerance of L".
- The quantifiers are: no matter which tolerance ε you name (even a tiny one), I can name a moment N after which the promise holds forever.

**Worked Example:**

Prove that a_n = 2 + 1/n → 2.

Let ε > 0 be given. Choose N = ceil(1/ε). Then for n > N we have 1/n < ε, hence | (2 + 1/n) − 2 | = 1/n < ε. Done.

## Uniqueness of Limits and Basic Properties

<!-- section: uniqueness-algebra -->

If a sequence converges, its limit is unique. (Proof: suppose two limits L and M, L ≠ M. Take ε = |L−M|/2; the sequence cannot be eventually inside both ε-balls.)

Algebra of limits: if a_n → L and b_n → M then a_n + b_n → L+M, a_n b_n → L M, and (provided M ≠ 0) a_n / b_n → L/M.

Also constant sequences converge to their constant, and if a_n → L then |a_n| → |L|.

**ELI5**

- A sequence cannot have two different "settling points"; if it did, the distance between those points would give a tolerance that the tail cannot satisfy for both.
- Limits respect the usual arithmetic operations (with the usual caveat about division by zero).

**Worked Example:**

Given a_n → 3 and b_n → 4, then (a_n + 2 b_n) → 3 + 8 = 11 by the algebra rules (sum and scalar multiple).

## Squeeze Theorem and Standard Limits

<!-- section: squeeze-standard-limits -->

Squeeze (sandwich) theorem: if g_n ≤ a_n ≤ h_n for all large n and both g_n → L and h_n → L, then a_n → L.

Classic limits:
- 1/n → 0
- r^n → 0 when |r| < 1
- n^{1/n} → 1
- (1 + 1/n)^n → e

These are proved using binomial theorem, logs, or squeeze.

**ELI5**

- If a number is trapped between two other numbers that are both squeezing toward the same value, it has nowhere else to go.
- The squeeze theorem is the analysis version of "guilt by association."

**Worked Example:**

Show that a_n = sin(n)/n → 0. Because −1 ≤ sin(n) ≤ 1, we have −1/n ≤ sin(n)/n ≤ 1/n. Both −1/n and 1/n → 0, therefore by squeeze sin(n)/n → 0.

## Common Mistakes

- **Reversing the quantifiers**: Students often write 'there exists ε >0 such that for all N …' which is useless. The correct order is 'for all ε there exists N'.
- **Thinking the N depends on the term n**: N is chosen once for the given ε and then works for every larger n.
- **Forgetting to prove the limit exists before using algebra rules**: You may only apply limit laws after you already know the individual limits exist.
- **Writing lim a_n = ∞ inside ℝ proofs**: In the strict sense the limit does not exist; we say diverges to infinity.
- **Ignoring that the definition uses strict inequality |a_n − L| < ε**: Using ≤ is harmless but the definition is stated with <.

A Cauchy sequence is one in which the terms eventually become arbitrarily close to each other, regardless of any candidate limit. The Cauchy criterion is often easier to check than producing a limit in advance, and in a complete space (such as ℝ) every Cauchy sequence converges.

This concept is central to the very definition of the real numbers via completion of the rationals.

## Definition

<!-- section: definition -->

A sequence (a_n) is Cauchy if for every ε > 0 there exists N such that for all m, n > N we have |a_m − a_n| < ε.

Note the quantifiers: m and n are both large, but they can be chosen independently of each other after N.

**ELI5**

- After a certain point in the movie, any two frames you pick are almost identical (within your tolerance ε); the picture has stopped changing appreciably.
- You do not need to know what the final settled picture looks like; you only observe that later frames look like each other.

**Worked Example:**

The sequence a_n = 1/n is Cauchy. Given ε>0 pick N > 2/ε. Then for m,n > N we have |1/m − 1/n| ≤ 1/m + 1/n < 2/N < ε.

## Cauchy Sequences in ℝ Converge (Cauchy Completeness)

<!-- section: cauchy-criterion-convergence -->

Theorem (Cauchy criterion for convergence in ℝ): A sequence of real numbers converges if and only if it is Cauchy.

One direction is easy (convergent ⇒ Cauchy) by triangle inequality: |a_m − a_n| ≤ |a_m − L| + |a_n − L|.

The converse uses the least-upper-bound property: a Cauchy sequence is bounded, hence has a convergent subsequence by Bolzano-Weierstrass; the whole sequence then converges to the same limit.

**ELI5**

- In the reals, "terms get close to each other" automatically means "they get close to some definite number that is already in the system."
- This fails in the rationals: you can have a Cauchy sequence of rationals whose limit would be √2, which is missing from ℚ.

**Worked Example:**

The sequence defined by a_1=1, a_{n+1} = (a_n + 2/a_n)/2 is Cauchy (one shows |a_{n+1} − a_n| decreases quadratically) and therefore converges in ℝ; its limit must satisfy L = (L + 2/L)/2, hence L²=2, L=√2.

## Relation to Completeness; Non-Cauchy Examples

<!-- section: relation-completeness -->

The statement "every Cauchy sequence converges" is one of the equivalent ways to express the completeness of ℝ.

A sequence that is not Cauchy cannot converge (contrapositive of the easy direction). Classic non-Cauchy: a_n = n (terms get farther apart), a_n = (−1)^n (terms jump by 2 forever), harmonic series partial sums (grow like log n, differences 1/n do not go to 0 uniformly).

**ELI5**

- If the terms keep jumping by a fixed amount no matter how far you go, they are not getting close to each other and therefore cannot be approaching any single target.
- Completeness of ℝ is exactly the guarantee that "getting close to each other" is enough to guarantee a limit exists inside the space.

**Worked Example:**

To show the partial sums of the harmonic series s_n = ∑_{k=1}^n 1/k are not Cauchy: for any N, choose m = N, n = 2N. Then s_n − s_m ≥ sum from N+1 to 2N of 1/k > N · (1/(2N)) = 1/2. Thus |s_n − s_m| > 1/2 for arbitrarily large indices, so not Cauchy.

## Common Mistakes

- **Writing 'for all m,n > N, |a_m − a_n| < ε' but allowing N to depend on m or n**: The N must be chosen first from ε alone.
- **Confusing Cauchy with 'the terms get small'**: a_n = n + 1/n is Cauchy? No, terms get large; differences |a_{n+1}-a_n|→1 not to 0. a_n = 5 + 1/n is Cauchy.
- **Thinking every bounded sequence is Cauchy**: Counter-example the oscillating (−1)^n.
- **Forgetting that in incomplete spaces Cauchy sequences may diverge**: The definition of completion of a metric space is precisely to add limits for all Cauchy sequences.
- **Misapplying the criterion to series**: For series we look at Cauchy on the partial sums; the terms a_n themselves must go to 0 but that is not sufficient.

A series is the sum of the terms of a sequence: ∑_{n=1}^∞ a_n. Because there are infinitely many terms, the sum is defined as the limit (if it exists) of the sequence of partial sums s_N = a_1 + … + a_N.

The study of series is the study of when these infinite sums converge and what their values are.

## Partial Sums and Convergence of Series

<!-- section: partial-sums-convergence -->

The series ∑ a_n converges to S if the sequence of partial sums s_N → S as N → ∞.

If the partial sums diverge, the series diverges.

The terms a_n themselves must tend to zero for the series to converge (necessary condition), but this is far from sufficient (harmonic series).

**ELI5**

- A series is like adding an infinite shopping list: you look at the running total after 1 item, after 2 items, after 10, … and ask whether those running totals settle down to a definite final bill.
- If the running totals keep growing without bound or keep oscillating, there is no final sum.

**Worked Example:**

Geometric series ∑ r^{n} from n=0 to ∞ equals 1/(1−r) when |r| < 1. The partial sum s_N = (1 − r^{N+1}) / (1 − r); as N→∞, r^{N+1}→0, leaving 1/(1−r).

## Necessary Condition a_n → 0 and the Harmonic Series

<!-- section: necessary-condition-harmonic -->

If ∑ a_n converges then a_n → 0. Proof: s_n − s_{n−1} = a_n and both s_n and s_{n−1} → S, so a_n → 0.

The harmonic series ∑ 1/n diverges (even though 1/n → 0) because its partial sums grow like ln N + γ (Euler's constant). Grouping terms shows s_{2^k} > 1 + k/2 → ∞.

**ELI5**

- If the added items do not get smaller and smaller, the running total cannot settle.
- Even if items get smaller, if they shrink too slowly (like 1/n) the total still creeps to infinity, just very slowly.

**Worked Example:**

The alternating harmonic series ∑ (−1)^{n+1}/n converges (to ln 2) even though the absolute harmonic diverges. The partial sums oscillate but the amplitude of oscillation shrinks.

## Tests for Convergence (Brief Survey)

<!-- section: convergence-tests -->

- Comparison, limit comparison, ratio, root tests.
- Integral test (for eventually decreasing positive terms).
- Alternating series (Leibniz) test for conditional convergence.
- Absolute vs conditional convergence (next topic).

These tests let us decide convergence without computing the sum explicitly.

**ELI5**

- Ratio test: if each term is eventually less than 3/4 the size of the previous, the tail is dominated by a geometric series with ratio <1 and must converge.
- Alternating series with decreasing terms to 0: the error is at most the size of the first omitted term (like rounding error).

**Worked Example:**

For ∑ 1/n² the integral test or comparison with geometric shows convergence (p-series with p=2>1). The exact sum is π²/6 (Basel problem), but the test only needs to decide yes/no.

## Common Mistakes

- **Thinking a_n → 0 is also sufficient**: The harmonic series is the classic counter-example.
- **Confusing the series with the sequence of terms**: The series is the sequence of partial sums; the terms a_n are only the differences.
- **Forgetting that conditional convergence is possible**: Rearrangements of conditionally convergent series can be made to converge to any number or diverge (Riemann rearrangement theorem).
- **Using the ratio test when the limit is 1**: The test is inconclusive; need another test (e.g. harmonic vs p-series).
- **Believing every convergent series has a 'nice' closed form**: Most convergent series do not; we study them for convergence alone.

A series ∑ a_n is said to converge absolutely if the series of absolute values ∑ |a_n| converges.

Absolute convergence is a stronger property than ordinary (conditional) convergence. If a series converges absolutely then it converges, and moreover any rearrangement of its terms converges to the same sum.

This is one of the most useful distinctions in the theory of infinite series.

## Definition and the Absolute Convergence Theorem

<!-- section: definition-absolute -->

∑ a_n converges absolutely when ∑ |a_n| < ∞ (the series of nonnegative terms converges).

Theorem: Absolute convergence ⇒ convergence.

Proof sketch: 0 ≤ |a_n| − a_n ≤ 2 |a_n|. If ∑ |a_n| converges then ∑ (|a_n| − a_n) converges (comparison), hence ∑ a_n = ∑ (|a_n| − (|a_n| − a_n)) converges as difference of two convergent series.

**ELI5**

- If the sum of the sizes of the terms is finite, then the signed sum cannot wander off to infinity; the positive and negative pieces cannot cancel in a way that breaks convergence.
- Absolute convergence is "robust convergence" — the sum does not rely on delicate cancellation.

**Worked Example:**

The alternating harmonic series ∑ (−1)^{n+1}/n converges, but ∑ | (−1)^{n+1}/n | = harmonic series diverges, so the convergence is conditional, not absolute.

## Rearrangements and Conditional Convergence

<!-- section: rearrangements-conditional -->

If ∑ a_n converges absolutely, then every rearrangement (permutation of the terms) converges to the same sum.

If a series converges only conditionally (converges but not absolutely), then by Riemann's rearrangement theorem one can rearrange the terms to converge to any prescribed real number, or to diverge to ±∞.

**ELI5**

- With absolute convergence the terms are "small enough in total size" that the order you add them does not matter; you always end up at the same place.
- With only conditional convergence the positive and negative pieces are both large (their absolutes diverge); by adding positives for a long time or negatives you can steer the partial sums wherever you want.

**Worked Example:**

For the alternating harmonic, group positives and negatives separately. By taking enough positive terms you can exceed any target M, then add one negative to drop below, then more positives, etc. A careful rearrangement can be made to approach any chosen number.

## Tests That Give Absolute Convergence

<!-- section: tests-absolute -->

Most of the standard tests (ratio, root, comparison, integral, Weierstrass M-test for series of functions) actually prove absolute convergence when they succeed.

The alternating series test is the classic example that can give only conditional convergence.

**ELI5**

- When the ratio test or root test returns a limit <1, it is really saying the absolute series behaves like a convergent geometric series.
- You get absolute convergence "for free" from those tests.

**Worked Example:**

∑ (−1)^n / n² converges absolutely because ∑ 1/n² converges (p-series p=2) and | (−1)^n / n² | = 1/n².

## Common Mistakes

- **Thinking absolute convergence is the same as convergence**: Many series converge conditionally.
- **Applying rearrangement carelessly**: You may change the sum (or destroy convergence) if you rearrange a conditionally convergent series.
- **Forgetting that absolute convergence allows term-by-term operations**: You can integrate or differentiate power series inside the radius of convergence precisely because of absolute convergence on compact subintervals.
- **Confusing absolute convergence of series with absolute value of the sum**: |∑ a_n| ≤ ∑ |a_n| always holds when the right side converges (triangle inequality for infinite sums).
- **Using the alternating test when the terms do not decrease monotonically**: The Leibniz test requires |a_n| decreasing to 0.

A power series is an expression of the form ∑_{n=0}^∞ c_n (x − a)^n , where the coefficients c_n are fixed numbers and a is the center.

Power series are the infinite-degree analogues of polynomials. Inside their interval (or disk) of convergence they behave like very nice functions: they are infinitely differentiable, can be integrated and differentiated term by term, and equal their own Taylor series.

## Radius of Convergence

<!-- section: radius-convergence -->

For a power series there exists R ∈ [0, ∞] (the radius of convergence) such that the series converges for |x − a| < R and diverges for |x − a| > R.

At the endpoints x = a ± R (when R finite) convergence must be checked separately.

The radius can be found by the ratio test: 1/R = lim |c_{n+1}/c_n| (when the limit exists), or by the root test 1/R = lim sup |c_n|^{1/n}.

**ELI5**

- Around the center a the series converges inside a symmetric interval of "radius" R; outside that interval the terms eventually grow and the partial sums blow up.
- At exactly distance R the series may converge at one end, both, or neither (three separate cases to test).

**Worked Example:**

For the geometric series ∑ x^n , |x| < 1 converges, |x| > 1 diverges. Radius R=1, center a=0. At x=1 it becomes the harmonic series (diverges); at x=−1 the alternating harmonic (converges).

## Term-by-Term Differentiation and Integration

<!-- section: termwise-diff-int -->

Inside the open interval of convergence a power series may be differentiated and integrated term by term, and the resulting series have the same radius of convergence.

Consequently a power series defines a C^∞ function on (a−R, a+R) whose derivatives of all orders are given by the differentiated series.

**ELI5**

- You can treat the infinite polynomial almost exactly like a finite polynomial for calculus operations, provided you stay strictly inside the radius.
- The new series after differentiation still converges in the same open interval (though possibly not at the endpoints).

**Worked Example:**

Start with geometric ∑_{n=0}^∞ x^n = 1/(1−x) for |x|<1. Differentiate term by term: ∑_{n=1}^∞ n x^{n−1} = 1/(1−x)^2 . Integrate term by term: ∑_{n=0}^∞ x^{n+1}/(n+1) = −ln(1−x) for |x|<1.

## Taylor Series and Analytic Functions

<!-- section: taylor-analytic -->

Every power series is the Taylor series of the function it defines inside the radius: c_n = f^{(n)}(a) / n! .

A function is analytic at a point if it equals its Taylor series in some neighborhood of the point. Not every C^∞ function is analytic (classic counter-example f(x) = e^{-1/x^2} for x>0, 0 at 0, all derivatives at 0 are zero but the function is not identically zero).

**ELI5**

- Inside the radius the power series "knows" all the derivatives of the function at the center; you can read the coefficients directly from the derivatives.
- Some very smooth functions are "flat" at a point (all derivatives zero) yet not zero; their Taylor series is the zero series and does not represent the function.

**Worked Example:**

The exponential series ∑ x^n / n! has R=∞ and equals e^x everywhere. All derivatives of e^x are e^x, so the Taylor coefficients at 0 are exactly 1/n! .

## Common Mistakes

- **Forgetting to check the endpoints separately**: Radius tells you only the open interval; endpoints are case-by-case.
- **Thinking the radius formula always gives a number**: When lim |c_{n+1}/c_n| does not exist one must use lim sup |c_n|^{1/n}.
- **Differentiating or integrating at the endpoints without justification**: Term-by-term operations are guaranteed only inside the open disk/interval.
- **Assuming every smooth function equals its Taylor series**: Counter-examples exist that are C^∞ but not analytic.
- **Confusing power series in real vs complex variables**: In complex analysis the disk of convergence is fundamental and absolute convergence holds inside; on the real line one must still worry about conditional convergence at endpoints.
