# Fundamental theorem of calculus

The fundamental theorem of calculus (FTC) establishes that differentiation and (Riemann) integration are inverse operations, in two complementary parts.

It is the reason the indefinite integral (antiderivative) and definite integral are so closely linked, and why we can compute areas by finding antiderivatives.

## FTC Part 1: The Integral as a Function of the Upper Limit

<!-- section: ftc-part-one -->

Let f be Riemann integrable on [a,b]. Define F(x) = ∫_a^x f(t) dt for x ∈ [a,b].

Then F is continuous on [a,b]. If in addition f is continuous at c ∈ [a,b], then F is differentiable at c and F'(c) = f(c).

In other words, "the derivative of the integral is the integrand" (at points of continuity of f).

**ELI5**

- F(x) is the accumulated signed area from a fixed left endpoint a up to the variable right endpoint x. As x moves a tiny bit, the added area is approximately f(x) times the tiny width, so the rate of change of F is f(x).
- If f has a jump, F is still continuous (area doesn't jump) but may have a corner (derivative jumps).

**Worked Example:**

Let f(x)=x on [0,1]. Then F(x) = ∫_0^x t dt = x²/2. F'(x) = x = f(x), as predicted (and f is continuous).

## FTC Part 2: Evaluation of Definite Integrals via Antiderivatives

<!-- section: ftc-part-two -->

If f is continuous on [a,b] and F is any antiderivative of f (F' = f on (a,b), F continuous up to ends), then ∫_a^b f = F(b) − F(a).

This is the familiar "take antiderivative, plug in limits" rule used in calculus.

**ELI5**

- The net signed area under f from a to b equals the total change in any antiderivative from a to b.
- It turns the hard problem of summing infinitely many infinitesimal areas into the (often) easier problem of finding one antiderivative.

**Worked Example:**

∫_0^1 x dx = [x²/2 ]_0^1 = 1/2 − 0 = 1/2. The antiderivative x²/2 changes by 1/2 over the interval, which is exactly the area.

## Proof Ideas and the Role of Continuity

<!-- section: proof-ideas-continuity -->

Part 1 is proved by writing the difference quotient [F(x+h)−F(x)]/h = (1/h) ∫_x^{x+h} f , then using that when f is continuous at x the integral average is close to f(x) (by bounding |f(t)−f(x)| small on a small interval).

Part 2 follows from Part 1: if F' = f then the function G(x) = ∫_a^x f − F(x) has G' = 0, hence G constant, hence G(b) = G(a) which rearranges to the desired equality.

Continuity of f guarantees both the existence of the integral and that every antiderivative works.

**ELI5**

- The average value of f over a tiny interval around x is forced to be close to f(x) by continuity; that average is exactly the difference quotient for the accumulated-area function.
- Once you know the derivative of the integral recovers f, any other function with the same derivative differs by a constant, so the net changes match.

**Worked Example:**

To prove Part 2: let G(x) = ∫_a^x f(t)dt − F(x). By Part 1, G'(x) = f(x) − F'(x) = 0 on (a,b). Thus G is constant on [a,b] (by MVT or connectedness). Therefore G(b) − G(a) = 0, i.e. ∫_a^b f = F(b) − F(a).

## Generalizations and Limitations (Improper, Lebesgue)

<!-- section: generalizations-limitations -->

FTC requires f integrable (for Part 1) and usually continuous for the strong form of Part 2. For improper integrals one takes limits of proper integrals.

In Lebesgue theory the FTC holds under weaker assumptions (absolute integrability, almost-everywhere differentiability of the indefinite integral, etc.), and the fundamental theorem of calculus for Lebesgue integrals recovers f almost everywhere.

**ELI5**

- If f has a bad discontinuity the indefinite integral may still be differentiable almost everywhere and recover f a.e. (Lebesgue).
- Improper integrals at endpoints or with singularities are handled by limits; the "FTC" then says the improper integral equals the limiting difference of the antiderivative (when the limit exists).

**Worked Example:**

The improper integral ∫_1^∞ (1/x²) dx = lim_{b→∞} [−1/x ]_1^b = lim (0 − (−1)) = 1, which equals the "antiderivative evaluated at infinity minus at 1".

## Common Mistakes

- **Applying Part 2 when f is not continuous (only integrable)**: The antiderivative may fail to exist in the classical sense or the formula may need interpretation.
- **Forgetting the constant of integration when finding antiderivatives**: The evaluation F(b)−F(a) automatically cancels the constant.
- **Confusing the two parts**: Part 1 says differentiation undoes integration (under continuity); Part 2 says integration undoes differentiation (for any antiderivative).
- **Using FTC on functions that are not integrable**: e.g. 1/√x on (0,1] is improperly integrable but the proper integral on [ε,1] is fine; the theorem applies after taking limits.
- **Thinking every derivative is integrable**: There exist functions differentiable everywhere whose derivative is not Riemann (or even Lebesgue) integrable on [a,b]. The FTC still holds in a weaker sense for the Lebesgue integral when f' exists a.e. and is integrable.
