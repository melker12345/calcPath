# Taylor's theorem

Taylor's theorem gives a polynomial approximation to a sufficiently smooth function together with an explicit remainder term that quantifies the error.

It is the theoretical basis for polynomial approximations, Taylor series, numerical methods (Newton, Euler), and asymptotic analysis.

## Statement (Lagrange Form of Remainder)

<!-- section: statement-lagrange -->

Let f be (n+1) times differentiable on an open interval containing a and x. Then there exists c strictly between a and x such that

f(x) = ∑_{k=0}^n [f^{(k)}(a)/k!] (x − a)^k  +  [f^{(n+1)}(c)/(n+1)!] (x − a)^{n+1} .

The sum is the Taylor polynomial of degree n centered at a; the last term is the Lagrange remainder.

**ELI5**

- The Taylor polynomial is the unique polynomial of degree ≤ n that matches f and its first n derivatives at a. Taylor's theorem tells you how far this polynomial deviates from f at a nearby point x, in terms of the (n+1)th derivative at some intermediate c.
- For n=0 it reduces to the mean value theorem: f(x) = f(a) + f'(c)(x−a).

**Worked Example:**

f(x) = e^x around a=0, n=2: 1 + x + x²/2 + (e^c / 6) x^3 for some c between 0 and x. For small x the remainder is roughly (e^x / 6) x^3, which is O(x^3).

## Other Forms of the Remainder (Integral, Cauchy, Peano)

<!-- section: other-remainder-forms -->

The Lagrange form is the most commonly stated, but there is also:

- Integral remainder: R_n(x) = 1/n! ∫_a^x (x−t)^n f^{(n+1)}(t) dt .
- Cauchy form, Peano form (little-o as x→a: R_n(x) = o( (x−a)^n ) when f^{(n+1)} exists at a).

The integral form is often the most useful for estimates and for proving that the Taylor series converges to the function when the remainder →0.

**ELI5**

- The integral form comes from integrating the fundamental theorem of calculus repeatedly (or from integration by parts on the Lagrange form).
- Peano form says that near a the error is smaller than any constant times (x−a)^n ; it is the minimal information needed for many local arguments.

**Worked Example:**

To derive the integral remainder, start from f(x) = f(a) + ∫_a^x f'(t) dt, integrate by parts with u = f'(t), dv = dt, etc., repeating n times. The boundary terms produce the Taylor polynomial; the final integral is the remainder.

## Taylor Series and When It Represents the Function

<!-- section: taylor-series-representation -->

The Taylor series of f at a is the infinite sum ∑ [f^{(k)}(a)/k!] (x−a)^k from k=0 to ∞.

The series may or may not converge to f(x). Even if it converges, it may converge to the wrong function (the classic e^{-1/x^2} flat function whose Taylor series at 0 is 0 but the function is positive for x>0).

A sufficient condition for the series to equal f is that the remainder R_n(x) → 0 as n→∞ for each fixed x in some interval.

**ELI5**

- The Taylor series is the "best" polynomial approximation of every degree, all rolled into one formal power series.
- For many functions (exp, sin, cos, 1/(1−x) inside radius) the remainder does go to zero and the series equals the function. For others it does not.

**Worked Example:**

For f(x) = e^x the Lagrange remainder after n terms is e^c (x)^{n+1}/(n+1)! . For fixed x, as n→∞ this →0 by properties of the exponential series or ratio test on the terms. Hence the Taylor series sums to e^x everywhere.

## Applications: Approximations, Error Bounds, Asymptotics

<!-- section: applications-error-bounds -->

- Linear approximation (n=1): f(x) ≈ f(a) + f'(a)(x−a), error ≈ (f''(c)/2)(x−a)^2 .
- Quadratic approximation for better accuracy.
- Newton's method can be viewed as using the linear Taylor approximation to solve f(x)=0.
- Asymptotic expansions: even if the series diverges, the partial sums may still give useful approximations with controlled error (e.g. Stirling's series for n!).

**ELI5**

- For small changes, the tangent line (linear Taylor) gives a good first guess; the quadratic term corrects for curvature.
- Error bounds from the remainder let engineers and scientists know how many terms are "safe" to keep for a desired precision.

**Worked Example:**

Approximate sin(0.1) by its degree-3 Taylor at 0: 0.1 − (0.1)^3 /6 ≈ 0.1 − 0.0001667 ≈ 0.099833. The next term is (0.1)^5 /120 ≈ 8.3e-9, so the error is smaller than 10^{-8} for |x|=0.1.

## Common Mistakes

- **Assuming the Taylor series always equals the function**: The flat function counter-example shows this is false even for C^∞ functions.
- **Forgetting that the c in the remainder depends on x and n**: You cannot treat c as fixed when taking limits.
- **Using Taylor with remainder when f is not sufficiently differentiable**: The hypotheses must hold on an interval containing a and x.
- **Confusing the center a with the expansion point x**: The polynomial is built from derivatives at the fixed center a; x is the evaluation point.
- **Thinking a divergent Taylor series is useless**: Asymptotic series can still be used for approximation when truncated optimally, even if they diverge as n→∞.
