# Differentiation

The derivative, mean value theorem, Taylor approximations, and L'Hôpital's rule.

The derivative of f at a is the instantaneous rate of change of f at a, defined as the limit of the difference quotient [f(a+h) − f(a)] / h as h → 0 (when the limit exists).

Geometrically it is the slope of the tangent line. The derivative is the central concept that turns calculus into a powerful tool for optimization, approximation, and modeling change.

## The Difference Quotient and Definition

<!-- section: definition-difference-quotient -->

f'(a) = lim_{h→0} [f(a + h) − f(a)] / h , provided the limit exists (as a real number).

Equivalently, f is differentiable at a if there exists a number L such that f(a + h) = f(a) + L h + o(h) as h → 0 (little-o notation: the error is negligible compared with h).

The tangent line is y = f(a) + f'(a)(x − a).

**ELI5**

- The difference quotient is the average slope over a small interval of width h. As you let the interval shrink to a point (h→0), if the average slopes settle to a definite number, that number is the instantaneous slope — the derivative.
- The little-o form says the function is almost linear near a, with error much smaller than the distance |h|.

**Worked Example:**

For f(x) = x² at a=3: [ (3+h)² − 9 ] / h = (9 + 6h + h² − 9)/h = 6 + h → 6 as h→0. So f'(3) = 6. The tangent is y = 9 + 6(x−3) = 6x − 9.

## Differentiability Implies Continuity; Higher Derivatives

<!-- section: differentiability-implies-continuity -->

If f is differentiable at a then f is continuous at a (because f(a+h) − f(a) = h · (difference quotient) → 0 · L = 0).

The converse is false: |x| is continuous everywhere but not differentiable at 0.

If f' exists and is itself differentiable, we obtain f'', and so on. A C^∞ function has derivatives of all orders.

**ELI5**

- If the slope exists, the function cannot jump; the linear approximation forces continuity.
- |x| has a sharp corner at 0; the left and right slopes differ (−1 and +1), so no single tangent line.

**Worked Example:**

f(x) = |x| at 0: the difference quotient is |h|/h = sign(h), which → 1 as h→0+ and → −1 as h→0−. The two-sided limit does not exist, so not differentiable at 0. But f is continuous at 0.

## Basic Differentiation Rules and the Chain Rule

<!-- section: rules-chain-rule -->

From the definition one derives the usual rules: (f + g)' = f' + g', (cf)' = c f', (f g)' = f' g + f g', (f/g)' = (f'g − f g') / g² (g≠0).

The chain rule: (f ∘ g)'(a) = f'(g(a)) · g'(a), proved by writing the increments and using the little-o characterization or careful limit manipulation.

**ELI5**

- The rules let you differentiate "by algebra" without going back to the limit definition each time.
- Chain rule: rates of change multiply when one quantity depends on another which depends on the input (like "speed of the shadow" = "speed of the ball" times "how shadow position depends on ball position").

**Worked Example:**

Differentiate sin(x²). Let u = x², v = sin u. v' = cos u · u' = cos(x²) · 2x.

## Mean Value Theorem Preview and Taylor

<!-- section: mvt-taylor-preview -->

The mean value theorem (next lesson) is the fundamental theorem relating derivatives to function values: if f is continuous on [a,b] and differentiable on (a,b) then there is c with f'(c) = [f(b)−f(a)]/(b−a).

As a corollary, if f' = 0 on an interval then f is constant. This is the basis for all the "increase/decrease" tests and for proving inequalities via derivatives.

**ELI5**

- Between any two points on the graph of a differentiable function there is a point where the tangent slope exactly equals the secant slope connecting the two points.
- If the derivative is zero everywhere, the function is flat — constant.

**Worked Example:**

To prove that sin x ≤ x for x ≥ 0: consider g(x) = x − sin x. g(0)=0, g'(x) = 1 − cos x ≥ 0, so g is non-decreasing, hence g(x) ≥ 0.

## Common Mistakes

- **Confusing the derivative existing with the difference quotient being defined**: At a point the difference quotient must have a limit; for |x| it is defined for h≠0 but the limit fails.
- **Thinking differentiability on [a,b] requires one-sided derivatives at endpoints**: Usually we say differentiable on (a,b) and continuous up to the ends for MVT.
- **Forgetting that derivatives need not be continuous**: There exist functions differentiable everywhere with discontinuous derivative (Volterra's function or simpler examples).
- **Misapplying the chain rule**: (f(g(x)))' = f'(g(x)) g'(x), not f'(x) g'(x) or f(g'(x)).
- **Believing that if f' exists then f is C^1**: No; f' can exist and be discontinuous. C^1 means f' exists and is continuous.

The mean value theorem (MVT) is the fundamental link between the derivative (local rate of change) and the net change of the function over an interval.

It says that the average rate of change over [a,b] equals the instantaneous rate at some interior point. All the standard tests for monotonicity, convexity, and error bounds in approximations flow from MVT or its corollaries (Rolle's theorem, Taylor with remainder).

## Statement and Geometric Meaning

<!-- section: statement-geometric -->

Let f be continuous on [a,b] and differentiable on (a,b). Then there exists c ∈ (a,b) such that

f'(c) = [f(b) − f(a)] / (b − a).

Geometrically: the slope of the secant line from (a,f(a)) to (b,f(b)) equals the slope of the tangent line at some point c in between.

**ELI5**

- The secant line is the "straight-line average speed" from a to b. MVT says that at some instant your instantaneous speed exactly matched that average speed.
- You cannot go from height f(a) to f(b) in time b−a with a continuously changing speed without at some moment having speed exactly equal to the overall average.

**Worked Example:**

For f(x) = x² on [1,3]: [f(3)−f(1)]/(3−1) = (9−1)/2 = 4. f'(x)=2x, so 2c=4 ⇒ c=2, which lies in (1,3). Indeed at x=2 the tangent slope is 4, same as the secant.

## Rolle's Theorem (Special Case)

<!-- section: rolles-theorem -->

Rolle's theorem: if f(a)=f(b) and f continuous on [a,b], differentiable on (a,b), then there is c with f'(c)=0.

Proof: apply MVT; the right-hand side is zero, so f'(c)=0.

Rolle's is often proved first (using EVT to locate a max or min in the interior, then Fermat's stationary point theorem if the extremum is interior), then MVT is deduced from Rolle by considering an auxiliary function.

**ELI5**

- If a function starts and ends at the same height, then either it is flat somewhere (derivative zero) or it went up and came back down, in which case the tangent must be horizontal at the top or bottom.
- This is the "there is a flat point" theorem.

**Worked Example:**

To show that the equation x^3 + 3x + 1 = 0 has exactly one real root: let f(x)=x^3+3x+1. f'(x)=3x²+3 >0 always, so f is strictly increasing, hence at most one root. f(0)=1>0, f(−1)=−3+−3+1=−5<0, so by IVT there is at least one. (MVT not directly needed here, but Rolle would be used to show that if there were two roots then f' would have a zero, contradiction.)

## Consequences: Monotonicity, Inequalities, Taylor Remainder

<!-- section: consequences-monotonicity-taylor -->

- If f' > 0 on (a,b) then f is strictly increasing on [a,b] (apply MVT to any subinterval).
- If f'(x) ≥ g'(x) and f(a) ≥ g(a) then f(x) ≥ g(x) for x ≥ a (consider h = f−g).
- Taylor's theorem with Lagrange remainder: f(b) = f(a) + f'(a)(b−a) + (f''(c)/2)(b−a)^2 for some c, and higher orders similarly. The remainder term is controlled by bounds on the higher derivative.

**ELI5**

- Positive derivative means the function is always climbing, so overall it increases.
- The Taylor expansion with remainder tells you not only the linear (or quadratic) approximation but also a precise bound on the error, using the next derivative at some unknown point c.

**Worked Example:**

Prove that √(1 + x) ≤ 1 + x/2 for x ≥ 0. Let f(x) = 1 + x/2 − √(1+x). f(0)=0, f'(x) = 1/2 − 1/(2√(1+x)) ≥ 0 because √(1+x) ≥ 1. Thus f' ≥ 0 ⇒ f ≥ 0.

## Why Differentiability on the Open Interval Is Needed

<!-- section: necessity-differentiability -->

If we only assume continuity on [a,b] and differentiability at most points, the conclusion can fail. Classic counter-example: the function that is x sin(1/x) or functions with derivative existing almost everywhere but wild.

A function can be continuous everywhere, differentiable almost everywhere, strictly increasing, yet have f'=0 almost everywhere (devil's staircase / Cantor function). In that case the "average slope" is positive but the derivative is zero a.e., so MVT in its strong form fails because there is no c where f'(c) equals the average.

**ELI5**

- You can have a function that overall rises (like a staircase with flat parts and steep rises) but is "flat" almost everywhere; the rises are concentrated on a set of measure zero. Then the derivative (where it exists) is zero, yet net change is positive.
- This shows why we need differentiability everywhere on (a,b) for the classical MVT.

**Worked Example:**

The Cantor (devil's staircase) function is constant on the intervals removed in the construction of the Cantor set, increases only on the Cantor set itself (measure zero), is continuous, and has derivative 0 almost everywhere, yet goes from 0 to 1. On [0,1] the average slope is 1, but there is no point where the derivative equals 1 (in fact derivative is 0 or does not exist).

## Common Mistakes

- **Stating MVT with differentiability only at endpoints or on a dense set**: The theorem requires differentiability throughout the open interval.
- **Confusing MVT with the mean value property for harmonic functions**: Different context (PDE).
- **Thinking that f' > 0 everywhere implies f is onto an interval**: It implies strictly increasing, hence one-to-one, but the image may still miss values if the domain is not connected or if f is not continuous up to the boundary.
- **Applying MVT to |x| on [−1,1]**: Not differentiable at 0, and indeed the secant slope is 0 but there is no c with derivative 0 in a way that matches? |x| derivative is ±1, secant from −1 to 1 is 0, and indeed no c where f'(c)=0.
- **Forgetting the conclusion is existence of some c, not for all c**: MVT does not say the derivative equals the average everywhere.

Taylor's theorem gives a polynomial approximation to a sufficiently smooth function together with an explicit remainder term that quantifies the error.

It is the theoretical basis for polynomial approximations, Taylor series, numerical methods (Newton, Euler), and asymptotic analysis.

## Statement (Lagrange Form of Remainder)

<!-- section: statement-lagrange -->

Let f be (n+1) times differentiable on an open interval containing a and x. Then there exists c strictly between a and x such that

f(x) = ∑_{k=0}^n [f^{(k)}(a)/k!] (x − a)^k  +  [f^{(n+1)}(c)/(n+1)!] (x − a)^{n+1} .

The sum is the Taylor polynomial of degree n centered at a; the last term is the Lagrange remainder.

**ELI5**

- The Taylor polynomial is the unique polynomial of degree ≤ n that matches f and its first n derivatives at a. Taylor's theorem tells you how far this polynomial deviates from f at a nearby point x, in terms of the (n+1)th derivative at some intermediate c.
- For n=0 it reduces to the mean value theorem: f(x) = f(a) + f'(c)(x−a).

**Worked Example:**

f(x) = e^x around a=0, n=2: 1 + x + x²/2 + (e^c / 6) x^3 for some c between 0 and x. For small x the remainder is roughly (e^x / 6) x^3, which is O(x^3).

## Other Forms of the Remainder (Integral, Cauchy, Peano)

<!-- section: other-remainder-forms -->

The Lagrange form is the most commonly stated, but there is also:

- Integral remainder: R_n(x) = 1/n! ∫_a^x (x−t)^n f^{(n+1)}(t) dt .
- Cauchy form, Peano form (little-o as x→a: R_n(x) = o( (x−a)^n ) when f^{(n+1)} exists at a).

The integral form is often the most useful for estimates and for proving that the Taylor series converges to the function when the remainder →0.

**ELI5**

- The integral form comes from integrating the fundamental theorem of calculus repeatedly (or from integration by parts on the Lagrange form).
- Peano form says that near a the error is smaller than any constant times (x−a)^n ; it is the minimal information needed for many local arguments.

**Worked Example:**

To derive the integral remainder, start from f(x) = f(a) + ∫_a^x f'(t) dt, integrate by parts with u = f'(t), dv = dt, etc., repeating n times. The boundary terms produce the Taylor polynomial; the final integral is the remainder.

## Taylor Series and When It Represents the Function

<!-- section: taylor-series-representation -->

The Taylor series of f at a is the infinite sum ∑ [f^{(k)}(a)/k!] (x−a)^k from k=0 to ∞.

The series may or may not converge to f(x). Even if it converges, it may converge to the wrong function (the classic e^{-1/x^2} flat function whose Taylor series at 0 is 0 but the function is positive for x>0).

A sufficient condition for the series to equal f is that the remainder R_n(x) → 0 as n→∞ for each fixed x in some interval.

**ELI5**

- The Taylor series is the "best" polynomial approximation of every degree, all rolled into one formal power series.
- For many functions (exp, sin, cos, 1/(1−x) inside radius) the remainder does go to zero and the series equals the function. For others it does not.

**Worked Example:**

For f(x) = e^x the Lagrange remainder after n terms is e^c (x)^{n+1}/(n+1)! . For fixed x, as n→∞ this →0 by properties of the exponential series or ratio test on the terms. Hence the Taylor series sums to e^x everywhere.

## Applications: Approximations, Error Bounds, Asymptotics

<!-- section: applications-error-bounds -->

- Linear approximation (n=1): f(x) ≈ f(a) + f'(a)(x−a), error ≈ (f''(c)/2)(x−a)^2 .
- Quadratic approximation for better accuracy.
- Newton's method can be viewed as using the linear Taylor approximation to solve f(x)=0.
- Asymptotic expansions: even if the series diverges, the partial sums may still give useful approximations with controlled error (e.g. Stirling's series for n!).

**ELI5**

- For small changes, the tangent line (linear Taylor) gives a good first guess; the quadratic term corrects for curvature.
- Error bounds from the remainder let engineers and scientists know how many terms are "safe" to keep for a desired precision.

**Worked Example:**

Approximate sin(0.1) by its degree-3 Taylor at 0: 0.1 − (0.1)^3 /6 ≈ 0.1 − 0.0001667 ≈ 0.099833. The next term is (0.1)^5 /120 ≈ 8.3e-9, so the error is smaller than 10^{-8} for |x|=0.1.

## Common Mistakes

- **Assuming the Taylor series always equals the function**: The flat function counter-example shows this is false even for C^∞ functions.
- **Forgetting that the c in the remainder depends on x and n**: You cannot treat c as fixed when taking limits.
- **Using Taylor with remainder when f is not sufficiently differentiable**: The hypotheses must hold on an interval containing a and x.
- **Confusing the center a with the expansion point x**: The polynomial is built from derivatives at the fixed center a; x is the evaluation point.
- **Thinking a divergent Taylor series is useless**: Asymptotic series can still be used for approximation when truncated optimally, even if they diverge as n→∞.

L'Hôpital's rule is a powerful tool for evaluating indeterminate forms 0/0 or ∞/∞ by replacing the limit of f/g with the limit of f'/g' (when the latter exists).

It is one of the most frequently used limit techniques in calculus and analysis, with careful hypotheses required for validity.

## The 0/0 Form (Basic Statement)

<!-- section: zero-over-zero -->

Let f and g be differentiable on (a,b) except possibly at c ∈ (a,b), with g'(x) ≠ 0. Suppose lim_{x→c} f(x) = 0 and lim_{x→c} g(x) = 0, and suppose lim_{x→c} f'(x)/g'(x) = L (finite or ±∞). Then lim_{x→c} f(x)/g(x) = L.

The rule also holds for one-sided limits and for limits as x→±∞ (with appropriate adjustments to the hypotheses).

**ELI5**

- When both numerator and denominator are approaching 0, their ratio may be anything; looking at the ratio of their slopes (derivatives) often resolves the "competition" between them.
- Geometrically, near c both curves are close to 0; their tangents' slopes tell you the relative rates at which they approach 0.

**Worked Example:**

lim_{x→0} (sin x)/x . Both →0. Derivatives cos x / 1 →1. Hence the limit is 1. (This is the standard limit used to prove that (sin x)' = cos x.)

## The ∞/∞ Form and Other Indeterminate Forms

<!-- section: infinity-over-infinity -->

If lim f = ±∞ and lim g = ±∞ (same or mixed signs), and if lim f'/g' exists, then lim f/g = lim f'/g'.

Other indeterminate forms (0·∞, ∞−∞, 0^0, 1^∞, ∞^0) are usually reduced to 0/0 or ∞/∞ by algebraic rewriting (taking logs for exponential forms, writing products as quotients, etc.).

**ELI5**

- When both go to infinity, the ratio may still settle; the derivatives often grow at rates whose ratio is easier to see.
- For 0·∞ rewrite as 0 / (1/∞) which becomes 0/0 or similar.

**Worked Example:**

lim_{x→∞} (x^2 + 3x) / e^x . ∞/∞ form. Differentiate twice: 2 / e^x → 0. Hence the original limit is 0 (exponential beats any polynomial).

## Proof Ideas (Cauchy Mean Value Theorem)

<!-- section: proof-cauchy-mvt -->

The standard proof uses the Cauchy mean value theorem (generalized MVT): for f,g continuous on [a,b], differentiable on (a,b), there is c with [f(b)−f(a)] g'(c) = [g(b)−g(a)] f'(c).

To prove L'Hôpital, one considers an auxiliary interval from a point x near c to c itself (or a sequence of points), applies Cauchy MVT, and takes limits, using that f and g →0 (or ∞) to simplify the boundary terms.

**ELI5**

- Cauchy MVT is the "weighted" version of ordinary MVT that relates increments of two functions.
- By applying it on [x,c] (or suitable) the f and g increments appear in the numerator and denominator exactly as in the original quotient, while the derivatives appear at the intermediate c; letting x→c forces the intermediate point to c as well and yields the result.

**Worked Example:**

For the 0/0 case with finite L: fix x near c, apply Cauchy MVT on the interval with endpoints x and a point t between x and c or directly on [x,c] (assuming c finite). Then f(x)/g(x) = f'(ξ)/g'(ξ) for some ξ between x and c. As x→c, ξ→c, and if f'/g' → L then the quotient → L.

## Common Pitfalls and When the Rule Does Not Apply

<!-- section: pitfalls-when-not-apply -->

- The limit of f'/g' must exist (or be ±∞). If it does not, the rule says nothing; the original limit may still exist or not.
- g' must not be zero in the punctured neighborhood (otherwise the quotient f'/g' may not even be defined).
- Classic counter-example where lim f'/g' does not exist but lim f/g does: f(x) = x + sin x, g(x) = x, as x→∞. f'/g' = 1 + cos x oscillates, does not exist; but f/g → 1.
- Another: lim f/g may exist while lim f'/g' does not, or they may exist but be unequal if hypotheses violated.

**ELI5**

- L'Hôpital is a "if the derivatives' ratio settles, then the original does" implication, not an "if and only if".
- You must check the hypotheses; blindly differentiating numerator and denominator can give wrong answers or inconclusive results.

**Worked Example:**

The counter-example above: (x + sin x)/x = 1 + (sin x)/x → 1, but the derivatives' ratio (1 + cos x)/1 oscillates between 0 and 2 and has no limit. L'Hôpital does not apply (hypothesis fails) and indeed one cannot conclude anything from the oscillating derivative ratio.

## Common Mistakes

- **Applying L'Hôpital when the limit of f'/g' fails to exist**: The theorem gives no information; the original limit may exist anyway (see counter-example).
- **Repeatedly differentiating without checking the form remains indeterminate**: After one application the new limit may be determinate; further applications are unnecessary or wrong.
- **Forgetting that 0^0, ∞^0 etc. require rewriting (usually via exp and ln)**: You cannot directly differentiate the base and exponent.
- **Using L'Hôpital for limits that are not indeterminate**: If lim f and lim g are both nonzero finite, the rule is not needed and may give a different (wrong) answer if misapplied.
- **Ignoring that g' ≠ 0 is required in a whole neighborhood**: A zero of g' at isolated points can be handled by continuity arguments, but the statement usually assumes g' ≠ 0 to keep the quotient defined.
