# Lebesgue Integration

Measure-theoretic integration, convergence theorems, and the dominated convergence theorem.

The Lebesgue integral is a generalization of the Riemann integral that integrates over a much larger class of functions (including all Riemann integrable functions) and has far superior convergence properties (monotone convergence, dominated convergence, Fatou's lemma).

It is the integral used in modern analysis, probability theory (expectation), and functional analysis (L^p spaces).

## Measure and Measurable Functions (Brief)

<!-- section: measure-measurable -->

Lebesgue measure on ℝ^n assigns to intervals their usual volume and extends by countable additivity to a large class of sets (measurable sets). A set has measure zero if it can be covered by intervals of arbitrarily small total volume (e.g. countable sets, the Cantor set, rationals).

A function f is measurable if the sets {x | f(x) > a} are measurable for all a (or equivalent conditions). All continuous functions, all monotone functions, and pointwise limits of measurable functions are measurable.

**ELI5**

- Lebesgue measure is "length/area/volume" made rigorous so that it works for very wild sets (as long as they are not too pathological).
- Measurable functions are those for which we can sensibly talk about "the set where the function is above a certain height" having a well-defined size.

**Worked Example:**

The Dirichlet function (1 on rationals, 0 on irrationals) is measurable (the set where it > 1/2 is the rationals, which have measure zero). It is not Riemann integrable.

## Simple Functions and the Integral Definition

<!-- section: simple-functions-definition -->

A simple function is a finite linear combination of characteristic functions of measurable sets: φ = ∑ c_k χ_{E_k} .

For nonnegative simple φ the integral is defined as ∑ c_k m(E_k) (m = Lebesgue measure), with the convention 0·∞ = 0.

For a general nonnegative measurable f the integral is the sup of integrals of simple functions 0 ≤ φ ≤ f.

For signed f one splits into positive and negative parts (provided at least one has finite integral).

**ELI5**

- First integrate the simplest possible functions: constants on measurable "bricks". The integral is height times measure of the brick.
- For a general nonnegative function, approximate it from below by simple functions (like a histogram that gets finer); the integral is the least upper bound of the areas of those approximations.

**Worked Example:**

The characteristic function of the rationals in [0,1] is simple: 1·χ_ℚ∩[0,1] + 0·χ_{irrationals}. Its integral is 1·m(ℚ∩[0,1]) + 0 = 0 because m(rationals)=0.

## Comparison with Riemann Integral; When They Agree

<!-- section: comparison-riemann-agree -->

If f is Riemann integrable on [a,b] then it is Lebesgue integrable and the two integrals coincide.

The converse is false: the Dirichlet function is Lebesgue integrable (integral 0) but not Riemann integrable.

A bounded function on [a,b] is Riemann integrable if and only if its set of discontinuities has Lebesgue measure zero (Lebesgue's criterion).

**ELI5**

- Every "nice" function you integrated with Riemann sums in calculus is also Lebesgue integrable and gives the same number.
- Lebesgue can handle functions that jump on dense sets (as long as the jumps are on a set of measure zero) because it doesn't care about values on measure-zero sets.

**Worked Example:**

Thomae's function is Riemann integrable (hence Lebesgue) with integral 0. The Dirichlet function is Lebesgue 0 but not Riemann.

## Convergence Theorems (Monotone, Dominated, Fatou)

<!-- section: convergence-theorems -->

- Monotone convergence: if 0 ≤ f_n ↑ f pointwise (measurable), then ∫ f_n ↑ ∫ f (possibly ∞).
- Fatou's lemma: ∫ liminf f_n ≤ liminf ∫ f_n (for nonnegative measurable).
- Dominated convergence: if f_n → f pointwise and |f_n| ≤ g integrable, then ∫ f_n → ∫ f and f integrable.

These are the reasons Lebesgue theory is vastly superior for passing limits inside integrals.

**ELI5**

- Monotone: if you increase the function pointwise the areas can only increase, and in the limit you get the area of the limit function (no "mass escapes to infinity").
- Dominated: if everything is squeezed under an integrable "umbrella" g, then you can pass the limit inside; the domination prevents the areas from oscillating or escaping.

**Worked Example:**

Let f_n(x) = n^2 x (1−x)^n or standard examples where Riemann would have trouble but dominated applies. Classic: f_n = χ_{[n,n+1]}, ∫ f_n =1 but f_n →0 pointwise, ∫ lim =0 ≠ lim ∫ (mass escapes to infinity); no dominating integrable g.

## Common Mistakes

- **Thinking Lebesgue integral is always 'harder' to compute**: For most explicit functions the value is the same as Riemann; the advantage is theoretical (theorems) and for wilder functions.
- **Forgetting that the domination or monotonicity hypotheses are necessary**: Without them limits and integrals may not commute (escaping mass, oscillation).
- **Confusing 'almost everywhere' with 'everywhere'**: Functions are identified if they differ on a set of measure zero; derivatives and integrals recover the function only a.e.
- **Believing every derivative is Lebesgue integrable**: There exist functions differentiable everywhere with non-Lebesgue-integrable derivative on [a,b]; the FTC holds in a more subtle way.
- **Mixing Lebesgue measure zero with 'empty' or 'finite'**: The Cantor set has measure zero but is uncountable and perfect; the rationals are countable hence measure zero.

The dominated convergence theorem (DCT) is one of the most powerful and frequently invoked results in Lebesgue integration theory. It justifies passing limits inside integrals under a domination hypothesis that is often easy to check in practice.

It is the reason we can differentiate under the integral sign, interchange limits and expectations in probability, and prove many approximation and convergence results in analysis.

## Statement of the Theorem

<!-- section: statement-dct -->

Let (X, μ) be a measure space (e.g. ℝ with Lebesgue measure). Let f_n be a sequence of measurable functions such that f_n(x) → f(x) for almost every x, and suppose there exists an integrable function g (∫ |g| dμ < ∞) such that |f_n(x)| ≤ g(x) for almost every x and all n.

Then f is integrable and ∫ f_n dμ → ∫ f dμ .

Moreover, ∫ |f_n − f| dμ → 0 (convergence in L^1).

**ELI5**

- If the functions are all dominated in absolute value by a single integrable "umbrella" g, and they converge pointwise a.e., then their integrals converge to the integral of the limit, and the areas of the differences go to zero.
- The domination prevents the "mass" from escaping to infinity or oscillating too wildly.

**Worked Example:**

On ℝ with Lebesgue measure, let f_n(x) = n χ_{(0,1/n)}(x). Then f_n → 0 pointwise for x≠0, and at 0 it oscillates or whatever but single point doesn't matter. |f_n| ≤ 1 (for n≥1), but 1 is not integrable over ℝ. Indeed ∫ f_n =1 for all n, while ∫ lim =0, so limit and integral do not commute. There is no integrable dominating g on the whole line.

## Proof Sketch (Using Fatou and Monotone Convergence)

<!-- section: proof-sketch-fatou-monotone -->

Apply Fatou's lemma to the nonnegative functions g + f_n and g − f_n (or |g| + f_n etc.).

Since |f_n| ≤ g a.e., the functions g ± f_n are nonnegative for large n or after adjustment.

Fatou gives ∫ (g + f) ≤ liminf ∫ (g + f_n) = ∫ g + liminf ∫ f_n , which rearranges to ∫ f ≤ liminf ∫ f_n .

Applying the same to g − f_n yields the reverse inequality limsup ∫ f_n ≤ ∫ f .

Hence the limit exists and equals ∫ f.

For L^1 convergence one applies the same argument to |f_n − f| (dominated by 2g).

**ELI5**

- By adding and subtracting the dominator g you turn the signed f_n into nonnegative sequences to which Fatou applies.
- The resulting inequalities squeeze liminf and limsup of the integrals to be equal to the integral of the limit.

**Worked Example:**

To pass the limit inside ∫ e^{-x} sin(n x) / n dx or similar; here |e^{-x} sin(n x)/n | ≤ e^{-x} which is integrable on [0,∞), pointwise →0, hence integral →0 by DCT.

## Differentiation under the Integral Sign (Parametrized Integrals)

<!-- section: differentiation-under-integral -->

A typical application: consider I(α) = ∫_a^b f(x,α) dx . Under suitable domination on the partial derivative ∂f/∂α one may differentiate under the integral: I'(α) = ∫ ∂f/∂α (x,α) dx .

The domination hypothesis of DCT (applied to difference quotients) justifies the passage to the limit that defines the derivative.

**ELI5**

- If you have a family of functions parametrized by α, and the rate of change with respect to α is controlled by an integrable function independent of α (in a neighborhood), then you can compute the derivative of the total integral by integrating the partial derivative.
- This is the rigorous justification for many calculations in physics and probability (e.g. differentiating moment generating functions).

**Worked Example:**

Let I(α) = ∫_0^1 e^{α x} dx . Then I(α) = (e^α − 1)/α for α≠0. Differentiating under the sign: I'(α) = ∫_0^1 x e^{α x} dx , which can be computed directly and matches the derivative of the closed form.

## Relation to Other Convergence Theorems; When DCT Is Not Needed

<!-- section: relation-other-theorems -->

- Monotone convergence requires only monotonicity and nonnegativity; no domination needed (but the limit integral may be infinite).
- Fatou's lemma is a one-sided inequality that often serves as a stepping stone to DCT.
- Bounded convergence theorem is the special case of DCT on a finite-measure space with |f_n| ≤ M (constant, which is integrable when measure finite).

When the measure space is finite (e.g. probability space) and the f_n are uniformly bounded, DCT reduces to bounded convergence and is very easy to apply.

**ELI5**

- On a probability space, if your random variables are bounded by a fixed constant (or by an integrable random variable), you can pass limits inside expectations freely.
- Monotone convergence is the tool for increasing sequences without needing an a-priori bound.

**Worked Example:**

On [0,1] with Lebesgue, f_n(x) = n x (1−x)^n or bounded by 1 sequences converging pointwise; bounded convergence gives ∫ f_n → ∫ f with almost no work.

## Common Mistakes

- **Forgetting to verify the domination hypothesis**: Pointwise convergence alone is not enough (escaping mass counter-examples).
- **Using a dominator that depends on n**: The g must work for all n simultaneously.
- **Applying DCT on an infinite-measure space with a constant bound**: A constant is not integrable over ℝ; one needs decay (e.g. e^{-|x|}).
- **Confusing almost-everywhere convergence with everywhere**: The theorem only requires a.e. convergence and a.e. domination; values on a null set can be redefined arbitrarily.
- **Thinking DCT implies L^1 convergence of f_n to f**: It does, under the stated hypotheses (∫ |f_n − f| →0). This is sometimes called "dominated convergence in L^1".
