# Advanced Topics

Functions of bounded variation, absolute continuity, Lᵖ spaces, Fourier series, and rigorous multivariable calculus.

A function f : [a,b] → ℝ is of bounded variation if the total variation (the supremum of sums of absolute increments over all partitions) is finite.

Functions of bounded variation are precisely the differences of two increasing functions. They are differentiable almost everywhere, have at most countably many discontinuities (jumps), and can be used as integrators in Riemann–Stieltjes integrals.

They form the natural class of "not too wild" functions that still allow jumps and singular continuous parts.

## Definition via Total Variation

<!-- section: definition-total-variation -->

Let P be a partition a = x_0 < … < x_n = b of [a,b]. The variation of f over P is V(f,P) = ∑ |f(x_i) − f(x_{i−1})| .

The total variation of f on [a,b] is V(f) = sup_P V(f,P) (sup over all partitions).

f has bounded variation (BV) if V(f) < ∞.

**ELI5**

- For each way of slicing the interval, add up the absolute sizes of the ups and downs of f across the slices.
- If no matter how you slice, the total "travel" (up plus down) stays bounded by some number M, then f has bounded variation M.
- A smooth function with a bounded derivative has variation at most (sup |f'|)·(b−a). A function that oscillates infinitely often with fixed amplitude has infinite variation.

**Worked Example:**

f(x) = x on [0,1]: for any partition the variation is exactly the telescoping sum |1−0| =1, so V(f)=1 <∞, bounded variation.

f(x) = sin(1/x) on (0,1] extended by 0 at 0: the oscillations become infinitely rapid and the total up-and-down accumulates like the harmonic series near 0, giving infinite variation.

## Jordan Decomposition: BV = Increasing Minus Increasing

<!-- section: jordan-decomposition -->

If f has bounded variation on [a,b], define the positive variation function v(x) = sup { V(f,P) | P partition of [a,x] } (the total variation from a up to x).

Then v is increasing, and g(x) = v(x) − f(x) is also increasing, and f = v − g.

This is the Jordan decomposition: every BV function is the difference of two increasing functions.

Conversely, any difference of two increasing functions has bounded variation (the variation is at most the sum of the variations of the two increasing functions).

**ELI5**

- The total "up" accumulated so far is v(x); then f(x) = v(x) − "down so far". Both v and the down function are increasing.
- This reduces many questions about BV functions to questions about monotone functions (which are much better behaved: differentiable a.e., etc.).

**Worked Example:**

f(x) = x − 2⌊x⌋ on [0,2] (a sawtooth). It goes up with slope 1 then jumps down by 2 at integers. The total variation on [0,2] is 1+2+1+2=6 (or compute via the decomposition into increasing parts).

## Differentiability and Discontinuities of BV Functions

<!-- section: differentiability-discontinuities -->

Every function of bounded variation is differentiable almost everywhere (this follows from the Jordan decomposition and the fact that monotone functions are differentiable a.e.).

The derivative f' is integrable, but f need not equal the integral of f' (there can be a singular part: jumps + Cantor-like singular continuous increase).

A BV function can have at most countably many discontinuities, and all discontinuities are jumps (the left and right limits exist everywhere).

**ELI5**

- BV functions can jump (countably many times) and can have a "Cantor staircase" part that increases only on a set of measure zero, but they cannot oscillate too wildly; they are differentiable almost everywhere.
- The fundamental theorem may fail: the integral of the derivative recovers only the absolutely continuous part.

**Worked Example:**

The Cantor function (devil's staircase) is constant on the complementary intervals of the Cantor set, increases from 0 to 1, is continuous, has derivative 0 almost everywhere, yet is not constant. It has bounded variation (total variation =1) and is singular (its increase is not given by integrating its derivative).

## Absolute Continuity (Stronger than BV)

<!-- section: absolute-continuity-preview -->

A function f is absolutely continuous if for every ε>0 there is δ>0 such that for any finite collection of disjoint intervals the total length <δ implies the total |Δf| <ε.

Absolutely continuous functions are precisely the indefinite integrals of L^1 functions: f(x) = f(a) + ∫_a^x g with g ∈ L^1.

They are BV, continuous, and satisfy the fundamental theorem: f' exists a.e., is integrable, and f(x) − f(a) = ∫_a^x f'.

BV functions that are not absolutely continuous have a nontrivial singular part (jumps or Cantor-like).

**ELI5**

- Absolute continuity is a strong uniform continuity that controls the total oscillation on collections of small intervals.
- It is exactly the condition that makes f the integral of its derivative (no singular increase).

**Worked Example:**

f(x) = x is absolutely continuous (g=1). The Cantor function is continuous and BV but not absolutely continuous (it maps the measure-zero Cantor set onto [0,1], violating the ε-δ control).

## Riemann–Stieltjes Integration against BV Functions

<!-- section: riemann-stieltjes-bv -->

If α has bounded variation one can define Riemann–Stieltjes integrals ∫ f dα by splitting α into increasing parts (Jordan) and integrating against each.

Integration by parts holds: ∫_a^b f dα + ∫_a^b α df = f(b)α(b) − f(a)α(a), when the integrals exist.

If f is continuous and α is BV they have no common discontinuities (continuous f has none), so the integral exists.

**ELI5**

- BV integrators let you integrate against functions that jump or increase singularly; the integral picks up the jumps of α weighted by f at the jump points.
- Integration by parts lets you move the "differential" from one function to the other.

**Worked Example:**

If α is the Heaviside step at c, ∫ f dα = f(c) (for f continuous at c). Integration by parts recovers the usual formula for summation by parts when α is a step function.

## Common Mistakes

- **Thinking every BV function is continuous**: Jumps are allowed (countably many).
- **Assuming that if f' exists a.e. and is integrable then f(x) = f(a) + ∫ f'**: This holds only for absolutely continuous f; general BV functions have a singular part.
- **Confusing bounded variation with bounded derivative**: A function can have unbounded derivative yet bounded variation on a compact interval? No on a compact interval BV implies the derivative (where exists) is integrable, but the function can still be quite steep on small sets.
- **Forgetting that the total variation is the sup over all partitions, not just uniform ones**: Sometimes the sup is achieved only on very irregular partitions.
- **Applying Riemann–Stieltjes when f and α share a jump**: The integral may fail to exist; one must use a convention or pass to Lebesgue–Stieltjes.

Absolutely continuous functions are the "good" functions of bounded variation: they are precisely the indefinite integrals of integrable functions. They satisfy the fundamental theorem of calculus in full strength: they are differentiable a.e., their derivative is integrable, and they are recovered by integrating the derivative.

They form the natural domain for the fundamental theorem in Lebesgue integration theory and are closed under many operations that preserve the integral representation.

## Definition (ε-δ Uniform Control on Increments)

<!-- section: epsilon-delta-definition-abs-cont -->

A function f : [a,b] → ℝ is absolutely continuous if for every ε > 0 there exists δ > 0 such that for any finite collection of pairwise disjoint subintervals (a_i , b_i) of [a,b] with ∑ (b_i − a_i) < δ we have ∑ |f(b_i) − f(a_i)| < ε.

This is a strong uniform continuity condition that controls the total oscillation on arbitrarily many small intervals, provided their total length is small.

**ELI5**

- Ordinary uniform continuity says that on one small interval the change in f is small.
- Absolute continuity says that even if you have many tiny disjoint intervals, as long as the total length of all of them together is <δ, the sum of all the changes of f across those intervals is still <ε.
- This prevents f from having "steep spikes" or "Cantor-like increases" that concentrate change on very small total length.

**Worked Example:**

f(x) = x on [0,1] is absolutely continuous: |f(b_i)−f(a_i)| = b_i − a_i, so the sum of changes equals the total length; choose δ=ε.

The Cantor function is continuous and of bounded variation but not absolutely continuous: it increases by a total of 1 while mapping a set of measure zero (the Cantor set) onto an interval of length 1; one can cover the Cantor set with intervals of arbitrarily small total length whose images under the Cantor function still cover almost the whole [0,1], violating the ε-δ control.

## Equivalence with Being an Indefinite Integral

<!-- section: equivalence-indefinite-integral -->

Theorem: f is absolutely continuous on [a,b] if and only if there exists g ∈ L^1([a,b]) such that f(x) = f(a) + ∫_a^x g(t) dt for all x (Lebesgue integral).

In this case g = f' almost everywhere.

This is the fundamental theorem of calculus for the Lebesgue integral: absolutely continuous functions are exactly those that are integrals of their derivatives.

**ELI5**

- If f is the accumulated area under an integrable "height" function g, then the total change of f over any collection of intervals equals the integral of |g| over those intervals (or at least is bounded by it), which can be made small by making the total length small (because g is integrable).
- Conversely, any absolutely continuous f must arise this way; the derivative (which exists a.e.) serves as the density g.

**Worked Example:**

f(x) = √x on [0,1]. This is absolutely continuous: it is the integral of g(x) = 1/(2√x) which is integrable on (0,1] (∫_0^1 x^{-1/2} dx = 2). Indeed f'(x) = 1/(2√x) a.e. and recovers f.

## Properties Inherited from Integrals

<!-- section: properties-inherited -->

Absolutely continuous functions are continuous (in fact uniformly continuous) and of bounded variation.

They are differentiable almost everywhere (by the Lebesgue differentiation theorem or because they are BV).

They map sets of measure zero to sets of measure zero (Lusin's condition (N)).

They preserve null sets in both directions in a controlled way.

The composition of an absolutely continuous function with a Lipschitz function is absolutely continuous.

**ELI5**

- Because they are integrals of L^1 functions they inherit all the good mapping properties of the integral (absolute continuity of the integral with respect to Lebesgue measure).
- In particular they cannot "create measure from nothing" or concentrate positive increase on a null set.

**Worked Example:**

If f is absolutely continuous and E has Lebesgue measure zero, then m(f(E)) = 0 (Lusin (N)). This fails for the Cantor function (continuous, BV, but maps the null Cantor set onto [0,1], which has positive measure).

## Relation to Bounded Variation and Singular Functions

<!-- section: relation-bv-singular -->

Every absolutely continuous function is of bounded variation (take g integrable; the total variation is at most ∫ |g|).

The converse is false: the Cantor function is continuous and BV but singular (derivative 0 a.e., yet increases).

In the Jordan decomposition of a BV function, the absolutely continuous part is the integral of f', while the remainder is the singular function (jumps + singular continuous increase).

**ELI5**

- BV is the largest class where we still have differentiability a.e. and a Jordan decomposition into increasing pieces.
- Absolute continuity carves out exactly the piece that is "purely integral," with no jumps and no Cantor-like singular increase.

**Worked Example:**

Any BV function f admits a unique decomposition f = f_ac + f_sing + f_jump (up to constants), where f_ac is absolutely continuous, f_sing is continuous singular (derivative 0 a.e., increase on a null set), and f_jump is the pure jump part (saltus function). Absolute continuity means the singular and jump parts vanish.

## Absolute Continuity of the Integral; Radon–Nikodym

<!-- section: absolute-continuity-integral-radon-nikodym -->

The indefinite integral of an L^1 function is absolutely continuous with respect to Lebesgue measure (this is the ε-δ definition of the integral).

More generally, a signed measure ν is absolutely continuous with respect to μ (ν ≪ μ) if ν(E)=0 whenever μ(E)=0.

The Radon–Nikodym theorem says that if ν ≪ μ and μ is σ-finite, then there exists an integrable density g such that ν(E) = ∫_E g dμ.

When μ is Lebesgue measure this recovers exactly that absolutely continuous functions are indefinite integrals.

**ELI5**

- Absolute continuity of a set function (measure) with respect to another means "no mass on sets that the other measure sees as null."
- Radon–Nikodym is the abstract fundamental theorem: it produces the density g that plays the role of "derivative" of ν with respect to μ.

**Worked Example:**

If ν is the measure with density g (i.e. ν(E)=∫_E g), then clearly ν ≪ Lebesgue, and the Radon–Nikodym derivative dν/dLeb = g recovers the density.

## Common Mistakes

- **Confusing absolute continuity with uniform continuity or ordinary continuity**: Absolute continuity is strictly stronger than uniform continuity on a compact interval (though they coincide for monotone functions? No).
- **Thinking that continuous + BV ⇒ absolutely continuous**: The Cantor function is the standard counter-example.
- **Forgetting the 'collections of intervals' in the definition**: The δ must work uniformly for any finite number of disjoint intervals whose lengths sum to less than δ.
- **Applying the fundamental theorem to a BV function that is not absolutely continuous**: You recover only the absolutely continuous part; the singular part is missed.
- **Confusing absolute continuity of functions with absolute continuity of measures**: They are closely related (the function induces a measure via its increments), but the measure-theoretic notion is more general (works for any measure space).

The Lebesgue differentiation theorem says that for an integrable function f the average of f over balls (or intervals) centered at x tends to f(x) as the radius shrinks to zero, for almost every x.

This is the rigorous justification for the intuitive idea that "the value at a point is the limit of averages around the point" and is the foundation for the fundamental theorem of calculus in several variables, for the theory of Hardy-Littlewood maximal functions, and for differentiation of measures in general.

## Lebesgue Differentiation Theorem (Statement)

<!-- section: lebesgue-differentiation-statement -->

Let f ∈ L^1_loc (ℝ^n). Then for almost every x (Lebesgue measure),

lim_{r→0} (1 / m(B(x,r))) ∫_{B(x,r)} f(y) dy  = f(x) .

The same holds with balls replaced by cubes or other regular families of sets shrinking nicely to x (Vitali covering).

At such points x is called a Lebesgue point of f.

**ELI5**

- The average value of f in a small ball around x approaches the actual value f(x) as the ball shrinks, for almost every x.
- This is true even if f is wildly discontinuous, as long as it is integrable; the bad points (where the averages do not settle to f(x)) form a set of measure zero.

**Worked Example:**

For f the characteristic function of a measurable set E, at almost every x in E the density is 1 (the proportion of the ball inside E tends to 1), and at almost every x outside E the density is 0. This is the Lebesgue density theorem, a special case.

## Hardy-Littlewood Maximal Function and Weak-Type Inequality

<!-- section: maximal-function-weak-inequality -->

The Hardy-Littlewood maximal function is Mf(x) = sup_{r>0} (1/m(B(x,r))) ∫_{B(x,r)} |f| .

It controls the averages that appear in the differentiation theorem.

Mf is not necessarily integrable even if f is, but it satisfies the weak-L^1 inequality: m({x | Mf(x) > λ}) ≤ (C/λ) ∫ |f| (for a constant C depending only on dimension).

This weak-type bound is the key tool in the proof of the Lebesgue differentiation theorem (via a covering argument or rising-sun type lemma in 1D).

**ELI5**

- The maximal function looks at the worst (largest) average of |f| around x over all scales.
- Even though it may be infinite on a large set, the set where it exceeds a height λ cannot be too large (measured in terms of the integral of |f|).

**Worked Example:**

For f = χ_{[0,1]} in ℝ, Mf(x) is roughly 1 when x is near [0,1] and decays like 1/dist outside. The set where Mf > λ is an interval of length roughly 1 + 2/λ or similar; its measure is O(1/λ), consistent with the weak bound (∫|f|=1).

## Proof Ideas (Vitali Covering or Rising Sun in 1D)

<!-- section: proof-ideas-vitali-rising-sun -->

In one dimension a simple proof uses the rising-sun lemma (or just careful analysis of maximal intervals where the average exceeds a value).

In higher dimensions one uses the Vitali covering lemma: from a collection of balls covering a set in a vitali sense (fine covering) one can select a disjoint subcollection whose 5-times enlarged balls still cover the set (with a dimensional constant).

Combined with the weak-type bound on the maximal function, this controls the measure of the set where the limsup of averages differs from f(x) by more than ε, showing it has measure zero.

**ELI5**

- You cover the bad set (where averages stay away from f(x)) with balls on which the average of |f − f(x)| is large.
- Vitali lets you pick a disjoint subcollection that still "controls" the bad set after moderate enlargement.
- The weak bound then says the enlarged balls cannot have too much measure unless the integral of |f − f(x)| is large, which can be made small by choosing a preliminary set where f is continuous in integral average sense (Lusin or just integrability).

**Worked Example:**

In 1D, suppose at a point the limsup of averages > f(x) + ε. Then there are arbitrarily small intervals around the point whose average exceeds f(x)+ε. The rising-sun lemma or a direct selection produces disjoint intervals covering the bad set up to a null set, each contributing a definite excess in the integral, forcing the total measure to be controlled by the integral of the excess, which goes to zero.

## Differentiation of Measures (Radon-Nikodym Derivative)

<!-- section: differentiation-measures-radon-nikodym -->

More generally, let μ be a locally finite signed Radon measure on ℝ^n and let ν be another measure. At μ-almost every point one can define the derivative

dν / dμ (x) = lim_{r→0} ν(B(x,r)) / μ(B(x,r))   (when the limit exists).

If ν ≪ μ (ν absolutely continuous w.r.t. μ), the Radon-Nikodym theorem already gives a density g such that ν = g μ; the differentiation theorem says that this density is recovered as the above limit for μ-a.e. x.

When μ is Lebesgue measure this recovers the Lebesgue differentiation theorem.

**ELI5**

- You can "differentiate" one measure with respect to another by looking at the ratio of their masses in small balls.
- When ν is absolutely continuous w.r.t. μ the ratio tends to the Radon-Nikodym density almost everywhere (w.r.t. μ).

**Worked Example:**

Let μ be Lebesgue, ν the measure with density the Cantor function's "derivative" (which is zero a.e. but the measure is singular). Then the ratio ν(B(x,r))/μ(B(x,r)) tends to 0 a.e. (Lebesgue), but the singular measure still sees the Cantor set.

## Lebesgue Points and Lusin's Condition (N)

<!-- section: lebesgue-points-lusin-n -->

A point x is a Lebesgue point of an integrable f if the averages of |f(y) − f(x)| over balls →0 as r→0.

At Lebesgue points the differentiation theorem holds.

For f in L^1 the set of non-Lebesgue points has measure zero.

Absolutely continuous functions (indefinite integrals) map null sets to null sets (Lusin (N)); this follows from the differentiation theorem or directly from the integral representation.

**ELI5**

- Lebesgue points are the points where f is "continuous in the average sense."
- Almost every point (for an integrable f) is a Lebesgue point.
- Absolutely continuous functions cannot spread a null set over a positive-measure image because their local stretching is controlled by the integrable derivative.

**Worked Example:**

The characteristic function of a fat Cantor set of positive measure has Lebesgue points of density 1 inside the set and 0 outside, a.e.

## Common Mistakes

- **Thinking the differentiation theorem holds everywhere**: It fails on a null set that can be dense (e.g. at rationals for the characteristic function of irrationals, or at the Cantor set for the Cantor function's "derivative").
- **Confusing the maximal function bound with integrability of Mf**: Mf is only weak-L^1 in general; it is in L^1 only if f is in L log L or better.
- **Applying the theorem to non-integrable functions**: Local integrability L^1_loc is the minimal assumption; globally integrable is often convenient but not necessary.
- **Forgetting the 'a.e.' with respect to the reference measure when differentiating general measures**: The limit recovers the density μ-a.e., not everywhere.
- **Assuming that if the averages converge then the function is continuous**: Convergence of averages does not imply pointwise continuity (only in L^1 sense).

The L^p spaces are the spaces of (equivalence classes of) measurable functions whose p-th power is integrable, equipped with the norm ||f||_p = (∫ |f|^p)^{1/p} (for 1≤p<∞) or the essential supremum for p=∞.

They are Banach spaces (complete normed spaces) and form the basic function spaces of modern analysis, probability (moments), PDEs, and harmonic analysis.

## Definition and Basic Properties (1 ≤ p ≤ ∞)

<!-- section: definition-basic-properties-lp -->

For 1 ≤ p < ∞, L^p(μ) consists of (classes of) measurable f with ∫ |f|^p dμ < ∞, with norm ||f||_p = (∫ |f|^p dμ)^{1/p}.

For p=∞, L^∞ consists of essentially bounded functions, norm ess sup |f|.

Functions equal a.e. are identified; the spaces are of equivalence classes.

They are vector spaces; the norm satisfies the triangle inequality (Minkowski inequality) and homogeneity.

**ELI5**

- L^p collects functions that are not too large "on average" when measured with the p-power (like p=2 for energy, p=1 for total mass, p=∞ for peak height).
- Because we integrate |f|^p, spikes that are too tall or too wide make the norm infinite and kick the function out of the space.

**Worked Example:**

On [0,1] with Lebesgue, f(x)=x^{-α} belongs to L^p if and only if p α <1 (near 0 the integral behaves like ∫ x^{-pα} dx which converges at 0 when -pα > -1).

## Hölder, Minkowski, and Other Inequalities

<!-- section: holder-minkowski-inequalities -->

Hölder's inequality: for 1/p + 1/q =1, ∫ |f g| ≤ ||f||_p ||g||_q .

Minkowski (triangle): ||f + g||_p ≤ ||f||_p + ||g||_p .

These are proved by Young's inequality for products or by Jensen/convexity.

They make L^p a normed space and are the basis for duality, interpolation, convolution estimates, etc.

**ELI5**

- Hölder is the generalized Cauchy-Schwarz that lets you bound the integral of a product by the product of L^p and L^q norms.
- Minkowski says the p-norm of a sum is at most the sum of the norms (the p-"length" satisfies triangle inequality).

**Worked Example:**

For p=2, Hölder reduces to Cauchy-Schwarz for the inner product <f,g> = ∫ f g on L^2.

## Completeness (L^p Are Banach Spaces)

<!-- section: completeness-lp-banach -->

L^p is complete: if (f_n) is Cauchy in L^p norm, there is a subsequence that converges pointwise a.e. to some f, and the whole sequence converges to f in L^p norm.

The proof uses the maximal function or direct estimates with the monotone convergence theorem on the tails, plus completeness of the scalars.

Thus L^p(μ) is a Banach space for 1≤p≤∞.

**ELI5**

- A Cauchy sequence in L^p means the functions are getting close in the p-average sense.
- One extracts a pointwise convergent subsequence (using that the integrals of |f_{n_{k+1}} − f_{n_k}|^p sum to a convergent series, hence the series of differences converges a.e.), then shows the limit is in L^p and the original sequence converges to it in norm.

**Worked Example:**

The partial sums of a Fourier series that converges in L^2 (by Parseval) form a Cauchy sequence in L^2 and therefore converge in L^2 to some function (which is the L^2 function whose Fourier coefficients were given).

## Duality and Reflexivity (for 1 < p < ∞)

<!-- section: duality-reflexivity-lp -->

The dual of L^p (1<p<∞) is L^q where 1/p + 1/q=1: every continuous linear functional on L^p is of the form φ(g) = ∫ f g for a unique f in L^q, with ||φ|| = ||f||_q .

L^1 dual is L^∞ (under σ-finiteness), but L^∞ dual is larger than L^1 (contains finitely additive measures, etc.).

For 1<p<∞, L^p is reflexive (the dual of the dual is the space itself, canonically).

**ELI5**

- Continuous linear functionals on L^p are given by integration against an L^q "kernel".
- Reflexivity for 1<p<∞ means the space is "as nice as Hilbert space" from the point of view of weak topologies and geometry; L^1 and L^∞ are not reflexive.

**Worked Example:**

On L^2 the dual is L^2 itself via the inner product; the Riesz representation theorem for Hilbert spaces is the special case p=2.

## L^p on Finite Measure Spaces; Relations between L^p Spaces

<!-- section: relations-lp-finite-measure -->

On a probability space (finite measure), if p < q then L^q ⊂ L^p and ||f||_p ≤ ||f||_q (by Jensen or Hölder).

On infinite measure spaces the inclusions reverse in a certain sense (L^p ∩ L^∞ ⊂ L^q for p<q, etc.).

Interpolation (Riesz-Thorin, Marcinkiewicz) lets one obtain L^r bounds from L^p and L^q bounds.

**ELI5**

- On a finite-measure space, being in a higher L^q (q>p) is a stronger condition (more integrability); the norms satisfy ||f||_p ≤ C ||f||_q .
- On the whole line the spaces are incomparable: a function can be in L^1 but not L^2 (heavy tails) or in L^2 but not L^1 (tall thin spikes).

**Worked Example:**

On [0,1], L^∞ ⊂ L^2 ⊂ L^1 with ||f||_1 ≤ ||f||_2 ≤ ||f||_∞ .

On ℝ, 1/√(1+|x|) is in L^2 but not L^1; 1/(1+|x|) is in L^1 but not L^2.

## Applications (Fourier, PDE, Probability, Sobolev)

<!-- section: applications-lp -->

- Fourier analysis: Hausdorff-Young, Plancherel (L^2 isometry), restriction theorems, etc. live in L^p.
- PDEs: energy methods in L^2, maximum principles via L^∞, Calderón-Zygmund theory for singular integrals giving L^p bounds.
- Probability: moments, L^p convergence of random variables, martingale inequalities (Doob).
- Sobolev spaces W^{k,p} embed into L^q or continuous functions depending on the relation between k, p and dimension (Sobolev embedding theorem).

**ELI5**

- Many inequalities that control solutions of differential equations or transforms are statements that an operator is bounded from one L^p to another.
- In probability, convergence in L^p is a strong form of convergence that implies convergence in probability and gives rate information via moments.

**Worked Example:**

Plancherel theorem: the Fourier transform is an isometry on L^2(ℝ^n). This is the p=2 case of Hausdorff-Young and is proved via density of Schwartz functions and Parseval for Gaussians or via the L^2 theory of the Fourier transform on the circle plus limits.

## Common Mistakes

- **Confusing L^p with pointwise values**: Functions in L^p are defined only up to a.e. equivalence; pointwise statements must be qualified "a.e."
- **Assuming L^p ⊂ L^q or vice versa independently of the measure**: Inclusions depend heavily on whether the measure is finite or infinite.
- **Thinking the dual of L^1 is L^1**: No, dual of L^1 is L^∞.
- **Forgetting that L^∞ is not separable**: The unit ball is not separable in L^∞ norm (characteristic functions of sets far apart).
- **Applying Hölder without the conjugate exponents**: 1/p + 1/q must be 1 (or generalized Hölder for more functions).

A Fourier series is an expansion of a periodic function (or a function on a circle, or on an interval with suitable boundary conditions) as an infinite sum of sines and cosines (or complex exponentials) whose frequencies are integer multiples of a fundamental frequency.

The theory of Fourier series is the historical origin of much of modern analysis (convergence questions led to Cantor's set theory, Lebesgue integration, etc.) and remains central to harmonic analysis, PDEs, signal processing, and number theory.

## Trigonometric Polynomials and Orthogonality

<!-- section: trigonometric-polynomials-orthogonality -->

On the circle (or [−π,π] with periodic identification) the functions e^{i n x} (n ∈ ℤ) or {1, cos(nx), sin(nx)} (n≥1) form an orthogonal system with respect to the L^2 inner product (1/(2π)) ∫ f \bar g dx .

The L^2 norm of each e^{i n x} is 1; the trigonometric polynomials are finite linear combinations.

Any continuous (or L^2) function has Fourier coefficients ĉ(n) = (1/(2π)) ∫ f(x) e^{-i n x} dx .

The partial sums S_N(f)(x) = ∑_{|n|≤N} ĉ(n) e^{i n x} are the orthogonal projections onto the span of the first 2N+1 harmonics.

**ELI5**

- Sine and cosine waves of integer frequencies are orthogonal: their integrals of products over a period are zero unless they have the same frequency.
- The Fourier coefficients measure "how much" of each frequency is present in the function; the partial sum is the best L^2 approximation using only frequencies up to N.

**Worked Example:**

For f(x) = x on (−π,π) (periodic), the Fourier series is 2 ∑_{k=1}^∞ (−1)^{k+1} sin(k x)/k . This is the classic sawtooth wave expansion.

## Pointwise Convergence: Dirichlet and Jordan Theorems

<!-- section: pointwise-convergence-dirichlet-jordan -->

If f is periodic and of bounded variation (or just has left and right limits everywhere and is integrable), then at each point x the partial sums S_N(f)(x) converge to (f(x+) + f(x−))/2 (the average of the left and right limits).

In particular, at points of continuity the series converges to f(x).

The proof uses the Dirichlet kernel D_N(θ) = sin((N+1/2)θ)/sin(θ/2) and integration by parts or summation by parts on the Dirichlet integral, plus the Riemann-Lebesgue lemma.

**ELI5**

- Even if the function has jumps, the Fourier series at the jump converges to the average of the two sides (like the Gibbs phenomenon overshoot but the value at the point is the average).
- For a continuous function of bounded variation the series converges pointwise everywhere to the function.

**Worked Example:**

The square wave (sign of sin x) has Fourier series (4/π) ∑_{k odd} sin(k x)/k . At the jump points (multiples of π) it converges to 0, which is the average of +1 and −1.

## L^2 Convergence and Parseval's Identity

<!-- section: l2-convergence-parseval -->

For f ∈ L^2 (periodic), the partial sums S_N converge to f in L^2 norm (mean-square), and Bessel's inequality becomes equality: (1/2π) ∫ |f|^2 = ∑ |ĉ(n)|^2 (Parseval).

This follows because the trigonometric system is a complete orthonormal system in L^2 (the span of trigonometric polynomials is dense in L^2 by Stone-Weierstrass for continuous functions plus density of continuous in L^2, or by Fejér means, or by other approximation).

**ELI5**

- In the L^2 sense (average squared error) the Fourier series always converges to the function, for any square-integrable periodic function.
- Parseval says that the total "energy" (L^2 norm squared) of the function equals the sum of the energies of its frequency components.

**Worked Example:**

For f(x) = x on (−π,π), Parseval gives (1/π) ∫_0^π x^2 dx = ∑ (coefficients of sin)^2 , which evaluates to π²/3 on the left and a known series on the right, recovering the Basel problem value ζ(2)=π²/6 after a little algebra.

## Cesàro (Fejér) Means and Uniform Convergence

<!-- section: cesaro-feier-uniform -->

The Fejér (Cesàro) means σ_N = (S_0 + … + S_N)/(N+1) are the averages of the partial sums.

They are given by convolution with the Fejér kernel F_N ≥0, which is an approximate identity (positive, integral 1, concentrates at 0 as N→∞).

For f continuous and periodic, σ_N converges uniformly to f (Fejér's theorem). This gives a constructive way to recover a continuous function from its Fourier coefficients via uniformly convergent trigonometric polynomials.

**ELI5**

- Instead of taking the raw partial sums (which may oscillate), average the first N partial sums; the averaging smooths things out and produces a trigonometric polynomial that converges nicely.
- For continuous functions the Fejér means give uniform approximation, proving that trigonometric polynomials are dense in C(T) uniform norm (Stone-Weierstrass is another way).

**Worked Example:**

For any continuous periodic f the Fejér means σ_N are trigonometric polynomials that converge uniformly to f. This is the historical way one proves that the Fourier series of a continuous function is Cesàro summable to f, even if it does not converge pointwise.

## Divergence and Gibbs Phenomenon; Carleson's Theorem

<!-- section: divergence-gibbs-carleson -->

There exist continuous functions whose Fourier series diverges at some points (du Bois-Reymond). There even exist L^1 functions whose Fourier series diverges everywhere (Kolmogorov).

Near a jump discontinuity the partial sums overshoot by a fixed percentage (about 9% of the jump height) no matter how large N; this is the Gibbs phenomenon. The overshoot does not go away; it just gets narrower.

Nevertheless, Carleson's theorem (1966) states that for any f ∈ L^2 the Fourier series converges pointwise almost everywhere to f. (Hunt extended to L^p for p>1; the L^1 case remains open in full generality, but a.e. convergence fails for some L^1 functions.)

**ELI5**

- Even for nice continuous functions the raw Fourier series may misbehave at points; averaging (Fejér) rescues uniform convergence.
- At jumps there is a persistent "ringing" or overshoot whose width shrinks but whose relative height stays constant (Gibbs).
- Carleson says that for square-integrable functions the misbehavior can happen only on a set of measure zero; pointwise a.e. convergence holds.

**Worked Example:**

The square wave's partial sums exhibit Gibbs ringing near the jumps: they overshoot by about 18% of the half-jump (9% of full jump) on each side, and the ringing gets squeezed closer to the discontinuity as N increases, but never disappears in height.

## Applications and Extensions (Fourier Transform, PDEs, Number Theory)

<!-- section: applications-extensions -->

- Heat equation on the circle: the Fourier series of the initial data decays exponentially in time for each mode; this solves the PDE explicitly.
- Fourier analysis on groups, abstract harmonic analysis.
- Number theory: Weyl equidistribution, Hardy-Littlewood circle method, etc. rely on exponential sums (Fourier analysis on the circle).
- Signal processing: FFT computes partial Fourier sums efficiently; filtering is multiplication of coefficients.
- The Fourier transform on ℝ^n is the continuous analogue; Plancherel, inversion, etc. extend the theory.

**ELI5**

- Expanding the initial temperature distribution in sines/cosines on a ring, each frequency component decays at its own rate under the heat equation; the solution is the sum with the decayed coefficients.
- Many problems in analysis and number theory are solved by "looking at the frequencies."

**Worked Example:**

Solve u_t = u_xx on the circle, u(0,·)=f periodic. Expand f = ∑ ĉ_n e^{i n x}; then u(t,x) = ∑ ĉ_n e^{-n^2 t} e^{i n x}. Each mode decays exponentially fast (high frequencies die instantly); this instantly smooths the solution for t>0.

## Common Mistakes

- **Assuming the Fourier series of a continuous function always converges pointwise**: False (du Bois-Reymond counter-examples); uniform convergence requires more (e.g. Dini condition or BV).
- **Confusing pointwise convergence with L^2 convergence**: L^2 convergence always holds for L^2 functions; pointwise is much harder (Carleson).
- **Forgetting the normalization constants**: Different conventions for the 2π factor or for a_0 vs a_0/2 appear in textbooks; always check the formulas for the coefficients.
- **Thinking Gibbs phenomenon means the series does not converge at jumps**: It does converge to the average of the jumps; the overshoot is in the rate of convergence near the jump.
- **Applying Parseval without L^2**: Parseval is an L^2 identity; for continuous functions one needs additional justification or Fejér means to pass to the limit.

A rigorous treatment of multivariable calculus uses the language and tools of real analysis (limits, continuity, differentiation in normed spaces, integration on manifolds or via Fubini, differential forms) to prove the fundamental theorems (Green, Stokes, Gauss/divergence) that relate derivatives and integrals in several variables.

This is the natural continuation of one-variable real analysis and the foundation for differential geometry, PDEs, and much of modern physics and engineering mathematics.

## Differentiability in Several Variables (Total Derivative)

<!-- section: differentiability-several-variables -->

A map f : U ⊂ ℝ^n → ℝ^m (U open) is differentiable at a ∈ U if there exists a linear map Df(a) : ℝ^n → ℝ^m such that

f(a + h) = f(a) + Df(a) h + o(||h||)   as h→0 .

The linear map Df(a) is represented by the Jacobian matrix of partial derivatives (when they exist).

Existence of all partial derivatives does not imply differentiability (classic counter-examples); continuity of the partials does (via the mean value theorem on each coordinate).

**ELI5**

- Differentiability means the function is well-approximated by a linear function (its "tangent plane" or "best linear approximation") near the point, with error negligible compared to the distance.
- In several variables the derivative is a matrix (the Jacobian) that tells you how each output coordinate changes with each input coordinate, all at once.

**Worked Example:**

f(x,y) = x y / (x² + y²) for (x,y)≠0, f(0,0)=0. All directional derivatives at 0 exist and are zero, but f is not differentiable at 0 (the limit of [f(h)−f(0) − L h ] / ||h|| does not exist for any linear L; along y=mx the quotient behaves like m/(1+m²) which depends on direction).

## The Chain Rule and the Inverse/Implicit Function Theorems

<!-- section: chain-rule-inverse-implicit -->

If f and g are differentiable, the chain rule holds: D(f∘g)(a) = Df(g(a)) ∘ Dg(a) (composition of linear maps, i.e. matrix product of Jacobians).

The inverse function theorem: if Df(a) is invertible (Jacobian determinant nonzero), then f is locally invertible near a with differentiable inverse whose derivative is the inverse matrix.

The implicit function theorem: if F(x,y)=0 and ∂F/∂y is invertible at a point, then y can be solved locally as a C^1 function of x.

These are proved by reducing to the contraction mapping theorem or by Newton iteration in Banach spaces (the proofs are essentially one-variable arguments lifted to the linear-algebra setting).

**ELI5**

- The chain rule says that rates of change multiply (the derivative of the outer function at the inner value, times the derivative of the inner).
- Inverse function theorem: if the linear approximation is invertible (no collapsing of dimensions), then the map itself is locally invertible.
- Implicit function theorem: if a level set is "transverse" (the gradient in the y-directions is full rank), you can solve for some variables in terms of the others locally.

**Worked Example:**

F(x,y) = x² + y² − 1 =0 (unit circle). At (0,1), ∂F/∂y = 2y =2 ≠0, so y can be solved locally as a C^1 function of x near 0: y = √(1−x²). The derivative dy/dx = −x / √(1−x²) matches what one gets by implicit differentiation.

## Multiple Integrals and Fubini/Tonelli

<!-- section: multiple-integrals-fubini-tonelli -->

The Lebesgue integral on ℝ^n is defined via product measure (or completion of the Riemann integral on rectangles, etc.).

Fubini's theorem: if f is integrable over a product space (∫∫ |f| <∞), then the iterated integrals equal the double integral, and the slices are integrable for almost every fixed variable.

Tonelli's theorem: for nonnegative measurable f the iterated integrals (possibly infinite) equal the double integral (no absolute integrability needed).

These allow reduction of n-dimensional integrals to iterated one-dimensional ones and justify changing order of integration.

**ELI5**

- For "nice" (absolutely integrable) functions you can compute the integral by slicing in any order you like; the answers agree.
- For nonnegative functions you can always iterate, even if the integral is infinite; this is Tonelli.

**Worked Example:**

∫_0^1 ∫_0^1 1/(x+y) dy dx diverges (inner integral logs), but the double integral over the square is infinite; Fubini does not apply because the function is not absolutely integrable. Switching order gives the same divergence. For |f| the iterated integrals are both infinite, consistent with Tonelli.

## Change of Variables in Multiple Integrals

<!-- section: change-of-variables-multiple -->

If φ : U → V is a C^1 diffeomorphism (invertible, derivative continuous, Jacobian determinant nonzero), then

∫_V f(y) dy = ∫_U f(φ(x)) |det Dφ(x)| dx .

The absolute value of the Jacobian determinant is the local volume distortion factor.

The proof reduces to the one-variable case via Fubini after a preliminary linear change of variables (which contributes the determinant) and uses the inverse function theorem to guarantee local invertibility.

**ELI5**

- When you change coordinates, areas/volumes get stretched or shrunk by the absolute value of the determinant of the derivative matrix (the Jacobian).
- The integral picks up that factor so that the "mass" is preserved.

**Worked Example:**

Polar coordinates: x = r cos θ, y = r sin θ, Jacobian determinant r. Thus ∬_D f(x,y) dx dy = ∫∫ f(r cos θ, r sin θ) r dr dθ over the corresponding region in (r,θ). The extra r is the area element in polar coordinates.

## Differential Forms, Exterior Derivative, and Stokes' Theorem

<!-- section: differential-forms-stokes -->

A k-form on ℝ^n is a field of alternating multilinear maps (or, in coordinates, a sum of terms f_I dx_{i1} ∧ … ∧ dx_{ik} with I increasing).

The exterior derivative d turns a k-form into a (k+1)-form and satisfies d²=0. In ℝ^3 it unifies grad, curl, div.

Stokes' theorem: ∫_M dω = ∫_{∂M} ω for a compact oriented manifold with boundary (or a chain).

Special cases:
- Fundamental theorem of calculus (k=0 on an interval).
- Green's theorem (2D).
- Classical Stokes (surface with boundary).
- Divergence (Gauss) theorem (volume with boundary).

The proof in the smooth case reduces via partitions of unity and local charts to the fundamental theorem in one variable after applying Fubini.

**ELI5**

- Differential forms are the right objects that "integrate over k-dimensional pieces" and whose derivative is again a form.
- Stokes says that the integral of the derivative of a form over a domain equals the integral of the form over the boundary — exactly generalizing "integral of derivative = difference of values at ends."

**Worked Example:**

Green's theorem in the plane: ∫_P dx + Q dy over a region = ∬ (∂Q/∂x − ∂P/∂y) dx dy . This is Stokes for the 1-form P dx + Q dy.

## Common Mistakes

- **Assuming existence of partial derivatives implies differentiability**: Counter-examples exist even in two variables; continuity of partials is a sufficient (but not necessary) condition.
- **Forgetting the absolute value in the change-of-variables formula**: The Jacobian determinant can be negative (orientation reversal); the integral uses the absolute value.
- **Applying Fubini without absolute integrability**: Order can matter or both iterated integrals can exist but differ from the double integral if only conditional convergence.
- **Thinking Stokes requires the manifold to be embedded in Euclidean space**: The theorem is intrinsic (works on abstract manifolds), but the proof often uses an embedding or charts.
- **Confusing the exterior derivative with the ordinary differential**: d on 0-forms is the gradient (df), but on higher forms it involves curls and divergences in 3D; the general formula is the alternating sum with signs.
