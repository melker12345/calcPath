# Construction of the real numbers

The rational numbers have gaps. To do rigorous calculus we must fill them in a precise, axiomatically controlled way. Two classical constructions achieve this: Cauchy sequences of rationals (with a suitable equivalence) and Dedekind cuts. Both produce a complete ordered field that we call the reals, and they are isomorphic.

Understanding the constructions shows that the reals are not mysterious; they can be manufactured from simpler objects we already trust.

## Cauchy Sequence Construction

<!-- section: cauchy-construction -->

A sequence $(q_n)$ of rationals is Cauchy if for every rational $\varepsilon > 0$ there exists $N$ such that for all $m, n > N$, $|q_m - q_n| < \varepsilon$.

Two Cauchy sequences $(q_n)$ and $(r_n)$ are equivalent if $\lim (q_n - r_n) = 0$ (i.e., for every $\varepsilon > 0$ eventually $|q_n - r_n| < \varepsilon$).

A real number is defined to be an equivalence class of Cauchy sequences of rationals. The rational $q$ is identified with the constant sequence $q, q, q, \dots$

Addition and multiplication are defined termwise on representatives; the order is defined by eventual positivity of differences.

**ELI5**

- Think of each real as a "target" that a sequence of better and better rational approximations is chasing. Two sequences that chase the exact same target are declared the same real.
- The constant sequences give you all the old rationals back; the new numbers come from sequences that are Cauchy but do not converge inside $\mathbb{Q}$.

**Worked Example:**

The sequence defined by $q_1 = 1$, $q_{n+1} = \frac12 (q_n + 2/q_n)$ is Cauchy (it is the Babylonian method for $\sqrt{2}$) and does not converge to any rational. Its equivalence class is the real number $\sqrt{2}$.

## Dedekind Cut Construction

<!-- section: dedekind-construction -->

A Dedekind cut is a pair $(A, B)$ where $A \cup B = \mathbb{Q}$, $A \cap B = \emptyset$, every element of $A$ is less than every element of $B$, and $A$ has no greatest element.

The cut defines a real number: intuitively the "gap" between $A$ and $B$. The rational $r$ corresponds to the cut $A = \{q \in \mathbb{Q} \mid q < r\}$, $B = \{q \mid q \ge r\}$.

Order is by inclusion of the lower sets: cut $(A,B) \le (C,D)$ when $A \subseteq C$.

Addition: lower set of sum is $\{a + c \mid a \in A, c \in C\}$.

**ELI5**

- A cut is literally a "scissors cut" through the rationals: everything to the left of the cut is A, everything to the right is B. The place you cut is the real number, even if no rational sits exactly there.
- For $\sqrt{2}$ the lower set A is all rationals whose square is less than 2; there is no largest such rational.

**Worked Example:**

The cut for $\sqrt{2}$ has lower set $A = \{ q \in \mathbb{Q} \mid q \le 0 \text{ or } q^2 < 2 \}$. This A has no largest element (if $q^2 < 2$ you can find a slightly larger rational still with square <2). The corresponding real is positive and squares to 2.

## Equivalence of the Two Constructions

<!-- section: equivalence -->

It can be shown that the two constructions yield isomorphic ordered fields: every Cauchy equivalence class corresponds to a unique Dedekind cut (the cut consisting of all rationals strictly less than any term of a representative sequence, eventually), and vice versa.

Both satisfy the least-upper-bound property and contain a copy of $\mathbb{Q}$ as a dense subfield. Once we have either model we usually stop worrying about the particular construction and work with the axiomatic properties of $\mathbb{R}$.

**ELI5**

- Different recipes (sequences vs cuts) produce "the same cake": the resulting number systems behave identically, so mathematicians are free to use whichever mental picture is convenient.
- In practice we rarely unwind the definitions; we use the abstract properties (completeness, ordered field) that both constructions guarantee.

**Worked Example:**

Given a Cauchy sequence of rationals $(q_n)$ with limit candidate L (the real it defines), the corresponding Dedekind lower set is all rationals strictly smaller than all but finitely many $q_n$. One proves this set is indeed a cut and that the operations match.

## Common Mistakes

- **Believing the constructions are needed for everyday calculus**: In most analysis courses we take the reals as given by axioms; constructions are only shown once to prove consistency and existence of a complete ordered field.
- **Forgetting equivalence relation**: Not every sequence of rationals defines a new real; only the Cauchy ones, and only up to equivalence.
- **Confusing a sequence with its limit**: The sequence is not the real; the equivalence class is. Different sequences can define the same real.
- **Thinking Dedekind cuts are 'sets of reals'**: Cuts are partitions of the *rationals*; the reals are the cuts themselves.
- **Neglecting to verify the field axioms on the new objects**: Termwise operations on Cauchy sequences must be shown to preserve the Cauchy property and respect equivalence.
