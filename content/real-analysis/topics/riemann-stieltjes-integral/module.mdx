# Riemann–Stieltjes integral

The Riemann–Stieltjes integral generalizes the ordinary Riemann integral by replacing the "dx" (length element) with dα where α is a monotone integrator function.

When α(x) = x one recovers the Riemann integral. When α is a step function the integral reduces to a finite or infinite sum. It provides a unified language for both integrals and sums and is the natural setting for integration by parts in Stieltjes form.

## Definition via Riemann–Stieltjes Sums

<!-- section: definition-stieltjes-sums -->

Given a partition P of [a,b] and tags t_i in each subinterval, the Riemann–Stieltjes sum is ∑ f(t_i) (α(x_i) − α(x_{i−1})) .

f is Riemann–Stieltjes integrable with respect to α (written ∫ f dα) if there is a number I such that for every ε>0 there is a partition P_ε so that for any refinement and any choice of tags the sum is within ε of I.

When α is continuous and f continuous they are integrable wrt each other; when α has a jump the integral picks up f at the jump times the jump size (like a delta contribution).

**ELI5**

- Instead of adding f times width of slice, you add f times the change in the "weight function" α over the slice.
- If α is a staircase (jumps at certain points), the integral becomes a weighted sum of the f values at the jump locations, with weights the jump sizes.

**Worked Example:**

Let α(x) = 0 for x<0, α(x)=1 for x≥0 (Heaviside step on ℝ). Then ∫_{-1}^1 f dα = f(0) (the jump of α at 0), for continuous f. This is the "integral against a Dirac delta" in elementary language.

## Integration by Parts for Stieltjes Integrals

<!-- section: integration-by-parts-stieltjes -->

The formula ∫_a^b f dα + ∫_a^b α df = f(b)α(b) − f(a)α(a) holds under suitable conditions (when one integral exists the other does and the formula is true).

This is the Stieltjes version of the familiar ∫ u dv = u v − ∫ v du .

It is extremely useful for converting between sums and integrals or for moving derivatives from one factor to another.

**ELI5**

- The product f α "telescopes" its changes; the total change f(b)α(b) − f(a)α(a) equals the sum of "f changes α" plus "α changes f".
- This is the discrete product rule summed up, or the continuous product rule integrated.

**Worked Example:**

Take α(x) = floor(x) on [0,n]. Then ∫_0^n 1 dα = number of integers from 1 to n = n (jumps of size 1 at each integer). Integration by parts with f=1 recovers the same. More interestingly, summation by parts is exactly this formula when α is a step.

## Relation to Riemann and Lebesgue; When the Integral Exists

<!-- section: relation-riemann-lebesgue-existence -->

If α(x)=x the definition reduces exactly to the Riemann integral.

If α is absolutely continuous (α(x) = ∫^x g with g integrable) then ∫ f dα = ∫ f g dx (ordinary integral).

The integral ∫ f dα exists whenever f and α have no common discontinuities (one continuous where the other jumps).

**ELI5**

- Stieltjes with respect to x is ordinary area.
- Stieltjes with respect to a smooth increasing α is ordinary integral with weight α'.
- If f jumps exactly where α jumps the "product of jumps" creates an ambiguous contribution that prevents the integral from existing in the Riemann–Stieltjes sense.

**Worked Example:**

Let α be the Cantor function (continuous, constant on the complementary intervals of the Cantor set, increases from 0 to 1 on the Cantor set of measure zero). Then ∫ f dα is a "singular integral" that sees only the values of f on the Cantor set; it is not given by any ordinary density.

## Applications: Probability (CDFs), Summation by Parts, Spectral Theory

<!-- section: applications-probability-sums -->

- In probability, if F is the cumulative distribution function of a random variable, then E[g(X)] = ∫ g dF (Stieltjes integral). This unifies discrete (sum p_k g(x_k)), continuous (∫ g f dx), and singular distributions.
- Summation by parts is the discrete Stieltjes integration by parts.
- In spectral theory and functional analysis the spectral theorem is often stated with Stieltjes integrals against projection-valued measures.

**ELI5**

- The expectation of a function of a random variable is "integrate g against the probability mass" — when the distribution has atoms (jumps in the CDF) you get ordinary sums; when it has a density you get the familiar integral.
- This language lets probabilists and analysts treat discrete and continuous cases with the same formulas.

**Worked Example:**

For a discrete random variable taking values 1,2,3 with probabilities 1/2,1/3,1/6, the CDF F has jumps of those sizes at 1,2,3. Then ∫ x dF = 1·(1/2) + 2·(1/3) + 3·(1/6) = 1 + 2/3 + 1/2 = 13/6, the expectation.

## Common Mistakes

- **Assuming ∫ f dα always exists when f and α are both of bounded variation**: They must not share discontinuities.
- **Confusing dα with α' dx when α is not absolutely continuous**: The Cantor function has α'=0 almost everywhere yet increases; the Stieltjes integral is not the ordinary integral against 0.
- **Forgetting that the integrator α is usually taken monotone increasing**: The definition can be extended to α of bounded variation by splitting into positive and negative variations, but the basic theory is for monotone α.
- **Thinking the Riemann–Stieltjes integral is the same as the Lebesgue–Stieltjes integral**: They coincide when the integral exists in the Riemann–Stieltjes sense, but Lebesgue–Stieltjes is more general (completes with respect to the measure induced by α).
- **Writing ∫ f(x) dα(x) with the same variable on both**: The notation is conventional but the dummy variable in the differential is not the same as a free variable.
