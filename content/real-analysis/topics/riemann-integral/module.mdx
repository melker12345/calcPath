# Riemann integral

The Riemann integral is the rigorous definition of the area under a curve as the limit of Riemann sums (upper and lower sums over partitions) as the mesh of the partition tends to zero.

It is the integral used in elementary calculus; later the Lebesgue integral extends it to a much larger class of functions while preserving good limit theorems.

## Upper and Lower Sums; Partitions

<!-- section: upper-lower-sums -->

A partition P of [a,b] is a finite increasing sequence a = x_0 < x_1 < … < x_n = b.

On each subinterval [x_{i−1}, x_i] let m_i = inf f on that subinterval, M_i = sup f.

The lower sum L(f,P) = ∑ m_i Δx_i , upper sum U(f,P) = ∑ M_i Δx_i .

f is Riemann integrable on [a,b] if sup over all partitions of L(f,P) equals inf of U(f,P); that common value is the integral ∫_a^b f.

**ELI5**

- For a given way of slicing the interval into thin strips, the lower sum is the total area of rectangles that fit under the graph (using the lowest height in each strip); the upper sum uses the tallest height in each strip.
- If as you make the slices thinner and thinner (mesh →0) the lower and upper totals get squeezed together to the same number, that number is the Riemann integral.

**Worked Example:**

For f(x)=x on [0,1], any partition, the lower sum is the sum of areas of rectangles with height the left endpoint (or inf), upper with right. As mesh →0 both sums approach 1/2, which is the integral (area of the triangle).

## Criterion for Riemann Integrability (Riemann's Criterion)

<!-- section: riemann-criterion -->

A bounded function f on [a,b] is Riemann integrable if and only if for every ε > 0 there exists a partition P such that U(f,P) − L(f,P) < ε.

Equivalently, the upper and lower integrals agree.

Continuous functions on [a,b] are Riemann integrable (uniform continuity implies that on fine enough partitions the oscillation on each subinterval is small, making U−L small).

**ELI5**

- The difference U−L measures the total "uncertainty" or "slack" in the area estimate coming from the variation of f inside the slices.
- If you can make that slack arbitrarily small by choosing a fine enough partition, the area is well-defined.

**Worked Example:**

A step function that jumps once is still Riemann integrable: on a partition that isolates the jump in one tiny interval of width δ, the contribution to U−L from that interval is at most (jump size)·δ, which can be made <ε; on the other intervals f is constant so oscillation zero.

## Discontinuities and Integrability

<!-- section: discontinuities-integrability -->

A bounded function with finitely many discontinuities is Riemann integrable (isolate each discontinuity in a small interval whose total length is <ε / (bound on |f|)).

Thomae's function (1/q at p/q rational, 0 at irrationals) is Riemann integrable (integral 0) even though discontinuous at every rational.

The Dirichlet function (1 on rationals, 0 on irrationals) is not Riemann integrable (U=1, L=0 for every partition).

**ELI5**

- A few jumps or spikes of zero width do not affect the area; you can hide their uncertainty in tiny slices.
- If discontinuities are dense (rationals), the upper sums stay high (using the 1's) and lower sums stay low (using the 0's) no matter how fine the partition; the area is ambiguous.

**Worked Example:**

The characteristic function of the rationals in [0,1] has, in every subinterval, inf=0 and sup=1, so for any partition U−L = sum 1·Δx_i = b−a =1, which cannot be made <1/2. Hence not integrable.

## Common Mistakes

- **Thinking every bounded function is Riemann integrable**: The Dirichlet function is the standard counter-example.
- **Confusing Riemann with Lebesgue**: Lebesgue integrates the Dirichlet function (integral 0) because the rationals have measure zero.
- **Forgetting that the partition must be chosen after ε (the definition is existential on partitions)**: You do not have to use equal-width partitions or regular grids; arbitrary partitions are allowed and often necessary.
- **Believing that if f is integrable then f must be continuous a.e.**: True for Riemann (a theorem of Lebesgue), but the proof is nontrivial; there exist integrable functions discontinuous on a dense set (Thomae).
- **Mixing upper/lower sums with Riemann sums using tags**: The definition via upper/lower Darboux sums is equivalent to the limit of Riemann sums over tagged partitions with mesh→0.
