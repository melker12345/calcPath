# Improper integral

An improper integral is a definite integral in which either the interval of integration is unbounded or the integrand has a discontinuity (singularity) inside the interval. It is defined as a limit of proper (ordinary Riemann) integrals.

Improper integrals appear throughout analysis, physics, and probability (e.g., the Gaussian integral, Laplace transforms, expected values of continuous random variables with unbounded support).

## Unbounded Intervals (Limits at Infinity)

<!-- section: unbounded-intervals -->

The integral ∫_a^∞ f(x) dx is defined as lim_{b→∞} ∫_a^b f(x) dx , provided the limit exists (finite).

Similarly for ∫_{-∞}^b and the two-sided ∫_{-∞}^∞ (which requires both one-sided improper integrals to exist separately; the Cauchy principal value may exist even when the ordinary improper does not).

**ELI5**

- You compute the integral out to a large but finite B, then let B run off to infinity and see whether the accumulated area settles to a definite finite number.
- If the tails keep adding a fixed positive amount no matter how far you go, the integral diverges to infinity.

**Worked Example:**

∫_1^∞ dx / x^p converges for p>1 (antiderivative x^{1−p}/(1−p), limit at ∞ is 0 when 1−p<0 i.e. p>1) and diverges for p≤1 (logarithmic or positive power growth).

## Integrable Singularities (Limits at Finite Points)

<!-- section: singularities-finite -->

If f has a discontinuity at c ∈ [a,b], one defines the improper integral as lim_{ε→0^+} ( ∫_a^{c−ε} + ∫_{c+ε}^b ) when the two limits exist.

Common singularities: 1/√x or 1/x^α near 0, with convergence depending on the exponent α<1 for integrability near 0.

**ELI5**

- The graph may shoot up to infinity or oscillate wildly at c, but if the "spike" is narrow enough and not too tall the area under it may still be finite.
- You cut out a small symmetric (or one-sided) neighborhood around the bad point, integrate on the good parts, then let the neighborhood shrink to zero and see if the missing piece's area contribution tends to a limit.

**Worked Example:**

∫_0^1 dx / √x . Antiderivative 2√x. From ε to 1: 2 − 2√ε → 2 as ε→0. Hence the improper integral converges to 2.

## Convergence Tests (Comparison, Limit Comparison, Absolute vs Conditional)

<!-- section: convergence-tests-improper -->

For positive integrands the usual comparison and limit comparison tests apply (compare with a known p-integral or exponential).

Absolute convergence: ∫ |f| < ∞ implies the improper integral of f converges (and equals the difference of integrals of positive and negative parts).

Conditional convergence for improper integrals is more subtle (oscillatory tails); Dirichlet test for integrals, etc.

**ELI5**

- If |f| has finite area, then the signed f cannot accumulate infinite positive or negative area; cancellations are controlled.
- For oscillating tails that decay slowly, absolute area may be infinite while signed cancellations still produce a finite net area (like alternating series).

**Worked Example:**

∫_1^∞ sin x / x dx converges (Dirichlet test or integration by parts: the antiderivative of sin is bounded, 1/x ↓0), but ∫ |sin x / x| dx diverges (harmonic-like over intervals of length π).

## Principal Value and Other Regularizations

<!-- section: principal-value -->

The Cauchy principal value for ∫_{-∞}^∞ f takes lim_{R→∞} ∫_{-R}^R f (symmetric limits).

For a singularity at c inside a finite interval, PV ∫_a^b = lim_{ε→0^+} (∫_a^{c−ε} + ∫_{c+ε}^b).

The PV may exist when the ordinary improper integral does not (e.g. ∫_{-1}^1 dx/x has PV=0 but ordinary improper diverges).

**ELI5**

- By approaching the bad point or infinity symmetrically from both sides, positive and negative infinities (or oscillations) may cancel in a principal-value sense even if one-sided they do not settle.
- PV is a useful regularization in physics and Fourier analysis, but it is not the same as the Lebesgue or ordinary improper integral.

**Worked Example:**

PV ∫_{-1}^1 dx/x : the antiderivative ln|x|, from −R to −ε plus from ε to R gives (ln R − ln ε) + (ln R − ln ε) wait no: actually for symmetric: ln| R | − ln|ε| + ln R − ln ε wait properly: integral from -R to -ε is lnε - ln R (negative), from ε to R is ln R - ln ε, they cancel to 0 for any ε,R. Taking ε→0, R→∞ still 0. But the one-sided improper from 0+ to 1 diverges to +∞, from -1 to 0- to −∞.

## Common Mistakes

- **Assuming that if the antiderivative has a limit at infinity then the integral converges**: True only if the integrand is the derivative of that antiderivative and the fundamental theorem applies in the limit; counter-examples exist when f is not integrable but F has limits.
- **Confusing absolute and conditional convergence for improper integrals**: Absolute is safer for term-by-term operations; conditional requires more care (like alternating series).
- **Forgetting that existence of lim_{b→∞} F(b) − F(a) requires that F'(x) = f(x) and f integrable on every finite piece**: If f has non-integrable singularities the antiderivative may not be defined in the classical sense.
- **Thinking every improper integral that 'looks convergent' by comparison is Lebesgue integrable**: On the real line Riemann and Lebesgue improper integrals coincide for positive functions; differences appear for signed functions with conditional convergence.
- **Using the same variable for the limit and the dummy integration variable**: Always write lim_{b→∞} ∫_a^b f(x) dx with distinct variables.
