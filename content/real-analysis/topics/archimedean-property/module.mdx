# Archimedean property

The Archimedean property expresses that the natural numbers are not bounded above in the real numbers: no matter how large a positive real number you pick, some natural number is even larger. Equivalently, the reciprocals of the naturals get arbitrarily close to zero.

This property is true in $\mathbb{R}$ but can fail in certain non-Archimedean ordered fields (which contain "infinite" and "infinitesimal" elements). It is a direct consequence of the least-upper-bound property.

## Statement and Equivalent Forms

<!-- section: statement -->

The following are equivalent in an ordered field:

1. (Archimedean) For every real $x > 0$ there exists a natural number $n$ such that $n > x$.
2. For every $\varepsilon > 0$ there exists $n \in \mathbb{N}$ such that $0 < 1/n < \varepsilon$.
3. $\mathbb{N}$ is not bounded above in the field.
4. For any two positive reals $x, y$ there exists $n$ with $nx > y$.

**ELI5**

- No real length is "infinitely long" compared with the unit length; you can always lay enough unit segments end-to-end to exceed it.
- Infinitesimals (positive numbers smaller than every 1/n) cannot exist in the reals.

**Worked Example:**

Given $x = 10^{100}$, the number $n = 10^{100} + 1$ is a natural number larger than $x$. In general take any integer strictly larger than $x$ (exists by definition of reals via decimals or LUB applied to the set of naturals).

## Proof from the Least-Upper-Bound Property

<!-- section: proof-lub -->

Suppose for contradiction that $\mathbb{N}$ is bounded above by some real $M$. Let $s = \sup \mathbb{N}$. Then $s - 1$ is not an upper bound, so there exists a natural $k$ with $k > s - 1$. But then $k + 1 > s$, contradicting that $s$ is an upper bound. Hence $\mathbb{N}$ is unbounded.

The other forms follow quickly by taking reciprocals or scaling.

**ELI5**

- If the naturals had a "highest floor" s, then s-1 would still leave room for a larger natural (k), and k+1 would be bigger than s — impossible. So no ceiling exists.
- This is one of the first places we see LUB doing real work.

**Worked Example:**

To prove form 2 from form 1: let $\varepsilon > 0$. By form 1 applied to $x = 1/\varepsilon$ there is n > 1/ε, hence 1/n < ε.

## Consequences and Applications

<!-- section: consequences -->

- Between any two reals there is a rational (actually follows from Archimedean + density of ℤ in a coarser sense, but often proved together).
- The set {1/n | n ∈ ℕ} has infimum 0.
- For any real x the integer part ⌊x⌋ exists: the greatest integer ≤ x (apply Archimedean to find candidates, then LUB on the finite set below x).
- Limits of the form lim n→∞ 1/n = 0, lim n→∞ n = ∞ are justified.

**ELI5**

- You can always find a big enough denominator to make a unit fraction as small as you like.
- Every real has a "whole number part" because naturals go far enough.

**Worked Example:**

Existence of floor: let S = {k ∈ ℤ | k ≤ x}. By Archimedean, S is nonempty (some negative large enough) and bounded above by x, hence has a max m = sup S (finite set of integers). Then m ≤ x < m+1.

## Common Mistakes

- **Thinking Archimedean is part of the field axioms**: It is independent; there exist ordered fields that are non-Archimedean.
- **Confusing with well-ordering of naturals**: Well-ordering says every nonempty subset of ℕ has a least element; Archimedean is about ℕ being unbounded in ℝ.
- **Believing it prevents all 'infinities'**: Extended reals allow ±∞ as symbols, but they are not real numbers; inside ℝ everything is finite.
- **Misapplying to sequences**: A sequence a_n can still tend to infinity even though each term is finite; Archimedean only says no single term is infinite.
- **Forgetting the converse implication**: In the presence of LUB, Archimedean holds; without LUB one can have non-Archimedean complete fields (but those are exotic).
