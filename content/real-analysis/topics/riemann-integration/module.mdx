# Riemann Integration

Riemann sums, the fundamental theorem of calculus, improper integrals, and Riemann–Stieltjes integration.

The Riemann integral is the rigorous definition of the area under a curve as the limit of Riemann sums (upper and lower sums over partitions) as the mesh of the partition tends to zero.

It is the integral used in elementary calculus; later the Lebesgue integral extends it to a much larger class of functions while preserving good limit theorems.

## Upper and Lower Sums; Partitions

<!-- section: upper-lower-sums -->

A partition P of [a,b] is a finite increasing sequence a = x_0 < x_1 < … < x_n = b.

On each subinterval [x_{i−1}, x_i] let m_i = inf f on that subinterval, M_i = sup f.

The lower sum L(f,P) = ∑ m_i Δx_i , upper sum U(f,P) = ∑ M_i Δx_i .

f is Riemann integrable on [a,b] if sup over all partitions of L(f,P) equals inf of U(f,P); that common value is the integral ∫_a^b f.

**ELI5**

- For a given way of slicing the interval into thin strips, the lower sum is the total area of rectangles that fit under the graph (using the lowest height in each strip); the upper sum uses the tallest height in each strip.
- If as you make the slices thinner and thinner (mesh →0) the lower and upper totals get squeezed together to the same number, that number is the Riemann integral.

**Worked Example:**

For f(x)=x on [0,1], any partition, the lower sum is the sum of areas of rectangles with height the left endpoint (or inf), upper with right. As mesh →0 both sums approach 1/2, which is the integral (area of the triangle).

## Criterion for Riemann Integrability (Riemann's Criterion)

<!-- section: riemann-criterion -->

A bounded function f on [a,b] is Riemann integrable if and only if for every ε > 0 there exists a partition P such that U(f,P) − L(f,P) < ε.

Equivalently, the upper and lower integrals agree.

Continuous functions on [a,b] are Riemann integrable (uniform continuity implies that on fine enough partitions the oscillation on each subinterval is small, making U−L small).

**ELI5**

- The difference U−L measures the total "uncertainty" or "slack" in the area estimate coming from the variation of f inside the slices.
- If you can make that slack arbitrarily small by choosing a fine enough partition, the area is well-defined.

**Worked Example:**

A step function that jumps once is still Riemann integrable: on a partition that isolates the jump in one tiny interval of width δ, the contribution to U−L from that interval is at most (jump size)·δ, which can be made <ε; on the other intervals f is constant so oscillation zero.

## Discontinuities and Integrability

<!-- section: discontinuities-integrability -->

A bounded function with finitely many discontinuities is Riemann integrable (isolate each discontinuity in a small interval whose total length is <ε / (bound on |f|)).

Thomae's function (1/q at p/q rational, 0 at irrationals) is Riemann integrable (integral 0) even though discontinuous at every rational.

The Dirichlet function (1 on rationals, 0 on irrationals) is not Riemann integrable (U=1, L=0 for every partition).

**ELI5**

- A few jumps or spikes of zero width do not affect the area; you can hide their uncertainty in tiny slices.
- If discontinuities are dense (rationals), the upper sums stay high (using the 1's) and lower sums stay low (using the 0's) no matter how fine the partition; the area is ambiguous.

**Worked Example:**

The characteristic function of the rationals in [0,1] has, in every subinterval, inf=0 and sup=1, so for any partition U−L = sum 1·Δx_i = b−a =1, which cannot be made <1/2. Hence not integrable.

## Common Mistakes

- **Thinking every bounded function is Riemann integrable**: The Dirichlet function is the standard counter-example.
- **Confusing Riemann with Lebesgue**: Lebesgue integrates the Dirichlet function (integral 0) because the rationals have measure zero.
- **Forgetting that the partition must be chosen after ε (the definition is existential on partitions)**: You do not have to use equal-width partitions or regular grids; arbitrary partitions are allowed and often necessary.
- **Believing that if f is integrable then f must be continuous a.e.**: True for Riemann (a theorem of Lebesgue), but the proof is nontrivial; there exist integrable functions discontinuous on a dense set (Thomae).
- **Mixing upper/lower sums with Riemann sums using tags**: The definition via upper/lower Darboux sums is equivalent to the limit of Riemann sums over tagged partitions with mesh→0.

The fundamental theorem of calculus (FTC) establishes that differentiation and (Riemann) integration are inverse operations, in two complementary parts.

It is the reason the indefinite integral (antiderivative) and definite integral are so closely linked, and why we can compute areas by finding antiderivatives.

## FTC Part 1: The Integral as a Function of the Upper Limit

<!-- section: ftc-part-one -->

Let f be Riemann integrable on [a,b]. Define F(x) = ∫_a^x f(t) dt for x ∈ [a,b].

Then F is continuous on [a,b]. If in addition f is continuous at c ∈ [a,b], then F is differentiable at c and F'(c) = f(c).

In other words, "the derivative of the integral is the integrand" (at points of continuity of f).

**ELI5**

- F(x) is the accumulated signed area from a fixed left endpoint a up to the variable right endpoint x. As x moves a tiny bit, the added area is approximately f(x) times the tiny width, so the rate of change of F is f(x).
- If f has a jump, F is still continuous (area doesn't jump) but may have a corner (derivative jumps).

**Worked Example:**

Let f(x)=x on [0,1]. Then F(x) = ∫_0^x t dt = x²/2. F'(x) = x = f(x), as predicted (and f is continuous).

## FTC Part 2: Evaluation of Definite Integrals via Antiderivatives

<!-- section: ftc-part-two -->

If f is continuous on [a,b] and F is any antiderivative of f (F' = f on (a,b), F continuous up to ends), then ∫_a^b f = F(b) − F(a).

This is the familiar "take antiderivative, plug in limits" rule used in calculus.

**ELI5**

- The net signed area under f from a to b equals the total change in any antiderivative from a to b.
- It turns the hard problem of summing infinitely many infinitesimal areas into the (often) easier problem of finding one antiderivative.

**Worked Example:**

∫_0^1 x dx = [x²/2 ]_0^1 = 1/2 − 0 = 1/2. The antiderivative x²/2 changes by 1/2 over the interval, which is exactly the area.

## Proof Ideas and the Role of Continuity

<!-- section: proof-ideas-continuity -->

Part 1 is proved by writing the difference quotient [F(x+h)−F(x)]/h = (1/h) ∫_x^{x+h} f , then using that when f is continuous at x the integral average is close to f(x) (by bounding |f(t)−f(x)| small on a small interval).

Part 2 follows from Part 1: if F' = f then the function G(x) = ∫_a^x f − F(x) has G' = 0, hence G constant, hence G(b) = G(a) which rearranges to the desired equality.

Continuity of f guarantees both the existence of the integral and that every antiderivative works.

**ELI5**

- The average value of f over a tiny interval around x is forced to be close to f(x) by continuity; that average is exactly the difference quotient for the accumulated-area function.
- Once you know the derivative of the integral recovers f, any other function with the same derivative differs by a constant, so the net changes match.

**Worked Example:**

To prove Part 2: let G(x) = ∫_a^x f(t)dt − F(x). By Part 1, G'(x) = f(x) − F'(x) = 0 on (a,b). Thus G is constant on [a,b] (by MVT or connectedness). Therefore G(b) − G(a) = 0, i.e. ∫_a^b f = F(b) − F(a).

## Generalizations and Limitations (Improper, Lebesgue)

<!-- section: generalizations-limitations -->

FTC requires f integrable (for Part 1) and usually continuous for the strong form of Part 2. For improper integrals one takes limits of proper integrals.

In Lebesgue theory the FTC holds under weaker assumptions (absolute integrability, almost-everywhere differentiability of the indefinite integral, etc.), and the fundamental theorem of calculus for Lebesgue integrals recovers f almost everywhere.

**ELI5**

- If f has a bad discontinuity the indefinite integral may still be differentiable almost everywhere and recover f a.e. (Lebesgue).
- Improper integrals at endpoints or with singularities are handled by limits; the "FTC" then says the improper integral equals the limiting difference of the antiderivative (when the limit exists).

**Worked Example:**

The improper integral ∫_1^∞ (1/x²) dx = lim_{b→∞} [−1/x ]_1^b = lim (0 − (−1)) = 1, which equals the "antiderivative evaluated at infinity minus at 1".

## Common Mistakes

- **Applying Part 2 when f is not continuous (only integrable)**: The antiderivative may fail to exist in the classical sense or the formula may need interpretation.
- **Forgetting the constant of integration when finding antiderivatives**: The evaluation F(b)−F(a) automatically cancels the constant.
- **Confusing the two parts**: Part 1 says differentiation undoes integration (under continuity); Part 2 says integration undoes differentiation (for any antiderivative).
- **Using FTC on functions that are not integrable**: e.g. 1/√x on (0,1] is improperly integrable but the proper integral on [ε,1] is fine; the theorem applies after taking limits.
- **Thinking every derivative is integrable**: There exist functions differentiable everywhere whose derivative is not Riemann (or even Lebesgue) integrable on [a,b]. The FTC still holds in a weaker sense for the Lebesgue integral when f' exists a.e. and is integrable.

An improper integral is a definite integral in which either the interval of integration is unbounded or the integrand has a discontinuity (singularity) inside the interval. It is defined as a limit of proper (ordinary Riemann) integrals.

Improper integrals appear throughout analysis, physics, and probability (e.g., the Gaussian integral, Laplace transforms, expected values of continuous random variables with unbounded support).

## Unbounded Intervals (Limits at Infinity)

<!-- section: unbounded-intervals -->

The integral ∫_a^∞ f(x) dx is defined as lim_{b→∞} ∫_a^b f(x) dx , provided the limit exists (finite).

Similarly for ∫_{-∞}^b and the two-sided ∫_{-∞}^∞ (which requires both one-sided improper integrals to exist separately; the Cauchy principal value may exist even when the ordinary improper does not).

**ELI5**

- You compute the integral out to a large but finite B, then let B run off to infinity and see whether the accumulated area settles to a definite finite number.
- If the tails keep adding a fixed positive amount no matter how far you go, the integral diverges to infinity.

**Worked Example:**

∫_1^∞ dx / x^p converges for p>1 (antiderivative x^{1−p}/(1−p), limit at ∞ is 0 when 1−p<0 i.e. p>1) and diverges for p≤1 (logarithmic or positive power growth).

## Integrable Singularities (Limits at Finite Points)

<!-- section: singularities-finite -->

If f has a discontinuity at c ∈ [a,b], one defines the improper integral as lim_{ε→0^+} ( ∫_a^{c−ε} + ∫_{c+ε}^b ) when the two limits exist.

Common singularities: 1/√x or 1/x^α near 0, with convergence depending on the exponent α<1 for integrability near 0.

**ELI5**

- The graph may shoot up to infinity or oscillate wildly at c, but if the "spike" is narrow enough and not too tall the area under it may still be finite.
- You cut out a small symmetric (or one-sided) neighborhood around the bad point, integrate on the good parts, then let the neighborhood shrink to zero and see if the missing piece's area contribution tends to a limit.

**Worked Example:**

∫_0^1 dx / √x . Antiderivative 2√x. From ε to 1: 2 − 2√ε → 2 as ε→0. Hence the improper integral converges to 2.

## Convergence Tests (Comparison, Limit Comparison, Absolute vs Conditional)

<!-- section: convergence-tests-improper -->

For positive integrands the usual comparison and limit comparison tests apply (compare with a known p-integral or exponential).

Absolute convergence: ∫ |f| < ∞ implies the improper integral of f converges (and equals the difference of integrals of positive and negative parts).

Conditional convergence for improper integrals is more subtle (oscillatory tails); Dirichlet test for integrals, etc.

**ELI5**

- If |f| has finite area, then the signed f cannot accumulate infinite positive or negative area; cancellations are controlled.
- For oscillating tails that decay slowly, absolute area may be infinite while signed cancellations still produce a finite net area (like alternating series).

**Worked Example:**

∫_1^∞ sin x / x dx converges (Dirichlet test or integration by parts: the antiderivative of sin is bounded, 1/x ↓0), but ∫ |sin x / x| dx diverges (harmonic-like over intervals of length π).

## Principal Value and Other Regularizations

<!-- section: principal-value -->

The Cauchy principal value for ∫_{-∞}^∞ f takes lim_{R→∞} ∫_{-R}^R f (symmetric limits).

For a singularity at c inside a finite interval, PV ∫_a^b = lim_{ε→0^+} (∫_a^{c−ε} + ∫_{c+ε}^b).

The PV may exist when the ordinary improper integral does not (e.g. ∫_{-1}^1 dx/x has PV=0 but ordinary improper diverges).

**ELI5**

- By approaching the bad point or infinity symmetrically from both sides, positive and negative infinities (or oscillations) may cancel in a principal-value sense even if one-sided they do not settle.
- PV is a useful regularization in physics and Fourier analysis, but it is not the same as the Lebesgue or ordinary improper integral.

**Worked Example:**

PV ∫_{-1}^1 dx/x : the antiderivative ln|x|, from −R to −ε plus from ε to R gives (ln R − ln ε) + (ln R − ln ε) wait no: actually for symmetric: ln| R | − ln|ε| + ln R − ln ε wait properly: integral from -R to -ε is lnε - ln R (negative), from ε to R is ln R - ln ε, they cancel to 0 for any ε,R. Taking ε→0, R→∞ still 0. But the one-sided improper from 0+ to 1 diverges to +∞, from -1 to 0- to −∞.

## Common Mistakes

- **Assuming that if the antiderivative has a limit at infinity then the integral converges**: True only if the integrand is the derivative of that antiderivative and the fundamental theorem applies in the limit; counter-examples exist when f is not integrable but F has limits.
- **Confusing absolute and conditional convergence for improper integrals**: Absolute is safer for term-by-term operations; conditional requires more care (like alternating series).
- **Forgetting that existence of lim_{b→∞} F(b) − F(a) requires that F'(x) = f(x) and f integrable on every finite piece**: If f has non-integrable singularities the antiderivative may not be defined in the classical sense.
- **Thinking every improper integral that 'looks convergent' by comparison is Lebesgue integrable**: On the real line Riemann and Lebesgue improper integrals coincide for positive functions; differences appear for signed functions with conditional convergence.
- **Using the same variable for the limit and the dummy integration variable**: Always write lim_{b→∞} ∫_a^b f(x) dx with distinct variables.

The Riemann–Stieltjes integral generalizes the ordinary Riemann integral by replacing the "dx" (length element) with dα where α is a monotone integrator function.

When α(x) = x one recovers the Riemann integral. When α is a step function the integral reduces to a finite or infinite sum. It provides a unified language for both integrals and sums and is the natural setting for integration by parts in Stieltjes form.

## Definition via Riemann–Stieltjes Sums

<!-- section: definition-stieltjes-sums -->

Given a partition P of [a,b] and tags t_i in each subinterval, the Riemann–Stieltjes sum is ∑ f(t_i) (α(x_i) − α(x_{i−1})) .

f is Riemann–Stieltjes integrable with respect to α (written ∫ f dα) if there is a number I such that for every ε>0 there is a partition P_ε so that for any refinement and any choice of tags the sum is within ε of I.

When α is continuous and f continuous they are integrable wrt each other; when α has a jump the integral picks up f at the jump times the jump size (like a delta contribution).

**ELI5**

- Instead of adding f times width of slice, you add f times the change in the "weight function" α over the slice.
- If α is a staircase (jumps at certain points), the integral becomes a weighted sum of the f values at the jump locations, with weights the jump sizes.

**Worked Example:**

Let α(x) = 0 for x<0, α(x)=1 for x≥0 (Heaviside step on ℝ). Then ∫_{-1}^1 f dα = f(0) (the jump of α at 0), for continuous f. This is the "integral against a Dirac delta" in elementary language.

## Integration by Parts for Stieltjes Integrals

<!-- section: integration-by-parts-stieltjes -->

The formula ∫_a^b f dα + ∫_a^b α df = f(b)α(b) − f(a)α(a) holds under suitable conditions (when one integral exists the other does and the formula is true).

This is the Stieltjes version of the familiar ∫ u dv = u v − ∫ v du .

It is extremely useful for converting between sums and integrals or for moving derivatives from one factor to another.

**ELI5**

- The product f α "telescopes" its changes; the total change f(b)α(b) − f(a)α(a) equals the sum of "f changes α" plus "α changes f".
- This is the discrete product rule summed up, or the continuous product rule integrated.

**Worked Example:**

Take α(x) = floor(x) on [0,n]. Then ∫_0^n 1 dα = number of integers from 1 to n = n (jumps of size 1 at each integer). Integration by parts with f=1 recovers the same. More interestingly, summation by parts is exactly this formula when α is a step.

## Relation to Riemann and Lebesgue; When the Integral Exists

<!-- section: relation-riemann-lebesgue-existence -->

If α(x)=x the definition reduces exactly to the Riemann integral.

If α is absolutely continuous (α(x) = ∫^x g with g integrable) then ∫ f dα = ∫ f g dx (ordinary integral).

The integral ∫ f dα exists whenever f and α have no common discontinuities (one continuous where the other jumps).

**ELI5**

- Stieltjes with respect to x is ordinary area.
- Stieltjes with respect to a smooth increasing α is ordinary integral with weight α'.
- If f jumps exactly where α jumps the "product of jumps" creates an ambiguous contribution that prevents the integral from existing in the Riemann–Stieltjes sense.

**Worked Example:**

Let α be the Cantor function (continuous, constant on the complementary intervals of the Cantor set, increases from 0 to 1 on the Cantor set of measure zero). Then ∫ f dα is a "singular integral" that sees only the values of f on the Cantor set; it is not given by any ordinary density.

## Applications: Probability (CDFs), Summation by Parts, Spectral Theory

<!-- section: applications-probability-sums -->

- In probability, if F is the cumulative distribution function of a random variable, then E[g(X)] = ∫ g dF (Stieltjes integral). This unifies discrete (sum p_k g(x_k)), continuous (∫ g f dx), and singular distributions.
- Summation by parts is the discrete Stieltjes integration by parts.
- In spectral theory and functional analysis the spectral theorem is often stated with Stieltjes integrals against projection-valued measures.

**ELI5**

- The expectation of a function of a random variable is "integrate g against the probability mass" — when the distribution has atoms (jumps in the CDF) you get ordinary sums; when it has a density you get the familiar integral.
- This language lets probabilists and analysts treat discrete and continuous cases with the same formulas.

**Worked Example:**

For a discrete random variable taking values 1,2,3 with probabilities 1/2,1/3,1/6, the CDF F has jumps of those sizes at 1,2,3. Then ∫ x dF = 1·(1/2) + 2·(1/3) + 3·(1/6) = 1 + 2/3 + 1/2 = 13/6, the expectation.

## Common Mistakes

- **Assuming ∫ f dα always exists when f and α are both of bounded variation**: They must not share discontinuities.
- **Confusing dα with α' dx when α is not absolutely continuous**: The Cantor function has α'=0 almost everywhere yet increases; the Stieltjes integral is not the ordinary integral against 0.
- **Forgetting that the integrator α is usually taken monotone increasing**: The definition can be extended to α of bounded variation by splitting into positive and negative variations, but the basic theory is for monotone α.
- **Thinking the Riemann–Stieltjes integral is the same as the Lebesgue–Stieltjes integral**: They coincide when the integral exists in the Riemann–Stieltjes sense, but Lebesgue–Stieltjes is more general (completes with respect to the measure induced by α).
- **Writing ∫ f(x) dα(x) with the same variable on both**: The notation is conventional but the dummy variable in the differential is not the same as a free variable.
