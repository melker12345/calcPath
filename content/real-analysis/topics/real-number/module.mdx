# Real number

The real numbers are the numbers we use for measurement, geometry, and all of calculus. They include all the rationals together with irrationals such as $\sqrt{2}$, $\pi$, and $e$. Intuitively they are "all possible lengths" or "all possible points on the number line."

The decisive property that distinguishes the reals from the rationals is completeness: there are no "gaps."

## Definition and Basic Examples

<!-- section: definition -->

A real number can be thought of as any number that can be written as an infinite decimal $3.14159\ldots$, or as the limit of a sequence of rationals, or via a Dedekind cut. The set of all real numbers is denoted $\mathbb{R}$.

Examples: all integers, all fractions, $\sqrt{2}\approx 1.414213\ldots$ (which cannot be written as a ratio of integers), and every number whose decimal expansion eventually repeats or not.

**ELI5**

- Imagine the number line: rationals are points you can reach with a ruler marked in fractions; reals fill every possible point, even those you can never name exactly with a simple fraction.
- $\sqrt{2}$ is real because you can approximate it arbitrarily closely by decimals or fractions (1.4, 1.41, 1.414, …) and the "target" position is a definite place on the line.

**Worked Example:**

Is $0.999\ldots$ (infinite 9s) equal to 1? Yes, they are the same real number. The difference would be $0.000\ldots1$ with the 1 infinitely far to the right, which is zero. Thus $1 - 0.999\ldots = 0$, so they name the same point.

## The Reals as an Ordered Field

<!-- section: ordered-field -->

The real numbers form a field: you can add, subtract, multiply, and divide (except by zero) and the usual algebraic rules hold. They are also totally ordered: for any two reals exactly one of $x < y$, $x = y$, $x > y$ is true, and the order respects the operations (if $x < y$ then $x + z < y + z$, and if $0 < z$ then $xz < yz$).

These properties make $\mathbb{R}$ an ordered field. The rationals $\mathbb{Q}$ are also an ordered field, but they are not complete.

**ELI5**

- Field rules are the "calculator laws": $(x+y)+z = x+(y+z)$, $x\cdot(y+z)=xy+xz$, etc. Order rules say bigger plus anything stays bigger, and multiplying by a positive number preserves inequality.
- You can do all ordinary arithmetic on reals exactly as you do on fractions or decimals.

**Worked Example:**

Prove that if $x > 0$ then $1/x > 0$. Suppose not. If $1/x \le 0$, multiply both sides by $x$ (positive) to obtain $1 \le 0$, contradiction. Hence $1/x > 0$.

## Completeness of the Reals

<!-- section: completeness -->

The reals are complete: every nonempty subset that is bounded above has a least upper bound (supremum) that is itself a real number. Equivalently, every Cauchy sequence of reals converges to a real limit.

This is the property that "fills the gaps" left by the rationals. For example, the set $\{x \in \mathbb{Q} \mid x^2 < 2\}$ is bounded above in $\mathbb{Q}$ but has no least upper bound inside $\mathbb{Q}$; that least upper bound is the real number $\sqrt{2}$.

**ELI5**

- Completeness means you cannot fall through a crack in the number line; if a bunch of numbers get closer and closer together (Cauchy), they must be approaching some definite real number that is already there.
- The rationals have holes: you can have a sequence of rationals approaching $\sqrt{2}$ but $\sqrt{2}$ itself is not rational, so the "limit address" is missing until you enlarge the number system.

**Worked Example:**

Let $A = \{ x \in \mathbb{R} \mid x^2 < 2 \}$. This set is nonempty (contains 1) and bounded above (by 2). Its least upper bound is $\sqrt{2}$, which is real, and $\sqrt{2}$ is not in $A$ (since $(\sqrt{2})^2 = 2$ is not $<2$). Every rational upper bound can be improved.

## Common Mistakes

- **Thinking every real has a 'simple' expression**: Most reals are transcendental or at least irrational and have no finite description; we only ever work with approximations or symbolic names.
- **Confusing $\mathbb{Q}$ and $\mathbb{R}$ completeness**: The rationals are not complete; the set of rationals with square less than 2 has no rational least upper bound.
- **Assuming decimals are the definition**: Infinite decimals are one convenient representation, but they are not the only or the most rigorous one (0.999… = 1 shows the care needed).
- **Forgetting that reals are uncountable**: There are 'more' reals than rationals or naturals; you cannot list them all in a sequence.
- **Mixing up supremum and maximum**: A set may have a least upper bound that is not attained (not a maximum element of the set).
