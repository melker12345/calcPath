# Dominated convergence theorem

The dominated convergence theorem (DCT) is one of the most powerful and frequently invoked results in Lebesgue integration theory. It justifies passing limits inside integrals under a domination hypothesis that is often easy to check in practice.

It is the reason we can differentiate under the integral sign, interchange limits and expectations in probability, and prove many approximation and convergence results in analysis.

## Statement of the Theorem

<!-- section: statement-dct -->

Let (X, μ) be a measure space (e.g. ℝ with Lebesgue measure). Let f_n be a sequence of measurable functions such that f_n(x) → f(x) for almost every x, and suppose there exists an integrable function g (∫ |g| dμ < ∞) such that |f_n(x)| ≤ g(x) for almost every x and all n.

Then f is integrable and ∫ f_n dμ → ∫ f dμ .

Moreover, ∫ |f_n − f| dμ → 0 (convergence in L^1).

**ELI5**

- If the functions are all dominated in absolute value by a single integrable "umbrella" g, and they converge pointwise a.e., then their integrals converge to the integral of the limit, and the areas of the differences go to zero.
- The domination prevents the "mass" from escaping to infinity or oscillating too wildly.

**Worked Example:**

On ℝ with Lebesgue measure, let f_n(x) = n χ_{(0,1/n)}(x). Then f_n → 0 pointwise for x≠0, and at 0 it oscillates or whatever but single point doesn't matter. |f_n| ≤ 1 (for n≥1), but 1 is not integrable over ℝ. Indeed ∫ f_n =1 for all n, while ∫ lim =0, so limit and integral do not commute. There is no integrable dominating g on the whole line.

## Proof Sketch (Using Fatou and Monotone Convergence)

<!-- section: proof-sketch-fatou-monotone -->

Apply Fatou's lemma to the nonnegative functions g + f_n and g − f_n (or |g| + f_n etc.).

Since |f_n| ≤ g a.e., the functions g ± f_n are nonnegative for large n or after adjustment.

Fatou gives ∫ (g + f) ≤ liminf ∫ (g + f_n) = ∫ g + liminf ∫ f_n , which rearranges to ∫ f ≤ liminf ∫ f_n .

Applying the same to g − f_n yields the reverse inequality limsup ∫ f_n ≤ ∫ f .

Hence the limit exists and equals ∫ f.

For L^1 convergence one applies the same argument to |f_n − f| (dominated by 2g).

**ELI5**

- By adding and subtracting the dominator g you turn the signed f_n into nonnegative sequences to which Fatou applies.
- The resulting inequalities squeeze liminf and limsup of the integrals to be equal to the integral of the limit.

**Worked Example:**

To pass the limit inside ∫ e^{-x} sin(n x) / n dx or similar; here |e^{-x} sin(n x)/n | ≤ e^{-x} which is integrable on [0,∞), pointwise →0, hence integral →0 by DCT.

## Differentiation under the Integral Sign (Parametrized Integrals)

<!-- section: differentiation-under-integral -->

A typical application: consider I(α) = ∫_a^b f(x,α) dx . Under suitable domination on the partial derivative ∂f/∂α one may differentiate under the integral: I'(α) = ∫ ∂f/∂α (x,α) dx .

The domination hypothesis of DCT (applied to difference quotients) justifies the passage to the limit that defines the derivative.

**ELI5**

- If you have a family of functions parametrized by α, and the rate of change with respect to α is controlled by an integrable function independent of α (in a neighborhood), then you can compute the derivative of the total integral by integrating the partial derivative.
- This is the rigorous justification for many calculations in physics and probability (e.g. differentiating moment generating functions).

**Worked Example:**

Let I(α) = ∫_0^1 e^{α x} dx . Then I(α) = (e^α − 1)/α for α≠0. Differentiating under the sign: I'(α) = ∫_0^1 x e^{α x} dx , which can be computed directly and matches the derivative of the closed form.

## Relation to Other Convergence Theorems; When DCT Is Not Needed

<!-- section: relation-other-theorems -->

- Monotone convergence requires only monotonicity and nonnegativity; no domination needed (but the limit integral may be infinite).
- Fatou's lemma is a one-sided inequality that often serves as a stepping stone to DCT.
- Bounded convergence theorem is the special case of DCT on a finite-measure space with |f_n| ≤ M (constant, which is integrable when measure finite).

When the measure space is finite (e.g. probability space) and the f_n are uniformly bounded, DCT reduces to bounded convergence and is very easy to apply.

**ELI5**

- On a probability space, if your random variables are bounded by a fixed constant (or by an integrable random variable), you can pass limits inside expectations freely.
- Monotone convergence is the tool for increasing sequences without needing an a-priori bound.

**Worked Example:**

On [0,1] with Lebesgue, f_n(x) = n x (1−x)^n or bounded by 1 sequences converging pointwise; bounded convergence gives ∫ f_n → ∫ f with almost no work.

## Common Mistakes

- **Forgetting to verify the domination hypothesis**: Pointwise convergence alone is not enough (escaping mass counter-examples).
- **Using a dominator that depends on n**: The g must work for all n simultaneously.
- **Applying DCT on an infinite-measure space with a constant bound**: A constant is not integrable over ℝ; one needs decay (e.g. e^{-|x|}).
- **Confusing almost-everywhere convergence with everywhere**: The theorem only requires a.e. convergence and a.e. domination; values on a null set can be redefined arbitrarily.
- **Thinking DCT implies L^1 convergence of f_n to f**: It does, under the stated hypotheses (∫ |f_n − f| →0). This is sometimes called "dominated convergence in L^1".
