# Quantization (signal processing)

Quantization is the irreversible mapping of a continuous-amplitude signal (or a high-precision discrete signal) onto a finite set of representation levels. It is the fundamental lossy step in every digital acquisition system: A/D converters, image sensors, audio codecs, and learned compressors all rely on it.

While quantization always introduces error, careful design minimizes the perceived or measured distortion for a given number of bits.

## Scalar Quantization

<!-- section: scalar-quantization -->

A scalar quantizer Q maps a real number x to the nearest (or otherwise chosen) reproduction level \hat{x}_i from a codebook of size 2^b (for b-bit quantizer). The decision regions (Voronoi cells) partition the real line.

A uniform quantizer spaces levels equally with step size Δ; a non-uniform quantizer places levels more densely where the source density is high or where distortion is more costly.

The quantization error e = x - Q(x) is usually modeled as additive noise, approximately uniform over [-Δ/2, Δ/2] for fine uniform quantization and high-resolution sources.

**ELI5**

- Think of rounding a real number to the closest value on a ruler with only 256 marks. The marks are the representation levels; everything between two marks gets snapped to one or the other.
- Uniform spacing is like a regular ruler; non-uniform spacing crowds more marks where the signal spends most time (e.g., near zero for speech).

**Worked Example:**

An 8-bit uniform quantizer for audio with peak-to-peak range 2 V (from -1 V to +1 V) has Δ = 2/256 ≈ 0.0078 V. A sample x=0.123 V is quantized to the level closest to it, say 0.121 V or whatever the grid gives; error |e| ≤ Δ/2.

## Optimal Quantizer Design (Lloyd-Max)

<!-- section: lloyd-max -->

The Lloyd-Max conditions for a minimum-MSE quantizer are:

1. Each reproduction level \hat{x}_i is the centroid (conditional mean) of its decision region: \hat{x}_i = E[X | X ∈ V_i].

2. Each decision boundary between \hat{x}_i and \hat{x}_{i+1} lies at the midpoint (for MSE): ( \hat{x}_i + \hat{x}_{i+1} )/2 .

These yield an iterative algorithm: start with some levels, assign points to nearest, update levels to centroids, repeat until convergence. For many common densities (Gaussian, Laplacian) the solution is tabulated or can be computed numerically.

For non-MSE distortion measures the conditions generalize (e.g., median for absolute error).

**ELI5**

- The best place to put the reconstruction value for a bin is the average of all the values that fall into that bin (the centroid).
- The best place to draw the fence between two bins is right in the middle between their two reconstruction values.

**Worked Example:**

For a standard normal source and 2-bit (4-level) Lloyd-Max quantizer, the optimal levels are approximately ±0.4528, ±1.510, with boundaries at 0 and ±0.9816 (symmetric). The resulting MSE is about 0.117, better than a uniform quantizer's 0.125 or so.

## Performance and High-Rate Approximations

<!-- section: performance-highrate -->

For high rate (small Δ), the signal-to-quantization-noise ratio (SQNR) for a uniform quantizer on a signal with variance σ² and peak factor is approximately

SQNR ≈ 6.02 b + 10 log_{10}(σ² / (Δ²/12)) dB,

where b is bits. Each extra bit buys roughly 6 dB improvement.

Companding (compressor + uniform quantizer + expander) approximates the optimal non-uniform quantizer for a given density; μ-law and A-law are classic examples in telephony.

Vector quantization (VQ) jointly quantizes blocks and can approach the rate-distortion bound more closely than scalar methods, at the price of complexity (see separate topic).

**ELI5**

- Every extra bit of precision you buy halves the step size and therefore quarters the average squared error — a 6 dB SNR gain.
- For signals that are not uniformly distributed you get free improvement by spending your bits where the signal actually lives most of the time.

**Worked Example:**

A 16-bit uniform quantizer on a full-scale sine wave achieves theoretical SQNR around 98 dB (6.02*16 + 1.76 ≈ 98). Real ADCs fall a few dB short because of thermal noise, jitter, and non-idealities.

## Common Mistakes

- **Modeling quantization noise as white and signal-independent without checking**: At low rates or with correlated inputs the error is highly structured and can be signal-dependent.
- **Using uniform quantization on highly peaked sources**: You waste levels in the tails; a properly designed non-uniform or companded quantizer uses far fewer bits for the same MSE.
- **Confusing number of levels 2^b with the rate in bits after entropy coding**: If the quantizer outputs are highly nonuniform you can entropy-code the indices and achieve rate close to the entropy of the quantized symbols, which may be << b.
- **Assuming Lloyd-Max gives the global optimum**: The iteration finds a locally optimal quantizer; different initializations can yield different performance; for scalar quantizers it is usually fine.
- **Forgetting overload distortion**: If the source exceeds the outermost decision boundaries the error is huge; good designs balance granular and overload regions.
- **Thinking scalar quantization is always far from R(D)**: For many sources at high rate the gap is only about 1.53 dB (πe/6 factor) between scalar and the ultimate vector rate-distortion limit; entropy-coded scalar quantization closes most of the gap.
