# Secret sharing

Secret sharing is a cryptographic primitive that distributes a secret value s among a set of participants so that only certain authorized subsets of participants (the access structure) can reconstruct s, while any unauthorized subset learns absolutely nothing about s (perfect security) or only negligible information (statistical/computational security).

The canonical example is threshold secret sharing: any k out of n participants can recover the secret, but any k-1 or fewer learn nothing. This is information-theoretically secure and does not rely on computational hardness assumptions.

## Threshold Secret Sharing

<!-- section: threshold-ss -->

In a (k,n)-threshold scheme a dealer splits secret s into n shares s1,...,sn such that:

- Any set of k or more shares determines s uniquely.
- Any set of k-1 or fewer shares reveals no information about s (the distribution of the shares is independent of s).

The scheme is perfect if the 'no information' condition is information-theoretic (the mutual information I(s; any k-1 shares) = 0).

Each share must be at least as large as the secret itself (in bits) for perfect threshold schemes; this is an information-theoretic lower bound.

**ELI5**

- Imagine a combination to a safe. You want any 3 out of 5 bank managers to be able to open it together, but any 2 should learn nothing that helps them guess the combination.
- The dealer gives each manager a 'piece' of the combination. Only when 3 pieces are brought together do they fit into the full combination; any 2 pieces look like random garbage unrelated to the real combination.

**Worked Example:**

1. A $(2,3)$ scheme for a 128-bit secret. The dealer picks a random 128-bit $r$, gives share1 = $r$, share2 = $s \oplus r$, share3 = $s$.
2. Any single share is uniformly random (independent of $s$). Any two shares determine $s$: share1+share2 $\to s$, share1+share3 $\to s$ (share3 = $s$), share2+share3 $\to s$.
3. But share1 and share2 together give $r$ and $s\oplus r$, hence $s$, as required.

## Shamir's Polynomial Scheme

<!-- section: shamir -->

Shamir's (k,n) scheme works over a finite field F_q with q > n and secret s ∈ F_q.

The dealer chooses a random polynomial p(x) of degree at most k-1 with p(0) = s (the constant term). The n shares are the points (i, p(i)) for i=1 to n (distinct nonzero field elements).

Any k points determine a unique degree-(k-1) polynomial (Lagrange interpolation), hence determine p(0)=s.

Any k-1 points are consistent with exactly q possible degree-(k-1) polynomials (one for each possible value of the missing coefficient that would set p(0) to any field element); thus the secret is completely undetermined.

**ELI5**

- The secret is the y-intercept of a random polynomial of degree k-1. Each share is one point on that hidden curve.
- k points uniquely determine the whole curve (and therefore where it crosses the y-axis). k-1 points can be extended to a curve that crosses the y-axis at any value you like, so they give no clue.

**Worked Example:**

1. $(k=3, n=5)$, field $F_{17}$, secret $s=7$. Dealer picks random $a_1=3$, $a_2=12$; $p(x) = 7 + 3x + 12 x^2 \mod 17$.
2. Shares: $p(1)=7+3+12=22 \mod 17=5$.
3. $p(2)=7+6+48 \mod 17=7+6+14=27 \mod 17=10$.
4. $p(3)=7+9+108 \mod 17=7+9+6=22 \mod 17=5$ (calc: $12*9=108$, $108/17=6*17=102$, $108-102=6$).
5. Any 3 of these points interpolated recover the unique quadratic with $p(0)=7$.

## Properties, Extensions, and Impossibility Results

<!-- section: properties-extensions -->

Share size lower bound: in a perfect (k,n) scheme over a secret of entropy H, each share has entropy at least H (otherwise the k-1 shares plus the missing one could not uniquely determine a high-entropy secret while k-1 give zero info).

Linear secret sharing schemes (shares are linear functions of the secret and randomness) are equivalent to monotone span programs and have efficient realizations for many access structures.

General access structures (not just thresholds) can be realized by realizing the minimal authorized sets and using monotone formulas or by the Benaloh-Leichter construction.

Verifiable secret sharing (VSS) adds the ability for participants to check that their shares are consistent with some secret (protects against a cheating dealer). Proactive secret sharing periodically refreshes shares so that an adversary who compromises shares over time still cannot collect k at any epoch.

Multi-secret and ramp schemes trade off reconstruction threshold vs privacy for multiple secrets or partial leakage.

**ELI5**

- You cannot make shares smaller than the secret in the perfect information-theoretic setting; information has to 'flow' to the qualified sets.
- For general who-can-reconstruct rules (e.g., 'any two managers plus the CEO, or any four managers') there are systematic ways to build the sharing from the simple threshold building blocks or from linear algebra over vector spaces.

**Worked Example:**

1. The lower bound proof: fix an unauthorized set $A$ of size $k-1$. Their shares are independent of $s$ (by privacy).
2. For a qualified set $B = A \cup \{p\}$, the share of $p$ plus the shares of $A$ must determine $s$.
3. Since $A$ gives no info, the share of $p$ must carry all the entropy of $s$; hence $| \text{share}_p | \geq H(s)$.

## Common Mistakes

- **Thinking that in Shamir's scheme the polynomial is evaluated at the participant identities only**: The x-coordinates are public identifiers; the y-values (or the pair) are the actual shares that must be kept secret.
- **Believing that k-1 shares give 'some' partial information about s**: In a perfect scheme they give zero information; the posterior on s is identical to the prior (uniform).
- **Using a small field for a large secret**: Shamir is usually applied to field elements; for a long secret one either uses a large field or shares the secret bit-by-bit / block-by-block with independent polynomials.
- **Forgetting that the dealer must be trusted to choose the polynomial correctly**: If the dealer is malicious he can give inconsistent shares. Verifiable secret sharing solves this.
- **Assuming computational secret sharing (e.g., using one-way functions) is the same as information-theoretic**: Computational schemes can have shorter shares but rely on hardness assumptions and may be broken by future algorithms or quantum computers.
- **Confusing secret sharing with threshold cryptography or multiparty computation**: Secret sharing is the distribution step; threshold crypto uses shares of a key to perform operations without reconstructing the key in the clear; MPC is the broader interactive protocol setting.
