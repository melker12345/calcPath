# Mutual information

Mutual information I(X;Y) is the most important derived quantity in information theory after entropy. It quantifies how much knowing one variable reduces uncertainty about the other — the strength of the statistical relationship in information units (bits).

It is symmetric, non-negative, and zero exactly when the variables are independent. It is the currency of communication: channel capacity is the maximum mutual information between input and output.

## Definition via Entropies

<!-- section: definition -->

Mutual information can be defined in several equivalent ways:

$I(X;Y) = H(X) - H(X|Y) = H(Y) - H(Y|X) = H(X) + H(Y) - H(X,Y).$

The last form shows it is the difference between the sum of marginal entropies and the joint entropy.

**ELI5**

- I(X;Y) is "how many bits of X's uncertainty are resolved by learning Y."
- It is also "how much smaller the joint description is compared with describing X and Y separately."

**Worked Example:**

X and Y identical fair bits. H(X)=1, H(Y)=1, H(X,Y)=1. Thus I(X;Y) = 1 + 1 - 1 = 1 bit. They share everything.

## Interpretation as Information Transfer

<!-- section: interpretation -->

I(X;Y) = 0 ⇔ X ⊥ Y (independent).

I(X;Y) = H(X) when Y determines X perfectly (H(X|Y)=0).

In a communication setting, X is the message, Y the received signal; I(X;Y) is the average information successfully transferred.

**ELI5**

- Zero mutual information means "Y tells you nothing about X on average" — like two separate fair coins.
- Maximum mutual information (equal to H(X)) means "Y tells you everything about X" — a perfect copy or noiseless observation.

**Worked Example:**

X uniform on 8 symbols (H(X)=3). After a noisy channel the receiver's uncertainty H(X|Y)=0.8 bits on average. Then I(X;Y)=3-0.8=2.2 bits were conveyed on average per transmission.

## Properties and Special Cases

<!-- section: properties-special -->

- Non-negativity: I(X;Y) ≥ 0.
- Symmetry: I(X;Y)=I(Y;X).
- Data processing inequality: if X—Y—Z Markov then I(X;Z) ≤ I(X;Y) ≤ min(H(X),H(Y)).
- I(X;X) = H(X) (self-information of the variable).
- For independent X,Y: I(X;Y)=0; for deterministic relation: I(X;Y)=H(X) when Y=f(X) bijectively.

**ELI5**

- Processing a signal can only lose or preserve information about the original; it cannot create more.
- Mutual information between a variable and itself is just its entropy.

**Worked Example:**

X fair bit, Z = X (perfect), Y any function of X. Then I(X;Y) = I(X;Z) = 1, illustrating the DPI chain.

## Common Mistakes

- **Thinking mutual information can be negative**: It cannot; I(X;Y) ≥ 0 always, with equality for independence.
- **Confusing I(X;Y) with H(X,Y) or conditional entropy**: I is the *shared* part; H joint is total, conditional is the leftover.
- **Assuming I(X;Y) = 0 implies no dependence of any kind**: It implies statistical independence; functional or higher-order dependence that averages to zero info is still possible in other senses but rare.
- **Forgetting symmetry**: Although defined asymmetrically via H(X)-H(X|Y), the value is always equal to H(Y)-H(Y|X).
- **Using I(X;Y) when the right quantity is I(X;Y|Z)**: Conditional mutual information is needed when there is a third variable (common cause, etc.).
- **Believing large I(X;Y) implies causation**: Mutual information is purely correlational/statistical; it says nothing about direction or mechanism.
