# Relative entropy

Relative entropy, also known as Kullback-Leibler (KL) divergence, is a fundamental non-symmetric measure of how one probability distribution p differs from another reference distribution q. It is not a distance (it fails symmetry and the triangle inequality) but it is the right notion of "divergence" for many information-theoretic and statistical purposes.

It appears in the definition of mutual information, in large-deviations theory, in variational inference, and as the natural loss for comparing distributions.

## Definition of KL Divergence

<!-- section: definition -->

For discrete distributions p and q (with p absolutely continuous w.r.t. q),

$D_{KL}(p \| q) = \sum_x p(x) \log_2 \frac{p(x)}{q(x)} = \mathbb{E}_{x\sim p}\Bigl[\log_2 \frac{p(x)}{q(x)}\Bigr].$

When the support of p is not contained in that of q the divergence is defined to be +∞.

**ELI5**

- Imagine you designed a compression code assuming the world follows q. If the world actually follows p, on average you waste D(p||q) extra bits per symbol.
- It is the penalty you pay for using the wrong model.

**Worked Example:**

Let p be Bernoulli(0.9), q Bernoulli(0.5). Then

D(p||q) = 0.9 log₂(0.9/0.5) + 0.1 log₂(0.1/0.5) ≈ 0.531 bits.

Using the fair-coin code lengths on biased-coin data wastes about half a bit per flip on average.

## Interpretation as Surprise or Inefficiency

<!-- section: interpretation -->

D(p||q) can be rewritten as H(p,q) - H(p) where H(p,q) is the cross-entropy -sum p log q.

Thus D(p||q) = cross-entropy - entropy = "extra nats/bits when coding with q instead of the true p-optimal code."

It is also the expected log-likelihood ratio under p.

**ELI5**

- Cross-entropy is the average number of bits you actually use when your code lengths are -log q. Subtract the true entropy and you get the waste.
- In hypothesis testing it is the expected log odds in favor of the true hypothesis when data are from p.

**Worked Example:**

Suppose true p has entropy 0.469 bits (the biased coin). The cross-entropy when using q=0.5 is 1 bit (because -log2(0.5)=1 for every outcome). Waste = 1 - 0.469 = 0.531 bits, matching D(p||q).

## Properties and Use in Information Theory

<!-- section: properties-uses -->

- D(p||q) ≥ 0, =0 iff p=q a.e.
- Not symmetric: D(p||q) ≠ D(q||p) in general.
- Joint convexity in the pair (p,q).
- I(X;Y) = D( p(x,y) || p(x)p(y) ), i.e., mutual information is the KL between the joint and the product of marginals.
- In rate-distortion and channel coding, many optimal quantities are characterized via KL minimizations or projections.

**ELI5**

- The "distance" from p to q is different from q to p because the expectation is taken under different measures.
- Mutual information is exactly how far the joint is from independence (the product measure) in KL.

**Worked Example:**

For any X,Y we have I(X;Y) = D(p_{X,Y} || p_X p_Y). When X,Y independent the joint equals the product and I=0. When they are dependent the joint "sticks out" from the independence assumption by exactly I bits on average.

## Common Mistakes

- **Treating KL as a symmetric distance**: D(p||q) is directed; the "cost" of approximating p by q is not the same as approximating q by p.
- **Ignoring infinite KL when supports mismatch**: If your model q assigns zero probability to something that can happen under p, D(p||q)=∞ and maximum-likelihood will be ill-behaved.
- **Using KL when total variation or other distances would be more natural**: KL is sensitive to tails and has information-theoretic meaning; sometimes Hellinger or TV distance is preferable for pure approximation.
- **Confusing D(p||q) with H(p) - H(q)**: The difference of entropies is unrelated in general; KL involves the cross term.
- **Forgetting that the expectation is under p**: The weights are p(x), not q(x); this is why it is not symmetric.
- **Applying the discrete formula directly to continuous densities without care**: For continuous variables the KL involves the densities and can be negative for differential entropy terms, but the divergence itself remains non-negative.
