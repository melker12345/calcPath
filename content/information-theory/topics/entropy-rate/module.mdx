# Entropy rate

For a single random variable, entropy tells the uncertainty per draw. For a stochastic process that generates a sequence X₁, X₂, X₃, … the right quantity is the entropy *rate* — the average information per symbol in the long run.

This is the quantity that actually governs the compressibility of long files, the capacity of channels with memory, and the fundamental limits for sources with temporal structure (speech, video, text, DNA).

## Definition of Entropy Rate

<!-- section: definition -->

The entropy rate of a discrete-time stochastic process is defined (when the limit exists) as

$h = \lim_{n\to\infty} \frac{1}{n} H(X_1, X_2, \dots , X_n).$

For stationary processes an equivalent expression is

$h = \lim_{n\to\infty} H(X_n | X_1, \dots, X_{n-1}).$

**ELI5**

- Imagine writing a very long book. The total number of bits needed to describe the whole book divided by the number of letters (or words) approaches the entropy rate.
- Early symbols may be surprising; after many symbols the next one becomes more predictable on average.

**Worked Example:**

An i.i.d. source with per-symbol entropy H=2 bits has, for any block length n,

(1/n) H(X₁..Xₙ) = 2 exactly. Hence the rate h=2 bits per symbol.

## Properties for Different Processes

<!-- section: properties -->

- i.i.d.: rate = single-symbol entropy H(X).
- Stationary Markov of order k: rate = H(X_{n} | X_{n-k} … X_{n-1}), the entropy of the next symbol given the last k symbols.
- The rate is always ≤ the marginal entropy H(X_i).
- For ergodic stationary processes the limit exists and equals the almost-sure limit of (1/n) log (1/p(X₁..Xₙ)) for typical sequences (AEP link).

**ELI5**

- Memoryless sources have rate equal to ordinary entropy.
- Sources with strong memory (repetitive patterns, grammar) have much lower rate than their single-symbol entropy suggests.

**Worked Example:**

A binary Markov chain that stays in the current state with probability 0.9 (rare flips). The stationary marginal entropy is nearly 1 bit, but H(next | current) ≈ 0.47 bits (same calc as biased coin). The entropy rate is therefore ≈0.47 bits/symbol, not 1.

## Relation to Compression and AEP

<!-- section: asymptotic -->

The entropy rate is the fundamental limit for lossless compression of long sequences from the process: you can compress at rates arbitrarily close to h bits per symbol but not below, asymptotically.

It is also the quantity appearing in the asymptotic equipartition property for processes: typical long sequences have probability roughly 2^{-n h}.

**ELI5**

- A good compressor for English text will achieve roughly the entropy rate (in bits per character) on long documents, not the naïve per-letter entropy.
- The AEP says that almost all long realizations look "typical" and are describable with about n h bits.

**Worked Example:**

A file of 1 000 000 symbols from a process with rate 1.2 bits/symbol can be losslessly compressed, in the limit of good coding, to roughly 1.2 million bits (150 kB). No scheme can do substantially better for typical files.

## Common Mistakes

- **Using the single-symbol entropy for a source with memory**: This overestimates the true compressibility; the rate is the correct number.
- **Forgetting that the limit may not exist**: Some contrived non-stationary processes have no rate; stationarity + ergodicity guarantees existence.
- **Confusing entropy rate with the entropy of the stationary distribution**: The stationary dist gives marginal entropy; the rate accounts for the transition structure.
- **Thinking (1/n)H(X₁..Xn) is always decreasing**: It is non-increasing, but the conditional form H(X_n|past) may not be monotone.
- **Applying the i.i.d. formula when symbols are dependent**: Real sources almost always have dependence; you must model the conditional distributions.
- **Neglecting that rate is an asymptotic concept**: For short sequences the effective rate (1/n)H(block) can be noticeably higher than the limit.
