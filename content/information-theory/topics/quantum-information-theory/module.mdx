# Quantum information theory

Quantum information theory studies the storage, processing, and transmission of information carried by quantum systems (qubits, photons, etc.). It replaces classical bits and Shannon entropy with qubits and von Neumann entropy, and reveals phenomena with no classical analogue: superposition, entanglement, no-cloning, and quantum teleportation.

While a full treatment requires quantum mechanics, the information-theoretic questions (compression, channel capacity, cryptography) have elegant and often surprising answers that are driving quantum computing and quantum communication research.

## Qubits and Quantum States

<!-- section: qubits-states -->

A classical bit is 0 or 1. A qubit is a unit vector in a 2-dimensional complex Hilbert space: |ψ⟩ = α|0⟩ + β|1⟩ with |α|² + |β|² = 1. Upon measurement in the computational basis the qubit collapses to 0 with probability |α|² or 1 with |β|².

A general quantum state is a density operator ρ (positive semidefinite, trace 1). Pure states have rank 1 (ρ = |ψ⟩⟨ψ|); mixed states are probabilistic mixtures of pure states.

The no-cloning theorem states that there is no unitary operation that can copy an unknown quantum state: you cannot make a perfect copy of an arbitrary qubit.

**ELI5**

- A qubit can be 'both 0 and 1 at the same time' until you look; the amplitudes α, β are like probabilities but with phases that let interference happen.
- Measurement forces a definite answer and destroys the superposition. You cannot duplicate the qubit without already knowing what it is.

**Worked Example:**

The state (|0⟩ + |1⟩)/√2 is an equal superposition. Measuring it yields 0 or 1 each with probability 1/2, and after measurement the state is the corresponding basis vector. Trying to 'clone' it would require a machine that outputs two copies of the same unknown superposition — impossible by linearity of quantum mechanics.

## Von Neumann Entropy and Quantum Information Measures

<!-- section: von-neumann -->

The von Neumann entropy of a density operator ρ is

S(ρ) = - Tr(ρ log₂ ρ)

It generalizes Shannon entropy: for a state diagonal in some basis with eigenvalues p_i it reduces to the classical H(p).

Quantum mutual information, conditional entropy, and relative entropy (quantum KL) are defined analogously using S(·). Quantum conditional entropy S(A|B) can be negative — a signature of entanglement.

The quantum relative entropy D(ρ||σ) = Tr(ρ log ρ - ρ log σ) is the basis for many quantum information bounds and is monotonic under completely positive trace-preserving maps.

**ELI5**

- S(ρ) measures the 'quantum uncertainty' or mixedness of the state. A pure state has S=0; a maximally mixed qubit has S=1.
- Negative conditional entropy means the two systems are so correlated (entangled) that knowing one 'creates' certainty about the other beyond what classical correlation allows.

**Worked Example:**

A Bell pair (maximally entangled two qubits) has joint state with S(AB)=0 (pure), while each reduced state is maximally mixed S(A)=S(B)=1. Thus S(A|B) = S(AB) - S(B) = -1 < 0.

## Quantum Channels and the Holevo Bound

<!-- section: quantum-channels-holevo -->

A quantum channel is a completely positive trace-preserving (CPTP) map Φ from density operators on one space to another. It can describe noisy evolution, measurement, or transmission.

The classical capacity of a quantum channel (rate at which classical bits can be sent reliably) is given by the Holevo quantity maximized over input ensembles: χ = S( ∑ p_x Φ(ρ_x) ) - ∑ p_x S( Φ(ρ_x) ). The Holevo bound says that the accessible information from a quantum measurement is at most χ.

Unlike classical channels, the capacity of quantum channels can be super-additive; regularization (n→∞ uses) is often needed and is hard to compute.

**ELI5**

- A quantum channel can turn your carefully prepared qubits into a noisy mess; the Holevo quantity tells you the best 'classical information' you can hope to extract after the noise.
- You cannot beat the Holevo bound by any fancy measurement at the receiver, even a joint measurement on many qubits.

**Worked Example:**

The depolarizing channel on a qubit with parameter p replaces the state with the maximally mixed state with probability p. Its Holevo capacity (and classical capacity) can be computed; for small p it is approximately 1 - h_2(p/2) or similar expressions derived from the eigenvalues of the output states.

## Entanglement, Teleportation, and Quantum Capacity

<!-- section: entanglement-teleportation -->

Entanglement is the resource that enables quantum teleportation: using a shared Bell pair and two classical bits, an unknown qubit can be transferred perfectly from Alice to Bob without sending the qubit itself through the quantum channel.

The quantum capacity Q of a channel is the rate at which qubits (or entanglement) can be transmitted reliably. It is given by a regularized coherent information, and is zero for some channels that still have positive classical capacity (e.g., entanglement-breaking channels).

Quantum error-correcting codes, stabilizer codes, and surface codes protect quantum information from noise, analogous to classical ECC but with the added difficulty of the no-cloning theorem (you cannot simply copy for repetition).

**ELI5**

- Teleportation: Alice and Bob share an entangled pair beforehand. Alice measures her unknown qubit together with her half of the pair, sends two classical bits of the measurement outcome to Bob, and Bob's half of the pair 'becomes' the unknown qubit (up to a Pauli correction he applies from the two bits).
- No quantum information traveled in the two classical bits; the entanglement 'pre-delivered' the quantum channel.

**Worked Example:**

To teleport one qubit, Alice performs a Bell-basis measurement on (unknown qubit + her entangled qubit). The four possible outcomes each occur with prob 1/4 and tell Bob which of I, X, Z, or XZ to apply to his qubit. After correction Bob holds an exact copy of the original unknown state; Alice's original is destroyed (consistent with no-cloning).

## Common Mistakes

- **Thinking a qubit is just a probabilistic classical bit**: The phases and interference matter; measurement in the wrong basis yields no information and collapses the state.
- **Assuming von Neumann entropy is always non-negative like Shannon entropy**: Conditional von Neumann entropy can be negative, quantifying entanglement.
- **Believing the Holevo quantity is the quantum capacity**: Holevo/χ gives the classical capacity of a quantum channel; the quantum capacity (sending qubits) is given by coherent information and is usually smaller.
- **Forgetting that entanglement is a resource that can be consumed and distilled**: Teleportation, super-dense coding, and many protocols consume ebits (Bell pairs) and classical communication.
- **Expecting all quantum channels to have additive capacities**: Many capacities require regularization over many uses; this is one of the hardest open problems in quantum information.
- **Confusing 'sending classical information over quantum channel' with 'sending quantum information'**: The former can use measurements and classical post-processing; the latter must preserve superpositions and entanglement.
