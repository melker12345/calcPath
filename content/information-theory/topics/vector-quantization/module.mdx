# Vector quantization

Vector quantization (VQ) replaces a k-dimensional input vector with the nearest codevector from a finite codebook of size 2^{kR}. By quantizing several samples together it exploits statistical dependencies (correlation, linear or nonlinear) that scalar quantization ignores, yielding lower distortion for the same rate (or lower rate for the same distortion).

As dimension k → ∞, the best VQ performance approaches the rate-distortion bound R(D) for the source; scalar quantization generally cannot.

## Why Vector Quantization Beats Scalar

<!-- section: why-vector -->

A scalar quantizer makes independent decisions on each coordinate. When the source components are correlated, the joint distribution is concentrated on a lower-dimensional manifold; a vector quantizer can place its codepoints along that manifold and achieve better packing.

Even for independent components, VQ can shape the quantization noise spectrum or use lattice structures that have better normalized second moment than the cubic lattice of independent scalar quantizers.

The famous result is that the ultimate gain of VQ over entropy-coded scalar quantization is bounded (about 1.53 dB for high-rate MSE Gaussian), but the gain over plain scalar (without entropy coding of indices) can be much larger.

**ELI5**

- Instead of rounding each pixel or sample by itself, you look at a small block of them together. The block might look like 'a smooth gradient' or 'an edge'; you have a dictionary of typical blocks and pick the closest one.
- Correlations mean many combinations of scalar values never occur; VQ wastes no codewords on impossible combinations.

**Worked Example:**

Two correlated zero-mean unit-variance Gaussians with correlation ρ=0.9. Scalar 1-bit quantizers on each give MSE ≈ 0.36 per component (total 0.72). A 2-dimensional VQ with 4 codewords (rate 1 bit per sample) can place points at (±a,±a) with appropriate a and achieve noticeably lower joint MSE by aligning with the principal axis of the data cloud.

## Design: The LBG / Generalized Lloyd Algorithm

<!-- section: lbg-design -->

The Linde-Buzo-Gray (LBG) algorithm is the vector generalization of Lloyd-Max iteration:

1. Start with an initial codebook (e.g., random or from a training set split).
2. Assign each training vector to the nearest codevector (Voronoi partition).
3. Replace each codevector by the centroid of the vectors assigned to it.
4. Repeat until the codebook (or distortion) stabilizes.

It is exactly k-means clustering with the Euclidean (or other) distortion. Good initialization (splitting, pairwise nearest neighbor) and multiple restarts help avoid poor local minima. For very large codebooks or dimensions, tree-structured VQ, product VQ, or lattice VQ are used as structured approximations.

**ELI5**

- Show the algorithm thousands of real example blocks from your data. Repeatedly: 'group the blocks by which dictionary entry they are closest to' then 'move each dictionary entry to the average of its group'.
- After many iterations the dictionary entries sit at the centers of the natural clusters in your data.

**Worked Example:**

Training a 256-codevector VQ (8 bits) on 4×4 image blocks (k=16, rate 0.5 bit/pixel). After LBG the codebook contains typical 4×4 patches: flat areas, edges at various angles, textures. Encoding a new block means finding the closest of the 256 patches (by MSE) and sending its 8-bit index.

## Performance, Complexity, and Structured VQ

<!-- section: performance-complexity -->

Full-search VQ encoding requires 2^{kR} distance computations per vector — exponential in dimension or rate, quickly impractical. Structured techniques (lattice quantization, trellis-coded quantization, multi-stage VQ, product codes) trade a small performance loss for polynomial complexity.

In practice, VQ appears inside hybrid codecs: transform coding + scalar quantization of coefficients + entropy coding often beats naive VQ because the transform approximately decorrelates and the entropy coder captures the remaining nonuniformity. Modern neural compressors learn both the transform and the 'quantization' (via soft or straight-through estimators) jointly.

**ELI5**

- Brute-force VQ is like having a huge photo album and for every new photo finding the most similar page by eye — works great but you cannot flip through millions of pages in real time.
- Clever structures (lattices, trees, stages) let you narrow the search quickly with only a small quality penalty.

**Worked Example:**

A lattice quantizer based on the hexagonal lattice in 2-D has about 0.17 dB better granular gain than the square (scalar) lattice for high-rate 2-D quantization. In higher dimensions the best known lattices (Leech, etc.) give several tenths of a dB improvement and admit fast encoding algorithms.

## Common Mistakes

- **Expecting VQ to always be used directly on raw samples**: Almost never; a linear or nonlinear transform (DCT, wavelet, autoencoder) is applied first so that the vector quantizer sees a more favorable distribution.
- **Confusing codebook size 2^{kR} with search complexity**: Even with a moderate codebook, naive search is expensive; fast search, tree search, or structured codebooks are mandatory for real-time use.
- **Thinking the LBG algorithm guarantees the globally optimal codebook**: It is a descent method that reaches a local minimum; initialization matters.
- **Ignoring the rate needed to describe the codebook itself**: For small data sets or one-shot compression the bits to send the codebook can dominate; universal or lattice quantizers avoid this.
- **Believing VQ always outperforms transform + scalar + entropy at the same total rate**: Well-designed scalar quantization after a good transform plus arithmetic coding of the indices is often competitive and far simpler.
- **Forgetting that in learned compression 'quantization' is often replaced by additive uniform noise during training**: This makes the whole pipeline differentiable so gradients can flow through the 'quantizer'.
