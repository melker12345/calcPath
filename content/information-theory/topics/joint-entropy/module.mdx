# Joint entropy

Joint entropy H(X,Y) extends the notion of entropy from a single random variable to a pair (or more generally a vector) of random variables. It quantifies the total uncertainty in the combined outcome of X and Y together.

It is the natural starting point for discussing how two variables interact information-theoretically.

## Joint Entropy Definition

<!-- section: definition -->

Let (X,Y) be a pair of discrete random variables with joint probability mass function p(x,y). Then

$H(X,Y) = -\sum_{x,y} p(x,y) \log_2 p(x,y).$

It is simply the ordinary entropy of the "big" random variable whose possible values are all pairs (x,y) that have positive probability.

**ELI5**

- Think of (X,Y) as one bigger roll of the dice. H(X,Y) tells the average surprise when you see both numbers at once.
- If X is the first card and Y the second card drawn without replacement, H(X,Y) measures uncertainty about the ordered pair.

**Worked Example:**

Two independent fair bits. There are four equally likely pairs: (0,0), (0,1), (1,0), (1,1). Each has prob 1/4, so H(X,Y) = -4*(1/4 log₂(1/4)) = 2 bits.

## Relation to Individual Entropies

<!-- section: relation-to-marginals -->

From the definitions one can prove the inequalities

$H(X) \le H(X,Y) \quad\text{and}\quad H(Y) \le H(X,Y).$

Equality holds on the left when Y is a deterministic function of X (H(Y|X)=0).

The difference H(X,Y) - H(X) equals the conditional entropy H(Y|X).

**ELI5**

- Knowing both variables together can never be less uncertain than knowing just one of them.
- When the second variable is completely determined by the first, joint entropy collapses to the entropy of the first.

**Worked Example:**

Let X be uniform on {0,1,2,3} (H(X)=2 bits) and Y = X mod 2. Then possible pairs are only (0,0),(1,1),(2,0),(3,1), each prob 1/4. H(X,Y) = 2 bits, equal to H(X). Indeed Y is a function of X.

## Chain Rule for Entropy

<!-- section: chain-rule -->

The chain rule (or product rule) for entropy is

$H(X,Y) = H(X) + H(Y|X) = H(Y) + H(X|Y).$

It is the information-theoretic analogue of the chain rule for probabilities p(x,y)=p(x)p(y|x).

It immediately implies that conditional entropy is non-negative: H(Y|X) ≥ 0.

**ELI5**

- The total uncertainty about the pair = uncertainty about X plus whatever uncertainty remains about Y after you have seen X.
- You can start with either variable; the math works symmetrically.

**Worked Example:**

A fair coin X and an independent fair coin Y. H(X,Y) = 1 + H(Y|X) = 1 + 1 = 2, since seeing X tells you nothing about Y, H(Y|X)=H(Y).

If instead Y=X (same coin flipped once), H(X,Y) = H(X) + H(Y|X) = 1 + 0 = 1.

## Common Mistakes

- **Thinking H(X,Y) = H(X) + H(Y) always**: This holds only under independence; otherwise the joint is strictly smaller than the sum.
- **Confusing joint entropy with mutual information**: H(X,Y) is total uncertainty in the pair; I(X;Y) = H(X)+H(Y)-H(X,Y) is the shared part.
- **Forgetting that joint support may be smaller than product of marginal supports**: When variables are dependent many (x,y) pairs have probability zero, lowering the joint entropy.
- **Using marginal probabilities in the joint formula by mistake**: You must use the true joint p(x,y), not p(x)p(y).
- **Assuming H(X,Y) grows without bound as alphabet size grows**: It is bounded by log of the size of the joint support, which may be much smaller than |X|×|Y|.
- **Mixing up H(X,Y) and H(X|Y)**: The former is joint (total), the latter is conditional (residual after one is known).
