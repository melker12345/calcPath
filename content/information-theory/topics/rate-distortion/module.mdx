# Rate–Distortion Theory

Lossy compression limits, quantization, vector quantization, and distributed source coding.

Rate-distortion theory answers the question: what is the lowest rate (bits per symbol) at which a source can be compressed if we are willing to tolerate a certain average distortion D in the reconstruction? The answer is the rate-distortion function R(D). It is the lossy counterpart of the source coding theorem (which is the special case D=0 for lossless).

Lossy compression is essential for images, video, speech, and any situation where perfect fidelity is either impossible or not worth the bits.

## The Rate-Distortion Trade-off

<!-- section: rate-distortion-tradeoff -->

A source produces symbols X ~ p(x). A reproduction alphabet and a distortion measure d(x, \hat{x}) are given (e.g., squared error, Hamming distance). An encoder maps a block of n symbols to an index at rate R bits per symbol; the decoder produces \hat{X}^n with average per-symbol distortion ≤ D.

R(D) is the infimum of rates R such that there exist codes with average distortion ≤ D for large n.

**ELI5**

- If you allow the reconstructed picture to look a little blurry (D>0), you can describe it with far fewer bits than the lossless case.
- The more distortion you tolerate, the lower the rate you need; R(D) traces this fundamental curve.

**Worked Example:**

1. A grayscale image pixel quantized to 8 bits. If we allow mean-squared error $D$ corresponding to 'barely visible' artifacts, a good compressor might use only 1-2 bits per pixel instead of 8, achieving $4$-$8\times$ compression.

## Definition of the Rate-Distortion Function

<!-- section: definition-rd -->

The rate-distortion function is defined as the minimum mutual information over all conditional distributions (test channels) p(\hat{x}|x) that satisfy the distortion constraint:

$R(D) = \min_{p(\hat{x}|x) : \mathbb{E}[d(X,\hat{X})] \le D} I(X;\hat{X})$

where the minimum is over all p(\hat{x}|x) inducing E[d] ≤ D. The joint is p(x) p(\hat{x}|x).

By the rate-distortion theorem (Shannon), R(D) is the fundamental limit: no scheme can do better asymptotically, and there exist schemes that approach it.

**ELI5**

- R(D) measures the smallest amount of information the reproduction \hat{X} must 'share' with the source X while keeping average distortion under D.
- The optimizing p(\hat{x}|x) tells you the best statistical relationship between original and reproduction.

**Worked Example:**

1. For a Bernoulli(1/2) source with Hamming distortion, $R(D) = 1 - h_2(D)$ for $D \leq 1/2$ (where $h_2$ is binary entropy).
2. At $D=0$, $R=1$ bit; at $D=0.5$, $R=0$.

## Computation and Properties

<!-- section: computation-properties -->

R(D) is convex, decreasing, R(0) = H(X) (lossless), and R(D_max)=0 where D_max is the minimum distortion achievable with rate 0 (e.g., the best constant reproduction).

The Blahut-Arimoto algorithm (and other methods) can numerically compute R(D) by alternating optimization of the test channel and the output distribution.

Important properties: R(D) ≤ H(X) - h(D) or other lower bounds from entropy power etc.; the slope of R(D) is related to the Lagrange multiplier for the distortion constraint.

**ELI5**

- R(D) always starts at the entropy when D=0 and gracefully falls to zero when you allow enough distortion that you can just guess the reproduction without looking at the source.
- Convexity means the 'bang for the bit' gets worse as you demand lower distortion.

**Worked Example:**

1. Typical table for a unit-variance Gaussian source under squared-error distortion:
2. | D | R(D) (bits) |
3. | 1.0 | 0 |
4. | 0.5 | 0.5 |
5. | 0.25 | 1.0 |
6. | 0.1 | ≈1.66 |
7. | 0.01 | ≈3.32 |
8. $R(D) = \frac12 \log(1/D)$ for $D \leq 1$.

## Examples: Gaussian and Binary Sources

<!-- section: examples-gaussian-binary -->

For X ~ N(0, σ²) and d(x,\hat{x})=(x-\hat{x})², the rate-distortion function is 

$R(D) = \frac12 \log_2 \left( \frac{\sigma^2}{D} \right)$ for 0 ≤ D ≤ σ², and 0 otherwise.

The optimal test channel is additive Gaussian noise with variance D; the reconstruction is a scaled version of the observation.

For binary sources the expressions involve binary entropy as above.

These closed forms are rare; most sources require numerical optimization.

**ELI5**

- For a Gaussian, the best you can do is pretend the reconstruction is the original plus independent Gaussian 'noise' of variance D; the mutual information then gives exactly the formula.
- The formula shows that each halving of allowed distortion costs an extra 0.5 bits per sample.

**Worked Example:**

1. A zero-mean Gaussian with variance 4 ($\sigma^2=4$). To achieve MSE $D=0.5$, $R(0.5) = 0.5 \log_2(4/0.5) = 0.5 \log_2(8) = 0.5 \cdot 3 = 1.5$ bits per sample.
2. This is achievable by a Gaussian test channel with noise variance 0.5; the MMSE estimator at the decoder is $(4/(4+0.5)) Y$ or similar scaling.

## Common Mistakes

- **Confusing R(D) with the distortion-rate function D(R)**: They are inverses; R(D) gives min rate for given D, D(R) gives min distortion for given rate.
- **Thinking R(D) is achieved by simple scalar quantization**: Optimal R(D) generally requires vector quantization (block coding); scalar quantizers have a gap (1.53 dB for high-rate Gaussian).
- **Using R(0) = H(X) for lossy sources**: When D>0 the rate can be strictly less than the entropy; entropy is only the lossless limit.
- **Forgetting that the distortion measure must be specified**: Changing from MSE to absolute error or perceptual distortion completely changes R(D).
- **Assuming the optimal reproduction alphabet is the same as the source**: For some problems the best \hat{X} alphabet is smaller or even continuous even if X is discrete.
- **Ignoring the asymptotic nature**: R(D) is a limit as block length n→∞; for small blocks there is a penalty and practical codecs use heuristics + entropy coding of quantized coefficients.

The rate-distortion function R(D) is the central object of lossy source coding. For a given source distribution and single-letter distortion measure, R(D) is the smallest number of bits per symbol that must be sent so that the receiver can reconstruct the source sequence with average distortion no larger than D.

It is both a lower bound (converse) that no code can beat and, by the rate-distortion theorem, an asymptotically achievable rate.

## The Rate-Distortion Function R(D)

<!-- section: the-rd-function -->

Formally,

$R(D) = \inf_{p(\hat{x}|x):\ \mathbb{E}[d(X,\hat{X})]\leq D} I(X;\hat{X})$

where the infimum is over all conditional pmfs (or pdfs) p(\hat x | x) such that the expected distortion is at most D. The infimum is achieved by some test channel p^*(\hat x | x) that is usually not the same as the forward channel that would be used in a communication problem.

R(D) is a non-increasing convex function of D, with R(0) = H(X) (or h(X) for continuous) and R(D) = 0 for all D ≥ D_max where D_max is the distortion of the best zero-rate reproduction (e.g. the value that minimizes E[d(X, c)] over constants c).

**ELI5**

- R(D) draws the curve 'how many bits do I really need if I'm allowed this much average error?'
- Because it is an infimum over all possible ways the reproduction can statistically depend on the source, it gives the absolute best possible compression performance.

**Worked Example:**

For any source, at D=0 the only allowed test channels have \hat X = X with probability 1, so I(X; \hat X) = H(X) and thus R(0) = H(X). This recovers the lossless source coding theorem as the special case of rate-distortion theory.

## Parametric Computation via the s-Parameter

<!-- section: parametric-s -->

It is often convenient to work with the Lagrangian formulation. For a slope parameter s ≤ 0 define

$R_s = \min_{p(\hat x|x)} [ I(X;\hat X) + s \mathbb{E} d(X,\hat X) ]$

Then the minimizing test channel for each s gives a point (D(s), R(s)) on the R(D) curve, where D(s) = - dR_s / ds (by envelope theorem). Varying s from 0 to -∞ traces the entire convex curve from (D_max, 0) to (0, H(X)).

This parametric form is the basis of the Blahut-Arimoto algorithm and of most analytic derivations.

**ELI5**

- Instead of fixing the distortion budget D and minimizing rate, we penalize distortion with a price s and minimize the combined 'cost' rate + s·distortion. The right price s gives exactly the operating point we want.
- Sweeping the price from 'distortion is free' (s=0) to 'distortion is infinitely expensive' (s→-∞) draws the whole R(D) frontier.

**Worked Example:**

For the Gaussian quadratic case the optimal test channel is always additive Gaussian noise whose variance is chosen so that total MSE = D. The parameter s turns out to be s = -1/(2 D ln 2) (in bits); plugging in recovers the familiar R(D) = ½ log(σ²/D).

## Closed-Form Examples and Bounds

<!-- section: closed-form-bounds -->

Only a handful of sources have known closed-form R(D):

- Bernoulli(p) with Hamming distortion: R(D) = h_2(p) - h_2(D) for D ≤ min(p,1-p) (more generally the binary R(D) formula).
- Gaussian with quadratic distortion: R(D) = ½ log(σ²/D) for D ≤ σ².
- Some other exponential-family sources with matching distortion measures.

Useful bounds include the Shannon lower bound R(D) ≥ h(X) - h(D) (for difference distortion measures), and various upper bounds obtained from specific test channels (e.g., the Gaussian test channel for non-Gaussian sources gives an upper bound on R(D)).

**ELI5**

- For the 'nicest' sources (Gaussian, binary) we have exact formulas that look like 'entropy minus the entropy of the allowed error'.
- For everything else we compute numerically or bound R(D) between a lower bound (often the Shannon lower bound) and the performance of a good practical scheme.

**Worked Example:**

Binary source with p=0.1 (entropy h_2(0.1)≈0.469). For Hamming D=0.05, R(D) = h_2(0.1) - h_2(0.05) ≈ 0.469 - 0.286 = 0.183 bits. This is strictly less than the entropy, as expected for lossy.

## Common Mistakes

- **Plotting or using R(D) for D larger than the support of the distortion measure**: R(D) is defined to be zero past the point where a constant reproduction already meets D.
- **Forgetting that R(D) depends on both the source distribution and the distortion measure**: Changing d(·,·) from squared error to absolute error or to a perceptual metric changes the function completely.
- **Confusing the test channel p(\hat x | x) with a communication channel**: The test channel is an artificial mathematical object used only to compute the mutual information; it need not be realizable as a physical channel.
- **Using the high-rate approximation R(D) ≈ h(X) - ½ log(2πe D) outside its validity region**: The approximation is good only when D is small relative to the source variance and the optimal reproduction is fine-grained.
- **Thinking that achieving R(D) is easy in practice**: The theorem is asymptotic in block length; for finite n one pays a penalty, and finding the optimal codebook or test channel is hard.
- **Overlooking that R(D) is an average per-symbol quantity**: For a long block the total rate is n R(D) + o(n); the o(n) term can matter for short blocks or when strong concentration is required.

Quantization is the irreversible mapping of a continuous-amplitude signal (or a high-precision discrete signal) onto a finite set of representation levels. It is the fundamental lossy step in every digital acquisition system: A/D converters, image sensors, audio codecs, and learned compressors all rely on it.

While quantization always introduces error, careful design minimizes the perceived or measured distortion for a given number of bits.

## Scalar Quantization

<!-- section: scalar-quantization -->

A scalar quantizer Q maps a real number x to the nearest (or otherwise chosen) reproduction level \hat{x}_i from a codebook of size 2^b (for b-bit quantizer). The decision regions (Voronoi cells) partition the real line.

A uniform quantizer spaces levels equally with step size Δ; a non-uniform quantizer places levels more densely where the source density is high or where distortion is more costly.

The quantization error e = x - Q(x) is usually modeled as additive noise, approximately uniform over [-Δ/2, Δ/2] for fine uniform quantization and high-resolution sources.

**ELI5**

- Think of rounding a real number to the closest value on a ruler with only 256 marks. The marks are the representation levels; everything between two marks gets snapped to one or the other.
- Uniform spacing is like a regular ruler; non-uniform spacing crowds more marks where the signal spends most time (e.g., near zero for speech).

**Worked Example:**

An 8-bit uniform quantizer for audio with peak-to-peak range 2 V (from -1 V to +1 V) has Δ = 2/256 ≈ 0.0078 V. A sample x=0.123 V is quantized to the level closest to it, say 0.121 V or whatever the grid gives; error |e| ≤ Δ/2.

## Optimal Quantizer Design (Lloyd-Max)

<!-- section: lloyd-max -->

The Lloyd-Max conditions for a minimum-MSE quantizer are:

1. Each reproduction level \hat{x}_i is the centroid (conditional mean) of its decision region: \hat{x}_i = E[X | X ∈ V_i].

2. Each decision boundary between \hat{x}_i and \hat{x}_{i+1} lies at the midpoint (for MSE): ( \hat{x}_i + \hat{x}_{i+1} )/2 .

These yield an iterative algorithm: start with some levels, assign points to nearest, update levels to centroids, repeat until convergence. For many common densities (Gaussian, Laplacian) the solution is tabulated or can be computed numerically.

For non-MSE distortion measures the conditions generalize (e.g., median for absolute error).

**ELI5**

- The best place to put the reconstruction value for a bin is the average of all the values that fall into that bin (the centroid).
- The best place to draw the fence between two bins is right in the middle between their two reconstruction values.

**Worked Example:**

For a standard normal source and 2-bit (4-level) Lloyd-Max quantizer, the optimal levels are approximately ±0.4528, ±1.510, with boundaries at 0 and ±0.9816 (symmetric). The resulting MSE is about 0.117, better than a uniform quantizer's 0.125 or so.

## Performance and High-Rate Approximations

<!-- section: performance-highrate -->

For high rate (small Δ), the signal-to-quantization-noise ratio (SQNR) for a uniform quantizer on a signal with variance σ² and peak factor is approximately

SQNR ≈ 6.02 b + 10 log_{10}(σ² / (Δ²/12)) dB,

where b is bits. Each extra bit buys roughly 6 dB improvement.

Companding (compressor + uniform quantizer + expander) approximates the optimal non-uniform quantizer for a given density; μ-law and A-law are classic examples in telephony.

Vector quantization (VQ) jointly quantizes blocks and can approach the rate-distortion bound more closely than scalar methods, at the price of complexity (see separate topic).

**ELI5**

- Every extra bit of precision you buy halves the step size and therefore quarters the average squared error — a 6 dB SNR gain.
- For signals that are not uniformly distributed you get free improvement by spending your bits where the signal actually lives most of the time.

**Worked Example:**

A 16-bit uniform quantizer on a full-scale sine wave achieves theoretical SQNR around 98 dB (6.02*16 + 1.76 ≈ 98). Real ADCs fall a few dB short because of thermal noise, jitter, and non-idealities.

## Common Mistakes

- **Modeling quantization noise as white and signal-independent without checking**: At low rates or with correlated inputs the error is highly structured and can be signal-dependent.
- **Using uniform quantization on highly peaked sources**: You waste levels in the tails; a properly designed non-uniform or companded quantizer uses far fewer bits for the same MSE.
- **Confusing number of levels 2^b with the rate in bits after entropy coding**: If the quantizer outputs are highly nonuniform you can entropy-code the indices and achieve rate close to the entropy of the quantized symbols, which may be << b.
- **Assuming Lloyd-Max gives the global optimum**: The iteration finds a locally optimal quantizer; different initializations can yield different performance; for scalar quantizers it is usually fine.
- **Forgetting overload distortion**: If the source exceeds the outermost decision boundaries the error is huge; good designs balance granular and overload regions.
- **Thinking scalar quantization is always far from R(D)**: For many sources at high rate the gap is only about 1.53 dB (πe/6 factor) between scalar and the ultimate vector rate-distortion limit; entropy-coded scalar quantization closes most of the gap.

Vector quantization (VQ) replaces a k-dimensional input vector with the nearest codevector from a finite codebook of size 2^{kR}. By quantizing several samples together it exploits statistical dependencies (correlation, linear or nonlinear) that scalar quantization ignores, yielding lower distortion for the same rate (or lower rate for the same distortion).

As dimension k → ∞, the best VQ performance approaches the rate-distortion bound R(D) for the source; scalar quantization generally cannot.

## Why Vector Quantization Beats Scalar

<!-- section: why-vector -->

A scalar quantizer makes independent decisions on each coordinate. When the source components are correlated, the joint distribution is concentrated on a lower-dimensional manifold; a vector quantizer can place its codepoints along that manifold and achieve better packing.

Even for independent components, VQ can shape the quantization noise spectrum or use lattice structures that have better normalized second moment than the cubic lattice of independent scalar quantizers.

The famous result is that the ultimate gain of VQ over entropy-coded scalar quantization is bounded (about 1.53 dB for high-rate MSE Gaussian), but the gain over plain scalar (without entropy coding of indices) can be much larger.

**ELI5**

- Instead of rounding each pixel or sample by itself, you look at a small block of them together. The block might look like 'a smooth gradient' or 'an edge'; you have a dictionary of typical blocks and pick the closest one.
- Correlations mean many combinations of scalar values never occur; VQ wastes no codewords on impossible combinations.

**Worked Example:**

Two correlated zero-mean unit-variance Gaussians with correlation ρ=0.9. Scalar 1-bit quantizers on each give MSE ≈ 0.36 per component (total 0.72). A 2-dimensional VQ with 4 codewords (rate 1 bit per sample) can place points at (±a,±a) with appropriate a and achieve noticeably lower joint MSE by aligning with the principal axis of the data cloud.

## Design: The LBG / Generalized Lloyd Algorithm

<!-- section: lbg-design -->

The Linde-Buzo-Gray (LBG) algorithm is the vector generalization of Lloyd-Max iteration:

1. Start with an initial codebook (e.g., random or from a training set split).
2. Assign each training vector to the nearest codevector (Voronoi partition).
3. Replace each codevector by the centroid of the vectors assigned to it.
4. Repeat until the codebook (or distortion) stabilizes.

It is exactly k-means clustering with the Euclidean (or other) distortion. Good initialization (splitting, pairwise nearest neighbor) and multiple restarts help avoid poor local minima. For very large codebooks or dimensions, tree-structured VQ, product VQ, or lattice VQ are used as structured approximations.

**ELI5**

- Show the algorithm thousands of real example blocks from your data. Repeatedly: 'group the blocks by which dictionary entry they are closest to' then 'move each dictionary entry to the average of its group'.
- After many iterations the dictionary entries sit at the centers of the natural clusters in your data.

**Worked Example:**

Training a 256-codevector VQ (8 bits) on 4×4 image blocks (k=16, rate 0.5 bit/pixel). After LBG the codebook contains typical 4×4 patches: flat areas, edges at various angles, textures. Encoding a new block means finding the closest of the 256 patches (by MSE) and sending its 8-bit index.

## Performance, Complexity, and Structured VQ

<!-- section: performance-complexity -->

Full-search VQ encoding requires 2^{kR} distance computations per vector — exponential in dimension or rate, quickly impractical. Structured techniques (lattice quantization, trellis-coded quantization, multi-stage VQ, product codes) trade a small performance loss for polynomial complexity.

In practice, VQ appears inside hybrid codecs: transform coding + scalar quantization of coefficients + entropy coding often beats naive VQ because the transform approximately decorrelates and the entropy coder captures the remaining nonuniformity. Modern neural compressors learn both the transform and the 'quantization' (via soft or straight-through estimators) jointly.

**ELI5**

- Brute-force VQ is like having a huge photo album and for every new photo finding the most similar page by eye — works great but you cannot flip through millions of pages in real time.
- Clever structures (lattices, trees, stages) let you narrow the search quickly with only a small quality penalty.

**Worked Example:**

A lattice quantizer based on the hexagonal lattice in 2-D has about 0.17 dB better granular gain than the square (scalar) lattice for high-rate 2-D quantization. In higher dimensions the best known lattices (Leech, etc.) give several tenths of a dB improvement and admit fast encoding algorithms.

## Common Mistakes

- **Expecting VQ to always be used directly on raw samples**: Almost never; a linear or nonlinear transform (DCT, wavelet, autoencoder) is applied first so that the vector quantizer sees a more favorable distribution.
- **Confusing codebook size 2^{kR} with search complexity**: Even with a moderate codebook, naive search is expensive; fast search, tree search, or structured codebooks are mandatory for real-time use.
- **Thinking the LBG algorithm guarantees the globally optimal codebook**: It is a descent method that reaches a local minimum; initialization matters.
- **Ignoring the rate needed to describe the codebook itself**: For small data sets or one-shot compression the bits to send the codebook can dominate; universal or lattice quantizers avoid this.
- **Believing VQ always outperforms transform + scalar + entropy at the same total rate**: Well-designed scalar quantization after a good transform plus arithmetic coding of the indices is often competitive and far simpler.
- **Forgetting that in learned compression 'quantization' is often replaced by additive uniform noise during training**: This makes the whole pipeline differentiable so gradients can flow through the 'quantizer'.

When correlated side information is available, the fundamental rate-distortion tradeoff changes. Depending on whether the side information Y is available at the encoder, the decoder, both, or neither, we obtain different rate-distortion functions and regions.

The four canonical cases are: no side information (ordinary R(D)), side info at decoder only (Wyner-Ziv), side info at encoder only, and side info at both (conditional rate-distortion R_{X|Y}(D)).

These settings model many practical scenarios: distributed sensor networks, video coding with previous frames, communication with channel state information, etc.

## Rate-Distortion with Side Information at Decoder (Wyner-Ziv)

<!-- section: rd-si-decoder -->

This is the Wyner-Ziv problem: lossy compression of X with distortion D when Y is available only at the decoder. The minimal rate is R_{WZ}(D) = min I(X; U | Y) over auxiliaries U generated from X alone and reconstructions that may depend on Y.

For the quadratic-Gaussian source, R_{WZ}(D) = R_{X|Y}(D) — there is no rate loss from the side information being absent at the encoder. For other sources (e.g., binary Hamming) there is generally a strict gap R_{WZ}(D) > R_{X|Y}(D).

**ELI5**

- Your friend has a noisy version Y of your photo X. You must send a lossy description that lets him reconstruct a good version using his Y, but you don't know his exact Y when you compress.
- For Gaussian noise the math works out so that you pay exactly the same rate as if you had known Y; for other distortions you may pay a penalty.

**Worked Example:**

Gaussian X ~ N(0,1), Y = X + Z, Z ~ N(0,0.5), target MSE D=0.1. The conditional variance is 1/(1+1/0.5)=1/3. The rate when Y known at encoder is ½ log((1/3)/0.1) ≈ 0.737 bits. For this source the WZ rate is the same; the optimal auxiliary is a Gaussian test channel whose noise variance is chosen so the MMSE given Y meets D.

## Side Information at Encoder Only

<!-- section: rd-si-encoder -->

When Y is known only at the encoder (the 'helper' knows the side information), the problem is sometimes called source coding with side information at the encoder or the 'dual' of Wyner-Ziv.

The rate needed can be as low as R(D) - I(X;Y) in some cases, but the exact function involves a different auxiliary and is generally not the same as the other settings. The encoder can use Y to choose the quantization or binning, but the decoder has no Y.

This setting appears when the transmitter has channel state or correlated observations that the receiver does not.

**ELI5**

- You know both the clean photo X and a noisy version Y; the receiver has nothing. You can use your knowledge of Y to send a smarter description of X (perhaps describing only the 'difference' in a clever way), but the receiver still has to reconstruct without ever seeing Y.
- The benefit is usually smaller than when the decoder has the side info, because the decoder cannot 'subtract' Y itself.

**Worked Example:**

If Y = X exactly (perfect side info at encoder), the encoder can simply send nothing and the 'reconstruction' at the decoder can be a constant; but if the decoder does not know Y, the distortion may be large unless the constant is the right one. More interesting: when Y is a noisy observation, the encoder can allocate bits only to the innovation X - E[X|Y].

## Side Information at Both Encoder and Decoder (Conditional RD)

<!-- section: conditional-rd -->

When both encoder and decoder know Y, the problem reduces to ordinary rate-distortion on the conditional distribution of X given Y. The rate-distortion function is

R_{X|Y}(D) = min I(X; \hat X | Y)

where the min is over p(\hat x | x, y) such that E[d(X, \hat X)] ≤ D (the expectation is also conditional on Y in the natural way).

This is the smallest possible rate among all side-information configurations; extra side info at the encoder cannot hurt.

**ELI5**

- Both you and your friend have the noisy version Y. You only need to describe the 'new information' in X that is not already predictable from Y. Your friend uses his copy of Y to combine with your description.
- This is the most favorable case and gives the absolute lowest rate for a given fidelity.

**Worked Example:**

Same Gaussian numbers as above: R_{X|Y}(D) = ½ log( var(X|Y) / D' ) where D' is the target after the conditional variance. With var(X|Y)=1/3 and D=0.1 the rate is ½ log((1/3)/0.1) ≈ 0.737 bits — the lowest of the four cases.

## The General Rate-Distortion Region with Side Info

<!-- section: general-rd-si -->

When there are multiple sources or more complex side-information patterns (e.g., side info at some encoders but not others, or common and private side info), one obtains rate-distortion regions with several auxiliaries and Markov constraints.

The Heegard-Berger problem (side info at decoder may be different for different receivers) and the multiple-description problem with side info are further generalizations. Many of these regions remain open or only partially solved.

In all cases the inner bounds are obtained by test-channel + binning (quantize then Slepian-Wolf the indices, possibly with different binning rates for different receivers).

**ELI5**

- Real networks have asymmetric side information: some nodes have extra sensor data, others don't; some receivers have previous packets, others joined late.
- The optimal strategy is usually 'quantize to a good auxiliary description, then bin the description at rates that match what each receiver already knows.'

**Worked Example:**

In the Heegard-Berger setting with two receivers having different quality side information Y1 (good) and Y2 (bad), the encoder sends a common description plus a refinement; the good receiver uses the common + refinement + Y1, the bad receiver uses only the common + Y2. The achievable region involves two auxiliaries with nested Markov chains.

## Common Mistakes

- **Assuming that side information at the encoder is always as useful as side information at the decoder**: For lossy coding the decoder having the side info is usually more powerful (it can 'subtract' at reconstruction time); encoder-only side info helps mainly by allowing smarter quantization or power allocation.
- **Forgetting that in the Gaussian case Wyner-Ziv has no rate loss**: Many students remember only the binary case where there is a gap and incorrectly generalize.
- **Confusing conditional rate-distortion R_{X|Y}(D) with ordinary R(D)**: R_{X|Y}(D) ≤ R(D) always; the gap can be large when Y is highly informative about X.
- **Thinking the Wyner-Ziv achievability is 'just send the quantization index at rate R(D)'**: The binning step is essential; without it the rate would be the higher ordinary rate-distortion of the auxiliary, not the conditional mutual information.
- **Ignoring that the reconstruction function may depend on the side information**: \hat x = f(u, y) is crucial; many practical 'Wyner-Ziv' codecs forget to exploit Y fully at reconstruction and pay a performance penalty.
- **Believing all rate-distortion problems with side information have single-letter characterizations**: Several important multi-terminal settings (e.g., the general Heegard-Berger problem with arbitrary side-info quality) still lack complete single-letter solutions.

Wyner–Ziv coding is the lossy counterpart of Slepian–Wolf: compress a source X to a reproduction \hat X with average distortion ≤ D, when correlated side information Y is available only at the decoder, not at the encoder. The minimal rate is the Wyner-Ziv rate-distortion function R_{WZ}(D).

Surprisingly, for many important cases (including the quadratic-Gaussian case) R_{WZ}(D) equals the rate that would be needed if the encoder also had Y, i.e., there is no rate loss from the side information being decoder-only.

## Wyner-Ziv Problem Setup

<!-- section: wz-setup -->

Encoder sees only X^n, produces a message at rate R. Decoder sees the message and the side-information sequence Y^n ~ p(y|x) jointly with X, and produces \hat X^n with (1/n) sum d(X_i, \hat X_i) ≤ D.

No communication from decoder to encoder; the encoder cannot adapt to the particular realization of Y.

Classic application: video coding where the previous frame (or a frame at another camera) is available as side information only at the decoder.

**ELI5**

- You have a photo X that you want to send in compressed form. Your friend already has a similar but noisy photo Y. You must send a description that works without knowing exactly which noisy version your friend has; your friend uses his Y to help reconstruct a good version of your photo.
- The description has to 'cover' all possible Y's that might be at the decoder.

**Worked Example:**

X ~ N(0,1), Y = X + Z with Z ~ N(0, N_z) independent, quadratic distortion. Even though the encoder does not know Y, the minimal rate to achieve distortion D is the same as if it did know Y (for the Gaussian case).

## Rate-Distortion with Decoder-Only Side Information

<!-- section: wz-rd -->

The Wyner-Ziv rate-distortion function is

R_{WZ}(D) = \min I(X; U | Y)

where the min is over auxiliary U and reconstruction functions \hat x(u,y) such that E[d(X, \hat x(U,Y))] ≤ D and the Markov chain U — X — Y holds (U is generated from X alone).

When side information is also at the encoder the minimal rate is the conditional rate-distortion R_{X|Y}(D) = min I(X; \hat X | Y) under the distortion constraint. In general R_{WZ}(D) ≥ R_{X|Y}(D); equality holds for the quadratic Gaussian source.

**ELI5**

- The auxiliary U is like a 'description' that the encoder prepares; the decoder, with Y, extracts the best \hat X from U and Y.
- The mutual information is conditioned on Y because Y is free side information at the decoder; the encoder still has to 'pay' for the uncertainty that remains after Y.

**Worked Example:**

For binary source with Hamming distortion and binary side information, there is generally a rate loss: R_{WZ}(D) > R_{X|Y}(D). The optimal auxiliary is a 'test channel' that adds extra 'virtual' noise before binning.

## Binning + Quantization Achievability

<!-- section: wz-binning -->

The optimal scheme quantizes X to an auxiliary codeword U (as in ordinary rate-distortion), then bins the U codewords exactly as in Slepian-Wolf (the bin index is the message sent). The decoder, given the bin index and its Y, finds the unique U in the bin that is jointly typical with Y, then produces the reconstruction \hat x(U, Y).

The binning rate is essentially I(U; X) - I(U; Y) = I(U; X | Y), which is exactly the expression for R_{WZ}.

This unifies quantization (to create U) with distributed lossless compression of the quantization index (binning).

**ELI5**

- First pretend you are doing ordinary lossy compression to an intermediate 'U' description (ignoring that the decoder has Y).
- Then compress the U index in a Slepian-Wolf fashion as if U were a source correlated with the decoder's Y. The decoder uses Y to disambiguate which U was sent inside the bin.
- The net rate is the 'extra' information in U beyond what Y already provides.

**Worked Example:**

In the quadratic Gaussian case the optimal U is X + W where W ~ N(0, variance chosen for the target D). The 'quantization' to U is like adding the right amount of noise; then the binning rate needed turns out to equal the conditional rate-distortion, so no loss.

## Common Mistakes

- **Assuming there is always a rate penalty for decoder-only side information**: For quadratic Gaussian (and some other cases) R_{WZ}(D) = R_{X|Y}(D); the side information at the encoder would not help the rate.
- **Confusing Wyner-Ziv with ordinary rate-distortion**: In ordinary R(D) there is no side information at all; here the decoder has free Y that must be accounted for in the test channel and reconstruction function.
- **Thinking the encoder can simply quantize as if Y were known**: The encoder must use a codebook and binning that works for the worst-case (or average) Y; it cannot adapt the quantization to the actual Y.
- **Forgetting that the reconstruction function at the decoder depends on Y**: \hat x = f(u, y), not just a function of u alone. This is crucial for achieving the bound.
- **Believing practical Wyner-Ziv video codecs have no loss**: Real implementations have losses from finite dimension, correlation estimation, and the fact that practical video is neither Gaussian nor memoryless; the information-theoretic result is still the benchmark.
- **Mixing up the auxiliary U with the final reconstruction \hat X**: U is an auxiliary 'description' variable; the actual output is a (usually deterministic) function of U and the side information Y.

Slepian–Wolf coding is the problem of lossless compression of two (or more) correlated discrete memoryless sources when the encoders cannot communicate with each other but the decoder receives all compressed streams and can exploit the correlation.

Surprisingly, the total rate needed is still the joint entropy H(X,Y); the encoders can operate separately at rates as low as the conditional entropies. This is the first and most famous result in network information theory for source coding.

## Distributed Source Coding Problem

<!-- section: distributed-sc-problem -->

Two sources X and Y are drawn i.i.d. from a joint pmf p(x,y). Encoder 1 sees only the X^n sequence and compresses it to a message at rate R1 bits per symbol. Encoder 2 compresses Y^n to rate R2 independently. The joint decoder, upon receiving both messages, must reconstruct both sequences with vanishing error probability.

No communication is allowed between the two encoders; they do not even know each other's realizations.

**ELI5**

- Two sensors in different rooms measure correlated temperatures or images. Each sensor must send a compressed description over its own link to a central server, but the sensors cannot talk to each other. The server, seeing both compressed descriptions, should recover both measurements perfectly.
- The correlation is 'free side information' that only the decoder sees.

**Worked Example:**

X and Y are the two bits of a (3,1) repetition codeword sent over independent BSCs; they are highly correlated. Each encoder can compress its own bit string at rate close to the conditional entropy H(X|Y) without knowing the other, yet the joint decoder recovers both perfectly.

## The Slepian-Wolf Rate Region

<!-- section: sw-rate-region -->

The achievable rate region is all (R1, R2) such that

R1 ≥ H(X | Y)

R2 ≥ H(Y | X)

R1 + R2 ≥ H(X, Y)

The region is a pentagon (for two sources). The corner point (H(X|Y), H(Y)) corresponds to encoder 2 sending at full entropy (no compression exploiting correlation) while encoder 1 sends only the 'innovation' given Y.

The surprising fact is that the sum rate equals the joint entropy even though encoders are separate — the same as if they could pool their observations before compressing.

**ELI5**

- You need at least H(X|Y) bits from the first sensor because even if the decoder already knew Y perfectly, it would still need that much to resolve the uncertainty in X.
- The total bits from both sensors cannot be less than H(X,Y) because together they must resolve the pair (X,Y).
- The magic is that these lower bounds are simultaneously achievable without the encoders talking.

**Worked Example:**

For binary symmetric correlation (X uniform, Y = X ⊕ Z, Z ~ Bern(p)), H(X,Y) = 1 + h(p), H(X|Y) = h(p). The region allows (R1,R2) = (h(p), 1) or (1, h(p)) or any point on the sum-rate line R1+R2 = 1+h(p) with each above h(p).

## Binning and Achievability (Channel Coding Dual)

<!-- section: binning-achievability -->

The achievability uses random binning, which is the dual of random channel coding.

Encoder 1 generates a random partition of the typical X^n sequences into 2^{n R1} bins and sends only the bin index. Encoder 2 does likewise for Y^n.

The decoder, knowing both bin indices, looks for the unique jointly typical pair (x^n, y^n) that lies in the announced bins. Because the number of bins is large enough, with high probability there is only one such pair inside the intersection of the two bins.

This is exactly analogous to the decoder in a multiple-access channel or to syndrome decoding; the 'bins' play the role of cosets.

**ELI5**

- Each encoder throws its possible sequences into a huge number of buckets (bins) at random and tells the decoder only which bucket its sequence landed in.
- The decoder knows the statistical relationship between X and Y, so among all sequences in bucket A for X and bucket B for Y there is (with high prob) only one pair that 'makes sense' together (jointly typical). That must be the one that was observed.

**Worked Example:**

For the binary symmetric case, encoder 1 can use a good channel code for the 'virtual' BSC(p) whose 'syndrome' (bin index) is sent at rate h(p). The decoder, treating Y^n as side information (the 'received word' for the virtual channel), uses the syndrome to correct the 'errors' and recover X^n. This is precisely syndrome-based Slepian-Wolf coding and is dual to channel coding.

## Common Mistakes

- **Thinking separate encoders must incur a rate penalty compared with joint encoding**: The sum rate is exactly H(X,Y) — the same as joint encoding. Only the individual rates have extra constraints.
- **Confusing Slepian-Wolf with Wyner-Ziv**: Slepian-Wolf is lossless; Wyner-Ziv allows lossy reconstruction of one source when the other is available as side information only at the decoder.
- **Assuming the encoders need to know the correlation parameter p**: In theory the binning is designed for a known p; in practice one uses universal or compound codes or estimates p.
- **Believing practical Slepian-Wolf is just 'send syndromes of two separate codes'**: While that works for certain correlation models (virtual channel is BSC), general p(x,y) requires more sophisticated binning or graph-based codes (LDPC for SW).
- **Forgetting that the decoder must be able to handle the union of many possible pairs**: The error analysis uses the union bound over the 'wrong' pairs that happen to land in the same bins and are jointly typical.
- **Thinking the result requires the sources to be i.i.d. binary**: The theorem holds for any discrete memoryless joint distribution; extensions exist for continuous, Markov, etc. sources.
