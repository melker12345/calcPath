# Information Measures

Entropy, joint and conditional entropy, mutual information, relative entropy, and entropy rate.

Information theory is the mathematical study of the quantification, storage, and communication of information. Founded by Claude Shannon in his landmark 1948 paper "A Mathematical Theory of Communication," it provides the theoretical limits for data compression, reliable transmission over noisy channels, and many modern technologies from the internet to machine learning.

It separates the engineering problem of communication from the semantics of the messages being sent.

## What is Information?

<!-- section: what-is-information -->

Information is fundamentally about reducing uncertainty. If an outcome is certain (probability 1), learning it tells you nothing new. The rarer or more surprising an event, the more information it carries when observed.

Formally, information theory models sources as random variables or stochastic processes and measures information via probabilities.

**ELI5**

- Imagine a weather forecast: "It will be sunny" on a desert planet carries almost zero information because it is expected. "It will snow" would be highly informative.
- A fair coin flip resolves one bit of uncertainty; learning it happened "heads" cuts the possibilities exactly in half.

**Worked Example:**

A message arrives saying a specific person won the lottery where there were 1,000,000 equally likely tickets. The self-information of that specific outcome is I = -log₂(1/1 000 000) = 20 bits approximately. Most other messages (someone else won) would carry far less surprise on average.

## Quantifying Information

<!-- section: quantifying-information -->

The self-information (or surprisal) of a specific outcome x with probability p(x) is

$I(x) = -\log_2 p(x)$ bits.

The entropy of a discrete random variable X is the expected self-information:

$H(X) = -\sum_x p(x) \log_2 p(x) = \mathbb{E}[-\log_2 p(X)]$.

Entropy is always non-negative, maximized for the uniform distribution over a given support, and is measured in bits when using log base 2 (or nats with natural log).

**ELI5**

- Entropy answers: on average, how many fair yes/no questions do you need to identify the value of X?
- A perfectly fair coin has 1 bit of entropy. A coin that always lands heads has 0 bits of entropy—you already know the answer.

**Worked Example:**

Consider a random variable with four outcomes having probabilities 1/2, 1/4, 1/8, 1/8.

H = -(1/2 log₂(1/2) + 1/4 log₂(1/4) + 1/8 log₂(1/8) + 1/8 log₂(1/8))

= 0.5*1 + 0.25*2 + 0.125*3 + 0.125*3 = 0.5 + 0.5 + 0.375 + 0.375 = 1.75 bits.

## Applications and Scope

<!-- section: applications-scope -->

Information theory gives fundamental limits: the source coding theorem bounds lossless compression by H(X); the noisy-channel coding theorem bounds reliable transmission rate by channel capacity C. These are not engineering suggestions but mathematical facts that no scheme can beat in the asymptotic regime.

Modern applications include zip files, error-correcting codes in hard drives and wireless, deep learning (cross-entropy loss), and cryptography.

**ELI5**

- Think of entropy as the "irreducible size" of a message on average. You can get close to it with good codes but never sustainably below it.
- Channel capacity tells you the highest speed (in bits per transmission) at which you can talk over a noisy phone line with arbitrarily small error probability.

**Worked Example:**

A source produces independent symbols A (p=0.5), B (p=0.25), C (p=0.25). Its entropy is 1.5 bits per symbol. For a long sequence of 1000 symbols the typical total information content is about 1500 bits, so any lossless compressor must output at least roughly 1500 bits on average.

## Common Mistakes

- **Confusing information with meaning or importance**: A random string of digits can have high information content (entropy) yet be completely meaningless to a human.
- **Using the wrong logarithm base**: For bits always use log₂; using log₁₀ or ln changes the numerical value by a constant factor (log₂e ≈ 1.4427 for nats to bits).
- **Treating entropy as a property of a single string**: Entropy is defined for a distribution (ensemble); Kolmogorov complexity is the individual-string analogue.
- **Assuming maximum entropy is always uniform over the alphabet**: It is, but only when all symbols are possible; if the support size is smaller the max entropy is lower.
- **Mixing up H(X) and I(X;Y)**: Entropy H(X) is uncertainty in X alone; mutual information I(X;Y) is the reduction in that uncertainty when Y is observed.
- **Forgetting that real sources have memory**: The simple i.i.d. entropy formula is only the starting point; entropy rate accounts for dependence in sequences.

Entropy is the central quantity of information theory. It measures the average amount of information (or equivalently, uncertainty) produced by a stochastic source. Claude Shannon introduced it in 1948 as the unique (up to scaling) function satisfying a few intuitive axioms about how information should behave when sources are combined or observed.

Entropy sets the fundamental limits for both data compression and many other information-processing tasks.

## Definition of Entropy

<!-- section: definition -->

For a discrete random variable X taking values in a finite alphabet with probability mass function p(x), the Shannon entropy is

$H(X) = - \sum_{x} p(x) \log_2 p(x).$

When the sum is over an infinite support the series must converge. By convention 0 log 0 = 0.

Entropy can be interpreted as the average number of bits needed to describe the outcome of X using an optimal code.

**ELI5**

- Entropy is the "average surprise." Every time you see a realization of X you get on average H(X) bits of new information.
- If you already know the distribution, entropy tells you how hard it is to guess the next draw on average.

**Worked Example:**

1. A biased coin with $P(\text{heads})=0.9$, $P(\text{tails})=0.1$ has entropy
2. $H = -0.9 \log_2(0.9) - 0.1 \log_2(0.1) \approx 0.469$ bits.
3. You can guess "heads" and be right $90\%$ of the time, so on average far less than 1 bit of uncertainty per flip.

## Properties of Entropy

<!-- section: properties -->

Key properties:

- $H(X) \ge 0$, with equality iff X is deterministic.
- $H(X)$ is maximized by the uniform distribution: $H(X) \le \log_2 |\mathcal{X}|$.
- $H(f(X)) \le H(X)$ for any function f (data processing).
- Entropy is concave in the distribution p.
- For independent X,Y: $H(X,Y) = H(X) + H(Y)$.

The binary entropy function $h(p) = -p\log_2 p -(1-p)\log_2(1-p)$ appears everywhere in information theory.

**ELI5**

- You cannot create uncertainty by relabeling or processing the outcomes deterministically; you can only lose information.
- Independent coins add their entropies: two fair coins together have 2 bits.

**Worked Example:**

1. Consider two independent fair bits $X$ and $Y$. Then $H(X,Y) = H(X) + H(Y) = 2$ bits.
2. The joint distribution is uniform over 4 possibilities, confirming $\log_2(4) = 2$.

## Calculating Entropy

<!-- section: calculating -->

To compute H for a given pmf, simply plug into the formula. For small alphabets it is straightforward; for large ones one often uses the binary entropy or closed forms (e.g., geometric, Poisson in limit).

A useful table of small examples:

| Distribution          | Probabilities          | Entropy (bits) |
|-----------------------|------------------------|----------------|
| Deterministic         | (1)                    | 0              |
| Fair coin             | (0.5, 0.5)             | 1              |
| Biased coin (0.9)     | (0.9, 0.1)             | ≈0.469         |
| Fair 4-ary            | (0.25 each)            | 2              |
| Example in text       | (0.5,0.25,0.125,0.125) | 1.75           |

**ELI5**

- Make a table of probabilities, multiply each by -log2 of itself, and average (sum). That is the entropy.
- When symbols are very unequal, entropy drops because you can predict the common ones easily.

**Worked Example:**

1. Symbols $A,B,C,D$ with $p = 0.4, 0.3, 0.2, 0.1$.
2. $H = -0.4 \log_2(0.4) -0.3 \log_2(0.3) -0.2 \log_2(0.2) -0.1 \log_2(0.1)$.
3. $\approx 0.4 \cdot 1.322 + 0.3 \cdot 1.737 + 0.2 \cdot 2.322 + 0.1 \cdot 3.322 \approx 1.971$ bits.

## Common Mistakes

- **Using log base e or 10 without converting**: The numerical value changes; remember the conversion factor log₂e ≈ 1.4427 when going from nats.
- **Forgetting the negative sign**: -sum p log p is positive; dropping the minus gives a negative nonsense value.
- **Treating 0 log 0 as undefined in code**: In math we define lim p→0 p log p = 0; always add a guard in implementations.
- **Confusing entropy of the distribution with entropy of a sample**: H is a property of p, not of any particular draw or finite sequence.
- **Assuming H(X|Y) = H(X) - something always**: Only the mutual information term subtracts; conditional entropy can be computed directly.
- **Ignoring that entropy rate ≠ single-symbol entropy for processes with memory**: Real text, images, etc. have dependence that must be modeled for accurate rate.

Joint entropy H(X,Y) extends the notion of entropy from a single random variable to a pair (or more generally a vector) of random variables. It quantifies the total uncertainty in the combined outcome of X and Y together.

It is the natural starting point for discussing how two variables interact information-theoretically.

## Joint Entropy Definition

<!-- section: definition -->

Let (X,Y) be a pair of discrete random variables with joint probability mass function p(x,y). Then

$H(X,Y) = -\sum_{x,y} p(x,y) \log_2 p(x,y).$

It is simply the ordinary entropy of the "big" random variable whose possible values are all pairs (x,y) that have positive probability.

**ELI5**

- Think of (X,Y) as one bigger roll of the dice. H(X,Y) tells the average surprise when you see both numbers at once.
- If X is the first card and Y the second card drawn without replacement, H(X,Y) measures uncertainty about the ordered pair.

**Worked Example:**

Two independent fair bits. There are four equally likely pairs: (0,0), (0,1), (1,0), (1,1). Each has prob 1/4, so H(X,Y) = -4*(1/4 log₂(1/4)) = 2 bits.

## Relation to Individual Entropies

<!-- section: relation-to-marginals -->

From the definitions one can prove the inequalities

$H(X) \le H(X,Y) \quad\text{and}\quad H(Y) \le H(X,Y).$

Equality holds on the left when Y is a deterministic function of X (H(Y|X)=0).

The difference H(X,Y) - H(X) equals the conditional entropy H(Y|X).

**ELI5**

- Knowing both variables together can never be less uncertain than knowing just one of them.
- When the second variable is completely determined by the first, joint entropy collapses to the entropy of the first.

**Worked Example:**

Let X be uniform on {0,1,2,3} (H(X)=2 bits) and Y = X mod 2. Then possible pairs are only (0,0),(1,1),(2,0),(3,1), each prob 1/4. H(X,Y) = 2 bits, equal to H(X). Indeed Y is a function of X.

## Chain Rule for Entropy

<!-- section: chain-rule -->

The chain rule (or product rule) for entropy is

$H(X,Y) = H(X) + H(Y|X) = H(Y) + H(X|Y).$

It is the information-theoretic analogue of the chain rule for probabilities p(x,y)=p(x)p(y|x).

It immediately implies that conditional entropy is non-negative: H(Y|X) ≥ 0.

**ELI5**

- The total uncertainty about the pair = uncertainty about X plus whatever uncertainty remains about Y after you have seen X.
- You can start with either variable; the math works symmetrically.

**Worked Example:**

A fair coin X and an independent fair coin Y. H(X,Y) = 1 + H(Y|X) = 1 + 1 = 2, since seeing X tells you nothing about Y, H(Y|X)=H(Y).

If instead Y=X (same coin flipped once), H(X,Y) = H(X) + H(Y|X) = 1 + 0 = 1.

## Common Mistakes

- **Thinking H(X,Y) = H(X) + H(Y) always**: This holds only under independence; otherwise the joint is strictly smaller than the sum.
- **Confusing joint entropy with mutual information**: H(X,Y) is total uncertainty in the pair; I(X;Y) = H(X)+H(Y)-H(X,Y) is the shared part.
- **Forgetting that joint support may be smaller than product of marginal supports**: When variables are dependent many (x,y) pairs have probability zero, lowering the joint entropy.
- **Using marginal probabilities in the joint formula by mistake**: You must use the true joint p(x,y), not p(x)p(y).
- **Assuming H(X,Y) grows without bound as alphabet size grows**: It is bounded by log of the size of the joint support, which may be much smaller than |X|×|Y|.
- **Mixing up H(X,Y) and H(X|Y)**: The former is joint (total), the latter is conditional (residual after one is known).

Conditional entropy H(Y|X) measures how much uncertainty remains about Y once the value of X is known. It is the information-theoretic version of "residual variance" or "prediction error" when trying to guess Y from X.

It plays a starring role in the definitions of mutual information, channel capacity, and rate-distortion functions.

## Definition of Conditional Entropy

<!-- section: definition -->

The conditional entropy of Y given X is the expected entropy of the conditional distributions:

$H(Y|X) = \sum_x p(x) H(Y|X=x) = -\sum_{x,y} p(x,y) \log_2 p(y|x).$

It can also be expressed as H(Y|X) = H(X,Y) - H(X).

**ELI5**

- After you look at X, on average how many more bits do you still need to describe Y?
- If X is the month and Y is the temperature in a city, H(Y|X) is the average remaining uncertainty about temperature once you know which month it is.

**Worked Example:**

X fair coin, Y = X (copy). Then given X you know Y perfectly, so H(Y|X=x)=0 for both values of x. Thus H(Y|X)=0.

## Interpretation and Properties

<!-- section: interpretation-properties -->

H(Y|X) ≥ 0 always.

H(Y|X) = H(Y) exactly when X and Y are independent.

H(Y|X) ≤ H(Y), with strict inequality unless I(X;Y)=0.

Conditioning on more variables can only decrease (or keep) the conditional entropy: H(Y|X,Z) ≤ H(Y|X).

**ELI5**

- Learning X can never make you more uncertain about Y on average; it can only help or do nothing.
- If X tells you nothing about Y they are independent and H(Y|X)=H(Y).

**Worked Example:**

X uniform on {1,2,3,4}, Y = X mod 2. Then H(Y)=1 bit (fair bit). But given X, Y is known, so H(Y|X)=0 < 1.

## Chain Rule and Relations

<!-- section: chain-rule-relations -->

The chain rule H(X,Y) = H(X) + H(Y|X) is the most useful identity.

It rearranges to H(Y|X) = H(X,Y) - H(X) and symmetrically H(X|Y) = H(X,Y) - H(Y).

Also, H(X,Y|Z) = H(X|Z) + H(Y|X,Z) (chain rule conditioned on Z).

**ELI5**

- Total joint uncertainty = uncertainty in X plus the extra uncertainty in Y once X is known.
- You can "pay" the uncertainty of X first, then pay only the conditional part for Y.

**Worked Example:**

Suppose H(X)=2, H(Y)=3, H(X,Y)=4. Then H(Y|X) = 4-2 = 2 bits, and H(X|Y) = 4-3 = 1 bit. X is more informative about Y than vice versa.

## Common Mistakes

- **Thinking H(Y|X) can be negative**: It cannot; conditional entropy is always ≥ 0.
- **Confusing H(Y|X) with I(X;Y)**: I(X;Y) = H(Y) - H(Y|X) is the reduction; H(Y|X) is what is left.
- **Writing H(Y|X) using only marginals p(y)**: You need the conditional p(y|x) or the joint.
- **Forgetting that H(Y|X) is an average**: For some particular x the conditional entropy H(Y|X=x) can be larger than H(Y), even though the average cannot.
- **Assuming H(Y|X)=0 implies X determines Y with probability 1 for every x**: It only requires that for almost every x (under p(x)) the conditional is deterministic.
- **Mixing conditional entropy with conditional probability**: They are related but one is a number (uncertainty), the other a function of the realized value.

Mutual information I(X;Y) is the most important derived quantity in information theory after entropy. It quantifies how much knowing one variable reduces uncertainty about the other — the strength of the statistical relationship in information units (bits).

It is symmetric, non-negative, and zero exactly when the variables are independent. It is the currency of communication: channel capacity is the maximum mutual information between input and output.

## Definition via Entropies

<!-- section: definition -->

Mutual information can be defined in several equivalent ways:

$I(X;Y) = H(X) - H(X|Y) = H(Y) - H(Y|X) = H(X) + H(Y) - H(X,Y).$

The last form shows it is the difference between the sum of marginal entropies and the joint entropy.

**ELI5**

- I(X;Y) is "how many bits of X's uncertainty are resolved by learning Y."
- It is also "how much smaller the joint description is compared with describing X and Y separately."

**Worked Example:**

X and Y identical fair bits. H(X)=1, H(Y)=1, H(X,Y)=1. Thus I(X;Y) = 1 + 1 - 1 = 1 bit. They share everything.

## Interpretation as Information Transfer

<!-- section: interpretation -->

I(X;Y) = 0 ⇔ X ⊥ Y (independent).

I(X;Y) = H(X) when Y determines X perfectly (H(X|Y)=0).

In a communication setting, X is the message, Y the received signal; I(X;Y) is the average information successfully transferred.

**ELI5**

- Zero mutual information means "Y tells you nothing about X on average" — like two separate fair coins.
- Maximum mutual information (equal to H(X)) means "Y tells you everything about X" — a perfect copy or noiseless observation.

**Worked Example:**

X uniform on 8 symbols (H(X)=3). After a noisy channel the receiver's uncertainty H(X|Y)=0.8 bits on average. Then I(X;Y)=3-0.8=2.2 bits were conveyed on average per transmission.

## Properties and Special Cases

<!-- section: properties-special -->

- Non-negativity: I(X;Y) ≥ 0.
- Symmetry: I(X;Y)=I(Y;X).
- Data processing inequality: if X—Y—Z Markov then I(X;Z) ≤ I(X;Y) ≤ min(H(X),H(Y)).
- I(X;X) = H(X) (self-information of the variable).
- For independent X,Y: I(X;Y)=0; for deterministic relation: I(X;Y)=H(X) when Y=f(X) bijectively.

**ELI5**

- Processing a signal can only lose or preserve information about the original; it cannot create more.
- Mutual information between a variable and itself is just its entropy.

**Worked Example:**

X fair bit, Z = X (perfect), Y any function of X. Then I(X;Y) = I(X;Z) = 1, illustrating the DPI chain.

## Common Mistakes

- **Thinking mutual information can be negative**: It cannot; I(X;Y) ≥ 0 always, with equality for independence.
- **Confusing I(X;Y) with H(X,Y) or conditional entropy**: I is the *shared* part; H joint is total, conditional is the leftover.
- **Assuming I(X;Y) = 0 implies no dependence of any kind**: It implies statistical independence; functional or higher-order dependence that averages to zero info is still possible in other senses but rare.
- **Forgetting symmetry**: Although defined asymmetrically via H(X)-H(X|Y), the value is always equal to H(Y)-H(Y|X).
- **Using I(X;Y) when the right quantity is I(X;Y|Z)**: Conditional mutual information is needed when there is a third variable (common cause, etc.).
- **Believing large I(X;Y) implies causation**: Mutual information is purely correlational/statistical; it says nothing about direction or mechanism.

Relative entropy, also known as Kullback-Leibler (KL) divergence, is a fundamental non-symmetric measure of how one probability distribution p differs from another reference distribution q. It is not a distance (it fails symmetry and the triangle inequality) but it is the right notion of "divergence" for many information-theoretic and statistical purposes.

It appears in the definition of mutual information, in large-deviations theory, in variational inference, and as the natural loss for comparing distributions.

## Definition of KL Divergence

<!-- section: definition -->

For discrete distributions p and q (with p absolutely continuous w.r.t. q),

$D_{KL}(p \| q) = \sum_x p(x) \log_2 \frac{p(x)}{q(x)} = \mathbb{E}_{x\sim p}\Bigl[\log_2 \frac{p(x)}{q(x)}\Bigr].$

When the support of p is not contained in that of q the divergence is defined to be +∞.

**ELI5**

- Imagine you designed a compression code assuming the world follows q. If the world actually follows p, on average you waste D(p||q) extra bits per symbol.
- It is the penalty you pay for using the wrong model.

**Worked Example:**

Let p be Bernoulli(0.9), q Bernoulli(0.5). Then

D(p||q) = 0.9 log₂(0.9/0.5) + 0.1 log₂(0.1/0.5) ≈ 0.531 bits.

Using the fair-coin code lengths on biased-coin data wastes about half a bit per flip on average.

## Interpretation as Surprise or Inefficiency

<!-- section: interpretation -->

D(p||q) can be rewritten as H(p,q) - H(p) where H(p,q) is the cross-entropy -sum p log q.

Thus D(p||q) = cross-entropy - entropy = "extra nats/bits when coding with q instead of the true p-optimal code."

It is also the expected log-likelihood ratio under p.

**ELI5**

- Cross-entropy is the average number of bits you actually use when your code lengths are -log q. Subtract the true entropy and you get the waste.
- In hypothesis testing it is the expected log odds in favor of the true hypothesis when data are from p.

**Worked Example:**

Suppose true p has entropy 0.469 bits (the biased coin). The cross-entropy when using q=0.5 is 1 bit (because -log2(0.5)=1 for every outcome). Waste = 1 - 0.469 = 0.531 bits, matching D(p||q).

## Properties and Use in Information Theory

<!-- section: properties-uses -->

- D(p||q) ≥ 0, =0 iff p=q a.e.
- Not symmetric: D(p||q) ≠ D(q||p) in general.
- Joint convexity in the pair (p,q).
- I(X;Y) = D( p(x,y) || p(x)p(y) ), i.e., mutual information is the KL between the joint and the product of marginals.
- In rate-distortion and channel coding, many optimal quantities are characterized via KL minimizations or projections.

**ELI5**

- The "distance" from p to q is different from q to p because the expectation is taken under different measures.
- Mutual information is exactly how far the joint is from independence (the product measure) in KL.

**Worked Example:**

For any X,Y we have I(X;Y) = D(p_{X,Y} || p_X p_Y). When X,Y independent the joint equals the product and I=0. When they are dependent the joint "sticks out" from the independence assumption by exactly I bits on average.

## Common Mistakes

- **Treating KL as a symmetric distance**: D(p||q) is directed; the "cost" of approximating p by q is not the same as approximating q by p.
- **Ignoring infinite KL when supports mismatch**: If your model q assigns zero probability to something that can happen under p, D(p||q)=∞ and maximum-likelihood will be ill-behaved.
- **Using KL when total variation or other distances would be more natural**: KL is sensitive to tails and has information-theoretic meaning; sometimes Hellinger or TV distance is preferable for pure approximation.
- **Confusing D(p||q) with H(p) - H(q)**: The difference of entropies is unrelated in general; KL involves the cross term.
- **Forgetting that the expectation is under p**: The weights are p(x), not q(x); this is why it is not symmetric.
- **Applying the discrete formula directly to continuous densities without care**: For continuous variables the KL involves the densities and can be negative for differential entropy terms, but the divergence itself remains non-negative.

For a single random variable, entropy tells the uncertainty per draw. For a stochastic process that generates a sequence X₁, X₂, X₃, … the right quantity is the entropy *rate* — the average information per symbol in the long run.

This is the quantity that actually governs the compressibility of long files, the capacity of channels with memory, and the fundamental limits for sources with temporal structure (speech, video, text, DNA).

## Definition of Entropy Rate

<!-- section: definition -->

The entropy rate of a discrete-time stochastic process is defined (when the limit exists) as

$h = \lim_{n\to\infty} \frac{1}{n} H(X_1, X_2, \dots , X_n).$

For stationary processes an equivalent expression is

$h = \lim_{n\to\infty} H(X_n | X_1, \dots, X_{n-1}).$

**ELI5**

- Imagine writing a very long book. The total number of bits needed to describe the whole book divided by the number of letters (or words) approaches the entropy rate.
- Early symbols may be surprising; after many symbols the next one becomes more predictable on average.

**Worked Example:**

An i.i.d. source with per-symbol entropy H=2 bits has, for any block length n,

(1/n) H(X₁..Xₙ) = 2 exactly. Hence the rate h=2 bits per symbol.

## Properties for Different Processes

<!-- section: properties -->

- i.i.d.: rate = single-symbol entropy H(X).
- Stationary Markov of order k: rate = H(X_{n} | X_{n-k} … X_{n-1}), the entropy of the next symbol given the last k symbols.
- The rate is always ≤ the marginal entropy H(X_i).
- For ergodic stationary processes the limit exists and equals the almost-sure limit of (1/n) log (1/p(X₁..Xₙ)) for typical sequences (AEP link).

**ELI5**

- Memoryless sources have rate equal to ordinary entropy.
- Sources with strong memory (repetitive patterns, grammar) have much lower rate than their single-symbol entropy suggests.

**Worked Example:**

A binary Markov chain that stays in the current state with probability 0.9 (rare flips). The stationary marginal entropy is nearly 1 bit, but H(next | current) ≈ 0.47 bits (same calc as biased coin). The entropy rate is therefore ≈0.47 bits/symbol, not 1.

## Relation to Compression and AEP

<!-- section: asymptotic -->

The entropy rate is the fundamental limit for lossless compression of long sequences from the process: you can compress at rates arbitrarily close to h bits per symbol but not below, asymptotically.

It is also the quantity appearing in the asymptotic equipartition property for processes: typical long sequences have probability roughly 2^{-n h}.

**ELI5**

- A good compressor for English text will achieve roughly the entropy rate (in bits per character) on long documents, not the naïve per-letter entropy.
- The AEP says that almost all long realizations look "typical" and are describable with about n h bits.

**Worked Example:**

A file of 1 000 000 symbols from a process with rate 1.2 bits/symbol can be losslessly compressed, in the limit of good coding, to roughly 1.2 million bits (150 kB). No scheme can do substantially better for typical files.

## Common Mistakes

- **Using the single-symbol entropy for a source with memory**: This overestimates the true compressibility; the rate is the correct number.
- **Forgetting that the limit may not exist**: Some contrived non-stationary processes have no rate; stationarity + ergodicity guarantees existence.
- **Confusing entropy rate with the entropy of the stationary distribution**: The stationary dist gives marginal entropy; the rate accounts for the transition structure.
- **Thinking (1/n)H(X₁..Xn) is always decreasing**: It is non-increasing, but the conditional form H(X_n|past) may not be monotone.
- **Applying the i.i.d. formula when symbols are dependent**: Real sources almost always have dependence; you must model the conditional distributions.
- **Neglecting that rate is an asymptotic concept**: For short sequences the effective rate (1/n)H(block) can be noticeably higher than the limit.
